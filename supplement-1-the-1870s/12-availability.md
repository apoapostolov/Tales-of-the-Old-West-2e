# 12. What Existed and What Did Not

> _"SHE BROUGHT THE LAMP DOWN OFF THE HIGH SHELF AND TRIMMED THE WICK AND LIT IT WITH A MATCH STRUCK ON THE IRON OF THE STOVE. THE FLAME CAUGHT AND STEADIED AND THREW HER SHADOW LONG AGAINST THE WALL."_

The out-of-time object breaks the world entirely. A cowhand drinking a Coca-Cola in 1879. A homesteader loading a .30-30. A sheriff thumbing a flashlight. A saloon burning electric bulbs over the bar. The decade runs from January 1, 1870, through December 31, 1879. The technology required a year to a decade to bleed from a New York factory down to a Texas ranch. A telephone sat in a Boston laboratory in 1876; it did not reach the west Texas grass for another twenty years. The rule stands absolute: default to _not yet_.

## 1. The Technology Timeline

### By 1870

The heavy repeating rifles stood available—the Henry, the Spencer, the Winchester '66. Men shot cartridge revolvers rarely; conversions of the old percussion iron were just appearing. The telegraph lines followed the railroad iron and boxed in every major city. The transcontinental telegraph connected the coasts in 1861. The clerk charged fifty cents to a dollar for a ten-word message; men did not use it casually. The transcontinental railroad joined at Promontory Summit on May 10, 1869. The Union Pacific ran from Omaha and the Central Pacific ran from Sacramento, pushing the heavy iron branches outward through the decade.

The steam-powered paddlewheelers ran the heavy freight and passenger traffic down the Missouri, the Mississippi, the Ohio, the Sacramento, and the Columbia. The stagecoaches crossed the dirt everywhere. The railroads killed the great overland lines, but the short local stages still ran. Photographers shot wet-plate collodion images. Daguerreotypes and tintypes sat in every parlor, while the professional studio shot glass-plate negatives. The flexible film did not exist. The women pumped Singer sewing machines in the towns.

The mercantile supplied canned food. The tin can dated to the 1810s. Borden shipped canned milk in 1856. Underwood pressed deviled ham into tins in 1868. A man with heavy silver bought canned peaches, tomatoes, and oysters in the town store. The grocer stocked refined sugar. The green coffee beans shipped west in bulk; the cowhand parched them in the skillet until Arbuckles packaged the pre-roasted Ariosa coffee in 1865. The bartender poured bonded whiskey—Old Crow, Old Forester—at the high-dollar tables, and cut the unbonded rotgut with river water at the long bar.

### Arriving 1870–1873

Colt stamped out the Single Action Army—the Peacemaker—in 1873. A man possessed it only after 1874. The Winchester Model 1873 levergun in .44-40 left the factory the same year. The Colt SAA adopted the .44-40 cartridge in 1878; the cowboy chambered one heavy bullet for both the rifle and the belt gun. Levi Strauss and Jacob Davis patented the copper-riveted canvas overalls on May 20, 1873; they sold nothing before that date, and the 501 stamped numbering arrived later. Joseph Glidden patented the twisting barbed wire in 1874 and boxed it by the ton from 1875 onward. It fenced the open grass through the late 1870s and 1880s; a horseman did not cut a barbed-wire strand in 1873. Sharps machined the heavy Model 1874 buffalo rifle for the great hide runners that year. Remington brought out the Sholes-Glidden typewriter from 1874; the office managers bought it slowly, and the machine stood rare in any western county before the 1880s. The cheap steel stock-water windmills pumped across the dirt plains from the late 1870s.

### Arriving 1874–1876

Alexander Graham Bell patented the telephone hard on March 7, 1876. The first commercial line opened a New Haven exchange on January 28, 1878. A man spoke into no telephone in 1875. He spoke into no telephone in 1876 unless he stood inside a Boston laboratory. Bissell marketed the carpet sweeper in 1876. Men threw the heavy Yablochkov-candle electric arc lights over city streets in the late 1870s, but no man wired a saloon or a boarding house. Edison proved the hot incandescent glass bulb in his October 1879 laboratory. The commercial grid arrived in 1882. No electric incandescent light burned anywhere in the 1870s West.

### Arriving 1877–1879

Edison filed the phonograph patent in December 1877. He cut the tinfoil cylinder publicly in 1878. It sat on the parlor tables of the rich as a novelty; it played no continuous music. The cowhand in 1879 drank to the fiddle and the piano, nothing recorded. The 1878 carbon microphone amplified the telephone wire but put no instrument in the bunkhouse. James Ritty patented the mechanical cash register in 1879. The merchants bought it mostly through the 1880s.

### Not yet anywhere in the 1870s

Smokeless powder did not exist. The French compounded Poudre B in 1884. Every 1870s gun burned black powder; the heavy white smoke hung dead in the street during every gunfight. The hunter shot no bolt-action smokeless Mauser in 1875. The Winchester Model 1894 levergun firing .30-30 smokeless left the line in 1894. Hugo Borchardt shaped the semi-automatic pistol frame in 1893. The reliable 1870s Western pistol ran strictly single-action; men cut rudimentary double-action revolvers (the Adams, the Tranter, the 1877 Colt Lightning), but the iron failed mechanically and broke in the dirt.

The automobile sat as an engineering diagram in an 1880s carriage shop. The penny-farthing bicycle ran the crushed stone in the Eastern cities from 1870 but missed the West. The safety bicycle—chained and balanced—is 1885. The Wrights lifted the powered flyer in 1903. Marconi sparked the radio in 1895. Edison spun the Kinetograph picture wheel in 1891. The cassette, the vinyl, the television, the screen—no.

Swift pushed the iced refrigerator meat car onto the railhead in 1878, but the house refrigerator is a 1920s machine. The 1870s home cooled the milk exactly as the saloon cooled the beer—with massive blocks of crosscut northern lake ice, dumped from the insulated railcars and sledded to the insulated box by the town iceman. Bayer synthesized aspirin powder in 1899. A man swallowed no aspirin on the 1870s grass. He injected no penicillin until 1928, no heavy antibiotic until the 1940s. The 1870s town doctor carried a bag holding bonded whiskey, opium, morphine by the hypodermic glass, quinine for the swamp fever, mercury for the syphilis, bismuth, camphor, ipecac, paregoric, and laudanum. Patent barkers laced the syrup with cocaine after 1885. Coca-Cola poured in 1886. Heroin is Bayer 1898. Jenner cracked smallpox with the cowpox scrape in 1796; the 1870s doctor vaccinated against it. Pasteur found the rabies cure in 1885; the diphtheria antitoxin broke in the 1890s. The 1870s doctor watched his patients die completely of diphtheria, scarlet fever, cholera, typhoid, and tuberculosis. He knocked his surgical patients flat with the 1846 ether bottle or the 1847 chloroform rag. The frontier tooth-puller possessed the gas sometimes; mostly, the man strapped his patient to the heavy chair.

## 2. Goods — What Is in the Mercantile

A general store in 1875 carries the inventory stacked to the rough framing. A man looking for an item not on this list verifies the iron fact before he writes it into the scene.

### Foodstuffs

The clerk scoops flour, cornmeal, oats, and barley directly from the canvas sack. He sells loaf sugar and granulated white sugar, and heavy brown sugar. He hacks salt from the block or pours the granulated dust. He sells the coffee—the Arbuckles' Ariosa pre-roasted beans from the late 1860s, or the raw green beans for parching. He weighs out the black tea and occasionally the green coastal tea. He shovels the pinto, the red, and the navy beans. The lard sits in the tin drum. The salt pork floats in the brine barrel. The butcher slices the bacon from the salted side or hacks the ham whole. The barrel holds the square hardtack, both army-surplus and town-baked, alongside the soda crackers. He cuts the heavy cheese directly from the wheel by the pound. The farmer trades his local butter and eggs.

Borden's pushes the condensed canned milk from 1856, and Eagle Brand from 1853. The tins of tomatoes, peaches, oysters, sardines, and Underwood deviled ham sit on the shelf. The clerk pulls the barrel pickles with the tongs. He draws the vinegar and the thick New Orleans cane molasses or Vermont sorghum from the barrel spigot. The women lay up the dried apples, peaches, and raisins. The spice rack holds the pepper, cinnamon, nutmeg, cloves, allspice, and ginger. The baker buys the 1868 yeast cakes; the chuckwagon cook nurses his sourdough starter. Royal Baking Powder ships from 1866.

The modern banana sits nowhere; it hits the eastern seities slowly in the 1880s and skips the 1875 frontier entirely. The orange ships from California or Florida over the rails and commands a heavy dollar. The modern cereal—granola from 1863, the corn flake waiting for 1894—does not exist; no man eats flakes from a bowl in the 1870s.

### Drinks

The bartender pours bonded whiskey at three to five dollars the bottle, and the raw rotgut at one or two. He taps the German lager from the draft barrel or the cold bottle; the heavy refrigerated rail cars drop the beer kegs at the depot by the late 1870s. The California vineyards push the domestic wine from the late 1860s; the parlor houses pour the imported champagne at five dollars the bottle. The city fountains blow the carbonated soda water. The 1886 Coca-Cola and the 1898 Pepsi stay in the future. Hires Root Beer hits the urban glass in 1876 but largely misses the frontier through 1875. The pharmacist dispenses the thick sarsaparilla syrup from the marked bottle. The women press the lemonade when the lemons arrive.

### Tobacco

The working man chews the square brown plug cut from the block. The 1858 Bull Durham fills his pipe. He twists his own quirly with the loose tobacco and the rice paper. Only the Eastern dandy smokes the pre-rolled cigarette in 1875 (Bonsack patents the rolling machine in 1881). The Cuban-American rollers push the heavy cigars for a nickel or a five-dollar gold piece. Men pack the Missouri Meerschaum corncob (1869), the briar, and the white clay pipe.

### Clothing

The local bootmaker hand-stitches the custom riding boot for fifteen to thirty dollars; the store sells the ready-made for five to ten. The heavy-heeled, narrow-toed cowboy boot shapes itself slowly through the decade; the standard 1870 cowhand rides in the square Wellington or the military boot. John B. Stetson blocked his _Boss of the Plains_ fur-felt hat in 1865; it commands five to ten dollars. Levi Strauss riveted his heavy canvas pants in 1873, pricing the copper studs at a dollar fifty. Men rode in the tough wool trousers. The mercantile stacked the ready-made linen and cotton shirts alongside the large red and blue paisley bandanas. The cowhand wore the heavy wool vest. He shielded the weather with the heavy canvas duster, the blanket coat, or the raw buffalo-hide winter robe. The men strapped themselves into the red one-piece union suit from 1868 against the cold.

The women cut their clothes from the heavy cloth bolts. The bustle shaped the silhouette through the decade. The working woman scrubbed in the calico or the gingham. She wore the corset strapped over the heavy whalebone or steel. She let the hoop skirt die and replaced it with the bustle entirely. She worked in the heavy leather gloves and walked in the knitted cotton or wool hosiery pinned by the garter.

### Hardware and tools

The merchant stacked the heavy iron-bodied hammer, chisel, plane, saw, brace, axe, hatchet, sledge, pick, and shovel. The carpenter drove the square, flat-tipped cut nails until the wire nails broke the market in the late 1880s. He turned slotted screws; the Phillips crosshead is 1933. John Deere forged the polished steel plow in 1837; it dominated the grass by 1870, with the riding sulky plow coming by 1875. McCormick pushed the reaper from 1834, and the heavy mowers leveled the wheat frontier. The men burned the heavy steam threshing rigs from the 1860s. The massive steam tractor rolled the dirt from the 1850s and broke the prairie fully from the 1880s. The internal combustion block stayed tied down in the late-1870s laboratory.

### Household goods

The heavy kerosene lamp burned universally from the refined petroleum, driving out the whale oil. The housewife dipped the tallow candle or bought the manufactured stearin. She struck the 1830s friction match or the 1855 sulfur safety lucifer. She boiled the lye soap in the yard or bought the Procter & Gamble 1879 Ivory bar. She scrubbed against the ribbed washboard and cranked the 1858 wringer tub. She heated the sad iron directly on the black cast-iron stove. The stove—a sheet-iron camp box or an Acme cast-iron range with the water reservoir—dominated the room. The wooden icebox insulated the heavy block ice. The Singer stitched the cloth. The 1858 Mason jar held the summer fruit. The bottled patent medicine stood thick on the shelf.

### Stationery and writing

The steel-nib pen and the heavy glass inkwell wrote the orders. The quill failed. The 1884 Lewis Waterman fountain pen sat a decade out. The 1761 Faber pencil and the 1830s mechanical pencil pushed the graphite. The 1858 rubber eraser wiped it. The clerks wrote the foolscap. The Remington Sholes-Glidden typewriter stamped ink from 1874, but the frontier clerk rarely saw the machine before 1880. The 1820s carbon paper layered the copies.

### Patent medicine

The Hostetter's Bitters poured out hundred-proof whiskey. The Lydia Pinkham's Vegetable Compound fixed the women's nerves. Dr. Williams sold the Pink Pills. The druggist weighed out the paregoric, the laudanum, and the morphine for the glass hypodermic syringe left over from the Civil War amputations. The coughing man drank the opiate syrup. The doctor dealt the calomel mercury and the blue mass. The barker did not drop cocaine into the syrup until the late 1880s. No heroin (1898), no aspirin (1899).

## 3. Services — What a Town Has

A county-seat town of fifteen hundred in 1875 holds the following on the dirt street.

The heavy mercantile. The saloon, and a cattle town holds twenty. The hotel and the boarding house. The stable, the livery, and the feed yard. The blacksmith firing the forge, often shoeing the horses and hooping the wagon wheels. The town doctor, or the circuit rider sharing the county. The dentist rarely; the doctor or the blacksmith pulls the teeth. The lawyer in any county seat. The small bank. The weekly newspaper printing the wire. The telegraph office clacking in any railroad town. The express office (Wells Fargo, American Express). The stage office. The post office, operating out of the mercantile. The land office moving the paper deeds. The railroad depot where the line runs. The saddler and harness maker. The gunsmith in the heavy towns. The tinsmith. The cobbler and bootmaker. The tailor. The barber and the adjoining bath house. The Chinese laundry where the Chinese community stands. The restaurant or chop house. The church. The one-room school. The courthouse in the county seat. The jail. The undertaker, operating out of the carpenter's shop. The brothel, the dance hall, the parlor house. The resident photographer in the stone building, the itinerant photographer working the tent.

What the town does _not_ hold. No hospital. The hospitals stood in the Eastern cities; the frontier town bled the sick in the home or the doctor's parlor, though the Sisters of Charity ran the cots in the heavy Western towns. No public library. The Carnegie steel money built the libraries in the 1890s. No fire department. The volunteers ran the bucket brigade. No sewer system. The town dug the privies, the outhouses, the cesspools. The municipal pipe did not lay beneath the small town dirt until the twentieth century. No indoor plumbing. The grand hotels piped it, and the rich built it in the 1880s, but the 1870s town pumped the water outside. No electric lighting. The commercial grid fired in 1882. No telephone exchange. New Haven wired it in 1878; the West strung the poles in the 1880s. No movie theater, no radio station, no record store.

## 4. Transportation — What Carries a Man

The horse carries the man. A man rides forty to fifty miles in a killing day, twenty-five in a working one. The pack mule hauls two hundred pounds of dead weight; the pack horse carries less. The oxen pull the freight wagon at two miles an hour in the mud; the horses pull it at three or four. The buggy, the buckboard, and the spring wagon carry the light passenger traffic.

The rail carried a man from New York to San Francisco in seven days by 1879, breaking the trip in Chicago and Omaha. First class cost a hundred dollars, second class forty, the wooden emigrant car twenty. The stockcar hauled the cattle; the hopper sorted the grain. The Pullman sleepers folded down the beds from 1864 for three dollars the night. The 1870s iron ran the following: the Union Pacific (Omaha to Promontory); the Central Pacific (Sacramento to Promontory); the Kansas Pacific (reaching Denver in 1870); the Atchison Topeka and Santa Fe (reaching La Junta by 1875, bleeding south through New Mexico until 1879); the Texas and Pacific (pushing eastern Texas through the decade); the Northern Pacific (reaching Bismarck by 1873, breaking in the Panic, finishing in 1883); the Southern Pacific (hitting Yuma by 1877); the Denver and Rio Grande (striking south to Pueblo in the late 1870s).

A man cannot buy a ticket from Dodge City to Tombstone in 1875. The Atchison hit Dodge in 1872, but the iron did not reach Tombstone. A man cannot ride the Texas and Pacific in 1873 where the track has not been laid. The referee checks the physical map of the iron before he writes the schedule.

The stagecoach ran the dirt where the iron stopped. Six to nine passengers crammed into the box. It covered four to six miles an hour through the dust, changing the hot teams at the swing stations. The ticket cost ten to fifteen cents a mile. The railroad broke the great overland lines, but the short runs held: Tucson to Phoenix, Cheyenne to Deadwood, El Paso to San Antonio.

A poor man walked three miles an hour, twenty-five in a long day. The steamboats pushed up the Missouri (Bismarck to St. Louis), the Mississippi, the Ohio, the Sacramento, the Columbia, the Snake, and the Colorado. A man in Wyoming Territory watched no steamboat.

## 5. Communication — What Reaches Whom and How Fast

The U.S. Postal Service rode to any town. The stamp cost three cents the half-ounce through the 1880s. The 1873 postcard cost a cent. The town did not deliver to the house; the man walked to the mercantile and asked the postmaster for his mail. An Eastern envelope reached the West in a week to ten days by 1879. Two Western towns passed a letter in three days. A letter from England crossed the Atlantic on the Cunard steamer to New York and rode the rail west, arriving in a month.

Western Union dominated the national telegraph wire from 1861. The clerk charged fifty cents to a dollar for the first ten words. The wire followed the railroad to every town. The 1866 transatlantic cable pushed the European news to New York in a day; the key tapped it to San Francisco in a matter of minutes. The operator sat in the railroad depot or the express office.

The local weekly newspaper printed the wire-service news. The rail dropped the New York and St. Louis dailies in the West; a man in Cheyenne read yesterday's _New York Times_ today. The Associated Press pumped the national wire to the local typesetters.

The telephone did not exist. Patent 1876. New Haven exchange 1878. The West strung the wire in the 1880s. A telephone in the 1870s breaks the world.

## 6. Food and Drink — What Is on the Table

The cattle-country breakfast for a working cowhand ran to coffee (Arbuckles', boiled, strong). The cook dealt the sourdough biscuit or the hardtack. The bacon or the salt pork fried in the grease. The cook warmed the beans over the fire from the night before. Sometimes the men broke eggs in the settled country where the farmers kept the chickens; sometimes they cut the beef.

The hotel breakfast in an 1875 county seat set the heavy table. Coffee or tea. Eggs, ham or bacon. Hash browns or the fried potatoes. The hot biscuits or the bread. Steak—the men ate the beefsteak for breakfast heavily. Pancakes cut with the syrup or the thick molasses. The stewed fruit. The oysters, standing canned or iced, where the town commanded them.

The Sunday dinner in an 1875 farmhouse set the roast meat. The beef, the ham, the pork roast, or the fried chicken. The smashed potatoes drowned in the brown gravy. The women boiled the greens in the spring, the squash in the fall, the root vegetables in the dead winter. The hot bread or the biscuits. The stewed dried fruit, the single pie, the heavy cake. Coffee or the cold buttermilk.

The chuck wagon cook fed the trail drive from the iron. Beans—sourdough beans boiling the day long in the Dutch oven. Beef or bacon. The sourdough biscuit. The hot Arbuckles' coffee. The stewed dried fruit—the peaches, the apricots, the raisins—when the drive held the money. Molasses. The wild onion when the cook cut it from the dirt.

The miner's camp table stood heavier, paid in the gold dust. Bacon, beans, the heavy biscuits, the beef when the rail dropped it. Coffee, heavy canned goods, the block tobacco. The hardrock miner pushing the drills in Leadville ate better than the Texas cowhand riding the trail.

The Mexican and Tejano table laid the corn or the wheat tortilla. The beans, the pinto _frijoles_. The red and the green chile, the rehydrated dried chiles boiled for the heavy sauces. (The commercial Hatch and the modern New Mexico chiles bred out later). The _carne asada_, the _carnitas_, the _machaca_, the _menudo_. The women folded the tamales against the Christmas cold. The Mexican coffee boiled with the cinnamon stick. The _atole_ washed the corn. The bittersweet Mexican chocolate broke on the tongue with the cinnamon.

The Chinese cook ran the table in the mining camps and the railroad cuts. The ships bumped the white rice from China through San Francisco. The salt fish, the dried fish, the dried mushroom. Pork, the chopped chicken. The bok choy and the Chinese vegetables pulled from the tight market gardens bordering the camps. The soy sauce, the cracked ginger, the raw garlic. The men drank the tea heavily; they left the coffee.

## 7. Money and Currency

### The coin

Gold: the twenty-dollar double eagle (one ounce of the yellow metal), the ten-dollar eagle, the five-dollar half eagle, the two-dollar-and-fifty-cent quarter eagle, the stamped gold dollar (running 1849–1889). Silver: the heavy dollar (the Liberty Seated dollar minted to 1873; the Trade dollar 1873–1885; the Morgan dollar 1878–1904). The half dollar. The quarter. The dime. The half dime to 1873. Nickel: the five-cent piece (the Shield nickel 1866–1883, the Liberty nickel stamped from 1883). Copper: the penny cent (the Indian Head cent 1859–1909). The two-cent piece (1864–1873). The three-cent piece (1851–1873).

### The greenback

The Federal paper currency. The U.S. Notes—the greenbacks—issued during the war stood in circulation. The national banks issued the National Bank Notes against the U.S. bonds. The Treasury resumed the specie payments in 1879; the greenback traded for the gold and the silver directly. Through the 1860s and the 1870s the greenback drifted against the gold. A man took his wages in the paper greenbacks worth ninety cents on the hard gold dollar.

### The Mexican coin

The Mexican silver peso crossed the dirt freely in the Southwest alongside the American dollar. The merchants valued it by the dead weight in the heavy trades, roughly matched to the U.S. silver dollar. The _real_—one-eighth of a peso, the _bit_ of _two bits, four bits, six bits_—survived as the counting unit. _Two bits_ makes twenty-five cents. _Four bits_ makes fifty. _Six bits_ makes seventy-five.

### Gold dust

In the rush dirt—California, Colorado, the Black Hills, and the later Yukon—the barkeep and the clerk weighed the gold dust at the counter. The merchant kept the small brass balance scale at the till.

### Credit

The long credit ran the frontier. The cowmen cleared the books with the mercantile at the shipping time once a year. The miners chalked the slate at the saloon. The doctors waited the long months for the folded money. The bankruptcy broke men constantly on the frontier because the credit chain stretched thin.

### Bank checks

The men wrote the paper checks through the decade. A merchant or a cowman paid the heavy debts by the drafted paper on a Kansas City bank or a San Francisco bank. The local banks discounted the paper checks from the distant towns.

### Paper bills

The 1869 _Rainbow_ Series one-dollar note carried Washington's dead face and the printed vignette of _Christopher Columbus in sight of land._ The U.S. Notes and the National Bank Notes crossed the counters through the decade. The Federal Reserve Notes—the modern dollar bill—waited until 1914. No man carried one in the 1870s.

## 9. Common Wrong-Decade Errors

A short field guide to the errors most often breaking the 1870s material life.

_The flashlight._ The battery-powered electric torch broke the dark in 1899. In the 1870s the man takes the oil lantern. _The matchbook._ The book matches folded in 1892. In the 1870s the matches rattled in a tin or a small wooden box, or the lucifers lay loose in the canvas pocket. _The flashbulb._ The magnesium flash powder burned from 1887. The photographer fired no flash before that; the studio portraits used the natural light or the heavy skylights. _The wristwatch._ The pocket watch rode alone; the wristwatch waited post-1900 for the military adoption of the First World War. A man in 1875 takes out his pocket watch, snaps the case open against the thumb, and consults it.

_The zipper._ The teeth patented in 1893 and spread post-1920. No zipper closed the 1870s clothing. The buttons, the hooks-and-eyes, the lacing, and the drawstrings tied the man. _The paper bag._ Margaret Knight's square-bottom machine folded the paper in 1879, just arriving. Earlier the merchant rolled the cones and the cylinders. The handled paper sack sat later. The mercantile wrapped the purchases in the brown paper and the heavy twine. _The Coca-Cola sign._ Hung 1886 onward. _The Marlboro Man._ The billboard painted him in 1955.

_The ten-gallon cowboy hat._ The high-crown white cowboy hat rode as a twentieth-century rodeo and movie shape. The 1870s working hat stood a _Boss of the Plains_ Stetson, flat-crowned, carrying a modest brim, built practical for the driving rain. _The cowboy quick-drawing from a low-slung holster._ The low-slung tied-down holster shot out of the twentieth-century movie camera. The period holsters rode high on the heavy belt. The man drew the pistol by lifting it up and forward, not by dropping the hand to the thigh. _The pearl-handled six-shooter._ It existed, but the metal stood rare. The standard SAA grip fit the heavy walnut. The pearl, the ivory, and the mother-of-pearl grips showed the gambler's or the showman's affectation.

_The cowboy in mirrored sunglasses._ The sunglasses for the general wear arrived in the early twentieth century. Eye protection in 1875 meant the pulled hat brim and the tied bandanna. (The tinted glasses for the medical use existed; Sam Colt wore the tinted spectacles). _The cowboy in a chrome belt buckle and a tooled belt._ The heavy show buckles rode the twentieth-century rodeo. The 1870s working cowboy's belt cut the plain leather, fastened by a simple brass or steel buckle two inches wide. _The cowboy listening to country music on the radio._ The radio tower broadcast in the twentieth century. _Singing_ made the music of the trail, by the men huddled around the fire and by the lone riders pushing the night-herd. _The whiskey served on the rocks._ The barkeep rarely dropped ice in a glass of whiskey in the 1870s saloon. The man drank the whiskey neat or cut with the well water (and sometimes the sugar—a _toddy_). The saloon held the heavy ice for the beer keg standing in the back room.
