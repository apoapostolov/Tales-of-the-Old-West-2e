# Chapter 11 — Frontier Survival and the Hunt

> _“SIXTY MILES TO THE NEXT WATER. WE MADE THE SPRING BY SUNDOWN. THREE DAYS WITHOUT SWEET WATER. THE PECOS WAS UP. WE WENT ON WITHOUT CROSSING.”_

The geography operates as a direct physical antagonist. A man riding the West must understand exactly what kills him on the Llano Estacado, exactly how far a horse can physically travel before it breaks down, and exactly how the professional buffalo runners extracted cash from the buffalo herds. This chapter details the hard mechanics of the land and the men who killed the game for a strict living.

---

## Part One — The Country

---

## 1. Water

Water constitutes the absolute physical limit of the western expansion. West of the 100th meridian—the line cutting down through the Dakotas, Nebraska, Kansas, Oklahoma, and central Texas—the physical rainfall drops below twenty inches a year. West of the 105th, it drops below fifteen. Nothing grows without direct irrigation. The available surface water completely dictates the travel routes.

### The Physical Requirement

A man walking in the summer heat requires a direct gallon a day strictly to live, and two gallons to execute manual labor. He dies in three days in 100°F sun. He dies in five days in cold weather. A saddle horse requires eight to twelve gallons a day. A working horse operating in heavy heat requires significantly more. A horse will refuse hard riding the day after a dry camp and dies in four to seven days without standing water. A heavy freight ox requires ten to fifteen gallons a day. A wagon train dragging ten families and their herd required a flowing creek every single night.

### The Sources

The springs anchored the trails. The heavy overland trails ran directly point-to-point between the known spring lines. The surface creeks and rivers frequently ran thick with white alkali or stood physically fouled by the massive buffalo herds crossing upstream. The travelers utilized the rock catchments—the _tinajas_ of the southern deserts holding the ancient rainwater. The homesteaders dug the dirt wells entirely by hand with iron shovels, sinking the shafts ten to sixty feet deep, and occasionally dropping two hundred feet on the dry high plains to hit the water table. The men drank the melted snow in the winter. A man riding the high plains after a heavy storm drank directly from the brown, stinking buffalo wallows holding the surface water.

### Bad Water

The alkali water crusted white on the high plains dirt. It triggered extreme physical diarrhea and killed both the stock and the men outright. The cattle and the buffalo standing in a stagnant hole churned it into thick mud. The stagnant water bred the heavy mosquitoes and carried typhoid, cholera, and dysentery.

### Purification

The travelers boiled the water to kill the disease when they had the fuel and the time. The massive consumption of boiled coffee operated fundamentally as a water purification mechanism. A man settling mud water in a wooden barrel threw in a heavy handful of ground cornmeal—the meal physically dragged the dirt to the bottom in minutes. A man utilized a charcoal filter constructed of layered sand and burnt wood inside a barrel. A man riding heavy pursuit drank what he found and took the physical risk.

## 2. Weather

The western weather killed men at a significantly higher rate than the Colt revolver. Each geographic region generated specific meteorological violence.

### The High Plains (Texas Panhandle through the Dakotas)

- **The Blue Norther.** A Canadian cold front dropping the physical temperature fifty degrees in two hours. The black-blue cloud bank rose on the northern horizon. The wind hit fifty miles per hour. The sudden freeze killed unprotected men and stock outright. Men froze entirely solid in the saddle. Common from October through April.
- **The Chinook.** A warm, dry winter wind blowing off the Rockies. The wind frequently spiked the temperature forty degrees in a single day and melted the heavy snowpack. The melted water refroze into a lethal sheet of hard ice when the sun dropped.
- **The Hailstorm.** A summer storm dropping ice from marble-sized to baseball-sized. The heavy ice killed the young calves and drove the massive herds directly into a blind stampede.
- **The Tornado.** A spring and summer storm throwing a distinct green cast across the western sky before the funnel dropped.
- **Lightning.** The absolute leading trigger for the cattle stampedes. A single heavy strike hitting the dirt near the herd drove two thousand steers to their feet in a blind run instantly.
- **The Hard Winter of 1886–87.** The snow started in November. The blizzards ran continuously through January. The physical temperature stood below −40°F entirely across Montana and Wyoming. The cattle starved in the hundreds of thousands. The massive die-up completely broke the open-range cattle economy.

### The Southwest Desert (Arizona, New Mexico, West Texas)

- **The Heat.** The summer afternoons ran over 110°F. A man walking without water died in hours.
- **The Cold Nights.** The desert temperature dropped a hard fifty degrees after sundown. A man sleeping without a heavy wool blanket froze in July.
- **The Dust Storm (Haboob).** A massive wall of brown dirt, frequently thousands of feet high, advancing ahead of a heavy thunderstorm. Physical visibility dropped to zero.
- **The Flash Flood.** A heavy summer thunderstorm breaking twenty miles upstream sent a solid wall of water rushing down the dry arroyos without warning. The dry wash a man camped in rapidly became the river that drowned him.
- **The Sandstorm.** The desert wind drove the sand at forty miles per hour. The blowing grit choked the eyes, the mouth, and the lungs.

### The Mountains (Rockies, Sierras, Cascades, Bitterroots)

- **The Snow.** The heavy snow ran from September through May at altitude.
- **The Avalanche.** The snow collapsed off the steep faces.
- **The Lightning.** The heavy strikes hit the riders exposed above the timberline.
- **Hypothermia.** A rider soaked in a cold Rocky Mountain summer rain at nine thousand feet degraded into severe physical trouble in two hours.
- **Altitude Sickness.** A man riding fast from the lowlands to ten thousand feet broke sick the first night.

### The Pacific Coast and Great Basin

- **The Sierra Winter.** The high Sierras caught the Pacific storms and dropped thirty solid feet of snow per season.
- **The Great Basin Summer.** High, cold desert. Salty, dry, and alkaline.
- **The Coast Fog.** Wet, cold, and heavy. A man riding the California coast in the winter remained physically wet for three months.

## 3. Fire

Fire dictated physical survival.

### Starting a Fire

- **Matches.** The commercial friction match stood available everywhere by the 1860s. A man carried the matches in a sealed tin or a heavy rubber bag.
- **Flint and Steel.** The period backup. A man with a piece of high-carbon steel, a sharp flint rock, and dry charcloth threw a spark and built a fire in two minutes in the dry wind, and in twenty minutes in the heavy rain.
- **The Burning Glass.** A glass lens focused the sun directly onto the dry tinder.
- **The Pistol Shot.** The man loaded the revolver with black powder and dry cotton tinder, pointed the muzzle into the kindling piled on the dirt, and fired it directly to ignite the pile.

### The Fuel

The man utilized whatever the dirt provided. Mesquite wood burned the best on the southern plains. Cottonwood stood as the heavy river-bottom fuel. The pine burned in the high mountains. The sage burned fast and hot in the high desert. On the treeless high plains, the men burned dried buffalo and cow chips (dung) entirely for fifty years.

### The Cooking Fire vs. The Warming Fire

The cooking fire ran small and contained, frequently built down in a shallow dirt pit. The man cooked his meat directly over the hot coals after the flame died. The warming fire ran significantly larger. The lone rider built a tiny three-stick fire directly under the tin coffee pot and huddled over it.

### The Wildfire

The prairie fire, driven by the heavy wind in the dry grass, ran faster than a saddle horse. The fire covered ten miles in an hour. Men survived the advancing fire exclusively by burning a backfire—they set the grass on fire in front of them, let it burn out a black circle, and stepped onto the charred dirt to let the main fire pass. A man caught in the open with no time shot his horse in the head, sliced the belly open with his knife, and crawled directly inside the bloody cavity while the fire burned over him.

---

## 4. Navigation

Men rode by the sun, the stars, the physical landmarks, and dead reckoning. The commercial maps frequently lied.

- **The Sun.** A man evaluated the sun's height in the sky. The noon shadow pointed directly north.
- **The Stars.** A man riding at night aligned entirely on Polaris, located off the two pointer stars of the Big Dipper.
- **The Landmarks.** A man memorized the mountain peaks, the buttes, and the notches cut into the skyline. The Native scouts held a detailed mental map of the dirt that no Anglo surveyor comprehended.
- **Dead Reckoning.** A man calculating his horse's physical speed against a known compass heading knew roughly his location after three days without seeing a single landmark.
- **The Trail Sign.** The plains tribes and the mountain men constructed physical trails: a clump of grass bent hard in the direction of travel, a small stone cairn stacked in the dirt, or a heavy blaze axed into a tree trunk. A man who read the sign followed a two-day-old trail cleanly.

Understand the physical limits of the meat and the wood.

- **The Horse.** A horse run hard for one day stands physically useless for a week. A horse run hard for two days dies or breaks down permanently. A man riding heavy pursuit swapped horses or killed his mount by the second sunset. Killing a man's horse in the deep desert killed the man outright.
- **Walking.** The cowhand absolutely refused to walk. The high-heeled boots physically crippled him on the ground.
- **The River.** A flooded river stopped the movement entirely. The men caulked the heavy wagon boxes with hot pitch, floated them across the brown water as barges, or waited for the flood to drop. Men drowned by the hundreds.

## 6. The Geography

- **The Llano Estacado (Staked Plains).** West Texas and eastern New Mexico. A massively flat, treeless, dry high plateau. The Comanche held it entirely until Ranald Mackenzie's 1874 cavalry operations broke them. The men survived by finding the few isolated _playas_ (rain-pools) scattered across the dirt.
- **The Brazos and Pecos.** The Brazos River anchored the central Texas ranch country. The Pecos River cut down through West Texas, running heavy with white alkali and deep quicksand.
- **The Sonoran Desert.** Southern Arizona. Massive stands of saguaro, cholla, and mesquite. The Apache controlled the rock.
- **The Mojave.** Southern California and Nevada. High, cold Joshua tree desert containing Death Valley.
- **The Sierra Nevada.** The massive eastern wall blocking California. The heavy timber caught the winter storms. The forty-niners ripped the gold from the western slopes.
- **The Rockies.** The absolute spine of the continent. The heavy miners extracted the gold and silver from the cold rock at Cripple Creek, Leadville, and Butte.
- **The Great Basin.** Nevada and western Utah. The high cold desert anchored salt and the massive Comstock Lode silver operations. The Mormon hierarchy governed the territory from Salt Lake City.
- **The Black Hills.** Western South Dakota. The Lakota sacred ground. George Custer's 1874 cavalry column confirmed the gold. The subsequent 1875 rush triggered the Great Sioux War, broke the treaty, and forced the blood at the Little Bighorn.
- **The Northern Plains (Wyoming, Montana, Dakotas).** High, cold, rolling grass. The Lakota, Cheyenne, Crow, Blackfoot, and Arapaho controlled the dirt. Massive cattle country after 1880. Destroyed entirely by the 1886–87 blizzards.
- **The Indian Territory.** Modern Oklahoma. The federal government forcibly resettled the Eastern tribes (Cherokee, Choctaw, Creek, Chickasaw, Seminole) into the eastern half. The Plains tribes and the unassigned lands occupied the west. _No-Man's Land_ sat on the panhandle. Re-settled gunmen hid in the heavy brush. Federal Judge Isaac Parker executed the absolute jurisdiction from the gallows at Fort Smith.
- **The Pacific Northwest.** Oregon, Washington, Idaho. Cold, wet, heavy timber.

## 7. Survival Skills

A working hand built a fire in the heavy rain. He caught a raw horse in a round corral and saddled it safely. He packed a horse for a multi-day ride. He found absolute north by the sun and the stars. He read human tracks entirely off the crushed grass. He skinned a deer and butchered a steer. He fired a revolver and a lever-action rifle to working accuracy. He doctored a lame horse. He rode fifty miles in a single day. He slept on the dirt in the freezing rain.

A man at the table who cannot operate the meat and wood is a greenhorn.

---

## 8. The Country as Antagonist

The land operates indifferently. The man moves across the dirt and the land breaks the man. Run the land entirely as a physical threat. The table must experience the West not as adventure, but as trespass.

## Part Two — The Hunt

The professional hunter operated purely as an industrial worker executing piece-rate labor. The buffalo runner shot the animals from a static dirt stand. The market hunter dropped the elk by the hundreds to feed the heavy rail camps. The wolfer poisoned the rotten carcasses with white strychnine strictly for the state bounty cash. The professional guide dragged Eastern sporting parties onto the mountain. The military contract hunter fed the cavalry mess.

Each occupation carried specific tools, rigid economics, and absolute consequences.

---

## 9. The Buffalo Hide Hunter

The heaviest professional occupation. Between 1871 and 1883, the commercial hide runners completely destroyed the northern and southern Plains herds. The men dropped fifteen million animals. The federal government operated the slaughter aggressively as a de facto military mechanism to starve the Plains nations onto the hard reservations.

### The Economics

The Pennsylvania and New York leather industry engineered a chemical tanning process that absorbed the buffalo hide. The Eastern factories stripped the hide for massive industrial drive belts and heavy winter sleigh robes.

A railhead hide in 1872 brought $3.50 for a heavy bull, $2.50 for a cow, and $1.50 for a calf. A working runner dropped fifty animals on a good day and grossed $150. After he paid the skinners, bought the lead, and paid the heavy freight, he cleared half the cash. A functional runner cleared $2,000 to $5,000 a season. A bad runner starved or died on the dirt.

### The Outfit

A standard outfit comprised the shooter, three skinners, a cook, and a teamster. The men operated out of two heavy wooden wagons pulling the lead, the camp gear, and the bloody hides.

The outfit tracked the herds. They blocked the camp miles from the animals. The shooter walked out before dawn. The skinners dragged the wagons up after the shooting stopped. The men skinned the carcasses exactly where they fell, dragged the heavy wet hides to the wagon, and hauled them to camp. The camp stood entirely as a stinking field of stretched hides and rotting offal.

The shooter killed two thousand animals a season. A skinner stripped fifteen carcasses a day.

### The Stand

The runner shot from the stand. He crept into the wind, dropped prone into the dirt behind a short rise, set the heavy rifle on cross-sticks two hundred yards out, and shot the herd's lead cow first. With the lead cow bleeding out in the grass, the herd lost its navigation structure and milled blindly. The runner simply worked the falling-block action methodically, shooting the outside buffalo first so the herd did not panic at the sight of the dropping bodies.

A standard stand dropped twenty animals. A legendary stand—Tom Nixon dropping 120 animals in forty minutes in 1873—defined a man completely.

The technique required the heavy Sharps rifle, absolute wind calculation, and the cold discipline to execute the meat systematically. The buffalo possessed no instinct for the rifle's distant report; the herd feared the visible running wolf, not the smoke flash three hundred yards downwind.

### The Rifle

The standard buffalo rifle of the trade was the Sharps single-shot breech-loading rifle in heavy calibers: the .50-90 Sharps, the .50-70 Sharps, the .45-120, the .44-90. The Sharps weighed twelve to fifteen pounds and fired a 400- to 550-grain lead bullet over a charge of 70 to 120 grains of black powder. The action was the falling-block breech invented by Christian Sharps in the 1850s and perfected through the 1860s and 1870s. The Remington Rolling Block rifle was the Sharps's principal competitor in the trade.

### The Rifle

The buffalo runner fired the Sharps single-shot falling-block breech-loader. He shot heavy, man-killing calibers: the .50-90, the .50-70, the .45-120, and the .44-90. The rifle weighed up to fifteen pounds. It drove a massive 500-grain lead bullet over 100 grains of black powder. The runner frequently fired the Remington Rolling Block as the secondary option.

The Sharps stood as a precision instrument. A cold runner regularly dropped a buffalo at four hundred yards. The heavy recoil bruised the shoulder permanently; the runner tied a thick leather pad over his collarbone. After fifty continuous rounds, the heavy iron barrel ran too hot to touch. The runner cooled the iron with a wet canvas rag or urinated directly down the hot barrel to drop the temperature.

The commercial outfits—Winchester, UMC—pressed the heavy cartridges, but the runners frequently hand-loaded the brass directly in camp to save cash. A heavy outfit burned hundreds of rounds a day. The Sharps represented the runner's absolute capital. A new rifle cost $125 in 1875—two months of cowboy wages.

### The Skinning

The skinner executed the hardest physical labor on the plains. He worked entirely with a heavy carbon-steel butcher knife and a grooved sharpening steel. He walked up to the dead buffalo, slit the hide from the throat to the anus, slit the four legs down to the hooves, and ripped the hide free of the fat.

A skinner utilizing a horse tied a heavy rope to the neck flap, dallied the rope to his saddle horn, and drove the horse forward. The horse physically peeled the hide backward off the carcass in one motion.

A fast skinner stripped fifteen animals a day. The work ran filthy. Within an hour, blood, yellow fat, and black hair completely caked his hands and canvas pants. He smelled of rotting meat for the entire season. He drew twenty-five to fifty cents a hide.

### The Meat

The men abandoned the carcasses to rot on the dirt. They cut the tongues and occasionally the hump meat for the camp, and salted the tongues in wooden barrels to ship east as delicacies. They left millions of tons of meat for the wolves and the sun.

The stench of a killed herd carried for miles. The rotting meat drew the coyotes, the wolves, and the ravens. Within two years, the bone collectors drove the empty wagons out to the killing grounds, loaded the bleached white skeletons, and shipped them east on the rail to burn into carbon black for the sugar refineries and crush into phosphate fertilizer. The massive piles of buffalo skulls stacked higher than the boxcars at the Dodge City railhead.

### The Camp

The hunting camp stood entirely austere. The men parked the heavy wagon, pitched a canvas lean-to, and threw their wool bedrolls onto the dirt. They ate salt pork, hard biscuit, black coffee, and fresh buffalo hump.

The men worked from first light until it was too dark to see the knife.

### The Hazards

The Comanche, the Kiowa, the Cheyenne, and the Lakota accurately identified the hide runners as the absolute destruction of their economic base. The nations hunted the hunters. Quanah Parker threw a massive combined force of Comanche and Cheyenne against the Adobe Walls trading post in the Texas Panhandle in June 1874. The runners held the adobe buildings strictly because the heavy Sharps rifles dropped the horses at eight hundred yards. War parties caught and mutilated isolated skinning crews continuously through the 1870s.

The physical weather killed the men in the open. The skinning knives slipped on the bloody fat and severed femoral arteries. The runners brought the heavy seasonal cash into the raw rail towns and died of gunshot wounds in the saloons.

### The End

The runners broke the southern herd entirely by 1878. The Northern Pacific Railroad pushed into Montana and the Dakotas in the early 1880s and unlocked the final northern herd. By 1883, the commercial hunt was completely over. By 1900, fewer than one thousand wild buffalo breathed on the entire continent.

## 10. The Market Hunter

The market hunter dropped deer, antelope, elk, and prairie chickens by the thousands to feed the heavy railroad construction camps and the upscale Eastern restaurants.

### The Economics

A gutted deer sold at the railhead for $3.00. An elk hindquarter brought $5.00. The game birds brought $2.00 a dozen. A man dropping ten deer a week cleared $30 cash.

The Union Pacific and Santa Fe rail camps required massive meat contracts to feed two hundred men pulling iron. The tie-hacks and the graders ate whatever the hunter dragged into camp.

### The Technique

The market hunter executed the single, clean shot. He refused to fire into a running herd because the panicked running ruined the meat and wasted the heavy lead.

He fired the army-surplus .45-70 Springfield, the Winchester 1873 in .44-40, or the lighter .44-77 Sharps. He carried a heavy 10-gauge scattergun for the birds.

### The Processing

The man field-dressed the elk on the dirt immediately, bleeding the meat out cleanly. He dragged the carcass back to his log meat-house on a mule, butchered it into commercial quarters, packed the meat into heavy wooden barrels layered with rock salt or winter ice, and dropped the barrels at the rail depot.

## 11. The Wolfer

The wolfer operated at the absolute bottom of the frontier social structure. He killed wolves strictly for the state bounty on the scalps and the commercial price of the winter pelts.

### The Economics

The western territories paid one to three dollars for a wolf scalp. The heavy winter pelt brought another five dollars at the fur house. A man pushing the work cleared two thousand dollars a year.

### The Method

The wolfer utilized white strychnine sulfate. He shot a buffalo or a wild horse, slit the belly open, dumped the crystallized poison directly into the raw meat and blood, and rode away. The wolves ate the poisoned meat and died in physical agony within hours. The wolfer rode back the next morning, skinned the dead wolves, cut off the ears and scalp for the bounty commissioner, and set the next bait.

### The Collateral Damage

The strychnine poisoned everything. It killed the coyotes, the eagles, the ravens, and the badgers. It killed the rancher's working dogs. It killed the Native nations' camp dogs. The Assiniboine clashed violently with the American wolfers in the Cypress Hills Massacre in 1873 specifically over stolen horses and poisoned dogs.

### The Standing

The wolfer worked entirely alone. He smelled permanently of rotting meat and chemical poison. The cowhands refused to let him drink at the bunkhouse. The Native nations killed him when they caught him. He wintered in a filthy line shack and drank his cash in the cheapest saloons in the spring.

### The End

The strychnine wiped the wolf off the plains entirely by 1900.

## 12. The Sporting Guide

The heavy Eastern money and the European nobility rode the rails west specifically to shoot trophy elk and bear.

### The Clientele

The New York banker rode the Pullman car west carrying custom English shotguns and a valet. The European royalty—the Earl of Dunraven in Colorado, the Grand Duke Alexis of Russia hunting with Custer in 1872—spent massive capital to operate on the plains.

### The Guide

The professional guide—frequently a washed-out mountain man or a former cavalry scout like Buffalo Bill Cody or Texas Jack Omohundro—organized the wagons, the remuda, the camp cook, and the physical security. He located the game and positioned the client for the shot.

### The Hunt

The sporting hunt operated as a heavy logistical movement. The guide pushed the wagons twenty miles a day, set the heavy canvas tents, and positioned the hunters at dawn. The men hunted for the massive horn mounts. The guide salted the capes and packed the skulls in wooden crates to ship back to the Philadelphia libraries.

The client paid the guide up to $1,500 for a six-week expedition. The guide possessed the absolute asset: he knew exactly where the elk bedded down.

---

## 13. The Contract Hunter

The frontier cavalry posts contracted civilian hunters to supply the mess with fresh meat, paying a flat monthly cash wage ($100) or piece-rate by the pound. The man lived near the sutler's store, operated outside military discipline, and shot the local deer to keep the soldiers off the salt pork.

---

## 14. The Residual Trapper

The massive beaver trade collapsed in the 1840s when the European silk hat replaced the beaver felt. A residual trapper population persisted in the high Rockies, working the heavy steel traps through the freezing water for marten, fox, and bear. He sold the pelts to the trading posts or the agency sutlers in the spring.

## 15. The Vocabulary

### The Buffalo

- **The Stand** — the prone shooting position.
- **The Sentinel** — the lead cow shot first to confuse the herd.
- **Shooting Sticks** — the crossed wooden sticks supporting the heavy Sharps barrel.
- **The Robe** — the thick winter hide.
- **The Bone Wagon** — the freight wagons collecting the bleached skeletons.
- **The Big Fifty** — the heavy .50-caliber Sharps.

### The Meat

- **Game/Meat** — _Game_ ran alive; _meat_ hung dead in the camp.
- **Jerk Meat** — meat stripped thin and sun-dried hard on a rack.
- **Pemmican** — the dried meat pounded into powder and mixed with rendered fat.

### The Poison

- **The Bait** — the poisoned carcass.
- **The Powder** — the strychnine sulfate.
- **The Tally** — the heavy string of bloody scalps turned in for the cash bounty.
