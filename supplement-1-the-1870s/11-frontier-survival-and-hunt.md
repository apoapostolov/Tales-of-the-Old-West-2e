# Chapter 11 — Frontier Survival and the Hunt

> _“SIXTY MILES TO THE NEXT WATER. WE MADE THE SPRING BY SUNDOWN. THREE DAYS WITHOUT SWEET WATER. THE PECOS WAS UP. WE WENT ON WITHOUT CROSSING.”_

The country in the West is a character, sometimes the antagonist. A player or GM who does not know what kills a man on the Llano Estacado, or how far a horse can travel in a day, cannot run the West. And the men who lived in that country lived by what they killed in it. The buffalo hide hunter, the market hunter, the wolfer, the sporting guide, the contract hunter at the post, the residual trapper — these were occupations in 1875, not pastimes. This chapter combines the country and the hunt: the land that kills, and the men who killed for a living in it.

---

## Part One — The Country

---

## 1. Water

Water is the first problem on the western half of the continent. West of the 100th meridian — roughly a line through central Texas, Oklahoma, Kansas, Nebraska, the Dakotas — rainfall drops below twenty inches a year. West of the 105th meridian it drops below fifteen. Nothing grows without irrigation. Travel is structured around water, not around terrain.

### Daily need

A man on foot in summer heat needs a gallon a day to live, two to work. He will die in three days without water in 100°F sun, in five in cooler weather. A horse needs eight to twelve gallons a day; a working horse in the heat needs more; a horse will not work hard the day after a dry night and will die in four to seven days without water. An ox needs ten to fifteen gallons a day. A wagon train of ten families and their stock needed a creek every night, period.

### Sources

Springs were the prized stop on a trail; many trails followed spring lines. Creeks and rivers — often alkali in the high plains, often fouled by buffalo or by stock above. Rain catchment in tanks: the _tinajas_ of southern Arizona, natural rock pools holding rainwater for weeks. Hand-dug wells on the homestead — ten to sixty feet typical, some reaching two hundred feet on the high plains. Windmill wells (after the 1860s; in widespread use 1880s onward). Snow, melted, in winter. Buffalo wallows after a rain — the wallow held water for days, brown and stinking but drinkable.

### Bad water

Alkali — white-crust water on the high plains. Causes severe diarrhea; can kill stock and men. Stock-fouled — cattle or buffalo standing in a hole turned it to soup. Stagnant. Mosquito-bred. Carrying typhoid, cholera, dysentery. Mineral — sulfur springs, salt springs.

### Purification

Boiling was the reliable method, when fuel and time allowed. Most camps boiled water into coffee to make it safe, and this is one reason coffee was constant. Settling in a barrel with a handful of cornmeal — the meal drew dirt to the bottom in a few hours. Charcoal filter — a barrel with charcoal and sand, pioneer filtration. A man on the move drank what he had and trusted to luck.

### Talking about water in prose

Distances on the western trails are described by water: _sixty miles to the next water; we made the spring by sundown; three days without sweet water; the Pecos was up, we went on without crossing._ A scene that ignores water in dry country reads false to anyone who has ridden it.

## 2. Weather

Western weather kills more often than men do. Each region has its own killers.

### The high plains (Texas Panhandle through the Dakotas)

- **Blue norther.** A cold front out of Canada that drops the temperature 50°F in two hours. Black-blue cloud bank on the northern horizon. Wind to fifty mph. Stock and unprepared men die. Stories of riders frozen to the saddle. Common October–April.
- **Chinook.** A warm dry wind off the Rockies in winter that raises the temperature 40°F in a day and melts snow. Welcome but treacherous — the melt re-freezes that night.
- **Hailstorm.** Summer. Hail the size of marbles to baseballs. Kills calves and drives a herd into stampede.
- **Tornado.** Spring and summer. The warning was the green cast in the western sky.
- **Lightning.** The leading cause of stampedes. A single bolt hitting a tree near the herd put 2,500 cattle on their feet in seconds.
- **Drought.** Multi-year droughts (notably 1886–87 on the northern plains) killed millions of cattle. The _Big Die-Up_ ended the open-range era.
- **The Hard Winter of 1886–87.** Snow in November, blizzards through January, temperatures below −40°F across Montana and Wyoming. Cattle starved by the hundreds of thousands. Charles M. Russell drew it on a postcard: a single starving cow surrounded by wolves, captioned _Waiting for a Chinook._

### The Southwest desert (Arizona, New Mexico, west Texas)

- **Heat.** Summer afternoons over 110°F. A man without water dies in hours.
- **Cold nights.** Desert temperatures drop 50°F after sundown; a man without a blanket can freeze in July.
- **Dust storm (haboob).** A wall of brown dust sometimes thousands of feet high, advancing across the desert ahead of a thunderstorm. Visibility to zero.
- **Flash flood.** A summer thunderstorm twenty miles upstream can send a wall of water down a dry arroyo without warning. The arroyo a man slept in last night becomes the river that drowns him today.
- **Sandstorm.** Wind off the desert with sand at forty mph. Eyes, mouth, lungs choked.

### The mountains (Rockies, Sierras, Cascades, Bitterroots)

- **Snow** September through May at altitude. Snowed-in passes, snowed-in cabins.
- **Avalanche** on steep slopes after fresh snow.
- **Lightning above timberline** strikes the ridge man.
- **Hypothermia** — a wet rider in a Rocky Mountain summer rain at 9,000 feet can be in serious trouble in two hours.
- **Altitude sickness** — a man from the lowlands climbing fast to 10,000 feet is sick the first night.

### The Pacific coast and Great Basin

- **Sierra winter.** Donner-Party country. The Sierras catch Pacific storms and dump thirty feet of snow per season at altitude.
- **Great Basin summer.** Hot, dry, alkali. Death Valley.
- **Coast fog.** Wet, cold, persistent. A man on horseback along the California coast in winter is wet for three months.

### The southern plains (Texas, Oklahoma)

- **Heat with humidity** in eastern Texas — dehydrating, sapping.
- **Norther** as on the high plains.
- **Tornado** alley.
- **Drought** cycles.
- **Hurricane** along the coast — the 1875 and 1886 Indianola hurricanes destroyed the Texas port.

## 3. Fire

Fire is life and fire is death.

### Starting a fire

- **Matches.** Available everywhere by the 1850s. Lucifer matches in the 1830s, friction matches by the 1860s. A man carried matches in a tin or a watertight rubber bag. Wet matches were useless.
- **Flint and steel** — older method, kept as backup. A flint-and-steel man with charcloth and good tinder could raise a flame in two minutes in dry weather, twenty in wet.
- **Burning glass** (a magnifying lens) in sun.
- **Pistol shot into a wad of tinder** — mountain man's trick.
- **Bow drill and friction** — Native technique. Slow.

### Tinder, kindling, fuel

Tinder: dry grass, cottonwood fluff, cedar bark shredded, pitch wood, the dry inside of a fallen log. Kindling: dry twigs, splints split from a dry stick. Fuel: whatever the country gave. Mesquite (best on the southern plains). Cottonwood (the river-bottom standby). Pine (the mountains). Sage (high desert; burns hot and fast). **Buffalo chips and cow chips** — dried dung, the prairie's universal fuel where wood was absent. The high plains burned chips for fifty years.

### Cooking fire vs warming fire

Cooking fire: small, contained, in a pit if possible. Cooked over coals after the flame burned down. Warming fire: larger. The reflector built behind the fire to throw heat at the bedroll. The lone-rider's small fire was three sticks under a coffee can.

### Wildfire

Prairie fires raced faster than a horse could run, fueled by dry grass. A grass fire could cover ten miles in an hour with a wind behind it. **Backfire** — the only defense. Burn the ground in front of the advancing fire, retreat onto the burned ground. A man caught in the open with no time to backfire shot a cow or his horse, slit it, and lay in the carcass while the fire passed over. (Documented practice. Survived.)

## 4. Navigation

There were maps. They were often wrong. The wagon trails were known and marked by ruts and graves. Off the trails a man navigated by sun, by stars, by landmarks, by dead reckoning.

- **Sun and time.** A pocket watch, kept wound and reset against true noon (the sun at zenith). Solar noon was the practical reset. A man without a watch read the sun's height to estimate time. The shadow at noon pointed north (in the northern hemisphere).
- **Stars.** The North Star (Polaris). Found by the pointer stars of the Big Dipper. A traveler at night moved by the stars.
- **Landmarks.** A mountain peak, a butte, a notch on the horizon. A traveler carried a mental map of his country. The Native scouts knew the country in detail no Anglo surveyor matched. The Army hired Pawnee, Apache, Crow scouts for this reason.
- **Dead reckoning.** Estimate of speed and direction over time gives position. A man riding thirty miles a day on a known compass heading knew approximately where he was on the third day even with no landmark.
- **Trail signs.** Plains tribes and mountain men marked trails: a bent grass clump with the bend pointing the direction; stones piled in a small cairn; a blaze cut on a tree (in country that had trees); a skull on a stake at the head of a draw. A man who could read sign could follow a trail two days old. A man who could not could pass within ten yards of a man hiding in the brush.

## 5. Distance and Travel Speed

The modern sense of distance is wrong. Get this right or lose verisimilitude.

| Mode                                           | Daily distance, good country                            |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Man on foot, packed                            | 15–20 miles                                             |
| Man on foot, lightly packed, hard              | 25–30 miles                                             |
| Saddle horse, walked, all day                  | 30–40 miles                                             |
| Saddle horse, alternating walk-trot            | 40–50 miles                                             |
| Saddle horse, hard ride, one day               | 60–80 miles (horse exhausted)                           |
| Pony Express rider, fresh horse every 10–15 mi | 200+ miles in 24 h                                      |
| Cattle drive (herd)                            | 10–15 miles                                             |
| Wagon train (ox)                               | 10–15 miles                                             |
| Wagon train (mule)                             | 15–20 miles                                             |
| Stagecoach, with team changes                  | 100–125 miles in 24 h                                   |
| Train                                          | 20–30 mph cruising; coast-to-coast in 7 days after 1869 |

- **Horse-killing facts.** A horse run hard for a day is no good for a week. A horse run hard for two days is dead or ruined. A man riding for his life changed horses if he could. If he could not, he killed the horse he was on by the second day. Killing a man's horse in the desert was killing the man.
- **Walking.** Hands hated to walk. Boots were not made for it. A cowhand afoot was a man in trouble, and the joke in the bunkhouse was _a cowboy on the ground is just a man with the wrong hat._
- **River crossings.** A flooded river stopped travel. Wagons were caulked (the box sealed with pitch and floated as a boat) or built up on logs or simply waited. Many drowned.

## 6. Regional Terrain — the Country Itself

A working knowledge of where things are.

- **The Llano Estacado (Staked Plains).** West Texas, eastern New Mexico. A flat, treeless, water-poor high plateau, escarpment on the east. Comanche country until Mackenzie's 1874 Palo Duro campaign broke them. Almost no surface water; the few _playas_ (rain-pools) were the only stops. The Comanchero traders crossed it east-to-west to trade with the tribes.
- **The Brazos and Pecos.** The Brazos River — central Texas, ran east, the heart of ranch country. The Pecos River — west Texas / eastern New Mexico, ran south, alkali, treacherous quicksand. _West of the Pecos_ came to mean lawless country (Judge Roy Bean's _Law West of the Pecos_).
- **The Sonoran Desert.** Southern Arizona, northern Mexico. Saguaro country. Cholla, ocotillo, mesquite. Apache country. Hot. Dry. Beautiful in the spring bloom.
- **The Mojave.** Southern California, southern Nevada. Higher and colder than the Sonoran. Joshua trees. Death Valley.
- **The Sierra Nevada.** California's eastern wall. The Donner Pass. Yosemite. Goldfields on the western slope (1849 onward).
- **The Rockies.** Spine of the continent. Pikes Peak, Long's Peak, the Tetons, the Wind Rivers, the Bitterroots. Mining country: gold and silver in Colorado (Cripple Creek, Leadville), Montana (Helena, Butte), the Black Hills.
- **The Great Basin.** Nevada and western Utah. Cold desert, high, salty. Mormon country (Salt Lake settled 1847). Comstock Lode (Virginia City) silver.
- **The Black Hills.** Western South Dakota / eastern Wyoming. Lakota sacred ground. Custer's 1874 expedition found gold; the Gold Rush of 1875–76 brought Deadwood, Lead, the breaking of the treaty, the Great Sioux War, the Little Bighorn.
- **The Northern Plains (Wyoming, Montana, Dakotas).** High, cold, rolling grass. Lakota, Cheyenne, Crow, Blackfoot, Arapaho country. Cattle country after 1880, ruined by 1886–87.
- **The Indian Territory.** Modern Oklahoma. Resettled tribes from the Trail of Tears (Cherokee, Choctaw, Creek, Chickasaw, Seminole) in the east. Wild tribes and the unassigned lands in the west. _No-Man's Land_ on the panhandle. Outlaws hid here. Federal court at Fort Smith (Judge Parker, the _Hanging Judge_) had jurisdiction.
- **The Pacific Northwest.** Oregon, Washington, Idaho. Wetter, forested. Nez Perce country (Chief Joseph's 1877 retreat). Settlement after the Oregon Trail in the 1840s.

## 7. Survival Skills the Cowhand Assumed

A working westerner could build a fire in wind and rain; catch a horse in a corral and saddle it; pack a horse or a mule for a multi-day trip; find north by the sun or the stars; read animal sign and human sign on the ground; skin and butcher a deer or a beef; cook over a fire (poorly, usually); shoot a pistol and a rifle to working accuracy; doctor a horse for cuts, sores, bad shoes, mild colic; doctor himself for cuts, splinters, snakebite (badly), gunshot (very badly); ride fifty miles in a day if he had to; sleep on the ground in any weather short of a blizzard.

A character at the table who cannot do these things is either a greenhorn (and play it for what it is) or a townsman, an Easterner, a tenderfoot. Set this expectation clearly and the country reads honest.

---

## 8. The Country as Antagonist

The land in the West does not care. It is the central fact and the central terror of the literature. Cormac McCarthy's _Blood Meridian,_ A. B. Guthrie's _The Big Sky,_ Walter Van Tilburg Clark's _The Track of the Cat_ — the land in these books is older than the men, larger than the men, and indifferent. The men move across it and the land goes on. Run the land like that and the table will carry the West's actual weight: not _adventure,_ but _trespass._ The men are passing through. The country was here before and will be here after.

## Part Two — The Hunt

The popular West has rendered hunting as the sportsman's pursuit and as a noble engagement with the wilderness. The reality of professional hunting in the period was different. The buffalo hide hunter was an industrial worker shooting from a prepared stand for a piecework wage. The market hunter killed deer, antelope, and elk by the hundreds for restaurants and railroad camps and shipped the carcasses by rail. The wolfer poisoned carcasses with strychnine for the bounty money and was held in low regard even on the frontier. The professional guide for Eastern and European sporting parties was a service worker in the nascent western tourism industry. The military hunter was a contract employee who supplied the fort's mess.

Each is a specific occupation with its own gear, vocabulary, economics, social standing, and consequences. Render the work and not the romance.

---

## 9. The Buffalo Hide Hunter

The largest and most consequential of the professional hunting occupations. Between roughly 1871 and 1883 the southern and northern Plains herds of the American buffalo (more accurately American bison) were destroyed by commercial hunters working for the hide trade. The estimated kill is fifteen million animals in a decade. The plains were emptied as policy: the federal government did not make the policy in writing but made it in practice by refusing to enforce the existing treaty protections and by treating the slaughter of the buffalo as the means by which the Plains nations would be brought to the reservation. (See Chapter 1 and Chapter 24 for the political and human consequences.)

### The economic basis

The eastern leather industry, principally in Pennsylvania and New York, developed in the late 1860s a tanning process suitable for buffalo hide. The hide became commercially valuable as industrial belting (drive belts for factory machinery), as carriage and buggy upholstery, and as the heavy lap robes used in winter travel by sleigh and wagon.

The price of a hide at the railhead in 1872 was roughly $3.50 for a prime bull, $2.50 for a cow, $1.50 for a calf. By the late 1870s the price had dropped as the supply increased and then risen again as the supply collapsed. By 1883 the remaining herds were too scattered for commercial hunting and the trade ended.

A working hunter who took fifty animals on a good day grossed perhaps $150. After costs (the skinners' wages, the wagon, the supplies, the ammunition, the freight to railhead, the percentage to the outfit's owner if the hunter was working for one) the hunter cleared perhaps half. A good hunter in a good season cleared $2,000 to $5,000. A poor hunter starved or quit or was killed.

### The outfit

The standard buffalo hunting outfit was the hunter, two or three skinners, a cook, and a teamster. They operated from one or two heavy wagons that carried the supplies, the ammunition, the camp gear, and the hides as they were taken.

The outfit moved between the herds. A camp was made within a few miles of a herd; the hunter went out on horseback or on foot at dawn; the skinners followed with the wagons; the carcasses were skinned where they fell; the hides were dragged to the wagon, loaded, and brought back to camp; the camp was a stinking field of stretched hides and offal until the wagons were filled and the outfit moved on.

A successful season might run from spring to fall with the outfit moving across hundreds of miles of range. The hunter might take two or three thousand animals in a season. The skinners might process ten or fifteen a day each.

### The stand

The hide hunter's technique was the stand. He approached a herd from downwind, established a prone position behind a rise or a clump of grass within range (two hundred to four hundred yards), and shot the dominant animal first — typically a sentinel cow that the rest of the herd watched for warning. With the sentinel down and the rest of the herd milling without leadership, the hunter shot animal after animal in succession, working the rifle methodically, picking the animals at the edges of the herd so the others did not see them fall.

A good stand might yield twenty to fifty animals before the herd finally broke and ran. A great stand — the legendary Tom Nixon stand of 120 animals in forty minutes in 1873; the J. Wright Mooar accounts of similar numbers — was the career-defining event.

The technique required the right rifle, the right position, the right wind, and the discipline to work calmly through the kill. The herd's failure to flee was the precondition. The buffalo of the hunted herd had no instinct for the rifle's distant report; the herd associated danger with the visible threat (the wolf, the rider, the running predator) and not with the muzzle flash two hundred yards off.

### The rifle

The standard buffalo rifle of the trade was the Sharps single-shot breech-loading rifle in heavy calibers: the .50-90 Sharps, the .50-70 Sharps, the .45-120, the .44-90. The Sharps weighed twelve to fifteen pounds and fired a 400- to 550-grain lead bullet over a charge of 70 to 120 grains of black powder. The action was the falling-block breech invented by Christian Sharps in the 1850s and perfected through the 1860s and 1870s. The Remington Rolling Block rifle was the Sharps's principal competitor in the trade.

The Sharps was a target-grade rifle of remarkable accuracy. A practiced hunter could put a bullet in a buffalo's lungs at four hundred yards. The recoil was severe; the hunter wore a pad on his shoulder and developed a callus there. The rifle was hot after a stand of fifty rounds and had to be rested or cooled with a wet rag.

The cartridges were factory-loaded by the major makers (Winchester, UMC, Sharps's own ammunition plant) or hand-loaded by the hunter or by his loader in camp. A working outfit went through hundreds of rounds a day at peak. The rifle was the hunter's principal capital. A new Sharps Sporting Rifle in heavy caliber cost $80 to $125 in 1875, more than two months' wages for a cowhand. A used rifle could be had for $40 to $60. The hunter cared for it fastidiously; a fouled or damaged rifle ended the season.

### The skinning

The skinner's work was harder than the hunter's and was lower-paid and lower-status. The skinner worked with a heavy butcher knife and a steel for sharpening. He approached the carcass, made the opening cuts (down the belly from throat to anus, down each leg from hoof to belly), and then worked the hide free with knife and hand and, where the country allowed, a horse.

The horse method: a rope tied to the hide at the neck and the other end to the saddle. The rider walked the horse forward; the hide peeled off the body in one piece. The rider returned, the skinner cut the hide free at the tail, the hide was rolled and dragged to the wagon.

A skinner did ten to fifteen animals a day in good country, fewer in difficult ground. The skinning was filthy work. The skinner's clothes, hands, and hair were caked with blood and grease and hair within an hour of starting. The smell followed him for the season. He was paid twenty-five cents per hide in the 1873 trade, rising to fifty cents as good skinners became scarce. He made the same as a hand on a cattle drive in a better season.

### The flesh

The carcass was abandoned. The buffalo was killed for the hide alone in the standard trade; the meat was left on the prairie. A small portion of the hump and the tongue might be cut for the camp's own use, and the tongues were sometimes salted and shipped as a delicacy to eastern markets at fifteen to twenty-five cents apiece. Almost everything else rotted.

The smell of a hunted herd within a week of the hunters' departure was enormous. The dead animals attracted wolves, coyotes, ravens, and within a few weeks the bone collectors who came behind the hunters with wagons to gather the skeletons for shipment east as fertilizer (carbon black for sugar refining and bone meal for fields). The bone trade was a secondary economy that lasted into the 1890s; the railhead piles of buffalo bones in Dodge City and the Texas panhandle towns are documented in photographs of the period.

### The camp

The hunter's camp was austere. A wagon, a tent or a brush shelter, a fire, the skinners' bedrolls on the ground. The food was salt pork, beans, biscuit, coffee, and what fresh meat the camp took from the day's kill. The cook was often a Mexican hand or a Black freedman, hired for the season at twenty to thirty dollars a month.

The work day ran from dawn to dark with the skinners pressing to keep up with the hunter. A hunter who shot fifty animals before noon left his outfit fifty animals to skin before dark. A hunter who shot a hundred outran his skinners and lost hides to the predators or to spoilage. The good outfits kept the hunter's pace calibrated to the skinners' capacity.

### The hazards

The buffalo herd was not the principal hazard. The hazards were the Comanche, the Kiowa, the Cheyenne, the Lakota — the Plains nations regarded the hide hunters as the existential enemy and attacked outfits when they could. The Adobe Walls fight (Texas panhandle, June 1874) saw a force of Comanche, Kiowa, and Cheyenne under Quanah Parker attack a hunters' settlement and was beaten off only by the Sharps rifles' long-range fire. Smaller engagements happened repeatedly through the 1870s. A hunting outfit caught in the open by a war party was usually killed.

The weather: the Plains blizzard could catch an outfit on open prairie with no shelter; the summer thunderstorm with hail killed horses and ruined hides. The accident: the wagon overturning, the rifle bursting, the skinner's knife slipping in the wet, the horse falling on the rider — the frontier accident rate was high. The disease: the unsanitary camp at the end of a long season produced typhoid, dysentery, and the standard frontier infections. The fight in town: the hunter at the end of the season with cash in his pocket coming into Dodge City or Hays or Mobeetie was a drunk armed man among other drunk armed men, and the mortality from interpersonal violence in the buffalo towns was substantial.

### Who they were

The hide hunters were predominantly Anglo, mostly Southern and Western, mostly young men in their twenties and early thirties. A substantial minority were Civil War veterans of both armies who had drifted west after the war and found in the hide trade a use for their rifle skills. A smaller number were Mexican, Black, and mixed-race. A handful of Native men worked the hide trade themselves, complicating any neat racial account.

The major figures: J. Wright Mooar (the documented hunter and memoirist who killed an estimated 22,000 buffalo over his career, who shot the white buffalo in 1876, and who lived to 1940 to write his memoir); his brother John W. Mooar; Frank Mayer (the hunter and memoirist whose 1957 oral history is one of the principal period sources); Tom Nixon; Billy Tilghman (later the lawman); Billy Dixon (who fired the famous 1538-yard Adobe Walls shot); Dirty Face Jones; Wyatt Earp (briefly, before his lawman career); and many others.

### The end

The southern herd was effectively destroyed by 1878. The northern herd lasted until the early 1880s when the railroads' expansion into Montana and the Dakotas opened the last range to the hunters. By 1883 the commercial hunt was over. By 1900 the wild buffalo population in the lower forty-eight states was under a thousand animals. The species was saved by private conservation efforts (Charles Goodnight's herd in Texas, the Wichita Mountains preserve, Yellowstone) at the last possible moment.

The hunters who survived their careers retired or moved to other work. Many became cowhands, some became lawmen (Tilghman, Earp), some became merchants, some drank up their stake and died in the saloons of the towns they had made rich.

## 10. The Market Hunter

Below the buffalo trade in scale and visibility, the market hunter killed game animals for the restaurant and the railroad camp. He worked deer, antelope, elk, mountain sheep, prairie chicken, quail, ducks, geese, sandhill cranes, the remaining buffalo on a smaller scale, and any other species that could be sold.

### The economics

A deer carcass in the 1870s sold at the railhead for $1.50 to $3.00 depending on the cut and the city of destination. An antelope sold for $1.00 to $2.00. An elk hindquarter sold for $3.00 to $5.00. Prairie chicken and quail sold by the dozen for $1.00 to $2.00. The hunter who took ten deer a week grossed $15 to $30 net of the freight and the bullets.

The principal markets were the urban restaurants of the East (New York, Boston, Philadelphia, Chicago) and the construction camps of the western railroads (Union Pacific, Central Pacific, Northern Pacific, Atchison Topeka & Santa Fe). The railroad camp market in particular was concentrated and immediate: a camp of two hundred working men needed several hundred pounds of fresh meat a week, and the contractor would buy whatever the market hunter brought in.

### The technique

The market hunter was a craftsman of approach and shot. He worked the country he knew (his range was typically a fifty- to two-hundred-mile radius around his home base), knew the watering places and the bedding grounds, knew the season patterns of the animals. He hunted on foot or horseback, often with one or two pack mules to carry the carcasses out.

The shot was the careful single shot at the unalerted animal at one hundred to two hundred yards. Multiple shots scattered the herd and wasted ammunition. The market hunter cared about his ammunition cost and about the carcass's condition; a heavily shot animal lost commercial value.

The rifle was usually a smaller-caliber rifle than the buffalo hunter's: the .44-77 Sharps, the .45-70 Springfield (the army's standard, sold cheap as surplus and widely used by civilian hunters), the Winchester 1873 in .44-40 (popular for the shorter-range shooting and for its repeating capacity), the Henry rifle in .44 rimfire (older, less common by the late 1870s). The shotgun (the breech-loading 10-gauge or 12-gauge) for ducks, geese, and prairie chicken.

### The processing

The market hunter field-dressed his game where it fell, gutting the animal and bleeding it out. He packed the carcass on a mule or, for the near-base hunting, dragged it on a travois or in a wagon to his cabin. There he hung the carcass in a meat house (a slatted wooden shed where the carcasses could cool with airflow), butchered it into commercial cuts, packed the cuts in barrels of ice or salt, and shipped them by wagon and rail to the buyer. In winter the cold did the refrigeration. In summer the hunter worked at a smaller scale or sold to nearer markets that did not require preservation.

The good market hunter had a permanent or semi-permanent base camp: a cabin, a meat house, a corral, a small horse herd, sometimes a wife or hired man to handle the camp while he hunted. The career market hunter was often a small businessman in this sense — a sole proprietor whose product was meat.

### Who they were

A more various population than the buffalo hunters. Many were homesteaders supplementing their farm income. Others were full-time hunters whose main living was the trade. A substantial number were former mountain men whose fur trade had collapsed in the 1840s and who had moved to game-meat hunting. Some were Native men adapting traditional hunting skills to the cash economy. Some were European immigrants — Germans, Scandinavians — with hunting traditions from the old country.

### The decline

The market hunting of game animals followed the buffalo into commercial collapse but more slowly. The deer and antelope herds of the open West were substantially reduced by 1900. The first state-level game laws came in the 1870s and 1880s and restricted commercial hunting to defined seasons; the federal Lacey Act of 1900 banned interstate trafficking in illegally taken game. The market hunting trade ended by the early 1900s with the species' near-extinction and the new regulatory framework.

For the 1870s and 1880s, the market hunter is at his peak and is a recognized profession. He is not yet villainized; he is the practical provider of fresh meat to markets that need it. The conservation conscience that would label him a poacher is twenty years away.

## The Stand at First Light

> Mooar was on the rest at first light. The herd had bedded a quarter mile out and was up and grazing now and the wind was right. He picked the cow that was watching back at him on the rise and put the bullet through her chest. She went down and the herd milled and did not run. He worked the breech and loaded and shouldered and shot the next cow and the next. The powder smoke hung in the still air. The herd would not break while the leaders kept dropping where the others could not see them fall. He shot for an hour. When he stopped he counted forty-three down and the herd had finally moved off and Tom and the boys were coming up with the wagon.

## 11. The Wolfer

The wolfer killed wolves for the bounty and for the pelts. He held the lowest social standing of any of the western hunters. The reason was the method.

### The economics

State and territorial governments paid bounties on wolf scalps from the 1860s through the 1890s. The bounty varied by jurisdiction and year: typically one to three dollars per wolf, more in some places, less in others. Coyote scalps paid fifty cents to a dollar. The Stockmen's Association in some districts added a private bounty on top of the public one. The pelt itself sold at a fur house for two to five dollars depending on the season and the condition. A working wolfer in a good year could clear a thousand to two thousand dollars between the bounty and the pelts.

### The method

The standard wolfer's technique was strychnine poisoning. The wolfer killed a buffalo, an antelope, or a horse, opened the carcass, laced the meat with strychnine sulfate crystals, and left the carcass in the open as bait. Wolves that fed on the carcass died within hours. The wolfer returned the next day or the day after, found the dead wolves around the bait, scalped them for the bounty, skinned them for the pelts, and moved on to set the next bait.

A single laced carcass could kill a dozen wolves in a night. A wolfer working a string of baits across a hundred-mile circuit might collect fifty to a hundred wolf scalps a week in good country.

### The collateral damage

Strychnine bait did not discriminate. It killed coyotes, foxes, badgers, eagles, vultures, ravens, magpies, dogs, and any other carnivore or scavenger that fed on the carcass. The dead predators in turn poisoned secondary scavengers that fed on them. A single laced carcass could sterilize a square mile of country of wildlife for weeks.

The wolfer's bait poisoned the dogs of any camp, settlement, or Native band that came near it. The Native nations of the northern Plains came to view the wolfer as a particular enemy because the wolfer's strychnine killed their dogs and, occasionally, their children who played with what they found. The Cypress Hills Massacre (June 1873, the southern Saskatchewan killing of an Assiniboine band by a party of American wolfers and traders) was the most famous of the wolfer-Native confrontations.

### Who they were

The wolfer was almost always a solo operator or a partnership of two or three men. He worked remote country for months at a time. He was viewed by other frontier men as a man who had chosen the wrong side of the wage scale and who could not associate with respectable hands. The cattlemen valued his service (the wolves killed calves) but did not invite him to the headquarters table. The cowhands sneered at him. The market hunters considered him beneath them. The Native nations hated him.

His clothes carried the smell of the bait and the carcasses. His hands were stained dark with the work. He stayed away from settled places because settled places did not want him. He spent the winter in his line shack and came in to a railhead in spring with his bundle of scalps and his pelts.

### The end

The wolf was substantially exterminated from the Plains by 1900. The wolfer's profession ended with the wolf. The few wolves that survived in the western mountains were poisoned out by federal and state predator-control programs in the early twentieth century. The wolf was declared a protected species in most western states only in the 1970s and 1980s; the restoration of the wolf to Yellowstone in 1995 is the recent counter-movement.

For the 1870s and 1880s, the wolfer is present and is one of the period's recognized low occupations. He is the character the other characters do not want at their fire.

## 12. The Professional Sporting Guide

By the late 1860s and through the 1870s and 1880s, the western frontier became a destination for the American Eastern and the British and European sporting tourist. The wealthy hunter came west to hunt buffalo, elk, deer, antelope, mountain sheep, and bear, and required a guide to organize the expedition.

### The clientele

- **The American Eastern sportsman.** The merchant or banker or attorney from Philadelphia or Boston or New York with the means and the leisure for a six-week hunt. He came by rail with his case of English shotguns and his .45-70 hunting rifle and his servant and his wife if she was sporting (the Eastern sporting wife was a defined type). He took the trip to write about for the local sporting paper, to bring back trophies, to escape his business.
- **The British and European nobleman.** The more famous version. The Earl of Dunraven made repeated trips to the Estes Park region of Colorado in the 1870s and eventually attempted to acquire the entire valley. Sir William Drummond Stewart had toured the Rockies in the 1830s and 1840s and set the template. Grand Duke Alexis of Russia made the famous 1872 buffalo hunt with George Custer and Buffalo Bill Cody. The European sporting party came with a retinue and a budget.
- **The American officer's hunt.** Not strictly professional guiding but related. Senior officers stationed at frontier posts often organized hunting trips with civilian scouts and Native guides. The officer's wife or visiting Eastern relative was often included.

### The guide

The professional guide was often a former mountain man, a former scout, a former hide hunter, a homesteader supplementing his income, or a Native man working the cash economy. He provided the local knowledge, the route, the camp, the cook, the horses, the wagon (where the country took a wagon), the hunting strategy, and the protection from hostile encounters.

Buffalo Bill Cody made his early reputation as a guide before his showman career. He guided Eastern and European parties through the late 1860s and early 1870s out of Fort McPherson and later from his ranch on the North Platte. His rates were among the highest in the trade: $50 a day for himself plus the costs of the camp and the horses. The Grand Duke Alexis hunt of 1872 was the high-water mark of the trade. Texas Jack Omohundro, Wild Bill Hickok briefly, California Joe Milner, and dozens of less famous figures worked the trade through the 1870s.

### The hunt

The Eastern sportsman's hunt was usually staged as a more elaborate version of the cattleman's hunt: a party of six to twelve riders, a wagon or two with the camp, a cook, several wranglers, a tent or two, the rifles, the trophy preparation gear (the salt for the hides, the boxes for the heads).

The hunting itself was the careful pursuit of trophy animals, the long stalk, the shot at two hundred yards, the photograph of the hunter beside the kill. The party covered twenty miles a day on horseback, hunted at dawn and at dusk, ate the day's game at the evening fire, slept in the tent. The trophies were salted and packed and shipped back east with the hunter. The mounted heads in the libraries of the East Coast clubs and country houses were the visible consequence.

### The economics

The Eastern hunting party paid the guide $300 to $1,500 for a six-week trip depending on the party's size and the guide's reputation. The European nobleman paid more. The guide netted a substantial portion of his year's income from two or three good parties. The guide also provided introductions and access to specific country (the Wind River for elk, the Rockies for bear, the Yellowstone country for the rare experience of country not yet overhunted). The country-knowledge was the guide's principal asset.

## 13. The Military and Contract Hunter

The frontier army post needed fresh meat. The post commissary supplied the bulk staples (salt pork, beans, hardtack, coffee) but the fresh meat (beef, mutton, game) was contracted locally. The contract hunter was a defined position.

### The work

The post hired a hunter (or a hunter and a small crew) on contract for a season or a year. The hunter supplied the post mess with a quota of fresh meat — typically three to five animals a week for a small post, more for a larger one. He hunted in the country around the post, brought in the game, and was paid by the pound or by the animal.

A contract hunter at a frontier post earned $50 to $100 a month plus a supply of ammunition. He sometimes had a small cabin near the post, sometimes lived with his family in the post sutler's settlement, sometimes had a homestead nearby. He was not a soldier and was not under military discipline; he was a civilian employee.

Many of the famous frontier figures held post hunter contracts at points in their careers. Buffalo Bill held one at Fort McPherson in the late 1860s before his guiding career took off. California Joe held one at various Western posts. The standard frontier scout (the civilian employed by the army to interpret country and people for the cavalry columns) often combined the scouting work with hunting.

## 14. The Trapper and the Trader

The fur trade as the dominant western occupation had ended by the early 1840s with the collapse of the beaver hat market. A residual fur trade in marten, fox, lynx, otter, mink, beaver (for trim and not for hats), bear, and badger persisted through the 1870s and 1880s, particularly in the Northern Rockies, the Northwest, the Sierras, and the Yellowstone country.

### The trapper

The fur trapper of the 1870s and 1880s was a diminished figure compared to the mountain man of the 1820s and 1830s but was not extinct. He worked a trapline of a hundred or more steel traps through a country he knew, checked them every few days, skinned and stretched the pelts at his cabin, and shipped them to the fur house in spring.

The major fur houses (the Hudson's Bay Company in Canada, the American Fur Company successors, the smaller St. Louis and St. Paul houses) bought through agents at frontier posts and trading houses. The price for a marten in 1880 was $3 to $5; for a fox $2 to $4; for a mountain lion $2 to $3 (more for the bounty where one was offered); for a bear $5 to $20 depending on size and condition. A working trapper in good country earned several hundred to a thousand dollars a season. He spent the winter in his cabin working the line and the summer with his family or in town.

### The trader

The frontier trading post (Bent's Fort in its later phases, the Hudson's Bay posts in the Northwest, the post sutlers, the Indian agency traders, the small town merchants) bought furs and hides from trappers and from Native bands and shipped them to the eastern markets. The trader extended credit to the trapper or the band against the next season's take, sold supplies (flour, ammunition, blankets, tobacco, knives, beads, alcohol where it was allowed and often where it was not), and took his percentage. The Native trade in particular was substantial through the 1870s and into the early reservation period. The agency trader in particular held a position of considerable power over the band's economic life and was often a corrupt one.

The trapper is a solitary figure. The trader is a social one. Both are working men in a specific economic system. The trapper's work is physical and slow; the trader's is commercial and continuous.

## 15. The Vocabulary of the Trade

### Buffalo hunting

- **Stand** — the hunter's prone position and the episode of shooting from it.
- **The herd** — the group of animals being hunted.
- **The sentinel, the leader, the watcher** — the dominant cow the hunter shot first.
- **Shooting sticks** — the crossed wooden sticks the hunter used as a rest for the rifle.
- **The hide, the robe** — the skin; _robe_ for the heavy winter coat used for sleighs and carriages.
- **Tongue, hump, marrow gut, fleece, boudins** — the cuts of buffalo meat the hunters ate (the tongues sometimes salted and shipped; the rest consumed in camp).
- **Stretching, fleshing, salting** — the preparation of the hide.
- **The bone trade, the bone wagon, the bone pile** — the secondary trade in skeletons for fertilizer.
- **The buffalo gun, the Big Fifty** — the Sharps rifle in heavy caliber.
- **Cartridge box, ammunition belt, primer pouch** — the hunter's gear.
- **Skinner, fleecer, fleshing knife** — the skinning vocabulary.
- **The runner** — the hunter who shot from horseback at a galloping herd, the older technique, mostly displaced by the stand.
- **The poison hunter** — disparaging, a hunter who used poison rather than the rifle, rare in the buffalo trade.

### Market hunting and meat

- **Game, meat, venison.** _Venison_ for deer meat specifically, not for game generally as in modern usage. _Game_ was the live animal; _meat_ was what came back to camp.
- **Field-dressing, gutting, bleeding out** — the immediate post-kill work.
- **Hanging, aging, curing** — the processing.
- **Quarter, side, saddle, hindquarter, forequarter** — the cuts.
- **Jerky, jerk meat** — sun-dried thin strips of meat.
- **Pemmican** — the pounded dried meat with fat and berries, a Native preparation adopted by the trappers and the freighters.

### Wolfing

- **Bait, set bait, lay a bait** — the poisoned carcass.
- **Strychnine, strych, the powder** — the poison.
- **Scalp, scalping, the scalp tally** — the bounty work.
- **Pelt, pelt house, fur shed** — the pelt trade.

### General

- **Skinning knife, butcher knife, boning knife, steel** — the blades.
- **Pack mule, pack horse, pack saddle, panniers** — the transport.
- **Cabin, line shack, base camp, hunting camp** — the hunter's accommodation.
- **Outfit** — the hunter's organized expedition with men, animals, equipment, and supplies.
- **Sign** — the tracks, droppings, browse marks, and other physical evidence of game presence; _cutting sign, reading sign._
- **Stalk, approach, shot** — the sequence of the hunt.
- **Kill, take, drop** — the hunter's terms for the successful shot. _Took ten head, dropped a five-point bull, killed a sow with two cubs._

## 16. Sound, Smell, Light

- **The buffalo hunter's stand.** The crack of the Sharps and the long roll of the echo across the rolling country. The smell of the burning powder hanging on the still air. The herd's confused bellowing as the leaders went down. The crash of the heavy bodies hitting the ground. The wind across the grass between shots. The hunter working the breech and loading and shouldering and firing again. The smell of the powder mixing with the smell of the blood as the kill grew. The skinners arriving at noon and the smell of opened animals rising from the field.
- **The market hunter's morning.** The cold dawn at the watering place. The buck coming in to drink. The hunter at the edge of the brush with the rifle on the rest. The single shot. The buck dropping. The hunter walking out across the meadow in the first light. The smell of the wet grass and the buck's last breath. The work with the knife opening the carcass. The blood pooling on the frosted ground.
- **The wolfer's bait.** The dead horse opened on the prairie three days. The smell carrying for miles. The dead wolves around it in the morning, contorted, their lips drawn back from their teeth. The ravens and the eagles dead at the edges. The silence of the country. The wolfer with his knife scalping the carcasses and stuffing the scalps in his bag.
- **The guide's evening camp.** The tent in the mountain meadow. The hobbled horses cropping in the dark. The cook at the fire. The Eastern sportsman in his city boots beside the fire with his glass of whiskey and his cigar. The guide with his coffee at the edge of the firelight. The talk of the next day's hunt. The stars in the sky over the mountains. The smell of the wood smoke and the meat cooking.

## 17. The Cheat Sheet

The buffalo hide hunter is the frontier industrial worker. Render him at the stand with the Sharps on the rest, the herd milling, the smoke hanging, the methodical work. The market hunter is a craftsman of the approach and the careful shot. He works alone or with a small crew. He knows his country. He ships meat to the railroad camp and the Eastern restaurant. He is not yet a poacher. The wolfer holds the lowest social standing of the hunters. He works strychnine bait. He kills indiscriminately. He smells of his work. The Native nations hate him. The other frontier men do not invite him to the fire. The professional sporting guide serves the Eastern and European hunting tourist. The scene is the East meeting the West. The contract hunter at the army post is a civilian employee; he brings in the fresh meat, knows the officers, is not one of them. The trapper of the residual fur trade is a diminished figure compared to the mountain man of the 1830s but is present. The political consequence of the buffalo slaughter is the destruction of the Plains nations' material base; the Native witness is the second character in any buffalo hunting scene that is being honestly played. The sound of the Sharps, the smell of the carcasses three days old, the silence of the country a month after the wolfer has worked it — these are the sensory anchors.

The frontier hunter was not a sportsman. He was a worker. The work emptied a continent of its animals in two generations. Render the work and the country and the consequence, and the hunter stands in his place.
