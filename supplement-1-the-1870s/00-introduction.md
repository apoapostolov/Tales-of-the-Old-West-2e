# 1870s: The Old West

The American West of the 1870s is a country built on dust, iron, horse sweat, and distance. It is a place where every sentence of life does a hard job. The screen tradition shrank this land to white men and Indians. It painted over the dirt with a clean brush. A western that flattens this mix into cowboys and savages is not a western about the West. It is a western about a movie.

The actual country held Mexicans whose families had been there for two centuries before the United States existed. It held Black freedmen riding as Buffalo Soldiers, Chinese laborers breaking rock for the railroads, German farmers turning sod, Irish navvies laying track, Cornish hard-rock miners deep under the earth, Bohemian and Czech homesteaders, French Canadians in the high trapping country. It held dozens of Native nations, each with its own language and law and history, fighting a slow retreat against the wire and the wheel.

This book is the record of that country. It is not decorated. It is built from the cold facts of the ledger.

It is the life in the mining camps and the cattle towns. It is the cost of a blanket and the wage of a working man. It is the violence that hung over the borderlands like weather. It is the sound a lead steer makes when it has been four days on the Cimarron without water.

> The fever came up the second night. By the third he could not be got to drink. The doctor pulled the dressing back and looked at the wound and put it back the way it had been. He did not say anything to the woman who sat by the bed but he stood a moment longer than he needed to before he picked up his bag and went out.
>
> She sat by him through the fourth day. He spoke twice. The first time he asked for his mother. The second time he spoke a name she did not know and after that he did not speak again. The smell that had come into the room on the third day stayed in her hair after he was buried. She would wash and wash and at night smell it still on the pillow.

Welcome to the 1870s. The land does not care. That indifference is the story.
