# Chapter 21 — Gambling

> _"HE HAD BEEN AT THE TABLE FOUR HOURS."_

Gambling was the activity of the working West. Cowhands gambled their wages on payday. Soldiers gambled garrison pay. Miners gambled the strike. Women in the towns ran games. The professional gambler had a hand and an eye. He was neither outlaw nor citizen but his own thing. What follows are the four games, the gambler, cheating methods, stakes, and the table as a space.

---

## 1. The Four Major Games

- **Faro.** The bank game of the saloon from the 1870s into the 1890s. A house game, played against the dealer (the "bank"). The house edge was one and one-half percent — the lowest of house games. Faro dominated.
- **Monte.** Two games sharing the name. Spanish monte is a bank game with a Spanish-deck heritage, dominant in the Southwest and border country, the standard alongside faro. Three-card monte is a confidence game where the dealer wins because the game is rigged, played by sharps on stages, in railroad cars, in saloons — a swindle, not a game. The two are not to be confused.
- **Poker.** The private game of the West. Played between players, with the house taking a rake or a table fee. Multiple variants in the period, evolving. The variants in the West were five-card draw (each player dealt five cards, may discard and draw replacements once, betting in two rounds) and five-card stud (first card down, four up; betting after each up card; took hold from the late 1860s). Texas hold 'em did not exist in the 1870s. Stud and draw were the games.
- **Other games.** Chuck-a-luck (a.k.a. birdcage) — a dice game with three dice in a wire cage; players bet on which numbers come up; a house game with a house edge; common in saloons. Roulette, present in the saloons; the American double-zero wheel (38 numbers) became standard in the West; played but never as common as faro. Keno, a lottery game with numbered balls or beans, common in the larger saloons. Brag, all-fours, euchre — card games of regional or class distribution; brag was a poker ancestor; euchre was popular in the army.

## 2. Faro — Procedure

- **The layout** is a green baize cloth or a printed leather mat showing the thirteen ranks (Ace through King) of one suit laid out in two rows. Players bet by placing chips (or coin) on the rank they predict. **The dealer's box** is a metal or wooden box with one open side, sized to hold a deck of cards face-up. The cards are inserted face-up. The top card is visible through the window.
- **The deal.** The top card (the "soda") is removed and set aside; it does not figure in the betting. The dealer pulls the next card and lays it to one side of the box — this is the "loser" or "banker's card"; all bets on cards of that rank lose. The dealer pulls the next card and lays it on the other side — this is the "winner" or "player's card"; all bets on cards of that rank win. The two cards together are a "turn." The dealer pays winning bets and rakes losing ones. The deck is dealt down in turns until two cards remain (with the soda set aside). This is the "hock," and the last turn is dealt for double or triple stakes.
- **The casebook (case keeper)** is a device tracking which cards have been played — a wooden frame with thirteen rows of four beads, one row per rank. As each card is dealt, the case keeper moves a bead. Players can see at any moment which cards remain in the deck. This is the faro player's instrument.

- **Copper.** A player can "copper" his bet — placing a hexagonal token (a copper) on top of his chips — which reverses the bet. Now he wins if the rank loses and loses if it wins. This reverses the bet.
- **The split.** If both winner and loser cards in a turn are the same rank, it is a "split." The dealer takes half of all bets on that rank. The split gave the bank its advantage.
- **The call.** On the last turn (the "hock"), if a player had tracked the casebook, he might "call the turn" — predict the order of the last three cards. Four to one. This was where the player could gain an edge.
- **The vocabulary.** _Bank_ (the dealer; he holds the bank); _lay_ (to place a bet — _he laid ten on the queen_); _soda_ (the top burned card); _hock_ (the last few cards); _casekeeper_ (the man tracking the deck); _copper_ (the reversing token); _heel_ (a bet specifying that one of two cards paired in a turn wins).

A faro game dealt without cheating had a house edge of one and one-half percent. Other house games had higher edges. This is why faro dominated. A player who tracked the casebook could beat the game in theory. In practice, dealers cheated — rigged boxes, marked cards, dealing from the center — and kept the edge.

A faro game has a rhythm. The dealer pulls; pause; he pulls; pause. The two cards land and the bets settle. The case keeper moves a bead. The next bets go down. In the pause between turns sat the game — men watched the layout, sipped whisky, tracked the casebook.

## 3. Spanish Monte

The card game of the Southwest and border country. Played with a Spanish 40-card deck (no 8s, 9s, 10s).

The dealer holds the deck face-down. He removes the top two cards and places them face-up in a row — the "top layout." He removes the bottom two cards and places them face-up in another row — the "bottom layout." Players bet that the next card turned from the deck will match (in rank) one of the four exposed cards. The dealer turns the top card of the deck. If it matches in rank one of the four exposed cards, players who bet on that card win. The dealer (the banker) pays even money on most bets. The house edge comes from the four-of-a-kind problem: if more than one card of a rank has already been dealt, the bet's mathematics shift.

The vocabulary is Spanish: _banco_ (the bank), _puerta_ (the door — the top layout), _albur_ (the bottom layout), _gallo_ (the rooster — a winning card).

Monte was the gambler's game of Mexico. A monte table in El Paso, Tucson, Las Vegas (New Mexico Territory), or any border town was a space where Spanish was spoken. The Anglo who sat there was the outsider in his own country.

## 4. Poker — Procedure

Poker is a private game between players. The house provides the table, the cards, sometimes the dealer, and takes a rake (a small percentage of each pot) or a table fee.

- **Five-card draw — the game of the 1870s.** Each player puts up an ante (a forced bet). Each player is dealt five cards face-down. A round of betting: each player in turn may check (if no bet has been made), bet, call (match the current bet), raise, or fold. After betting, each player may discard up to three cards and draw replacements (some house rules allowed four if the player held an Ace). A second round of betting. Showdown — best hand wins.
- **Five-card stud.** Each player put up an ante. One card dealt face-down (the hole card), one face-up. Betting round, opened by the high card showing. Another card up. Betting round. Another card up. Betting round. Last card up. Final betting round. Showdown.

- **The hand rankings (1870s standard),** from low to high: high card; one pair; two pair; three of a kind; straight; flush; full house; four of a kind; straight flush. The straight flush ranking was settled in the 1860s. The royal flush as a name came later. A wild card was not standard. Lowball variants existed but few men played them.
- **The vocabulary.** _Ante_ (forced opening bet); _pot_ (the bets in play); _call_ (match the current bet); _raise_ (match and increase); _fold_ (surrender the hand); _check_ (decline to bet without folding); _showdown_ (reveal hands); _tells_ (the signs a man gave about his hand); _bluff_ (bet a hand that was no good to drive men out); _the dead man's hand_ (aces and eights — the hand Wild Bill Hickok was reputedly holding when shot in Deadwood, August 1876; the phrase became a story in the West).

The professional has habits. He stacks his chips. He does not look at his hole card more than once. He plays slow when he means to play and fast when he folds. He does not drink at the table, or he drinks whisky-colored water. He sits with his back to a wall.

The amateur drinks. He is loud or silent depending on the strength of his hand. He counts his money during the play. He looks at his cards too often. He makes his bet with the wrong number of chips and corrects it. The professional reads him in three hands.

## Faro at Midnight

> The dealer turned a king to the soda and a four to the hock and the layout went still while the bettors looked at the cards. Three coppers came off the king. One bet stayed on the four. The dealer pulled the cases (a six and a nine), then began the next turn.
>
> A jack to the loser. A queen to the winner. The dealer paid the queen bets and pulled the jack bets and the caseman moved the abacus beads. Two more turns went by without a hit on the money on the table.
>
> Then the dealer turned a deuce to the loser and a deuce to the winner. A split. The house took half. The man who had the deuce stack — from the east — pushed his chair back and stared at the dealer for a moment.
>
> "A split," the dealer said. "House takes half. You want the other half down or pulled?"
>
> "Pulled."
>
> The dealer counted out the half stack and pushed it across the layout to him. The man picked it up and put it in his coat pocket and stood up and walked out without looking back. He had been at the table four hours.
>
> "Cards," the dealer said. The next turn began.

## 5. Cheating

The cheat had a repertoire. Each method fit its game.

- **The holdout.** A device worn under the coat—a "Kepplinger" or a strap holdout—that delivered a card into the hand or took one away. The player palmed the card between the holdout and the play. Detection: a player whose hand always had what he needed.
- **Marked cards.** Cards marked on the back so the cheat could read them without seeing the face. _Sand-tell_—cards touched with fine sandpaper at specific positions; the cheat felt the marks; an unsuspecting player did not. _Luminous reader_—a faint mark visible only through colored glasses or a specific light; mostly later than the 1870s but appearing. _Edge work_—cards trimmed slightly so a stripper deck cut to a desired card. _Daub_—a paste applied to specific positions on the card backs, visible to the cheat who knew where to look.
- **Dealing seconds.** The dealer dealt not the top card but the one underneath, holding the top for himself or his confederate. A skill that took years; a true second-deal was invisible.
- **The cold deck.** A deck arranged beforehand and switched in for the legitimate deck during play. The switch happened in a moment of distraction and the hand that followed was rigged.
- **The signaler / the partner.** Two cheats at the table communicating with signals—a touch of the ear, a cough, a particular phrasing. The partner knew the cheat's hand and bet accordingly. The partner often remained unsuspected because he played the role of an ordinary player.

A player who suspected cheating called out the cheat. The traditional response was to flip the table, draw a derringer or a knife from a vest, and fight. Many famous gunfights began at the gambling table. The clean cheat—the one who could not be caught—often went his whole career undetected. The famous gambler Soapy Smith built a town-running operation around organized cheating.

## 6. The Professional Gambler

The professional gambler was a distinct character with specific markers.

- **Dress.** Black broadcloth coat—frequently a frock coat—boiled white shirt, black string tie or a small bow, black wool trousers, polished boots, often a vest of patterned silk or brocade. The hat: a low-crown black wool. The watch chain across the vest. A diamond ring or a gold pin if successful. The dress signaled: this man did not work outdoors; this man had standing; this man had money. The working dress and the appearance of money were part of the bank he brought to the table.
- **Manner.** Soft-spoken. Slow-moving. Long looks. Never visibly angry. The gambler who showed emotion at the table had lost his edge. **Hands** clean, nails trimmed; the hands moved precisely; he did not gesture broadly; he did not slap the table; the hands were his instrument. **Voice** period-formal—the gambler spoke like a man who had read books: _I do believe, I am inclined to think, sir, ma'am._ The vocabulary was broader than a cowhand's. (See Chapter 9.)
- **The famous.** _Doc Holliday_—tubercular dentist turned gambler, friend of Wyatt Earp; educated, deadly, sick; the model for the educated gambler with a pistol. _Wild Bill Hickok_—former scout and lawman, turned to professional gambling in his last years; killed at the table. _Poker Alice (Alice Ivers)_—female professional poker player; operated in Deadwood and elsewhere; the skilled woman gambler was rarer than the man but real. _Bat Masterson_—lawman, gambler, eventually a New York sportswriter. _Luke Short_—dapper, deadly, quiet; the model for the small, well-dressed gambler.

Most successful gamblers died of disease—consumption was epidemic in the trade—or of violence at the table. A few retired and died in beds. The gambler who lived past forty was unusual.

## 7. Stakes — What the Dollar Amounts Meant

The weight of a gambling scene turned on what the stakes meant. (See Chapter 13.) Brief reference:

- **A nickel ante** — the penny-ante game; cowhands killing time in the bunkhouse.
- **A quarter ante** — standard saloon poker; a pot might run to ten or twenty dollars.
- **A dollar ante** — a serious game; a pot might run to a hundred dollars; a cowhand's monthly wage was about thirty.
- **A five-dollar ante** — a high game; the professional gambler's table; pots could run to several hundred dollars; a scene at this table carried the weight of months of a working man's wages on each hand.
- **A bank bet at faro** — could be anything; players laid five cents up to a hundred dollars depending on the house; the famous Bird Cage in Tombstone had no limit.

The gambler who lost his last roll was a recurring western figure. The scene's mathematics: the man came in with three hundred dollars—a year's saved wages, or the proceeds of a small cattle sale—and left with nothing. The reader who knew what three hundred dollars meant in 1875 felt the weight.

## 8. The Table as Social Space

A faro or poker table was not only a place to gamble. It was a social center, an information exchange, and a place where deals other than the game's were made.

- **The cowhand on payday.** The drover's wages went to the saloon, and a portion went to the table. The scene of the cowhand losing his roll was a fundamental western figure.
- **The cattle deal at the table.** Two ranchmen at the same table might negotiate a cattle sale between hands; the poker game was the cover for the business.
- **The rendezvous.** A man met another man at a specific table at a specific hour; information changed hands; money changed hands; the gambling was incidental.
- **The watcher.** A man sat at a table to watch—a Pinkerton detective tracking a fugitive, a sheriff watching a suspect, a relative watching a son; the watcher did not play seriously; he lost small amounts as the cost of the seat.

A scene at the table could carry any of these currents. The game was the visible activity. The real business happened between the hands.

## 9. Women at the Gambling Table

The respectable woman did not gamble. The disreputable woman did. The third category—the professional woman gambler—was rare but real.

- **The saloon girl.** Many saloons employed women whose job was to entice men to play and to drink. They were not professional gamblers; they steered customers to the games. They took a cut of the house's win and often a cut of the drinks.
- **The brothel madam.** Some madams ran gambling tables in their houses—the sporting house was often part-saloon, part-brothel. The madam might deal faro or run the games herself. Some madams were known for fair dealing; others for the opposite.
- **The professional.** Poker Alice. Lottie Deno (an alias; her real name was Carlotta Thompkins). Madame Mustache (Eleanore Dumont, a French-born faro dealer who worked the gold camps of California, Nevada, and Montana). These women were known by name and by reputation. The professional female gambler dressed well, played her own bank or sat at a private game, and was treated with the respect a successful gambler commanded. She was not, by virtue of being a gambler, a prostitute; the two trades overlapped but were not the same.
