# Chapter 9 — Language and the Paper World

> _“PEOPLE DO NOT GIVE IT CREDENCE THAT A FOURTEEN-YEAR-OLD GIRL COULD LEAVE HOME AND GO OFF IN THE WINTERTIME TO AVENGE HER FATHER'S BLOOD BUT IT DID NOT SEEM SO STRANGE THEN.”_
>
> — MATTIE ROSS, 1928, OF EVENTS IN 1878 (CHARLES PORTIS, _TRUE GRIT_)

The civilization rode west on paper. Messengers carried heavy canvas sacks of sealed letters across the blank plains. Printers pressed black ink into cheap rag-paper broadsheets in every raw railhead town. Clerks drafted heavy vellum land deeds, filed dark iron-gall ink ledgers in the county courthouses, and pushed high-voltage electrical current through the iron telegraph lines.

The spoken language held the same physical gravity. Men spoke a rigid, nineteenth-century English. The educated men utilized a heavy Latinate vocabulary. The preachers dragged the dark cadence of the King James Bible straight into the brush. Workingmen relied on raw, highly regional idioms that turned slowly over decades, anchored tightly to the physical tools, the dirt, and the animals they handled.

---

## 1. The Lexicon

A man in the 1870s did not speak the language of the modern world. His speech possessed absolute physical limits. To run the period accurately, the following modern words and phrases remain strictly banned.

- **OK / Okay:** Rarely spoken before 1900. Do not use.
- **Guy:** In the period, the word meant a strangely dressed person, derived from Guy Fawkes. The modern meaning arrived decades later.
- **Hopefully:** As a sentence adverb, it did not exist. Men said _I hope_.
- **Kid:** A slang word for child. Used increasingly by the 1870s.
- **Cool / Awesome:** Modern slang. A period man said _first-rate, bully,_ or _capital_, and reserved _awesome_ for the terrifying acts of God.
- **Sketchy / Creepy:** A man said _queer, fishy,_ or _suspicious_.
- **Teenager:** Did not exist. A boy of sixteen was a _lad, a youth,_ or a _young man_.
- **Folks / Howdy:** Solid, deep-rooted period words. Used heavily.
- **Passed Away / Kicked the Bucket:** Standard period euphemisms for death.
- **Gun:** In the 1870s, a _gun_ was a heavy artillery piece or a shotgun. Men called their revolvers _pistols, six-shooters,_ or _six-guns_. They called rifles _long guns_ or _rifles_.
- **Gunfighter / Gunslinger:** Twentieth-century Hollywood inventions. The men who killed other men were called _shootists, killers, bad men,_ or _shooters_.
- **Posse / Outlaw / Vigilante:** Heavily used period legal and extralegal terms.
- **The Wild West:** An eastern promotional phrase generated entirely by Buffalo Bill Cody's 1883 stage show. Men living in 1875 did not call their reality the Wild West.
- **Fast Draw / Quickdraw:** Did not exist as terminology. The Hollywood duel did not exist.
- **Hold up / Bushwhack / Dry-gulch:** The correct, violent terminology for armed robbery and lethal ambush.
- **Reckon / Allow / I expect / I figure / Fixin' to:** The dominant, continuous idioms of the southern and frontier workingman.

## 2. The Slurs

A grim section. Read carefully and decide deliberately, scene by scene.

The nineteenth-century West used racial slurs that are now correctly treated as harm. They were spoken in casual conversation by ordinary people; they appeared in newspapers, in court records, in the patter of saloon characters and the fond letters of otherwise gentle men.

The choice is one of three. **Period-accurate use.** Some characters say what they would have said. The audience is shown the casual cruelty of the period as it was. Cormac McCarthy does this; Larry McMurtry does it (_Lonesome Dove_'s Bolivar uses Spanish slurs; the cowboys use racial epithets). The risk is that the contemporary audience takes the slur as authorial endorsement. **Restricted use.** Slurs appear only in moments where they carry narrative weight (a man revealing his cruelty, a town's casual exclusion). Avoided in narration, used only in dialogue, used only when the moment demands it. **Soft-pedaled.** The writer leaves slurs implied, written as _the word he used,_ avoided on the page. This is a defensible choice for many books and is the choice most contemporary literary western novels make.

The N-word in particular is the hardest. Period dialogue from white characters about Black characters used it routinely. A Confederate veteran in Texas in 1875 used it without thought. A reader in the present cannot read it without thought. The gap between _period accuracy_ and _current harm_ is real.

The author's stance is _this was so._ The author does not endorse the slur or the men who used it. The author shows the world.

Practical guidance. If you use a slur, use it because the moment requires it; do not use it for color or for shock. Show the harm — the slur is not a piece of period flavor like a saddle horn, it is a wound being inflicted. The slur is more powerful when it is rare. Some books need the word; some books work without it; decide early and follow through. Slurs against Mexicans (_greaser, spik_), against Chinese (_chink, John, Celestial, Mongolian_), against Native people (_savage, redskin, buck, squaw_) are period-correct in white speech and require the same deliberate choice. Slurs spoken between members of a group follow different rules than across-group speech; consult a sensitivity reader if you are working outside your own background.

The bottom line: slurs are not decoration. They are a load-bearing element of the period and require deliberate handling.

## 4. Spanish in the Anglo West

The Anglo cowman of Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, southern Colorado, and southern California spoke a working English heavily salted with Spanish loans and pidgin Spanish for trade and stock work.

Period loans usable freely in southwestern setting: _adios, amigo, andale, bandido, bronco, buckaroo (vaquero), caballero, calaboose (calabozo), canyon (cañón), chaparral, chaps (chaparreras), corral, coyote, dally (dale vuelta), fandango, frijole, hacienda, hombre, hoosegow (juzgado), lariat (la reata), lasso (lazo), llano, loco, mesa, mesquite, mustang (mesteño), padre, palomino, peon, pinto, plaza, poncho, pronto, ranch (rancho), remuda, rodeo, rurales, sabe, savvy, señor, señora, señorita, sierra, sombrero, stampede (estampida), tortilla, vaquero._

A working cowhand spoke perhaps a hundred words of Spanish for his trade. Very few Anglos were fluent. The Tejano vaqueros and the Mexican peons spoke Spanish among themselves and adapted English with their employers.

For dialogue: an English-only Anglo character does not switch into formal Spanish phrases. He uses the words he learned at work, often mispronounced (the Anglo _lar-yet_ for _la reata_).

A Spanish-speaking character in English narration: write the English clean, indicate Spanish through occasional untranslated words and through syntax (_you have not eaten today, no?_). Resist the urge to write phonetic accents — they condescend. Trust the audience.

## 5. Registers

Men spoke exactly according to their station.

**The Working Man.** He spoke in short, heavily active sentences. He used zero Latinate vocabulary. He dropped grammar consistently (_I seen him. I done it. He don't. We ain't got none._) He swore constantly in male company and shut his mouth completely around women.

> _Well, I seen the man down at the corral and I told him I reckoned the bay had thrown a shoe. He didn't say nothing. Just went out there and looked._

**The Literate Man.** He possessed some formal schooling. He utilized occasional Latinate vocabulary gleaned from reading the weekly newspaper. He maintained basic grammatical structure.

> _I observed the man at the corral and informed him the bay had cast a shoe. He made no reply, but went out and looked her over._

**The Educated Man.** He commanded a massive, formal, Latinate vocabulary. He built long, periodic sentences. He quoted the King James Bible, Cicero, and Shakespeare from memory. He code-switched cleanly into the working register when addressing his hired men, and shifted back to absolute formality in the courtroom.

> _I encountered the gentleman at the corral and brought to his attention that the bay had thrown a shoe. He neither spoke nor acknowledged me, but proceeded to inspect the animal._

- **The Immigrant:** He imported the rigid syntax of his native tongue directly into English. A German homesteader placed the verb firmly at the end of the sentence.
- **The Military Man:** He spoke in brisk, flat declaratives. He relied completely on rigid military jargon (_detachment, skirmish line, double-time, vidette_). He addressed superiors exclusively as _Sir_ or by rank.
- **The Preacher:** He spoke in the heavy, rolling, parallel structure of the King James Bible.

## 6. Profanity

The nineteenth-century workingman generated an immense volume of profanity. It did not match modern usage.

- **The Rough Register:** Men in isolated male company relied on religious and localized physical profanity: _God damn, by God, hell-fire, son of a bitch, bastard, whoreson._ The words _shit_ and _piss_ existed as ancient, common working-class descriptors. The word _fuck_ existed heavily in the oral record but print laws and subsequent historical bowdlerization largely scrubbed it from the surviving paper.
- **The Constrained Register:** In the physical presence of a woman, a child, or a preacher, a man instantly suppressed his language. He substituted transparent euphemisms: _Dad-burn, by gum, doggone, gosh, golly, confound it._ A deeply violent man violently correcting his own speech because a woman walked into the mercantile is a highly accurate period mechanism.
- **The Application:** Do not over-write the profanity. A man trapped under heavy rifle fire swore. A man betting his winter wages on a single card swore. A man buying coffee did not. If you write the profanity heavily across every scene, the text degrades into a cheap modern parody. Make the hard words count.

## 7. Names

A man’s name anchored his reality.

- **Anglo Men:** Heavy, traditional, or deeply biblical. _William, Charles, James, John, Robert, George, Henry, Joseph, David, Daniel, Samuel, Edward._ The rural and southern men carried the Old Testament: _Hiram, Silas, Ezra, Ezekiel, Elijah, Enoch, Silas, Obadiah, Zachariah, Ebenezer, Phineas, Caleb, Amos, Cyrus, Levi._ Black freedmen frequently adopted these same heavy biblical names, or took names representing their new status: _Lincoln, Washington, Freeman, Liberty._
- **Anglo Women:** _Mary, Sarah, Elizabeth, Margaret, Anna, Jane, Susan, Catherine, Martha, Frances, Lucy, Ellen, Emma, Hannah, Rebecca, Caroline, Ruth._ The period virtue names: _Prudence, Patience, Mercy, Charity, Hope, Faith, Temperance, Constance._
- **Mexican Men and Women:** _José, Juan, Manuel, Pedro, Francisco, Antonio, Miguel, Jesús, Tomás, Diego, Felipe, Alejandro, Roberto, Vicente, Mateo._ For women: _María, Ana, Elena, Carmen, Rosa, Isabel, Luisa, Teresa, Juana, Dolores, Esperanza, Concepción, Guadalupe, Mercedes, Pilar._
- **Native Names:** Men carried specific, translated names taken from visions or deeds: _Crazy Horse, Red Cloud, Spotted Tail, Black Kettle, Roman Nose._ Use the accurate historical translations or the Native language itself (_Tȟašúŋke Witkó_). Do not invent cheap, generic Native names.
- **Saloons and Ranches:** Men named saloons for animals, Eastern elegance, or crude reality: _The Long Branch, The Crystal Palace, The Bird Cage, The Bull's Head, The Bucket of Blood._ Men named cattle ranches strictly for the iron brand burned into the hide (_The JA, the XIT, the Lazy S_) or the physical geography of the water (_The Pitchfork, The Spade_).

## 8. The Newspaper

By the late 1870s, a heavy iron Washington hand-press operated in nearly every western railhead and mining camp. A town of five hundred transient men supported a weekly four-page broadsheet.

### The Mechanics

The physical reality of the newspaper office dictated the product. The editor bought _patent inside_ stock from Chicago—huge sheets of cheap paper pre-printed on one side with national wire news, serialized romance fiction, and advertisements for eastern patent medicine.

In his raw wooden office, the printer hand-set the local news letter by letter into a heavy iron composing stick, backwards. He locked the lead type into a heavy iron chase, rolled thick, black, noxious ink across the metal, laid the crisp paper down, and pulled the massive iron lever of the press purely by back-strength to print the second side. The room smelled permanently of kerosene, lead oxidation, black ink, and stale pipe tobacco.

### The Voice

The editor wrote in a highly stylized, florid, and frequently vicious register. He attacked rival editors in neighboring towns with borderline libel. He recorded the brutal local violence with complete, clinical detachment.

> _It is our painful duty to record the demise of Mrs. Eliza Patten, beloved wife of the late William Patten. The deceased was forty-three years of age and is survived by two children. Funeral services will be held at the Methodist Church on Sunday at two o'clock. Friends of the family are respectfully invited to attend._

> _The deplorable conditions on the road south of town have been the subject of correspondence in these columns for upwards of three months, and the County Commissioners continue in their inexplicable negligence. The recent freshet has rendered Plum Creek impassable. The freight wagons are detouring six miles to the eastward over country which can only be described as execrable._

## 9. The Wanted Poster

The county sheriff and the heavy railroad syndicates generated the posters and distributed them completely across the terrority.

### The Structure

A standard 1870s run printed black ink on cheap paper. It opened with heavy block type: _$5,000 REWARD_. It stated the crime explicitly. It detailed the physical man: _Thirty-two years of age. Five feet ten inches tall. Light complexion. Sandy hair. Blue eyes. A scar above the left eyebrow. Carries himself with a slight stoop. Speaks with a southern accent._ It listed the payout terms: generally _arrest and conviction_, rather than the popularized _dead or alive_, though the U.S. Marshals operating in the Indian Territory understood a dead man draped over a saddle brought the same cash payout entirely.

It went out by rail and coach. The local sheriff took a heavy iron tack and drove it straight into the wooden telegraph pole in front of the mercantile.

## 10. Letters

Men and women wrote continuously. The U.S. Mail ran by heavy Concord coach and steam train. A man paid three cents to send a folded paper weighing a single ounce.

### The Voice

The period letter operated in a highly formal, frequently restrained register. A woman starving on a failing dryland homestead frequently wrote past the isolation, addressing her family through a rigid, practical screen of concrete nouns.

> _Dear Sister,_
>
> _Your letter of March the second came two weeks ago and I have been trying to find a quiet hour to answer it. The spring has come at last and we are very busy with the planting. James is in the field from before light to dark and I have the garden in and the chickens setting and the wash behind me, so this is the first letter I have written this month._
>
> _Little Hattie has had the croup these three weeks and I have not slept a full night the whole time. She is better now and the worst is past, I trust._
>
> _The country has not improved. We have had no rain since March and the wheat is yellow already. If we do not have rain by Decoration Day there will be no crop and we will have to think hard about what to do come fall. I do not say this to trouble you. I know you cannot help and I know you have your own troubles._

### A Letter Home

> _Lampasas, Texas, March 12, 1875._
>
> _Dear Mother,_
>
> _Your letter of last month finally found me here. I am sorry I did not answer sooner but we have been on a drive to Kansas and back. I am well. The bay horse you remember me writing of has been sold. I have a sorrel now and he is a good one. We had two men killed coming home, one by drowning at Red River and one by lightning on the Canadian. I thank God I am safe._
>
> _I will send you twenty dollars by Wells Fargo when this reaches Austin next week. I wish it was more. The pay is what it is._
>
> _Tell Sister to write me at Lampasas care of Wright & Harris. Tell her I think of her every day._
>
> _Your son,_
>
> _Tom Pruitt_

## 11. Telegrams

The telegraph lines paced the heavy rail completely across the plains. A man paid by the word. The extreme cost formed the language.

> _ARRIVED CHEYENNE STOP CATTLE SOLD AT 22 STOP HOME THURSDAY STOP HENDERSON_

The local operator translated the clicking brass sounder directly by ear and wrote the block letters onto the yellow paper blank. He held the town's absolute secrets.

## 12. Legal Documents

The paper locked the land down. Every massive cattle transfer, every surveyed section, and every hanging generated a corresponding physical document dragged into the county courthouse and filed in the heavy iron safes.

### Bills of Sale

The handwritten paper moved the stock legally.

> _Know all men by these presents that I, John Henderson, of Cottonwood County, in the Territory of Colorado, in consideration of the sum of one hundred and sixty dollars to me in hand paid by James Whittaker of the same county, do hereby grant, sell, and convey to the said James Whittaker the following described livestock, to-wit: Forty head of mixed beef cattle, branded with the J-bar-H brand on the left hip._

The paper was entirely vulnerable. A rustler stole the cattle, forged the original owner's signature on a blank bill of sale, walked the stock two hundred miles, and pushed the forged paper confidently across the desk to a legitimate buyer.

> _John Henderson._

The bill of sale was the cattle thief's forgery target. An altered bill of sale (the original seller's name forged, the brand description altered) could launder stolen cattle.

### Deeds

The deed transferred real dirt outright. The surveyor dragged the heavy iron chain to establish the physical boundaries, and the lawyer drafted the heavy paper transferring the _southwest quarter of Section 14, Township 6 North, Range 8 West of the 6th Principal Meridian_. The county clerk recorded the transfer strictly in a heavy leather-bound ledger in a fireproof vault.

### Wills

A dying man dictated the division of his material reality. He frequently copied the formal structure straight from a cheap printed form book, utilizing the heavy, biblical cadence.

> _To my eldest son William, my saddle horse named Big John, my saddle, my Winchester rifle, and the sum of two hundred dollars. To my second son Henry, my work team, my wagon, my plow, and the sum of two hundred dollars. To my daughters Mary and Elizabeth, the sum of one hundred dollars each, to be paid at their majority or marriage._

A quoted will firmly establishes exactly what physical stock and steel the family owned and exactly how the dying man valued his blood.

## The Forged Paper

> Lewes opened the wooden desk drawer and took out the bundle of papers and laid them flat on the oak table. He untied the brown string and went through them in the dark room.
>
> The first was the forged bill of sale. It read out fine in Lewes's own hand on the thickest paper in the bundle. _Forty head of mixed beef cattle branded with the J-bar-H brand on the left hip._ The date read the fourteenth of May. Lewes had not been within sixty miles of Henderson's place on the fourteenth of May.
>
> The second was the original. He had pinched it from the iron spike on the desk at Henderson's place the night he cut the cattle out. It read out in Henderson's slanted hand, _Forty head of mixed beef cattle_. The brand description matched exactly. The date was the fourteenth of May. The heavy ink signature read John Henderson.
>
> The forged paper legally listed James Whittaker as the buyer. The original paper had been left entirely blank. Lewes had filled the buyer line with his own confederate's exact name and explicitly forged Henderson's signature at the bottom.
>
> He held the two heavy papers up to the burning coal-oil lamp. The forgery looked passable in good light. In bad light, stuffed inside a trail-driver's leather saddlebag after a week of heavy rain, no lawman would look at it twice.
>
> He folded the forgery and pushed it deep into his vest pocket. He took the original to the cast-iron cookstove, lifted the iron lid, dropped the paper directly onto the cherry coals, and watched the fire take it.

## 13. Diaries and Journals

A massive fraction of the literate population documented their days in minimal, concrete prose. Men and women carried small, cloth-bound or leather-bound books. Modern, highly emotional diaries did not exist. The period diarist recorded an unsparing inventory of grueling manual labor, failing weather, heavy debts, and isolated brutal deaths.

> _May 14. Plowed the south field. Broke the plowshare on a rock. Wind from the west. Tom Henderson stopped by. Shot a rattlesnake by the barn._

The working diary stripped all self-pity from the record and anchored the writer completely to the physical reality of the dirt. If a character reads a journal found in a dead man's saddlebag, the entries track water, horse exhaustion, and heavy rounds fired, rather than interior monologue.

Reading: the published diaries of pioneer women (Lillian Schlissel's _Women's Diaries of the Westward Journey_ is a standard collection); the diaries of soldiers and officers' wives; the journals of preachers (Peter Cartwright's autobiography is a memoir built from his journal); James Henry Cook's _Fifty Years on the Old Frontier_ (memoir but heavily journal-based).

## 14. Photographs

By the 1870s photography was widespread in the West. The cabinet card and the stereograph were standard formats. Itinerant photographers traveled the country making portraits, and established studios operated in every town of size. Famous landscape photographers — William Henry Jackson, Timothy O'Sullivan, A. J. Russell — documented the West's geography.

- **Carte de visite.** A small (about 2.5 by 4 inch) photograph mounted on a card. Used as a calling card; collected by family and friends.
- **Cabinet card.** A larger (about 4.5 by 6.5 inch) photograph mounted on a stiff card. Standard portrait format from the late 1860s through the 1890s.
- **Tintype.** A photograph on a thin iron plate. Cheap, durable, common at fairs and street photographers.
- **Stereograph.** A pair of nearly identical photographs mounted side by side on a card. Viewed through a stereoscope (a household viewer) for a 3-D effect. Massively popular for landscapes.

A photograph in a scene can carry memory (the dead son's photograph on the mantel), identify (the sheriff has a photograph of the fugitive; the deputy compares the photograph to the man at the bar), establish past (the faded photograph from before the war), or be made (the scene of the family sitting for the photographer is itself a period event — the long exposure, the held still poses, the formality).

## 15. Voice — Period Prose Register

Beyond the basic 1870s formality already discussed, specific document types had specific registers.

- **Legal register.** Latinate, repetitive, formulaic. _Know all men by these presents. To-wit. The party of the first part. Hereby grant, sell, and convey. Witness my hand and seal._
- **Newspaper register.** Florid, formal, periphrastic. _It is our painful duty to record. We are reliably informed. Repaired to. Wended his way. The aforesaid. The lately deceased._
- **Telegraph register.** Telegraphic. Stripped to nouns and verbs. _Arrived. Goods sold. Returning Thursday._
- **Personal letter register.** Formal salutations, then variable. The writer's education and situation determine register beyond the openings and closings.
- **Diary register.** Often minimal — the labor and weather. The fuller diaries swing into reflection in their own characteristic voice.
- **Sermon register.** Scripturally saturated, rhythmic, building.
- **Government / military register.** Formal, bureaucratic, hierarchical. _I have the honor to report. In compliance with instructions. Most respectfully submitted._
- **Cattle-trade register.** Brand-and-tally. _Forty head, three-up, mixed yearlings and twos, branded J-bar-H on left hip, swallow-fork in left ear._ The vocabulary is specific to the trade.
