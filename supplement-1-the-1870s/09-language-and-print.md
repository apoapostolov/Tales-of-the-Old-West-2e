# Chapter 9 — Language, Literacy, and the Paper World

> _“PEOPLE DO NOT GIVE IT CREDENCE THAT A FOURTEEN-YEAR-OLD GIRL COULD LEAVE HOME AND GO OFF IN THE WINTERTIME TO AVENGE HER FATHER'S BLOOD BUT IT DID NOT SEEM SO STRANGE THEN.”_
>
> — MATTIE ROSS, 1928, OF EVENTS IN 1878 (CHARLES PORTIS, _TRUE GRIT_)

The popular West is largely an oral world: men talk, men shoot, men ride, men drink. The historical West was a paper world. Newspapers reached every town. Wanted posters were nailed to every post office and saloon door. Letters traveled constantly between families, business associates, and government offices. Legal documents — bills of sale, deeds, wills, court records — were generated by every significant transaction. Telegrams flew between cities. Diaries and journals were kept by an enormous fraction of the population.

What follows is the working dictionary and the working paper inventory both — the words a person of the period had in his mouth, and the documents that traveled in his pocket and across his desk.

## Part One — The Language

---

## 1. The Big Rule

A nineteenth-century westerner spoke a nineteenth-century English. The period English was: more formal in writing than ours; more biblical in cadence (the King James was the universal literary model); heavily Latinate among the educated; heavily idiomatic and regional among the working; profane among men in male company and constrained around women, children, in church, in print; and slow to take new words — the modern habit of fast slang turnover was not yet established.

Get this background right and the period speaks itself.

## 3. The Slurs — What to Do with Them

A grim section. Read carefully and decide deliberately, scene by scene.

The nineteenth-century West used racial slurs that are now correctly treated as harm. They were spoken in casual conversation by ordinary people; they appeared in newspapers, in court records, in the patter of saloon characters and the fond letters of otherwise gentle men.

The choice is one of three. **Period-accurate use.** Some characters say what they would have said. The audience is shown the casual cruelty of the period as it was. Cormac McCarthy does this; Larry McMurtry does it (_Lonesome Dove_'s Bolivar uses Spanish slurs; the cowboys use racial epithets). The risk is that the contemporary audience takes the slur as authorial endorsement. **Restricted use.** Slurs appear only in moments where they carry narrative weight (a man revealing his cruelty, a town's casual exclusion). Avoided in narration, used only in dialogue, used only when the moment demands it. **Soft-pedaled.** The writer leaves slurs implied, written as _the word he used,_ avoided on the page. This is a defensible choice for many books and is the choice most contemporary literary western novels make.

The N-word in particular is the hardest. Period dialogue from white characters about Black characters used it routinely. A Confederate veteran in Texas in 1875 used it without thought. A reader in the present cannot read it without thought. The gap between _period accuracy_ and _current harm_ is real.

The author's stance is _this was so._ The author does not endorse the slur or the men who used it. The author shows the world.

Practical guidance. If you use a slur, use it because the moment requires it; do not use it for color or for shock. Show the harm — the slur is not a piece of period flavor like a saddle horn, it is a wound being inflicted. The slur is more powerful when it is rare. Some books need the word; some books work without it; decide early and follow through. Slurs against Mexicans (_greaser, spik_), against Chinese (_chink, John, Celestial, Mongolian_), against Native people (_savage, redskin, buck, squaw_) are period-correct in white speech and require the same deliberate choice. Slurs spoken between members of a group follow different rules than across-group speech; consult a sensitivity reader if you are working outside your own background.

The bottom line: slurs are not decoration. They are a load-bearing element of the period and require deliberate handling.

## 4. Period Slang — by Trade and Region

A working selection. Add as you read.

### Cowhand and drover

_Aboard_ — in the saddle. _Bell mare_ — the lead mare with the bell on her, around whom the remuda gathered. _Bog rider_ — a hand whose job was to pull cows out of mud holes. _Brand-blotcher_ — a rustler who altered brands. _Brush-popper_ — a south Texas hand who worked cattle out of thick brush. _Bronc / bronco_ — an unbroken horse. _Buckaroo_ — California-Nevada term for cowhand (from _vaquero_). _Chaps / chinks_ — leather leggings. _Chuck_ — food (chuck wagon, chuck box, chuck up). _Cinch up_ — tighten the cinch; metaphorically, settle a matter. _Cookie / coosie / dough-puncher / biscuit-shooter_ — the cook. _Cowpoke_ — period-correct from poking cattle through stockcar slats. _Cut_ — to separate from the herd. _Dally_ — to wrap the rope around the saddle horn (Spanish _dale vuelta_). _Dogie_ — an orphan calf. _Drag_ — the rear of the moving herd. _Drift_ — to wander. _Fill his hand_ — draw a pistol. _Fork a horse_ — mount and ride. _Forty-rod_ — cheap whiskey (kills you at forty rods). _Greenhorn_ — newcomer. _Grub_ — food. _Hog-tie_ — bind the legs. _Honda_ — the loop at the rope's end. _Jingle_ — bring in the remuda. _Kick over the traces_ — break out of restraint. _Maverick_ — an unbranded calf. _Mossy horn_ — an old steer. _Nester_ — a homesteader (cattleman's pejorative). _Outfit_ — a ranch and crew, or a man's gear. _Point_ — the lead position on a moving herd. _Powder-burner_ — a gunfighter. _Pull leather_ — grab the saddle horn (a sign of poor riding). _Quirly_ — a roll-your-own cigarette. _Range hog_ — a man who claimed too much range. _Remuda_ — the herd of saddle horses. _Rope corral_ — a corral made by stretching a rope. _Rustler_ — a thief of stock. _Saddle bum_ — a drifting cowhand. _Savvy_ — to understand (from _sabe_). _Segundo_ — the second in command on a drive. _Sky pilot_ — a preacher. _Slick / slick-eared_ — unbranded. _Snake-eyes_ — two ones on the dice. _Sourdough_ — the cook, or a long-term hand. _Stove up_ — broken-down. _String_ — the horses assigned to one hand. _Tenderfoot_ — a newcomer (specifically one who could not yet take the saddle). _Top hand_ — the best hand. _Trail boss_ — head of the drive. _Waddy_ — a working cowhand. _Whoa_ — stop. _Wrangler / horse jingler_ — the boy who handled the remuda.

### Mining

_Color_ — gold visible in the pan. _Dust_ — gold dust. _Glory hole_ — a rich pocket of ore. _Grass roots_ — surface ore. _Hardrock_ — vein mining. _High-grade_ (verb) — to steal rich ore. _Jackleg_ — a stand-mounted air drill. _Long tom_ — the long sluice. _Make a stake_ — earn enough to leave. _Muck_ — broken ore. _Pay dirt_ — gravel that contains gold. _Placer_ — surface-gold deposit. _Single-jack / double-jack_ — drilling techniques. _Stope_ — the open space mined out. _Tailings_ — waste from milling. _Widowmaker_ — air drill (silica dust killed the operator).

### Soldiers

_Ball_ — a bullet. _Bonnyclabber_ — sour milk. _Coffee cooler_ — a malingerer. _Dough-foot_ — infantryman. _Gibbus_ — a stockade. _Hardtack_ — a hard square biscuit. _Hog and hominy_ — Army rations. _Long roll_ — the drumbeat to assembly. _Mick_ — Irish (used by Irish themselves and against them). _Mule meat_ — bad rations. _Old man_ — the colonel. _Old soldier_ — a veteran; or as a verb, _to old-soldier it,_ to feign illness. _Padre_ — chaplain. _Salt horse_ — preserved beef. _Skirmish line_ — extended formation for combat. _Sutler_ — civilian merchant on the post. _Top_ — the first sergeant. _Webfoot_ — sailor.

### Townsmen and professionals

_Banker, lawyer, sawbones, sky pilot, schoolmarm_ — period terms. _Cardsharp_ — a professional gambler suspected of cheating. _Drummer_ — a traveling salesman. _Faro bank_ — the most popular gambling game in the West. _Fancy lady, soiled dove, fallen woman, shady lady_ — prostitute (polite to condescending). _Hash slinger_ — a waitress. _Inkslinger_ — a writer or clerk. _Sport / sporting man_ — a gambler. _Tinhorn_ — a flashy, low-grade gambler.

### Outlaws and criminal cant

_Beef_ (verb) — to kill. _Big augur_ — the boss of a gang. _Bracelets_ — handcuffs. _Bull_ — a lawman, especially a railroad guard. _Calaboose_ — jail. _Chuckaluck_ — a dice game. _Curly wolf_ — a tough character. _Dry-gulch_ — to ambush. _Highbinder_ — a Chinese tong gunman; broadly, a thug. _Jug_ — jail. _Lay low_ — hide. _Mooch_ — beg. _On the dodge_ — fleeing the law. _Owl-hooter / owl-hoot trail_ — the outlaw life. _Plug_ (verb) — to shoot. _Pull the pin_ — leave a job or place. _Rustler_ — stock thief. _Sneak_ (verb) — burgle. _Tin star_ — a lawman (pejorative).

## 5. Spanish in the Anglo West

The Anglo cowman of Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, southern Colorado, and southern California spoke a working English heavily salted with Spanish loans and pidgin Spanish for trade and stock work.

Period loans usable freely in southwestern setting: _adios, amigo, andale, bandido, bronco, buckaroo (vaquero), caballero, calaboose (calabozo), canyon (cañón), chaparral, chaps (chaparreras), corral, coyote, dally (dale vuelta), fandango, frijole, hacienda, hombre, hoosegow (juzgado), lariat (la reata), lasso (lazo), llano, loco, mesa, mesquite, mustang (mesteño), padre, palomino, peon, pinto, plaza, poncho, pronto, ranch (rancho), remuda, rodeo, rurales, sabe, savvy, señor, señora, señorita, sierra, sombrero, stampede (estampida), tortilla, vaquero._

A working cowhand spoke perhaps a hundred words of Spanish for his trade. Very few Anglos were fluent. The Tejano vaqueros and the Mexican peons spoke Spanish among themselves and adapted English with their employers.

For dialogue: an English-only Anglo character does not switch into formal Spanish phrases. He uses the words he learned at work, often mispronounced (the Anglo _lar-yet_ for _la reata_).

A Spanish-speaking character in English narration: write the English clean, indicate Spanish through occasional untranslated words and through syntax (_you have not eaten today, no?_). Resist the urge to write phonetic accents — they condescend. Trust the audience.

## 6. Registers — How a Character Sounds at His Rank

**The illiterate working man.** Short sentences. Plain Anglo-Saxon vocabulary (no Latinate words; _got_ not _received_). Idioms heavy. Profane in male company; constrained around women, children, preachers. Drops grammar (_I seen him; I done it; he don't; we ain't got none_).

> _Well, I seen the man down at the corral and I told him I reckoned the bay had thrown a shoe. He didn't say nothing. Just went out there and looked._

**The literate working man.** Slightly fuller sentences. Some Latinate vocabulary from school. Less grammar-dropping but some. Newspaper-influenced phrasing.

> _I observed the man at the corral and informed him the bay had cast a shoe. He made no reply, but went out and looked her over._

**The educated man.** Latinate, formal, periodic sentences when he chooses. Code-switches into the working register depending on company. Quotes scripture, sometimes Shakespeare or Cicero. Speaks differently in court, in church, in the saloon, at home.

> _I encountered the gentleman at the corral and brought to his attention that the bay had thrown a shoe. He neither spoke nor acknowledged me, but proceeded to inspect the animal._

- **The immigrant.** Imports syntax from his first language. The German speaker puts the verb at the end (_the bay her shoe had thrown_). The Mexican speaker uses Spanish word order (_the bay, she has thrown a shoe_). The Chinese speaker (in pidgin English) drops articles and conjugates loosely (_bay horse lose shoe_). Use lightly. A heavy hand makes the character a cartoon. One marker per long speech is enough.
- **The military man.** Brisk, abrupt, declarative. Military jargon (_detachment, company, troop, picket, vidette, skirmish line, double time_). Profane in the field, formal in HQ. _Sir_ and _Captain_ and _Colonel_ used liberally.
- **The preacher.** Biblical cadence — the King James was the model. Long sentences, rolling parallel structure. Latinate when reading; plainer when extemporizing.

> _Brethren, the Lord did not bring us out of bondage, did not lead us through the wilderness, did not deliver us upon this land of milk and honey, that we should forget Him in the prosperity of our hand._

**The Eastern dude.** Latinate, formal, slightly stiff. Uses Eastern slang the locals do not. Pronounces foreign loans as foreign (the _vah-ker-oh_ of the Boston schoolboy versus the _back-ah-ro_ of the cowhand). Often overformal where the situation calls for plain speech.

## 7. Profanity

The nineteenth century was rich and fluent in profanity, but the profanity differed from ours.

- **The rough male register.** _God damn, goddamned, by God, damnation, hell, hell-fire, the very devil, son of a bitch, bastard, bastard's bastard, whoreson._ _Shit_ and _piss_ were old words and used in male company. _Fuck_ existed and was used (American working-class profanity has been continuous since at least the seventeenth century) but written record is poor because of print law and editorial bowdlerization. _Cock, prick_ — period-correct. The cunt-word — period; used; rare in mixed company.
- **Constrained registers.** Around women, in church, in print: euphemism. _Dad-burn, dad-blame, by gum, by jingo, dagnabbit, dang, darn, gosh, golly, gee whillikers, gee whiz, jiminy, jeez, blame me, doggone, blasted, confound it._ _Heck, dang, gosh_ are post-1850s euphemisms and period-correct. A rough character switching into the polite register in front of a lady is a common period beat. The audience watches the effort.
- **The rule.** If you write profanity, write it because the moment requires it. A man under fire swears. A man in church does not. A man at a card table swears in proportion to his hand. A man addressing a strange woman in the street does not swear at all. Underwriting profanity is more period-accurate than over-writing it. Most published nineteenth-century writing scrubbed the profanity out entirely. The audience that has read the period knows the prose was cleaner than the speech. A modern western that lays the profanity on thick can read like a 1970s parody. The trick is selective use — make every _goddamn_ land.

- **Anglo first names (men).** Common: William (Will, Bill), Charles (Charlie, Chas), James (Jim, Jimmie, Jamie), John (Jack), Robert (Bob, Rob), Thomas (Tom), George, Henry (Hank, Hal), Joseph (Joe), David (Dave), Daniel (Dan), Samuel (Sam), Edward (Ed, Ned, Ted), Richard (Dick, Rich), Frank, Andrew (Andy), Albert (Al, Bert), Walter (Walt), Arthur, Ernest, Frederick (Fred), Harry, Jesse, Patrick (Pat), Michael (Mike), Peter (Pete). - **Mexican names.** Men: José, Juan, Manuel, Pedro, Francisco, Antonio, Miguel, Luis, Jesús, Domingo, Tomás, Diego, Felipe, Enrique, Alejandro, Eduardo, Salvador, Roberto, Ramón, Ricardo, Vicente, Mario, Mateo, Pablo, Jorge. - **Native names.** A complex matter. Many Native people had multiple names: a birth name, a youth name, an adult name (taken on a deed or vision), sometimes a name taken on conversion or by Anglo imposition. The English names given by Anglos (Sitting Bull, Crazy Horse, Red Cloud, Spotted Tail, Black Kettle, Roman Nose, Geronimo, Cochise, Sequoyah) are translations of, or in Geronimo's case mispronunciations of, the original. Use the Native name where you can, in the language of the people. _Tȟašúŋke Witkó_ for Crazy Horse (Lakota). _Quanah Parker_ (Comanche, mixed Anglo). Quote names accurately; do not invent generic Native names.

Spanish names for older Mexican towns. Native-derived names mediated through Anglo mishearing (Wichita, Cheyenne, Omaha, Pawnee).

## 9. The Diction Test

Before you accept a line of dialogue, run it through these. Could this character have said this in this year? Does the syntax fit his rank, his region, his trade? Is the vocabulary period-correct? Is the profanity calibrated to the company? Does it sound like speech, not a written sentence? Read it aloud — does it ring? If yes to all six, keep it. If no to any, rewrite.

The Portis line at the head of this chapter is the example. Notice: _give it credence_ — Latinate, formal. _did not give it credence that_ — period syntax (we would say _believe that_). _avenge her father's blood_ — biblical / period diction. _I will say_ — period qualifier. _it did not happen every day_ — colloquial. The whole movement, formal then colloquial then qualified, is pure nineteenth-century narrator on the page. Portis got every word right. Aim there. The diction is everything.

## 10. The Newspaper

By the 1870s every western town of any size had a newspaper. A small town might have a weekly with a two-man staff (publisher and printer); a larger town might have a daily with a substantial staff and imported wire-service news.

### The form

A typical 1870s western weekly: four pages, six columns each, broadsheet size (about 22 by 28 inches). Pages 1 and 4 were often _ready-print_ or _patent inside_ — sheets printed in eastern cities with general-interest content (national news, serial fiction, household tips, advertisements for Eastern manufacturers) and shipped to small western papers, which then printed pages 2 and 3 (local news, local advertisements, editorials) themselves.

The local pages contained brief items — arrivals and departures, business openings, crimes, accidents, social events, marriages, deaths; editorials, the publisher's opinions on national and local matters (western editors were often vehement and partisan); letters to the editor from local citizens; advertisements from local merchants, lawyers, doctors, hotels, professionals, and the sheriff's legal notices; court news (district court calendars, jury lists, indictments); stage and railroad schedules; stock and produce prices.

### The voice

The 1870s newspaper voice was distinctive. Florid, formal, often comically ornate by modern standards.

Local item:

> _Mr. Hiram Whittaker, our esteemed merchant on Main Street, has lately returned from a journey to St. Louis, whither he went on business connected with the spring goods trade. He brings with him a handsome new line of dry goods which we are reliably informed will be on display at his establishment by the first of June._

Death notice:

> _It is our painful duty to record the demise of Mrs. Eliza Patten, beloved wife of the late William Patten and a respected resident of this community for the past seven years. The deceased was forty-three years of age and is survived by two children. Funeral services will be held at the Methodist Church on Sunday at two o'clock, the Reverend Heaston officiating. Friends of the family are respectfully invited to attend._

Editorial:

> _The deplorable conditions on the road south of town have been the subject of correspondence in these columns for upwards of three months, and the County Commissioners continue in their inexplicable negligence. We are reliably informed that the recent freshet has rendered the Plum Creek crossing impassable for any vehicle larger than a light buggy. The freight wagons are detouring six miles to the eastward over country which can only be described as execrable._

A scene that includes a newspaper on a kitchen table or a barbershop counter can render any of these voices. A character reading aloud from the paper carries the period's voice without explanation.

### Famous editors

Some were notable: Mark Twain at the Virginia City _Territorial Enterprise_ (1862–66), Bret Harte at various California papers, Joseph Pulitzer at the St. Louis _Westliche Post_ (German-language but shaping his career), Eugene Field at the Denver _Tribune_, William Allen White (slightly later, at the Emporia _Gazette_).

### The country newspaper office

A scene set in the office can render the period's mechanics: the hand-set type cases, the flatbed press, the editor at his desk wearing an ink-stained apron, the printer's devil (apprentice boy) cleaning type or running errands, the local merchant arriving to place an advertisement, the angry citizen arriving to complain about an editorial. The smell of the office: ink, kerosene (for cleaning the press), tobacco smoke, the sweet metal smell of type metal, the paper itself. The sound: the press when it was running (a rhythmic thump), the editor's typewriter (after the late 1870s; before that, all copy was handwritten).

## 11. The Wanted Poster

The wanted poster was the West's principal means of distributing the description of a fugitive. By the 1870s, posters were a routine product of sheriffs' offices and major railroads (the Union Pacific issued many notable posters for train robbers).

### The form

A typical 1870s wanted poster carried a headline (_$5,000 REWARD_ in large block letters or _WANTED_); the crime (_For the murder of_ or _For the robbery of the_); the name (_John Smith, alias "Slim" Smith, alias_ —); the description (height, weight, hair, eyes, complexion, distinguishing marks — scars, tattoos, missing fingers, peculiar gait — and age: _Thirty-two years of age. Five feet ten inches tall. Light complexion. Sandy hair. Blue eyes. A scar above the left eyebrow. Carries himself with a slight stoop. Speaks with a southern accent._); a photograph when available (rare in 1870 for fugitives, common by the 1890s; a line drawing was sometimes used); the reward terms (_$5,000 reward will be paid by the undersigned for the arrest and conviction of the said party,_ or _Dead or alive,_ less common in formal posters than in fiction; usually _arrest and conviction_ with the implicit understanding that dead delivery was acceptable); and the contact (the sheriff's office, the U.S. marshal's office, or the agency offering the reward — Wells Fargo, Pinkerton, Union Pacific).

### The mechanics

The poster was printed in quantity (a few hundred to a few thousand depending on the case) and distributed by mail to sheriffs, marshals, post offices, hotels, saloons, and railroad agents across the affected region. Many posters survived; collections are held by libraries and historical societies.

### Use in scenes

The wanted poster on the wall is a standard western visual. The fugitive sees his own face — the classic scene, the outlaw walks into a saloon and his poster is on the wall, the reaction is the scene. The bounty hunter studies the poster — the scene establishes who he is hunting and what he knows. The bystander recognizes the description — the rancher's wife realizes the man at her supper table matches the description she read in the paper this morning. The crooked sheriff covers the poster — removes it from the office wall to protect his confederates.

Render the poster as the period rendered it: the formal, slightly stilted, descriptive prose of an official document. Quote the relevant passage; do not paraphrase.

## 12. Letters and Correspondence

The letter was the period's primary long-distance communication. The mail was relatively reliable (weekly to most established western towns by the 1870s; daily to railroad-served towns) and relatively cheap (three cents for a domestic letter in the period).

### The form

A standard 1870s letter: dateline (_Cottonwood Springs, Colorado Territory, May 14th, 1875_); salutation (_My dear Mother, Dear Brother William, My dearest husband, Sir_); the body, generally formal in punctuation and paragraph structure, often opening with inquiry after the recipient's health and report on the writer's, the substance following; closing (_Your loving daughter, Eliza. Your affectionate brother, James. Your obedient servant, J. M. Henderson. With kindest regards, I remain very truly yours, William Patten_).

### The voice

Letters of the 1870s were often more formal than modern letters but also could be intensely personal. The educated correspondent followed nineteenth-century rhetorical conventions; the less-educated wrote less correctly but often with power.

A homestead woman's letter to her sister in the East:

> _Dear Sister,_
>
> _Your letter of March the second came two weeks ago and I have been trying to find a quiet hour to answer it. The spring has come at last and we are very busy with the planting. James is in the field from before light to dark and I have the garden in and the chickens setting and the wash behind me, so this is the first letter I have written this month._
>
> _Little Hattie has had the croup these three weeks and I have not slept a full night the whole time. She is better now and the worst is past, I trust._
>
> _The country has not improved. We have had no rain since March and the wheat is yellow already. If we do not have rain by Decoration Day there will be no crop and we will have to think hard about what to do come fall. I do not say this to trouble you. I know you cannot help and I know you have your own troubles._
>
> _I miss you all more than I can write down._
>
> _Your loving sister, Eliza_

The voice is the homestead woman's voice on the page in her own register: formal, practical, restrained about her suffering, intensely concrete about her work. A letter quoted in a scene can render her inner life in her own voice without the narrator intervening.

### Letter conventions worth knowing

Addressing the envelope: the recipient's name on three or four lines — name, town, county or state, country if international (_Mrs. Mary Henderson / Athens, Ohio / U.S.A._). Postage: three cents for a domestic letter (one ounce); two cents after 1883; international postage variable. Stamps: the standard three-cent stamp of the 1870s was green (Washington portrait). Letter paper: folded sheets of writing paper; envelopes were a separate item, increasingly standard. The hand: most adults wrote in a Spencerian or Palmer-method cursive hand; handwriting was a taught skill and varied considerably in legibility. The post office: a community institution; the arrival of the mail was an event; people gathered to wait; the postmaster (a federal appointee, often the local store owner who held the post office in a corner of his store) was a community figure.

A letter in a scene can establish character through voice, deliver information dramatically, mark the passage of time (reading a letter from weeks ago against the present), create dramatic irony (the audience knows something the character does not), or be the chapter's hinge (the letter that changes the protagonist's plan).

## 13. Telegrams

The telegraph reached the major western cities by the 1860s and was extending to smaller towns through the 1870s. By 1875 most established western towns had telegraph service.

A telegram was a short message paid by the word. Brevity was the form.

> _ARRIVED CHEYENNE STOP CATTLE SOLD AT 22 STOP HOME THURSDAY STOP HENDERSON_

Words were minimized. _STOP_ stood for the period (at extra cost the punctuation could be sent). The form encouraged a clipped, telegraphic prose that became its own register.

The telegram in a scene can arrive with urgent news (the sheriff receives the telegram about the train robbery in Cheyenne; the chase begins), arrive too late (the telegram about the wife's illness arrives the day after the funeral), carry coded language (business or military telegrams sometimes used code books), or be intercepted (telegraph lines were not secure; the villain's confederate at the telegraph office might read or alter messages).

The telegraph operator is a specific type — often a young man or woman trained in Morse code, working at a small office in a railroad station or general store. The operator was the town's link to the wider world. He or she — women were a substantial fraction of telegraph operators — heard the world's news first.

## 14. Legal Documents

The 1870s West generated substantial legal paperwork. Every land claim, every cattle sale, every will, every contract, every court case left a paper trail. The county courthouse held the records.

### Bills of sale

A handwritten document recording the transfer of property — typically livestock.

> _Know all men by these presents that I, John Henderson, of Cottonwood County, in the Territory of Colorado, in consideration of the sum of one hundred and sixty dollars to me in hand paid by James Whittaker of the same county, do hereby grant, sell, and convey to the said James Whittaker the following described livestock, to-wit: Forty head of mixed beef cattle, branded with the J-bar-H brand on the left hip, and further described as fourteen steers, sixteen cows with calves at side, and ten heifers, all being the issue and increase of my J-bar-H herd._
>
> _Witness my hand and seal this fourteenth day of May, in the year of our Lord one thousand eight hundred and seventy-five._
>
> _John Henderson._

The bill of sale was the cattle thief's forgery target. An altered bill of sale (the original seller's name forged, the brand description altered) could launder stolen cattle.

### Deeds

A deed transferred real property. Filed at the county courthouse. The deed described the property (_the southwest quarter of Section 14, Township 6 North, Range 8 West of the 6th Principal Meridian_), recorded the consideration paid, and was signed by the grantor before a notary. The reading of a deed is normally not dramatic, but a scene where the deed is in dispute can be central.

### Wills

Wills were drafted by lawyers (or copied from a form book by the testator), signed before witnesses, and filed for probate after the testator's death.

> _To my beloved wife Eliza, all my household furniture, kitchen utensils, and personal effects, and the use during her natural life of the homestead and surrounding one hundred and sixty acres._
>
> _To my eldest son William, my saddle horse named Big John, my saddle, my Winchester rifle, and the sum of two hundred dollars._
>
> _To my second son Henry, my work team, my wagon, my plow, and the sum of two hundred dollars._
>
> _To my daughters Mary and Elizabeth, the sum of one hundred dollars each, to be paid at their majority or marriage._
>
> _All the residue of my estate I bequeath to my beloved wife Eliza, to be hers absolutely._

A will quoted in a scene establishes the family's material world (the saddle horse, the Winchester, the work team) and the testator's relationships (the named bequests, the residual estate).

### Court records

Indictments, court orders, judgments, transcripts. The 1870s western courthouse was a busy paper factory. Court records survive in many county archives and are the period's most reliable source for understanding what cases actually came before the law.

## The Forged Bill of Sale

> Lewes opened the desk drawer and took out the bundle of papers and laid them on the table. He untied the string and went through them.
>
> The first was the bill of sale. It read out fine in Lewes's hand on the cleanest paper in the bundle. _Forty head of mixed beef cattle branded with the J-bar-H brand on the left hip._ The date was the fourteenth of May. Lewes had not been within sixty miles of Henderson's place on the fourteenth of May.
>
> The second was the original. He had pinched it from the desk at Henderson's place the night he took the cattle. It read out in Henderson's hand, _Forty head of mixed beef cattle._ The brand description was the same. The date was the fourteenth of May. The signature was John Henderson.
>
> The forged paper had James Whittaker as the buyer. The original had been left blank. Lewes had filled the buyer line with his confederate's name and signed Henderson's name at the bottom.
>
> He held the two papers up to the lamp. The forgery was passable in good light. In bad light, in a trail-driver's saddlebag after a week of rain, nobody would look at it twice.
>
> He folded the forgery and put it in his vest. He took the original to the cookstove and lifted the lid and watched it burn.

## 15. Diaries and Journals

A substantial fraction of the literate 1870s population kept some form of diary or journal. Pioneer women's diaries, soldiers' journals, ranchmen's account books with personal entries, preachers' records of sermons preached and souls converted — these constitute the period's largest single archive of personal voice.

The 1870s diary was typically a leather-bound or cloth-bound book with dated pages. Some printed diaries had the dates pre-printed at the top of each page, a few lines of space per day. Others were blank books in which the diarist wrote as he or she pleased.

Diary voice varies. The minimal diary recorded only weather, work, and visits (_May 14. Plowed the south field. Wind from the west. Tom Henderson stopped by._). The fuller diary recorded conversations, reflections, complaints, prayers, hopes. The homestead woman's diary often recorded her inner life in ways her letters did not.

Reading: the published diaries of pioneer women (Lillian Schlissel's _Women's Diaries of the Westward Journey_ is a standard collection); the diaries of soldiers and officers' wives; the journals of preachers (Peter Cartwright's autobiography is a memoir built from his journal); James Henry Cook's _Fifty Years on the Old Frontier_ (memoir but heavily journal-based).

## 16. Photographs

By the 1870s photography was widespread in the West. The cabinet card and the stereograph were standard formats. Itinerant photographers traveled the country making portraits, and established studios operated in every town of size. Famous landscape photographers — William Henry Jackson, Timothy O'Sullivan, A. J. Russell — documented the West's geography.

- **Carte de visite.** A small (about 2.5 by 4 inch) photograph mounted on a card. Used as a calling card; collected by family and friends.
- **Cabinet card.** A larger (about 4.5 by 6.5 inch) photograph mounted on a stiff card. Standard portrait format from the late 1860s through the 1890s.
- **Tintype.** A photograph on a thin iron plate. Cheap, durable, common at fairs and street photographers.
- **Stereograph.** A pair of nearly identical photographs mounted side by side on a card. Viewed through a stereoscope (a household viewer) for a 3-D effect. Massively popular for landscapes.

A photograph in a scene can carry memory (the dead son's photograph on the mantel), identify (the sheriff has a photograph of the fugitive; the deputy compares the photograph to the man at the bar), establish past (the faded photograph from before the war), or be made (the scene of the family sitting for the photographer is itself a period event — the long exposure, the held still poses, the formality).

## 17. Voice — Period Prose Register

Beyond the basic 1870s formality already discussed, specific document types had specific registers.

- **Legal register.** Latinate, repetitive, formulaic. _Know all men by these presents. To-wit. The party of the first part. Hereby grant, sell, and convey. Witness my hand and seal._
- **Newspaper register.** Florid, formal, periphrastic. _It is our painful duty to record. We are reliably informed. Repaired to. Wended his way. The aforesaid. The lately deceased._
- **Telegraph register.** Telegraphic. Stripped to nouns and verbs. _Arrived. Goods sold. Returning Thursday._
- **Personal letter register.** Formal salutations, then variable. The writer's education and situation determine register beyond the openings and closings.
- **Diary register.** Often minimal — the labor and weather. The fuller diaries swing into reflection in their own characteristic voice.
- **Sermon register.** Scripturally saturated, rhythmic, building.
- **Government / military register.** Formal, bureaucratic, hierarchical. _I have the honor to report. In compliance with instructions. Most respectfully submitted._
- **Cattle-trade register.** Brand-and-tally. _Forty head, three-up, mixed yearlings and twos, branded J-bar-H on left hip, swallow-fork in left ear._ The vocabulary is specific to the trade.
