# Chapter 10 — Religion and Faith

> _“THE LORD IS MY SHEPHERD; I SHALL NOT WANT. HE MAKETH ME TO LIE DOWN IN GREEN PASTURES; HE LEADETH ME BESIDE THE STILL WATERS. YEA, THOUGH I WALK THROUGH THE VALLEY OF THE SHADOW OF DEATH, I WILL FEAR NO EVIL: FOR THOU ART WITH ME.”_
>
> — PSALM 23, KING JAMES BIBLE

The 1870s West was a strictly religious landscape. The circuit-riding preacher rode the same dirt as the deputy sheriff. The revival meeting physically constructed the early towns. The Catholic mission held absolute dirt-level power in the Southwest. The Mormon settlements operated as a sovereign religious polity locked in the Great Basin. The Black church moved west entirely integrated with the Black migration. Men and women filled the wooden benches of the western church on Sunday morning.

---

## 1. The Denominations

### Methodism

The dominant Protestant denomination of the frontier. The Methodist Episcopal Church pushed more circuit riders, built more wooden frame churches, and organized more western towns than any other body.

Methodism functioned entirely on the circuit. A preacher—frequently unordained, titled a _local preacher_ or _exhorter_—rode a massive loop of designated preaching points on a strict schedule. He held services in sod soddies, clapboard schoolhouses, and open clearings until the congregation dragged enough lumber together to build a church.

Methodist worship operated on extemporaneous prayer, heavy hymn singing, expository preaching, and the summer camp meeting held in a cut-brush arbor.

### Baptist

The Baptist polity operated congregationally. Each church stood completely autonomous. The Baptist preacher frequently drew no pay and worked a heavy manual trade six days a week.

A visiting preacher held a week-long meeting. He drove the sinners forward to the wooden mourner's bench. The conversions were public and highly physical. The congregation walked down to the nearest creek and baptized the convert by full immersion in the cold water.

The Southern Baptist Convention dominated the western South. Black Baptist churches stood as the central structures in the freedmen's communities.

### Presbyterian

A smaller body numerically, but socially entrenched. Presbyterian congregations built the town churches. They paid an educated minister to conduct a strict, formal service. The Presbyterian preacher operated in a literate, reserved register.

### Episcopal

The denomination of the Eastern money moving west. Concentrated heavily in the larger rail towns. The Episcopal church frequently held the only piece of stained glass in the county.

### Catholic

The dominant religious iron of the Southwest. The Catholic mission system had completely structured the southwestern dirt long before the American annexation.

In the 1870s, the Catholic church held the Hispanic populations of the Southwest and California; the Irish labor in the army, the heavy rail camps, and the deep rock mines; the German and Italian immigrants; and the Native populations originally converted through the Spanish missions.

The Catholic priest was ordained, educated in Europe or Mexico, and operated under strict episcopal authority. The mission priest of the Southwest frequently held his adobe parish for decades. He spoke the local Spanish and the ecclesiastical Latin.

### Mormon (Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints)

The Mormons locked down the Great Basin in 1847. By the 1870s, they had constructed a hard network of irrigated settlements running from southern Idaho down to northern Arizona. Salt Lake City stood as the anchor. Brigham Young died in 1877.

The 1870s Mormon community practiced plural marriage openly. The federal government fought the practice with heavy statutory iron.

The Mormon community operated as an entirely distinct world: separate theology, separate cooperative mercantile economy, and separate theocratic political organization. A Mormon character operates in the West under completely different structural rules.

### Jewish

Jewish merchants anchored the larger towns and the heavy mining camps. Jewish peddlers ran freight wagons completely across the territories. The Bavarian-immigrant generation built massive mercantile operations like the Goldwaters in Arizona and the Spiegelbergs in New Mexico.

Synagogue life barely existed in the raw towns. The men traveled to the larger cities for the high holidays.

### The Black Church

The Black migrants—the Exodusters in Kansas, the freedmen in the Indian Territory, the Buffalo Soldiers, the trail hands—dragged their churches west with them. The African Methodist Episcopal (AME) Church, the AME Zion Church, and the Black Baptist congregations stood as the physical and political centers of the western Black settlements like Nicodemus, Kansas.

## 2. The Circuit Rider

The circuit rider rode a fixed loop of preaching points across hundreds of square miles of hard country.

### The Work

A man rode a circuit of twenty to forty preaching points on a six-week rotation. He preached, married the living, buried the dead, and baptized the children.

He rode the horse through the blizzards and the flash floods. He slept on the dirt floors of the parishioners' cabins. He ate the starvation rations. He drew roughly one hundred dollars a year in hard cash. He remained celibate because the circuit could freeze a family to death.

### The Voice

The circuit rider preached extemporaneously. He never read a written sermon. He drove the voice directly at the congregation, saturating the air with heavy scripture, and frequently dropping into a rhythmic, chanted delivery at the hard climax.

He opened with the Bible text, exposed the mechanics of the verse, drove the application directly into the congregation's physical reality, and built a massive emotional weight that broke at the mourner's bench.

---

## 3. The Revival

A multi-day physical operation held in late summer in a brush arbor, a heavy canvas tent, or a raw plank hall.

### The Structure

The day opened with the morning prayer service for the hard faithful. The afternoon preaching stood open to the county. The evening preaching carried the massive weight: maximum attendance, heavy emotion, and the physical drag to the altar.

### The Mourner's Bench

A raw wooden bench at the front of the dirt aisle. The seekers sat on the wood while the preacher and the believers laid hands on them and prayed. The conversion happened physically at the bench. The sinner routinely wept, shouted, or collapsed directly onto the dirt floor.

### The Camp Meeting

The heaviest form of the revival. Families drove the heavy wagons out to a standing campsite, dropped the canvas, and stayed for the week. Multiple preachers drove the word from multiple wooden stands simultaneously. Hundreds of men and women gathered. The singing carried out into the dark timber until midnight.

The camp meeting also organized the scattered farm families socially. Men brokered heavy horse trades. Women arranged the marriages.

---

## 4. The Catholic Mission

The adobe parish stood as the absolute center of the Southwestern community.

### The Church

The New Mexican village church rose from the dirt in thick adobe block walls, small high windows, and a flat mud roof supporting a heavy iron bell. The nave held a beaten earth floor. The parishioners knelt directly in the dust. The eastern altar held the _reredos_—a wooden screen painted with the bloody mechanics of the Passion. The _santos_ (carved wooden saints) stood in the corners. The stone-walled _campo santo_ (graveyard) flanked the building.

### The Priest

The Archbishop of Santa Fe controlled the territory. The local priest executed the Latin mass, heard the confessions, and administered the heavy physical sacraments.

### The Penitentes

A lay confraternity of Hispanic Catholic men operating in northern New Mexico and southern Colorado. The _Hermanos_ maintained the religious structure in the remote, priestless villages. They executed the heavy Holy Week observances with physical processions, public prayer, and actual bodily mortification.

The incoming Anglo clergy violently suppressed the _Penitentes_, driving the structure entirely underground. The _morada_ (meeting house) and the _alabados_ (ancient hymns) held the villages together.

### The Mission Indian

The colonial mission system had physically locked the Native populations into the Catholic structure. By the 1870s, the Pueblo and California Mission populations executed the Catholic sacraments in direct, syncretic parallel with their own ancient ceremonies.

## 5. The Mormon Settlement

A Mormon village in the 1870s was a distinct religious-economic community.

### Layout

A Mormon village was laid out on Joseph Smith's _Plat of Zion_: a grid of large blocks, each block divided into four to ten lots, each lot supporting a house, garden, and outbuildings. The streets were wide enough to turn a wagon. The center of the village had the meetinghouse and (in the larger settlements) the tithing office and the cooperative store.

### Ecclesiastical organization

A village was a _ward_, presided over by a bishop (an unpaid lay leader, not the same as a Catholic bishop). The ward conducted Sunday meetings (a sacrament service in the morning, a Sunday school for children, an evening meeting). The ward also organized welfare, mutual aid, and cooperative labor. A region of wards was a _stake_, presided over by a stake president and twelve high councilors.

### Polygamy

Plural marriage was practiced by perhaps twenty to thirty percent of Mormon families through the 1870s and 1880s. The men who took plural wives were often ecclesiastical leaders or men of substance. The practice produced its own household forms: a man might maintain separate households for separate wives, or wives might share a household, depending on circumstances.

The federal anti-polygamy laws produced _the underground_ — Mormon men evading federal marshals, some imprisoned, some hiding in remote settlements, some serving prison time. By the late 1880s the pressure forced the church's accommodation.

### The Mormon character at the table

The Mormon character has been written badly more often than any other religious character in western fiction. The dime-novel sinister Mormon (the polygamous patriarch with his harem and his Danites) is a Victorian fantasy. The honest Mormon character is a believer in a community with distinct theology, a specific economic organization, and complex relationships with surrounding non-Mormon populations.

A few sources: Wallace Stegner, _Mormon Country_ and _The Gathering of Zion_ (sympathetic but Gentile — i.e., non-Mormon — observation); Levi Peterson, _The Backslider_ (Mormon-written novel); Vardis Fisher's _Children of God_ (Mormon-raised author). The journals and personal letters of Mormon settlers are extensive and available.

## The Funeral

> The grave was a four-foot trench in the dry ground at the edge of the cemetery. Two men had dug it through the morning and the dirt stood in two piles beside it. The wind moved the piles slightly.
>
> Reverend Heaston stood at the head of the grave with his Bible open. The mourners stood around the grave: the widow Eliza Patten in her mother-in-law's black bonnet, her two children to either side, and a dozen people from the town behind them. Patten's partner Isaac Coberly stood with his hat in his hands.
>
> “The Lord is my shepherd,” Heaston said. “I shall not want.”
>
> The wind took some of the words. The widow's bonnet moved. The older child looked at the pine box and then looked at her mother.
>
> “He maketh me to lie down in green pastures,” Heaston said. “He leadeth me beside the still waters.”
>
> The town held no green pastures and no still waters. Coberly had built the pine coffin that morning. Patten was forty-one years old. He had died entirely of a kick from a heavy freight mule.
>
> Heaston closed the Bible.
>
> “We commit our brother to the ground,” he said. “Earth to earth. Ashes to ashes. Dust to dust. In the sure and certain hope of the resurrection unto eternal life.”
>
> The two men lowered the box into the trench on hemp ropes. Coberly stepped forward and threw the first handful of dirt onto the lid. The widow stepped forward and threw the second.
>
> The two men filled the grave with long-handled iron shovels while the others watched. The sound of the dirt hitting the wood lasted exactly as long as it lasted. When the trench was full, one of the men packed the dirt down hard with the back of the iron.
>
> Heaston put on his hat and walked over to the widow. “The supper at the church is ready when you are.”

## 5. The Black Church

A Black congregation in an 1870s western settlement organized the community's physical survival. The church executed the funerals, the weddings, the cash mutual aid, and the rigid political defense.

The building frequently stood as a single-room raw lumber frame. The preacher operated locally or rode a circuit. He drove the service long. The congregation physically participated, utilizing the massive, rhythmic call-and-response structure.

### The Music

The spiritual dominated the western Black church. The Fisk Jubilee Singers had pushed the music into the white consciousness by 1871. The western congregations sang the heavy iron history directly into the room. _Swing Low, Sweet Chariot. Steal Away. Go Down Moses. Wade in the Water._ The lyrics carried the literal mechanics of survival, physical exile, and the crossing of the river.

---

## 6. The Cemetery

The cemetery held the absolute physical record of the town.

### The Marker

The poor and the early dead took a raw wooden board or a cut timber cross. A man with cash bought a heavy sandstone or granite slab. The cutter struck the name, the dates, and occasionally a short biblical cut directly into the rock.

### The Ground

The surveyors platted the cemeteries tightly by denomination. The Catholic ground stood physically fenced from the Protestant ground.

The _boot hill_—the unconsecrated dirt for men blown apart by buckshot, the executed, and the suicides—operated as a literal geographic reality. The violent dead went into the raw dirt with no wood and no stone.

### The Mechanics of Burial

A Protestant burial required the preacher's service, the physical wagon procession, the graveside reading, the lowering of the pine box, and the shoveling of the dirt by the living.

A Catholic burial required the Latin Mass inside the adobe, the foot procession to the _campo santo_ behind the wooden cross, the physical burial, and the forty days of strict mourning.

A Mormon burial required the meetinghouse service, the strict physical testimonies, and the formal priesthood dedication of the dirt itself.

## 7. The Voice

### The Preaching

The Protestant preaching voice ran saturated with King James iron. The preacher built sentences from the biblical vocabulary. _Brethren. Sojourner. Sanctification. The Day of the Lord._

> _Brethren, the Word of God is sharper than any two-edged sword. It cuts strictly to the bone. There is no man here this evening who has not felt the blade. The Lord is calling you out of the dirt. He is calling you tonight._

### The Prayer

The extemporaneous Protestant prayer addressed God directly in the second person, demanding specific physical outcomes. _Heavenly Father, we thank thee for this food and ask thy blessing upon it. Amen._

The Catholic prayer operated purely on the fixed Latin liturgy. The priest and the faithful ground through the _Pater Noster_ and the _Ave Maria_ utilizing the heavy beads of the rosary.

### The Hymn

The western church sang continuously. A Protestant congregation dragged the heavy lyrics out of the hymnals. _Amazing Grace. Rock of Ages. Nearer My God to Thee._

The Catholic congregations sang the heavy Latin hymns of the liturgical year and the Spanish _alabados_ directly into the adobe walls.
