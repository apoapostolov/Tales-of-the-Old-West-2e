# Chapter 10 — Religion and Faith

> _“THE LORD IS MY SHEPHERD; I SHALL NOT WANT. HE MAKETH ME TO LIE DOWN IN GREEN PASTURES; HE LEADETH ME BESIDE THE STILL WATERS. YEA, THOUGH I WALK THROUGH THE VALLEY OF THE SHADOW OF DEATH, I WILL FEAR NO EVIL: FOR THOU ART WITH ME.”_
>
> — PSALM 23, KING JAMES BIBLE

The popular West has been written largely as a secular space: the cowboy and the gunman act in a world without God. The historical West was nothing of the kind. The 1870s West was a deeply religious country. The circuit-riding preacher was as common a figure as the deputy sheriff. The revival meeting shaped towns. The Catholic mission was a power in the Southwest. The Mormon settlements were a sovereign religious polity in the Great Basin. The Black church traveled west with Black migrants. The Sunday morning of any western town was full.

A campaign or table that ignores religion misrepresents the period. This chapter is the reference for getting it right.

---

## 1. The Major Denominations and Traditions

### Methodism

The dominant Protestant denomination of the western expansion. The Methodist Episcopal Church (and after 1844 its split with the Methodist Episcopal Church, South) sent more circuit riders, established more churches, and shaped more western towns than any other body.

Methodism's organizational genius was the circuit. A preacher — not necessarily ordained, a _local preacher_ or _exhorter_ might serve — was assigned a circuit of preaching points, riding the circuit on a regular schedule, holding services in homes, schools, and meeting houses until a congregation could build a church. The circuit rider was a specific western character.

Methodist worship featured extemporaneous prayer, hymn singing (Charles Wesley's hymns dominant), expository preaching, and the camp meeting (a multi-day revival held in summer in a brush arbor or clearing).

### Baptist

Strong throughout the South and migrating west with Southern settlers. Baptist polity was congregational — each church autonomous — which suited the frontier. The Baptist preacher was often unpaid and worked at another trade.

The Baptist revival was the central institution. A visiting preacher held a week-long meeting; sinners were brought forward to the mourner's bench; conversions were public and emotional; baptism by full immersion followed in the nearest creek or river.

By the 1870s the Southern Baptist Convention (founded 1845) was the dominant Baptist body in the western South. Black Baptist churches were emerging in the freedmen's communities.

### Presbyterian

Smaller numerically but socially established. Presbyterian congregations were typically town churches, with an educated minister and a more formal service. The Presbyterian preacher's voice was literate and reserved.

### Episcopal

The denomination of the Eastern establishment moving west. Smaller numerically; concentrated in the larger towns. The Episcopal church might be the only church with a stained-glass window in town.

### Catholic

The dominant religious body of the Southwest, present in California, New Mexico, Arizona, and Texas before American annexation. The Catholic mission system shaped the southwestern landscape: the chain of California missions (largely secularized by the 1830s but the church bodies remained), the Pueblo mission churches of New Mexico, the Texas missions.

In the 1870s the Catholic church was the church of the Hispanic populations throughout the Southwest and California; the Irish immigrants in the army, the railroad camps, the mining camps; the German and Italian immigrants in cities and some farming areas; and the Native populations that had been converted through the missions, overlapping with traditional practice in complex ways.

The Catholic priest was a specific figure: ordained, educated (often in seminaries in Spain, Mexico, France, or Ireland), holding sacramental authority, operating under a bishop. The mission priest of the Southwest had often spent decades in his parish and spoke the local language, Spanish, and ecclesiastical Latin.

### Mormon (Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints)

The Mormons settled the Great Basin in 1847 and by the 1870s had built a network of communities from southern Idaho to southern Nevada to northern Arizona. Salt Lake City was the capital. Brigham Young died in 1877, ending the founding generation's leadership.

The 1870s Mormon community was practicing polygamy — plural marriage was officially endorsed until 1890. The federal government's hostility to Mormon practice produced repeated conflicts: the Edmunds Act (1882), the Edmunds-Tucker Act (1887), and eventually the Manifesto of 1890 ending official polygamy.

The Mormon community was its own world: distinct theology (additional scripture, restorationist claims, temple ceremonies), distinct economic organization (cooperative mercantile, tithing-funded infrastructure), distinct political organization (theocratic in practice through much of the period). A Mormon character moves through the West differently from any other character.

### Jewish

A small but real presence in the frontier West. Jewish merchants in the larger towns (notably the mining camps), Jewish ranchers in some areas, Jewish peddlers traveling the country with goods. The Bavarian-immigrant generation of Jewish merchants included the Goldwaters of Arizona, the Spiegelbergs of New Mexico, the Levi Strauss enterprise of San Francisco.

Synagogue life was thin in the smaller towns. A Jewish family might travel to a larger city for holidays. Mixed marriage was common and led to assimilation in many cases.

### Black church traditions

Black migrants west — Exodusters to Kansas in 1879, freedmen to the Indian Territory, Black soldiers in the Buffalo Soldier regiments, Black cowhands on the trail — brought their churches with them. The African Methodist Episcopal (AME) Church, the AME Zion Church, and Black Baptist congregations were established in the western Black communities.

Nicodemus, Kansas (founded 1877 by Exodusters), is the canonical western Black settlement. Its churches, schools, and community life are documentable.

### Native traditions

Treated in Chapter 2. The 1870s saw aggressive missionary efforts among Native peoples, the suppression of traditional ceremonies (the Sun Dance was banned in 1881), the rise of syncretic movements (the Ghost Dance came in 1890, just past our period), and the persistence of traditional practice underground.

## 2. The Circuit Rider

The defining western Protestant figure. A horseback preacher serving a circuit of preaching points across hundreds of square miles.

### The work

The circuit rider's circuit might include twenty to forty preaching points visited on a six-week or quarterly rotation. He preached at each, performed weddings and funerals, baptized children and converts, organized class meetings (small groups for mutual support and discipline), and moved on.

He rode in all weather. He slept in the homes of parishioners. He ate what was offered. He earned almost nothing — Methodist circuit riders in the period earned about a hundred dollars a year. He was celibate by economic necessity if not by choice; the circuit could not support a family.

### The voice

The circuit rider's preaching voice was practiced and distinctive: extemporaneous (he did not read a written sermon), emotional, direct, scripturally saturated, often with a chanted or rhythmic delivery in the climactic passages.

A characteristic structure: opening text (a Bible verse), exposition of the verse's meaning, application to the congregation's daily life, narrative illustrations, building to an emotional appeal for conversion or commitment, closing with a hymn and the call to the mourner's bench.

The voice on the page can be rendered in fragments — a few minutes of preaching at the climax of a service. The full sermon is too long for a scene; the selected passage carries the weight.

### The figure

Some historical models: Peter Cartwright (the Methodist itinerant of an earlier generation, but his autobiography is the source); the Methodist itinerants of Texas (William Stevenson, Henry Stephenson); the Quaker preachers of the southern plains; the Baptist farmer-preachers of Missouri and Arkansas.

A circuit rider in a campaign is a way to bring religion onto the page without making the whole story a religious document. He arrives, he preaches, he moves on. The town is changed for a week or for a year.

## 3. The Revival Meeting

The principal Protestant religious event of the rural West. A multi-day meeting (typically four to seven days) held in late summer or early fall, in a brush arbor, a tent, or a meeting hall.

### The structure

A typical day at a revival began with morning prayer service, smaller, for the faithful. Afternoon preaching was open to all — a guest preacher (a famous evangelist if the meeting was large) preached a substantial sermon. Evening preaching was the main service: larger attendance, more emotional, the altar call and the mourner's bench.

### The mourner's bench (anxious bench)

A bench at the front of the meeting where seekers sat during the closing of the service. The preacher and other believers prayed over them, urged them, exhorted them. Conversions happened at the mourner's bench — the sinner experienced the moment of forgiveness, often emotionally (weeping, shouting, falling).

### The conversion experience

The Protestant revival theology centered on a specific moment of conversion: the sinner acknowledged sin, accepted Christ, was forgiven, and was changed. The experience was expected to be _felt_ — peace, joy, a weight lifted. The convert testified publicly to the experience.

A character's conversion is a powerful scene to play if it is rendered honestly. The temptation is to make it transformative or ironic; both are easier than the harder rendering, which is that the convert's life is now both changed and not changed. The same problems remain after conversion. The new relationship to those problems is what has shifted.

### The camp meeting

The largest form of revival. A campsite (often returned to year after year) where families brought wagons, set up camp, and stayed for the duration. Multiple preachers preached at multiple stands. Hundreds or thousands gathered. Singing went on into the night. Conversions and rededications were counted in the hundreds.

The camp meeting was also a social event — the year's gathering for scattered farm families. Young people met. Marriages were arranged. Business was done. Visiting was visited. The religious purpose held the structure but was not its only function.

## 4. The Catholic Mission and Parish

In the Southwest, the Catholic parish or mission was the religious center for most communities.

### The church

A New Mexican village church was typically of adobe construction — thick walls, small windows, a flat roof with the bell in a small bell-cote above the entrance. A single nave, floor of beaten earth or flagstone (pews introduced in the historic period; earlier, congregants stood or knelt). The altar at the east end, a reredos (carved wooden screen) behind it, often painted in vivid colors with scenes of saints and the Passion. The _santos_ — wooden carved figures of the saints, often by local _santeros_ (saint-makers) — are one of the great folk arts of the Southwest. The _bulto_ (free-standing carved figure) and the _retablo_ (painted wooden panel) are the major forms. Adjacent to the church, walled, the _campo santo_, the burial ground.

### The priest

The 1870s southwestern parish priest was often a French or Spanish missionary, sometimes a local-born priest. The Archbishop of Santa Fe in the period was Jean-Baptiste Lamy (the model for the bishop in Willa Cather's _Death Comes for the Archbishop_), who served until 1885.

The priest performed mass (in Latin until Vatican II, 1965), heard confessions, performed the sacraments of baptism, marriage, and last rites. He was addressed as _Father_ in English or _Padre_ in Spanish.

### The Penitentes

A lay confraternity of Hispanic Catholic men in northern New Mexico and southern Colorado. The brotherhood (_Los Hermanos Penitentes_, the Penitent Brothers) maintained religious life in priest-scarce villages, conducted Holy Week observances (including processions, prayer, and self-mortification), and provided mutual aid.

The Penitente practice — the _morada_ (the meeting house), the _alabados_ (the ancient Spanish religious hymns), the Holy Week rituals — was treated as heretical by some incoming Anglo Catholic clergy and went underground in some areas in the late nineteenth century. By the 1870s the Penitentes were a vital force in their villages.

A Penitente scene set in northern New Mexico is grounded in real practice. Treat the practice with respect; it is not horror-movie material.

### The mission Indian

The mission system through the colonial period had brought Native populations into the Catholic church. By the 1870s, populations in the Pueblo, the California Mission, and other groups maintained both Catholic practice and traditional ceremony, often in parallel and in syncretic forms. The _matachines_ dance — a Mexican-Spanish folk drama performed in Pueblo and Hispanic villages on saint's days — is one example of syncretic practice.

## 5. The Mormon Settlement

A Mormon village in the 1870s was a distinct religious-economic community.

### Layout

A Mormon village was laid out on Joseph Smith's _Plat of Zion_: a grid of large blocks, each block divided into four to ten lots, each lot supporting a house, garden, and outbuildings. The streets were wide enough to turn a wagon. The center of the village had the meetinghouse and (in the larger settlements) the tithing office and the cooperative store.

### Ecclesiastical organization

A village was a _ward_, presided over by a bishop (an unpaid lay leader, not the same as a Catholic bishop). The ward conducted Sunday meetings (a sacrament service in the morning, a Sunday school for children, an evening meeting). The ward also organized welfare, mutual aid, and cooperative labor. A region of wards was a _stake_, presided over by a stake president and twelve high councilors.

### Polygamy

Plural marriage was practiced by perhaps twenty to thirty percent of Mormon families through the 1870s and 1880s. The men who took plural wives were often ecclesiastical leaders or men of substance. The practice produced its own household forms: a man might maintain separate households for separate wives, or wives might share a household, depending on circumstances.

The federal anti-polygamy laws produced _the underground_ — Mormon men evading federal marshals, some imprisoned, some hiding in remote settlements, some serving prison time. By the late 1880s the pressure forced the church's accommodation.

### The Mormon character at the table

The Mormon character has been written badly more often than any other religious character in western fiction. The dime-novel sinister Mormon (the polygamous patriarch with his harem and his Danites) is a Victorian fantasy. The honest Mormon character is a believer in a community with distinct theology, a specific economic organization, and complex relationships with surrounding non-Mormon populations.

A few sources: Wallace Stegner, _Mormon Country_ and _The Gathering of Zion_ (sympathetic but Gentile — i.e., non-Mormon — observation); Levi Peterson, _The Backslider_ (Mormon-written novel); Vardis Fisher's _Children of God_ (Mormon-raised author). The journals and personal letters of Mormon settlers are extensive and available.

## The Funeral

> The grave was a four-foot trench in the dry ground at the edge of the cemetery. Two men had dug it through the morning and the dirt stood in two piles beside it. The wind moved the piles slightly.
>
> Reverend Heaston stood at the head of the grave with his Bible open. The mourners stood around the grave: the widow Eliza Patten in her mother-in-law's black bonnet, her two children to either side, and a dozen people from the town behind them. Patten's partner Isaac Coberly stood with his hat in his hands.
>
> “The Lord is my shepherd,” Heaston said. “I shall not want.”
>
> The wind took some of the words. The widow's bonnet moved. The older child looked at the box and then looked at her mother and then looked at the box.
>
> “He maketh me to lie down in green pastures,” Heaston said. “He leadeth me beside the still waters.”
>
> The town had no green pastures and no still waters. The coffin was pine and had been built by Coberly in the morning. Patten was forty-one years old. He had died of a kick from a mule.
>
> Heaston closed the Bible.
>
> “We commit our brother to the ground,” he said. “Earth to earth. Ashes to ashes. Dust to dust. In the sure and certain hope of the resurrection unto eternal life.”
>
> The two men who had dug the grave lowered the box into it on ropes. Coberly stepped forward and threw the first handful of dirt onto the lid. The widow stepped forward and threw the second.
>
> Then the two men with the shovels filled the grave while the others stood and watched. The sound of the dirt on the lid lasted as long as it lasted. When the trench was full one of the men packed it down with the back of the shovel.
>
> Heaston put on his hat and walked over to the widow and said, “The supper at the church is ready when you are.”

## 6. The Black Church on the Frontier

A Black congregation in a western town in the 1870s was the community's most important institution. The church organized funerals, weddings, schooling (especially before public education was available to Black children), mutual aid, and political life.

A small town's Black church might be a single-room frame building, with a small congregation. The preacher might be local or visiting. Services were extended (longer than white services), with substantial congregational participation in singing and shouting, and with the call-and-response preaching tradition.

### The music

The spiritual was the dominant Black religious music of the period. The 1871 tour of the Fisk Jubilee Singers brought the spirituals to white American attention. By the 1870s the spiritual was sung in Black churches everywhere, including the western ones. _Swing Low, Sweet Chariot. Steal Away. Go Down Moses. Wade in the Water._ The music carried specific theological and historical content — deliverance, exile, the river, the journey.

### The preacher

The Black preacher of the period might be ordained in the AME or AME Zion church, ordained Baptist, or serving without formal ordination. The preaching voice — the rhythmic, building, call-and-response voice — has been documented in many sources. James Weldon Johnson's _God's Trombones_ (1927) preserves the form.

A scene in a western Black church on a Sunday morning is a scene the canon has rarely included. Worth playing.

## 7. The Cemetery

Every western town had its cemetery. The cemetery tells the town's history.

### The marker

A wooden marker (a board, a cross) for the poor and the early dead. A stone marker (limestone, sandstone, later granite) for those who could afford it. The marker recorded name, dates, and often a brief inscription — a Bible verse, a sentiment.

### The arrangement

Cemeteries were typically arranged by church and by family. The Catholic cemetery was separate from the Protestant. Within a church's ground, family plots were maintained.

The _boot hill_ of legend — the unconsecrated burial ground for those who died with their boots on, by violence, by suicide, in disrepute — was a real informal practice. The unmarked or briefly marked grave was the common end for the man who died alone on the trail or in the saloon.

### The funeral

A Protestant funeral involved the preacher's service at the home or church, the procession to the cemetery, the service at the graveside, the lowering of the coffin (often a plain wood box made locally), the filling of the grave by the mourners (or by the gravedigger if hired). Hymns sung. The preacher's words. The wake or the meal at the home afterward.

A Catholic funeral involved the Mass for the dead in the church, the procession to the _campo santo_ behind the cross and the priest, the graveside prayers, the burial. The forty days of mourning.

A Mormon funeral involved the meetinghouse service, the testimonies of the bishop and family, the graveside prayer, the dedication of the grave.

The funeral scene is the western's recurring close. The body in the box. The words said. The dirt filling. The walk away from the grave.

## 8. The Voice — Sermon and Prayer

### The preacher's voice

Scripturally saturated. Quotations from the King James Version are interwoven with the preacher's own words. The preacher's vocabulary is biblical (_brethren, sojourner, the Day of the Lord, sanctification, the lost sheep, the prodigal son_).

A sermon passage on the page should be a fragment — a few sentences from the climactic moment, not the whole sermon. The audience's imagination supplies the context.

> _Brethren, the Word of God is sharper than any two-edged sword. It cuts to the quick. There is no man here this evening who has not felt the cut of it. There is no woman here who has not heard the still small voice in the wilderness of her heart. The Lord is calling you. He is calling you tonight._

### The prayer voice

Extemporaneous prayer in the Protestant tradition is addressed directly to God, in second person, with specific requests, confessions, and thanksgivings. A prayer at the table before a meal might be brief: _Heavenly Father, we thank thee for this food and ask thy blessing upon it. Amen._ A prayer at a deathbed might be extended.

The Catholic prayer is liturgical: the Our Father, the Hail Mary, the rosary's mysteries, the prayers of the Mass — often in Latin in the period (the ordinary of the Mass, the _Pater Noster_, the _Ave Maria_).

### The hymn

The western Protestant church sang. Common 1870s hymns: _Amazing Grace, Rock of Ages, Just As I Am, What a Friend We Have in Jesus, Nearer My God to Thee, How Firm a Foundation, Shall We Gather at the River._ A scene with hymn singing should name the hymn (or quote a recognizable line) so that anyone who knows it hears it.

Black congregations sang the spirituals listed earlier and many others. Catholic congregations sang the Latin hymns of the church year (_Tantum Ergo, Adoro Te Devote_, the Marian antiphons by season) and the Spanish folk hymns (_alabados_) in Hispanic communities.
