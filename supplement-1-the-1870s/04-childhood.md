# Chapter 4 — Childhood on the Frontier

> _"THE BABY WENT IN MARCH AND THE TWO OLDEST IN AUGUST AND THE LITTLE ONE THE NEXT MARCH AGAIN. THERE WERE STILL FIVE OF THEM AT THE TABLE. SHE COUNTED EVERY NIGHT."_

The frontier child worked from the time he could carry a wooden bucket without spilling it. He was at constant risk of death from twenty different diseases the East had already tamed by the 1880s. He was educated by his mother, his older sister, and a five-month schoolmarm if his county had the tax base to hire one. He buried his siblings and was buried by his parents at rates the modern reader finds difficult to credit. He played with what was at hand—a corn-husk doll, a willow whistle, the camp dog, the dirt in the yard. He was a small working person operating a small body in a massive, indifferent country, and the country shaped him.

---

## 1. The Day, by Age and Place

The frontier child's day was simply the family's day, with the child's fractional piece of the labor carved out by physical capacity. Age boundaries are approximate. A capable seven-year-old might handle an axe; an idle eleven-year-old might still be kept out of the barn.

### The Homestead Child, Three to Five

In the cabin or the soddy, directly underfoot. The mother set immediate, small tasks: carrying dry buffalo chips for the fire, fetching dropped tools, sitting on the porch with a slate while she worked the washboard. The child was rarely out of sight of an adult. The lethal dangers were immediate and domestic: the open hearth fire, the boiling laundry kettle, the deep well, the draft horses standing in the yard.

The vocabulary the child first learned was the brutal working vocabulary of the household. A frontier three-year-old knew _sorghum_ and _harness_ and _norther_ and _croup_ long before he knew _train_ or _street_.

### The Homestead Child, Six to Nine

Real physical labor begins. The boy carries water from the creek, hauls in split stovewood, gathers eggs, drives the milk cow to pasture and back, and learns to strip a teat. The girl shadows the mother—kneading heavy dough, scrubbing iron skillets, hauling the toddler on her hip, learning to spin wool and push a needle through heavy canvas. Both sexes straddle the garden rows to pull weeds, hoe the corn, gather kindling, and strip wild plums in season.

The "school year," if the district had built a sod schoolhouse, was a three-to-five-month winter block when field work was frozen shut. The child walked to the school, often two miles each way in the snow, carrying a cold biscuit and a slice of salt pork in a tin pail.

### The Homestead Child, Ten to Fourteen

Adult work, issued in smaller portions. The boy wrestles a plow behind a mule team, drives the heavy freight wagon into town alone, holds the hogs down during butchering, hunts rabbits with a single-shot .22 rifle, and pitches hay to the wintering stock. He is routinely rented out to neighbors during the autumn harvest for fifty cents a day plus board.

The girl is now a structural load-bearing pillar of the household economy: she cooks meals for ten men by herself when her mother is sick or in childbed, she tends three younger children for twelve hours a day, she operates the agonizingly heavy boiling-water laundry cycle on washdays, and she helps butcher and salt the winter meat. The family understands she is explicitly training to run her own house in three years.

### The Ranch Boy

A highly specialized path. By twelve, he is a horse-holder choking on dust at the spring branding. By fourteen, he is riding the home pastures alone to gather strays. By fifteen, the foreman hires him as a seasonal hand at half wages. By seventeen, he draws a full man's pay, sleeps in the bunkhouse with men three times his age, and has fully absorbed the brutal work, the specialized vocabulary, and the absolute required silence of the trade.

### The Town Child

Less physical labor, more formal schooling. The merchant's son attends the framed-lumber school for a full nine months and is beaten until he learns to read, cipher fractions, and write a clean copperplate hand. He sweeps the mercantile floor at dawn and runs deliveries.

The merchant's daughter learns the same academic subjects, plus the piano and the exhausting domestic social accomplishments her mother demands.

The teamster's child or the saloon-keeper's child inhabits the town's brutal working margins. He runs wild in the dirt streets, knows every blind alley, knows exactly which freight horses kick, knows the stinking back doors of the saloons, and knows the names of all the women on the line—whether his mother knows he knows them or not.

### The mining-camp child

The most varied of the lot. The Cornish miner's child in a company town attends the company school and the chapel and is destined for the mine. The boarding-house child grows up among single men and develops an early adult composure. The line woman's child is raised in the parlor house among the women, often by a wet nurse, often loved by half a dozen of the women, often sent to a convent school in a distant city when the mother can afford it.

## 2. The Nursery and the Cradle

The frontier infant slept in a heavy wooden cradle the father had sawn and nailed together, or simply in a bottom drawer pulled from the pine bureau. He was nursed for eighteen months to two years, significantly longer than the modern infant. His mother hauled him on her hip while she worked the stove, or set him on a quilt where she could watch him. He was wrapped in a long homespun gown, and in winter, a tight knit cap.

The names came straight from the family Bible: the father's name, the grandfather's name, the saint of the day, or the beloved aunt who had died of the fever. Exact repetition across generations was expected. A frontier family with a John, a John Jr., and a Little John operating simultaneously was standard practice. Daughters were named for mothers, grandmothers, and dead sisters.

If the family could afford the itinerant town photographer, the very first photograph taken of the child was often a memorial. The infant who had died was photographed in the pine casket, or propped up in the mother's arms with his eyes shut, before the burial. The image was printed on cabinet cards, sent to relatives back East, and kept permanently on the mantel.

## 3. Childbirth from the Child's Side

The child arrived in his mother's bed in the cabin or the soddy. The midwife, if there was an experienced woman within riding distance, was sent for. If there was not, the husband, an older daughter, or a neighbor woman handled the blood and the water. The town doctor was rarely present at the birth unless the labor went catastrophically wrong; the doctor was sent for afterward only if the mother was bleeding out or the child was failing.

The other children of the household were often banished to a neighbor's farm for the day. They returned to find the new sibling screaming in the cradle and the mother pale in the bed. The mother was kept in bed for a week or two, depending entirely on whether the household's economics could survive her absence from the stove and the churn. The neighbor women organized shifts to bring food.

Maternal mortality was a heavy, constant statistical reality. Every single frontier child knew at least one family in which the mother had died bleeding in the bed, and her children had been abruptly parceled out among aunts, grandmothers, or charitable neighbors. The motherless household—featuring a silent, desperate father and a ten-year-old daughter instantly promoted to keeping house for five younger siblings—was a recurring, tragic frontier archetype.

## 4. Mortality and the Small Grave

The graveyard behind the white frame church, or the fenced plot at the edge of the family's section, contained substantially more child markers than adult markers.

- **Diphtheria** was the great terror. A bacterial infection of the throat that produced a thick, leathery, gray membrane that grew until the child slowly suffocated. It hit preschool children hardest and could eradicate half the children in a county in a single brutal November.
- **Scarlet Fever** brought the characteristic sandpaper rash and a brain-boiling fever. It either killed the child outright or permanently scarred the heart valves and the kidneys, leaving the survivor physically ruined for life.
- **Whooping Cough** (pertussis) triggered long, racking, violent spasms in which the child could not draw breath. The coughing fits lasted for weeks. It killed infants by pure physical exhaustion and hypoxia.
- **Measles** brought the rash, the fever, and the cough; the measles virus itself sometimes killed, but the secondary bacterial pneumonia killed far more often.
- **Cholera Infantum** was the dreaded summer diarrhea in nursing infants, killing by catastrophic dehydration in seventy-two hours. Rural mothers lived in terror of the August heat wave that brought consecutive infant deaths to the neighborhood.
- **Typhoid Fever** came straight from the contaminated well water. The child spiked a fever, took to bed, declined steadily for three weeks, and either broke the fever or died of intestinal hemorrhage.
- **Accidents:** The kick of the draft mule, the fall under the iron-tired wagon wheels, the boiling water cascaded from the laundry kettle, the deep water in the spring creek, the open hearth fire catching a nightgown, the slipped axe, the rattlesnake in the woodpile.

The mother who buried three of her ten children was statistically average. The mother who buried five was well within the normal range. The grave markers in any frontier churchyard tell the story in carved stone; the period photographs of the cemeteries show row after row of small white markers with heartbreakingly short dates.

How the household carried the weight: the mother and a neighbor woman washed the small body; the father sawed and nailed the pine box; the neighbor men dug the hole in the hard earth; the circuit-riding minister or the father read the service; the other children stood at the edge of the hole; and the mother returned to her churning and her cooking the very next morning, because the work simply would not wait.

The mourning lasted longer in private than the merciless physical labor allowed it in public. The mother who lost a child in March might still be unable to speak the child's name in October. The father absorbed the blow differently; his grief manifested in long silences at the dinner table and sudden, explosive rage at a balky mule a year later.

## 5. School

The one-room school is one of the period's recurring images. The reality varied widely.

### The schoolhouse

A single room, twenty by thirty feet, of frame or of log. A door at one end, a teacher's desk and blackboard at the other. Benches or desks for the pupils. A pot-bellied stove in the middle. Outhouses behind. A water bucket and a dipper at the back. A hook for each child's coat and dinner pail.

The school year ran three to five months in most frontier counties through the 1870s and was extended to eight or nine months by the 1880s in the more settled counties. The terms were placed where the children could be spared from the farm: typically a winter term (December to March) and sometimes a summer term (June and July).

### The teacher

The schoolmarm was usually a young woman, often sixteen to twenty-two, often herself the eldest daughter of a respectable local family or a town spinster, sometimes a widow supporting children. She was paid twenty to forty dollars a month and boarded with families in the school district by rotation — a week with the Andersons, a week with the Halls.

The schoolmaster, less common in the West, was often a young man in transit: a college graduate from the East working a year before reading law, a preacher between charges, a veteran trying to settle. He was paid more than the schoolmarm — forty to sixty dollars a month — and was expected to handle the older boys.

### The curriculum

The McGuffey Eclectic Readers (six graded readers, revised editions through the 1870s and 1880s) were the dominant text in the West as in the East. The child read his way up the readers as he grew: First Reader at six or seven, Sixth Reader at twelve to fourteen if he stayed in school that long. The Readers contained moral selections, poems by Whittier and Longfellow and Bryant, prose by Webster and Irving, Bible passages, fables. The child memorized and recited.

Beyond the Readers came the speller — Webster's _Blue-Backed Speller_ in the older households, Sander's or Swinton's in the newer; the arithmetic (Ray's _Arithmetic_, in graded volumes); the geography (Mitchell's or Cornell's); a copybook for penmanship in the Spencerian hand. Older students read Greenleaf's _Algebra_ or Davies' _Surveying_ if the teacher could teach them. History meant Goodrich's _Pictorial History of the United States_ or Quackenbos's similar volume.

### The school day

Opening prayer in most districts; in the Mormon districts a Mormon prayer; in the Catholic districts a Catholic prayer; in the German districts sometimes a hymn. The day proceeded by recitation: each class — the First Reader class, the Second Reader class, and so on — was called to the front in turn while the others worked at their seats. Slates and chalk for arithmetic. Penmanship after dinner. Spelling bees on Friday afternoons. Dismissal at four.

The recess: the boys played at the woodpile or in the field, the girls at the porch or under the trees. The games included tag, dare base, anty-over (a ball game over the schoolhouse roof), drop the handkerchief, blind man's buff, marbles in season, mumblety-peg with pocketknives.

### The discipline

The teacher kept order with a hickory switch or a ferule. The big boys, who came to school in winter when not needed at the plow, were sometimes nearly the teacher's age and sometimes openly defiant. The schoolmaster who could not handle them did not last long. The schoolmarm who could not handle them often appealed to a respected father in the district who came down to the school and addressed the matter.

The traditional gauntlet: the new male teacher in a rough district was tested by the older boys on his first day or his first week. If he won the fight, the term proceeded smoothly. If he lost, the term collapsed and the trustees hired a replacement.

### Beyond school

A frontier child's formal schooling typically ended at thirteen or fourteen. A few continued: the academy in a larger town, the territorial normal school, in rare cases a college in the East. The girls who continued became teachers themselves or married. The boys who continued became preachers, lawyers, doctors, or merchants' sons fitted for the counting room.

The child who never attended school existed in substantial numbers: the homestead too remote, the parents indifferent, the labor too pressing. The illiterate frontier adult was common enough that the census takers in the 1880 census recorded as many as twenty percent of adults in some western counties as unable to read or write.

## 6. Toys, Games, Songs

The frontier child played with what the household contained.

### Toys

The corn-husk doll was made by the mother or the older sister from the husks of the autumn corn, with a face drawn in pencil or charcoal and a scrap dress. It was the girl's first doll, often her only doll until a town doll arrived as a Christmas present at age seven or eight. The rag doll was the next stage — stuffed with cotton or with wool, with embroidered features, carried until the child was nine or ten and then passed to a younger sister. The town doll had a china head, a painted face, and a cloth body; bought at a mercantile or sent from relatives in the East, it was a possession to be cared for, dressed, undressed, taken to bed.

The willow whistle was cut from a green willow branch in spring. It was the boy's first instrument, and the technique was passed from older boys to younger. The tin soldier was bought in town and sold at a nickel each; the boy's collection might number six or eight, and Civil War uniforms predominated through the 1870s. The sled was father-built of split planks and iron runners, used hard and repaired each winter. The hoop and stick — a barrel hoop driven with a stick — was a town child's game. Marbles cost a nickel for a dozen and were played for keeps in the schoolyard; the clay marble was the common stake, the agate and the glass were the prizes.

The pocketknife came to a boy at eight or nine, given by the father — a real tool, expected to be cared for, used for whittling, for cutting his food at table, for the thousand small cuttings of the day. The .22 single-shot rifle came at twelve or so on the homestead, sometimes earlier, used for small game, varmints, and target. Father taught him the safe handling and the maintenance. The boy with his .22 over his shoulder walking the field for rabbits was a daily frontier sight.

### Games

Tag, hide and seek, prisoner's base were universal. Anty-over involved a ball thrown over the schoolhouse roof while teams ran around the building. Crack the whip was a line of children holding hands, the leader running and turning until the child at the end was flung off. Marbles came with its specialized vocabulary — _aggie, taw, mibs, knuckle down, no fudging._ Mumblety-peg was played with pocketknives, the loser pulling a peg from the ground with his teeth. Hopscotch, jacks, and jump rope were primarily girls' games. Snap the whip on the ice was the winter version. Skating, sledding, snow forts filled the cold months. Swimming in the creek was a summer matter, boys generally naked, girls in shifts at a separate hole. Fishing with a hook and line on a willow pole was a summer activity, primarily the boys', with girls in some households welcome.

The ranch child rode from age three or four — first behind a parent, then on his own pony. The town child rode less, the merchant's son rented a horse from the livery for a Sunday. The play-party was a young people's social with singing games (_Skip to My Lou, Old Dan Tucker, Sandy Land_) substituting for the dancing the Methodist or Baptist parents forbade.

### Songs

The frontier child sang what the family sang and what the school sang. The lullabies were the old ones — _Hush Little Baby, Bye Baby Bunting, Rock-a-Bye Baby._ The play songs were _Ring Around the Rosie, London Bridge, Farmer in the Dell._ The school songs were the patriotic ones — _America, My Country 'Tis of Thee, Yankee Doodle, Battle Hymn of the Republic_ — and the Sunday school hymns _Jesus Loves Me_ and _There Is a Happy Land._ The trail child of the cattleman or the freighter learned the trail songs from the hands.

### Books

The household that owned books owned a Bible, a battered McGuffey Reader or two, a hymnal, and perhaps a copy of _Pilgrim's Progress_ or a Webster's dictionary. A well-fixed town household might add a few of the massively popular novels of the period: Fenimore Cooper, Sir Walter Scott, Dickens, or Harriet Beecher Stowe.

The dime novel (published by Beadle & Adams or Munro) was the absolute ruler of the boy's reading by 1870. The _Deadwood Dick_ and _Buffalo Bill_ serials were bought for ten cents at the mercantile or endlessly traded among friends. The mother almost universally disapproved of them as trash; the boy read them in the hayloft.

## 7. The Rites of Passage

The frontier child's progression was marked by highly specific, physical transitions.

- **First Trousers:** The boy went into long pants at five or six, having worn dresses and linen pinafores before that. The transition (being "breeched") was a recognized, formal milestone in most households.
- **First Wage Labor:** Usually herding a neighbor's cows out of the corn, or holding horses outside a town funeral for a few copper cents.
- **First Long Ride Alone:** The boy sent on horseback to the next ranch or the next town with a message, or sent whipping through the dark to fetch the doctor. Eight or nine years old for a short ride; twelve for a serious distance.
- **First Gun:** A .22 rifle at twelve. A heavy centerfire rifle (a single-shot Stevens or a surplus Springfield) at fourteen or fifteen. A revolver—if the family had the cash and the inclination—in the late teens.
- **First Horse:** A pony at six or seven in a dedicated ranch family. A green colt assigned to him to break and train at twelve. A grown, working saddle horse at fifteen.
- **First Work for Hire:** The boy hired out at fourteen for the autumn harvest, then for a season on a neighboring ranch, then for a full year as a hand. The girl hired out at the exact same age as a "hired girl" in a neighboring household or as kitchen help at the town hotel.
- **First Dance, First Courting:** The acoustic "play-party" at twelve or thirteen, the actual fiddle dance at fifteen, serious courting at sixteen or seventeen.
- **Public Profession of Faith:** The Methodist child went to the weeping mourner's bench at the summer camp meeting when the panic hit him, often at twelve or thirteen. The Catholic child made his first communion at the strict age set by the parish priest (usually seven). The Baptist child was physically immersed in the freezing creek when he professed, usually between twelve and fifteen.
- **Departure:** The boy left for the logging camps, the army, or the cattle trails. The girl married and moved to a claim three counties away. The household contracted. The mother counted her children at the table at age twenty, and counted them again at age thirty, and the numbers did not match.

## 8. The Native Child, the Mexican Child, the Black Child, the Chinese Child

### The Native Child

Specific tribal nation dictates the reality; the Comanche and Lakota cases are instructive baselines for the Plains.

The Native child was raised by the entire band, not exclusively by the biological parents. Aunts, uncles, and grandparents all held rigidly defined legal and social obligations to the child. Direct physical discipline was exceptionally rare; public shame, ridicule, and intense praise were the behavioral levers.

The Comanche boy was tied to a saddle at three, shooting a small bow by six, hunting rabbits with the older boys by ten, and riding with a serious war or hunting party by fourteen. The girl learned the crushing logistical work of the camp from her mother and her aunts: striking and packing the heavy lodges, processing raw buffalo hides into soft leather, and managing the meat. The girl was eligible for marriage at her first menses; the boy when he had formally counted coup in combat or made his first clean kill on a buffalo hunt.

(The systemic destruction of the Native child's world via the federal boarding school system—such as the Carlisle Indian Industrial School, which opened in 1879—was just beginning to grind into motion at the close of our period. The enforced prohibition of native dress and language, the forced cutting of hair, and the assigning of white names represent a specific trauma that the Native child of 1860 did not face, but the child of 1880 absolutely did.)

### The Mexican Child of the _Rancho_

In the established Mexican families of New Mexico, South Texas, and California, the child was raised in a massive, extended household of grandparents, unmarried aunts, and cousins. The religion was absolute: baptism within weeks of birth, the godparents (_padrinos_) forming a lifelong legal and spiritual relationship, and first communion at seven.

The boy of the _rancho_ learned the livestock trade from age six. He spoke Spanish at home, acquiring English only out of brutal economic necessity or in the Anglo-run schools (though Spanish-language schools did persist in some New Mexico parishes into the 1880s).

The girl of the _rancho_ was guarded fiercely. She remained deep inside the household until marriage, educated entirely by the women in the household arts, and, in literate families, in reading and writing Spanish. She was heavily chaperoned during any courting and invariably married within the extended Catholic network of related families.

### The Black Child After Reconstruction

The freedman's child of the 1870s West was the literal first generation of emancipation. The parents had been enslaved; the child was born free.

The Black homestead child in Kansas (specifically in the "Exoduster" settlements of 1879 onward, like Nicodemus) attended the community's own school, heavily supported by the Black Methodist or Baptist churches. The literacy rate, deliberately suppressed to near zero in the parents' generation by Southern law, exploded upward in the child's generation.

The Black ranch hand's child in Texas or New Mexico worked alongside his father in the dust and learned the cattle trade exactly as the white ranch child did, though he attended the local school only if the county permitted it (many did not). The Black cavalry soldier's child at a frontier post (the Buffalo Soldiers of the 9th and 10th Cavalry) attended the post school, sometimes integrated with the white officers' children, sometimes strictly segregated, depending entirely on the commanding officer's whim.

### The Chinese Child

The Chinese child in the 1870s West was a statistical anomaly. The brutal economics of the labor migration and the looming hostility of the Chinese Exclusion regime meant the vast majority of Chinese immigrants were single male workers. The few Chinese children who did exist were heavily concentrated in the tight, defensive Chinese quarters of San Francisco, Sacramento, and the larger mining camps.

The Chinese boy was raised entirely within the language and culture of his parents' home province, worked in the family laundry or mercantile from age ten, and was fiercely protected from the hostile Anglo street outside the quarter. The Chinese girl was kept even deeper inside the household. The catastrophic exception was the girl purchased in China by the _tongs_ and shipped into the West Coast brothels—a child slave whose entire childhood was the crib.

## 9. The Orphan, the Bound-Out, the Parceled-Out

A frontier child with both biological parents living to see his majority had beaten the odds. The unlucky cases were the standard reality.

### The Motherless Household

The mother dead in childbirth, or of consumption, or of typhoid. The father, left staring at a six-year-old, a four-year-old, and a nursing baby, kept the household from starving by hiring a teenage girl, wiring for his mother-in-law back East, or remarrying within six months. The desperate, rapid remarriage was often brutal on the children; the "wicked stepmother" of period fiction is a recurring trope precisely because the sudden insertion of a new, stressed woman into a grieving, overworked household was a recurring reality.

### The Fatherless Household

The father killed in the war, crushed in the mine, dragged by a horse, or gutted by disease. The widow kept the homestead if she was physically able, took in heavy laundry, baked for the town, or remarried immediately. The widow who simply could not feed the children broke the family apart: the older boys sent out to work for board with neighbors, the girls shipped to an aunt in Iowa, the baby handed to a childless cousin in town.

### The Full Orphan

Both parents dead. The siblings were immediately separated. The twelve-year-old boy sent to work an uncle's farm in Missouri, the nine-year-old girl to an aunt's town household in Illinois, the toddler to whoever would feed her. The siblings might not see one another for ten years. Sometimes they wrote letters. Often, the connection simply dissolved.

### The Orphan Train

Between 1854 and 1929, the "Orphan Train" movement shipped approximately 250,000 children out of the crushing poverty of eastern cities (mostly New York and Boston) to be placed on midwestern and western farms.

The children were unloaded at a country depot, marched to the local church or town hall, and displayed on the stage. Local farmers walked the line and inspected them like livestock. The placements were not adoptions; they were legal indentures. The farmer agreed to feed, clothe, and school the child decently in direct exchange for unpaid manual labor until the age of eighteen or twenty-one.

The child stood on the church steps. The farmer inspected the boy's teeth, felt his arms to see if he could lift a hundred-pound sack of grain, and checked his hair color. The strong children were taken first. The sickly or small children were taken last, or loaded back onto the train to try the next town down the line. The agent recorded the names in a ledger, the farmer signed the contract, and the wagon drove away into a country the child had never seen.

## 10. The Child in the Work

The frontier child was a working asset from an age that shocks the modern reader. The progressive child labor laws of the 1890s had not yet been written, and they would not reach agriculture for another century.

In the **mines**, the Cornish boy went underground at twelve as a "trapper boy" (sitting in the pitch black for ten hours opening and closing ventilation doors for the ore carts) or as a mucker's helper shoveling shattered rock. He worked the exact same ten- or twelve-hour shift as the men, breathing the same silica dust, for a third of the men's wages.

On the **railroad**, the child was the water boy, the message runner, or the "candy butcher" walking the swaying passenger cars selling newspapers and fruit.

In **domestic service**, the "hired girl" was fourteen. Sent out by her own impoverished family, she worked for her board and perhaps two dollars a month in a neighbor's house. She cooked, scrubbed floors with lye soap, and hauled toddlers. She slept on a pallet in the attic or in a cold room off the kitchen.

In the **trades**, the boy of fourteen was formally indentured to the printer, the wheelwright, or the blacksmith. The contract bound him for three to seven years. In exchange for his unpaid labor, he received board, clothes, the secrets of the trade, and, at the end of his term, a set of tools and his freedom.

## 11. The Sounds and Smells of the Frontier

The child experienced the country at waist-height.

**The Vocabulary:** _Babe, infant, suckling_ for the nursing child. _Toddler, little chap_ for two to four. _Tow-head, button, tyke_ for the small child. _Young 'un_ was the absolute standard spoken form. _Brat, whelp_ were fighting insults. _Half-grown, sprout, stripling_ for the twelve-year-old boy.

**The Smells:** The sharp wood smoke of the cook stove; the caustic bite of boiling lye soap on washday; the rendered beef tallow of the candles; the metallic tang of kerosene lamps; the heavy ammonia of horse urine in the barn; the salt-cure of the pork hanging in the smokehouse; the yeasty heat of rising bread; the father's damp wool coat; the terrifying copper smell of the blood bucket at butchering time.

**The Sounds:** The clock ticking in the silence of a snowbound cabin; the wind screaming in the eaves; the draft horses rattling their heavy trace-chains in the frozen yard; the hiss of spit on a hot iron stove; the schoolmarm's brass bell; the cattle lowing for their calves; the coyote in the timber after dark.
