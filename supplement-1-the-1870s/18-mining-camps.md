# Chapter 18 — Mining Camps

> _"THE STEEL RANG AGAINST THE ROCK AND THE DUST CAME OFF THE FACE AND THE CARBIDE LAMP THREW THEIR SHADOWS LONG ON THE TIMBERED WALLS. THEY HAD BEEN AT IT SIX HOURS. THEY HAD TWO MORE TO GO."_

The mine was dark and loud and thick with dust. What follows is the hardrock camp of the 1870s and 1880s — what kind of mine, what the underground looks like, the eight hours of a shift, the men in their order, the surface camp, the boarding house, the company store, the Cornish, the union and the Pinkertons, the hazards, and the vocabulary of the work.

---

## 1. What Kind of Mine

The 1870s West had two kinds of hard-rock mining and one kind of soft.

- **Hardrock gold and silver.** An underground mine following a vein of ore in consolidated rock — granite, quartzite, porphyry. Nevada (Comstock), Colorado (Leadville, Cripple Creek), Arizona (Tombstone, Globe), Idaho (Coeur d'Alene), Montana (Butte).
- **Placer gold.** Worked at the surface — streams and gravels, the prospector's world.
- **Coal.** A different world — underground, shaft-based, but in softer sedimentary rock, often in company towns. Coal does not appear in western fiction as often as silver and gold.

---

## 2. The Underground

### The shaft

The shaft opens to the mountain. A vertical opening, rectangular — six feet by eight feet in most operations — lined with square-set timber from top to bottom. The shaft drops level by level. The 500-foot level. The 700-foot level. The 1000-foot level. Each level is a horizontal floor. The men rode the **cage** — an iron platform on wire rope — up and down. The hoist engineer at the surface worked the cage. At depth the air was hot. Below 500 feet in many Comstock mines the temperature reached 120°F. The Comstock piped ice water underground. In most 1870s mines the deep levels were hot, wet, and dark.

### Drifts and crosscuts

From each level drifts run horizontally along the vein. A drift is a tunnel with a roof and two walls and a floor, square-set with timber. Seven feet high, six feet wide. The floor has a pair of iron rails. The **mucking car** — a steel tub holding half a ton of broken ore — runs on those rails. A man pushes it to the ore chute at the shaft.

**Crosscuts** run perpendicular to the drift, cutting across rock to reach a new vein or to connect workings. Driven blind. You do not know if the vein is there until the drill and powder tell you.

### Stopes, raises, winzes

The **stope** is the open space left by removed ore. Where the drift runs the vein horizontally, the stope opens vertically — ore taken out above and below the drift line. A stope might be twenty feet high and sixty feet long. Square-set timbers hold it open. Without the timbers it collapses.

A **raise** is a vertical opening driven upward from a lower level toward an upper one. A **winze** is driven downward. They connect levels. They ventilate the mine. They carry ore. A raise is driven by a man lying on his back on a plank, drilling upward into the rock above his face. The dust falls into his eyes.

### Square-set timbering

Deidesheimer's square-set system appeared at Comstock in 1860. It made deep hardrock mining viable. Timbers were pre-cut to standard lengths and fit together at right angles like a three-dimensional grid — each set interlocking with its neighbors above, below, and to the sides. A stope could be held open at any width. The system traveled west with every mine that opened after 1862. The smell of wet pine and fresh-cut Douglas fir marked a working mine — the timberman's crew worked one level behind the miners, setting new sets as the ore came out.

## 3. The Shift — Eight Hours

### First call

The day shift came on at six in the morning. The men changed in the **change house** — a building near the shaft collar where they swapped street clothes for working clothes. Working clothes were wool in winter (wool dries from the inside out), duck canvas in summer. The boots were heavy leather, nailed with hobnails for traction on wet timber. Each man carried his own candle holder — a bent spike that jammed into a timber crack — and a candle, or in better-equipped mines by the late 1870s, a carbide lamp on his hat. The carbide lamp made a white acetylene flame and hissed and smelled of a struck match.

### Going down

The men crowded the cage — six men, plus the shift boss who rode with them to the working level. The cage dropped fast enough to lift the stomach. The shaft walls flickered past the candle's reach. At depth the air changed. Warmer. Damper. Carrying the smell of spent powder from the previous shift and the rock smell — iron, wet clay, sometimes sulfur.

### The drill

Most of the shift was drilling. The miners worked in pairs. One man held the **drill steel** — a tapered iron rod — against the rock face and turned it a quarter revolution after each blow. The other man swung the **sledge** — a four-pound hammer in the single-jack, an eight-pounder in the double-jack. Strike, turn, strike, turn. The steel went in an inch at a time. A twelve-inch hole took twenty minutes. The noise in the drift — the ringing of the steel, the bass thud of the sledge on the striker's end — carried through the rock.

- **Single-jack** work. A man alone. Small hammer, lighter steel, slower. Used in tight spaces, in raises where a second man could not stand.
- **Double-jack** work demands trust. If the striker misses the steel, the holder's hands take the blow. The men who worked together for years did not look at each other during the strike. They listened for the rhythm.

By the 1870s mechanical drills powered by compressed air appeared in the larger mines. The men called them **widow makers** because the water injection that dampened the dust often ran dry and the dry dust went into the lungs. A man on a widow maker in an unventilated stope breathed his future silicosis with every breath.

### Loading and firing

When the holes were drilled — a face got eight to twelve holes in a pattern the shift boss marked — the **powder man** loaded them. Black powder until the early 1870s. Dynamite after 1867, increasingly common in the West through the 1870s. The powder man tamped the charge, cut the fuse, and began at the holes farthest from the exit. Each fuse was timed so the shots fired in sequence — the cut holes in the center first, the relievers next, the perimeter holes last. The sequence broke the rock efficiently. A bad sequence wasted powder or left a wall standing.

The shift boss counted the men out of the drift. The powder man lit the fuses. He counted: _One fuse burning. Two fuses burning. Out. Run._ The rule was never to run before all fuses were lit. The rule was never to walk. Between the two was a quarter of the dead men in any mine.

The shots went off in sequence. Flat concussions felt in the chest before they were heard. The rock breaking in waves. Dust and smoke filled the drift. The men waited outside the entry on the level until the smoke cleared — twenty minutes at minimum, longer in poor ventilation. Then the muckers went in.

### Mucking

The **muckers** were the bottom of the mine's hierarchy and the hardest-worked men in it. After a blast, the face was a pile of broken rock — **muck** — extending back ten or fifteen feet. The mucker loaded the muck into the car by hand with a square-faced iron shovel. The car held half a ton. He pushed it to the ore chute and tipped it. He went back and did it again. For eight hours. The mucker ate his lunch underground, sitting on a rock with the candle between his knees. He did not usually speak to the men around him. He had not enough to say.

### End of shift

The whistle at the shaft. The men rode the cage up. Daylight again. The change house. The walk to the boarding house or the saloon. The miner's day was either underground or sleeping off underground. There was not much middle.

## 4. The Hierarchy

A working mine of any size has a distinct chain.

The **superintendent** manages the whole operation. Often an engineer. The Comstock and later mines brought in graduates of the Columbia School of Mines or the Freiberg Mining Academy in Saxony. He answers to the owners or the board.

The **shift boss** is the foreman underground. He assigns men to faces. He decides which drifts to advance and which to hold. He knows the mine's geology better than the superintendent. A good shift boss is irreplaceable. He is the highest-paid hourly worker in the mine.

The **miner** — first-class miner or just miner — is the skilled man who drills, fires, reads rock. Paid $3–$4 a day in the 1870s. Not a laborer. A tradesman. He knows the difference between good ground and bad, can read a vein's dip and strike, knows when the timbers are talking.

The **powder man** is sometimes a specialized role, sometimes the senior miner on a face. The man who handles dynamite is the man the crew does not argue with.

The **timberman** sets the square-set. As skilled as the miner in his way. He reads the rock's pressure and knows where to put the timber so it does not move. The miner and the timberman work in sequence. The miner opens the ground. The timberman holds it open.

The **mucker** is the unskilled laborer at the face. Often a recent immigrant. Cornish, Irish, Italian, Croatian, Mexican. The job that kills the back before the lungs go. A mucker who lasted three years became a miner. Most did not last three years.

The **hoist man** operates the cage. Never seen underground by anyone who matters. He controls the lives of every man in the mine at the moment of the shift change and at every emergency. A good hoist man knows from the feel of the rope whether the cage is loaded or empty, whether it is stopping on a timber or a man.

The **blacksmith** is the mine's own smith, resharpening drill steels. He worked at the surface, at a coal-fired forge, sharpening steel all shift. One blacksmith could keep four or five drilling teams supplied. A mine that ran out of sharp steels stopped.

## Shift Change at Three

> Brennan came on at three and Dolan went off at three and they passed each other in the drift with the carbide lamps throwing their shadows on the timbered walls. Dolan's face was black with dust and his eyes were red where the dust had got in around the lamp.
>
> "Anything?" Brennan said.
>
> "The face is hard. We took out maybe four feet on the day shift. The vein's still there but she's narrower than she was on Tuesday."
>

> Brennan went on past him and Dolan went on past him and they did not say more. Brennan came up to the face and the two men of his shift were there setting up the drill. He set down his lunch pail and his second carbide tin in the niche at the side of the drift and he took up the sledge.

>

## 5. The Surface

### Headframe and hoist

The surface above a mine is a complex of buildings. The **headframe** — also called the gallows frame — stands over the shaft, an A-frame of wood carrying the sheave wheel that the hoist rope runs over. It is the mine's emblem. A camp with a headframe in operation is a camp with money. Beside the headframe sits the **hoist house**, containing the steam engine and hoist drum. The steam engine burns wood or coal. It runs all day. The camp above ground sounds like the steam engine.

### The ore dump and stamp mill

Ore from the cage goes to the **ore dump** — a pile of broken rock beside the shaft. From the dump it is sorted by grade. High-grade ore goes to the **stamp mill**: a set of iron stamps driven by a cam, dropping onto the ore in iron mortars, crushing it to sand. The mercury amalgam process extracts the gold or silver from the sand. The stamp mill produces a sound: a constant, rhythmic, low-frequency thunder that carries two miles. A man who has worked near a stamp mill for a year is hard of hearing.

### Change house and camp layout

The change house has two rooms: one for the men going down (their street clothes hang on pegs), one for the men coming up (wet, rock-dusted, sometimes bloody). The change house smells of wet wool, tallow candle smoke, and spent carbide.

A mining camp is not planned. It grows around the shaft collar. The boarding house sits closest to the shaft. The company office sits beside the hoist house. The assay office (a small room with a furnace and a scale) sits adjacent to the ore dump. The superintendent's house sits on the highest ground in reach, overlooking the works. The saloon sits opposite the boarding house. The camp's streets — when it has streets — are unpaved, churned to mud by the ore wagons, white with rock dust when dry. The dust is everywhere: on the grass between buildings, on the horses in the stable, on the laundry that women hang at the camp's edges.

## 6. The Boarding House

The miner's lodging. Not a hotel — a boarding house runs on contract. The miner pays a fixed weekly rate (in the 1870s, $5–$7 a week for room and board) which the company sometimes deducts from wages. The boarding house keeper is often a widow or a woman whose husband works in the mine.

A boarding house at a large camp holds thirty to sixty men. Two sittings at the long table — day shift at six in the evening, night shift at six in the morning. The food is beef stew or salt pork, beans, bread, coffee. The Cornish miners brought their own tradition: the **pasty** — a half-moon of pastry filled with beef, potato, and turnip, crimped so a miner can hold it by the crust with his rock-dusted hands and not contaminate the food. (See Chapter 6.)

The room: six to a room, sometimes more in a boom camp. Double bunks. A single lamp. The man who works nights sleeps through the stamp mill's thunder because there is nothing else to do.

## 7. The Company Store

In some mining operations — particularly coal, but in hardrock mines in remote locations — the company owned the store. The miners bought their goods with scrip redeemable only at the company store. This system (called the **truck system** in British usage) kept the workforce in debt. A man who owed the company store $40 did not leave the camp.

In the 1870s hardrock districts the company store was more common in isolated camps; in Leadville or Butte, where independent merchants competed, the miners had options. The company store is not a universal of the hardrock west, but it is a feature of the more remote operations.

## 8. The Cousin Jacks

The Cornish miners — men from Cornwall, England's own tin and copper mining country — were the dominant labor in the American hardrock mines of the 1860s–1880s. They came because Cornwall's own mines were failing. They went everywhere silver and gold was found. They ran the shift bosses. They trained the muckers in drilling.

The Cornish were called **Cousin Jacks** — the standard explanation is that a Cornishman asked to recommend someone for a job always had a cousin named Jack. A Cornish miner speaks with a southwestern English accent (not the Irish accent often assumed), uses the mining vocabulary of Cornwall (a **kibble** for a bucket, a **skip** for the ore car, **pare** for the crew assigned to a face), and has opinions about the right way to timber that he will share with anyone who stands still long enough.

Other nationalities at the mines: **Irish** were muckers, some skilled men. **Mexican and Mexican-American** workers were common in Arizona and New Mexico mines and often did the most hazardous surface work. **Scandinavian** — Norwegian and Swedish immigrants in the northern districts (Montana, Idaho, Colorado). **Chinese** miners were largely excluded from underground work by union pressure; some worked in the mills and on surface operations.

## 9. Labor and Capital — Union and Pinkerton

By the mid-1870s the miners in the major districts were organized. The **Miners' Union** (formalized as the Western Federation of Miners in 1893, but functioning in the major camps from the early 1870s) fought for the eight-hour day, the $3–$4 daily wage, and safety conditions. The union controlled who worked. A non-union man — a **scab,** a **blackleg** in Cornish usage — could not get work at a union camp. The union's power was this: without skilled miners, the mine did not produce. The mine owners knew this and resented it with the resentment of men who owned everything except the knowledge in other men's hands.

The **Pinkerton National Detective Agency,** founded 1850, provided espionage and strikebreaking on contract. At a mine facing a strike or a suspected union organizing drive, the superintendent wrote to Chicago. The Pinkerton sent a man undercover — hired as a mucker, worked at the face, reported to the superintendent what the men said in the boarding house at night. The Pinkertons' undercover men wore the same clothes and ate the same food and listened in the dark. They were good at the work. They were also, by the logic of their work, required to be trusted by men they were preparing to betray.

The mining wars of the 1890s (Coeur d'Alene, 1892; Cripple Creek, 1894) were the climax of this conflict. For the 1870s fiction writer the tension is already in place: every large camp has a union, every mine owner has considered a Pinkerton, and the miner who is too curious about his neighbors' politics is noticed.

## 10. The Hazards

A mine in the 1870s killed men reliably.

- **Cave-in.** A timber that failed, a stope that had been mined too wide, a section of rock that moved without warning. The cave might bury a single man or collapse a level. Most bodies were recovered. Some were not. A section of the Comstock known as the Dead Zone was sealed off in 1869 after a cave trapped eleven men. The bodies stayed.
- **Fire.** The mine's worst enemy in a timbered operation. A candle knocked from a timber crack, a carbide lamp dropped into wood shavings, a fuse left burning. Once the timber caught, the fire spread through the mine's ventilation like a chimney. The men below the fire were in a chimney. The Yellow Jacket fire at the Comstock in 1869 killed thirty-seven men. No fire doors were standard in 1870s mines. In fiction, the mine fire is the catastrophe from which there is no waking up.
- **Flooding.** Every mine at depth is wet. The shallower levels are drained by pumps. The pumps stop when the steam engine stops. A flooded lower level means pumping for weeks before it can be worked again. A man who falls into a flooded sump does not come up. The sump is black water in the dark at the bottom of everything.
- **Gas.** Carbon monoxide and hydrogen sulfide accumulate in dead-end drifts and raises. A canary carried by the shift boss to a worked-out face was not a metaphor when the practice began — it was equipment. The canary died first. The shift boss then had thirty seconds to turn around.
- **Silicosis.** The slow killer. Rock dust into the lungs, deposited over years, forming scar tissue that replaced lung. The dry-drill was its agent. A miner who had been underground twenty years often could not climb the stairs from the boarding house dining room to his bunk without stopping to breathe. He did not usually say what he had.

## 11. Vocabulary, Sound, Smell, Light

- **Vocabulary.** **Drift** — horizontal tunnel. **Crosscut** — tunnel perpendicular to drift, cutting across to reach a vein. **Stope** — open working space above or below the drift. **Raise** — vertical opening driven upward. **Winze** — vertical opening driven downward. **Sump** — the lowest point in the mine, where water collects. **Collar** — the surface opening of the shaft. **Square-set** — the interlocking timber frame. **Muck** — broken ore and waste rock after a blast. **Mucking car** or **car** — the steel ore tub on rails. **Skip** — another term for the ore car. **Cage** — the elevator in the shaft. **Kibble** — a bucket used to lift rock in shaft-sinking before a cage is installed. **Single-jack** — one man, small hammer, alone at the face. **Double-jack** — two men, large sledge, one holds the steel. **Widow maker** — a mechanical drill. **Fuse** — the burning cord that fires the charge. **Hangfire** — a fuse that does not fire on schedule; the most dangerous thing underground. **Blackleg / scab** — a non-union worker. **Cousin Jack** — a Cornish miner.
- **Sounds.** Underground is not silent. It is the ring of steel on steel when a double-jack pair is drilling — a note that echoes down the drift and back. The crack and rumble of a blast behind the sealed drift door — felt in the chest before it reaches the ears. The drip of water on rock, irregular, constant. The creak of the timber under pressure — the mine is always moving, always settling, always talking in the language of wood under load. After a big blast, the absolute silence before the dust arrives — a second of nothing, then the roar.
- **Smells.** Spent black powder or dynamite: acrid, sulfurous, long-lasting, clings to wool. Carbide lamp: sharp, like a struck match. Wet timber: pine sap and rot at once. The miner's sweat: salt and iron. Hot rock: dry, mineral, faintly metallic at depth.
- **Light.** A candle gives three to four feet of usable light. A carbide lamp gives eight to ten feet. Beyond that is the dark. The dark is total. Darker than any dark above ground because there is no sky for the eyes to reach toward. The man who drops his candle underground goes still and does not move until he finds it. The worked face catches the candle's light in the facets of rock — quartz, flecks of native silver, the dull yellow of iron pyrite that fools no one who has been underground a week. The ore the company wants is often undistinguished: gray, heavy, sulfidic, nothing that looks like wealth.
