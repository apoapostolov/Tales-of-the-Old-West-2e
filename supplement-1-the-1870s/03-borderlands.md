# Chapter 3 — The Borderlands

> _"POBRE MÉXICO, TAN LEJOS DE DIOS Y TAN CERCA DE LOS ESTADOS UNIDOS."_
> — PORFIRIO DÍAZ

The popular West is almost always written from the north looking south. The historical West did not work that way. The U.S.–Mexico border was a violent, porous, intermarried, and hotly contested zone.

The same families lived on both sides. The same cattle and horses crossed the river in both directions—usually in the dark, and usually without a bill of sale. To operate in this space requires understanding that the international line was a legal fiction drawn by surveyors in 1848, completely ignored by the Apaches, the Comanches, the cattle rustlers, and the people whose families had lived in the river valleys for two hundred years before the Americans arrived.

---

## 1. The Geography of the Line

The border established by the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848) and the Gadsden Purchase (1854) ran from the mouth of the Rio Grande on the Gulf of Mexico to the Pacific tidewater below San Diego. In the 1870s, it was entirely unfenced, marked only by occasional piled-stone surveyor monuments in the deep desert, and guarded by scattered, under-manned cavalry outposts.

### The River Border

From El Paso down to the Gulf of Mexico, the border is a river: the Rio Grande (the Río Bravo del Norte to the Mexicans). It ran shallow and sandy in the dry months, and ripped out its own banks during the spring floods.

Because the river constantly shifted its muddy channel, it stranded pockets of American land on the Mexican side, and vice versa. It was crossable at dozens of shallow, unnamed plunging-fords known only to local smugglers and cattle thieves. The major legal crossings anchored twin cities on opposite banks: Brownsville and Matamoros, Laredo and Nuevo Laredo, El Paso and Ciudad Juárez.

### The Desert Border

West of El Paso, the border is a surveyor’s ruler laid across the Chihuahuan and Sonoran deserts. This was the most hostile terrain on the continent. The line meant absolutely nothing here. The Apaches treated the entire region—New Mexico, Arizona, Chihuahua, and Sonora—as a single, borderless raiding ground, striking ranches in Mexico and driving the horses north to trade in the U.S., or striking American mining camps and vanishing south into the Sierra Madre.

### The Mexican States

The Mexican states bordering the U.S. (Tamaulipas, Nuevo León, Coahuila, Chihuahua, and Sonora) were isolated from the government in Mexico City by hundreds of miles of brutal desert. They were dominated by massive, feudal cattle _haciendas_ run by wealthy _patrones_, and isolated agricultural villages. In the 1870s, northern Mexico was bleeding—caught in a grinding, simultaneous war against the Apaches, the Comanches, and the political instability surrounding the rise of Porfirio Díaz.

## 2. Population and Language

### The Northern Claim

The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo guaranteed U.S. citizenship and the retention of property rights to the Mexicans living in the newly conquered territory. It was a guarantee honored mostly in the breach.

However, in the 1870s, the Southwest was still fundamentally Hispanic. In northern New Mexico, the old _Hispano_ families (the Oteros, Lunas, and Chaveses) controlled the sheep economy and the territorial legislature. In southern Texas, the _Tejano_ ranchers ran the cattle. In southern Arizona, the Sonoran merchants built Tucson.

In these territories in 1870, court proceedings, town councils, and daily commerce were conducted entirely in Spanish. An Anglo immigrant either learned the language or did not do business. The overwhelming demographic shift toward English would absolutely conquer the region by 1900, but in the 1870s, that conquest was far from complete.

### The Languages

- **Spanish:** The absolute dominant tongue of the borderlands. It was spoken in its formal, educated register by the wealthy _hacendados_ of Chihuahua and the _Hispano_ politicians in Santa Fe, and in a heavily localized, working dialect by the vaqueros and farmers.
- **English:** Pushing hard down from the north, carried by the U.S. Army, the railroad surveyors, and the massive influx of Anglo cattlemen.

### The Languages in the Room

On the border, the language mix ran continuously through the day. Spanish speakers and English speakers traded at every counter and worked every corral.

The Spanish words that had no clean English equivalent—_mesa, arroyo, rebozo, alcalde, jefe, vaquero_—went straight into the Anglo’s working speech. The Spanish-speaker’s English, when he had it, was often the stiff, precise English of an educated bilingual; the Anglo who had been on the border long enough spoke the battered, functional Spanish of a working cowboy. The _usted/tú_ distinction carried heavy social and physical weight—a distinction that a Spanish-speaker heard instantly, and that the English _you_ completely erased.

## 3. The Cattle and Horse Trade

The border's principal economic engine, both legal and illegal, was livestock.

The legal trade was massive. Mexican cattle crossed into Texas formally at the major river towns, paying customs duties by the tens of thousands of head before being driven north to the Kansas railheads.

The illegal traffic was likely just as large. Mexican cattle stolen by Texas thieves were driven north; Texas cattle stolen by Mexican thieves were driven south. The Apaches and the Comanches treated the entire border region as a wholesale livestock warehouse, driving stolen Mexican horse herds north to trade in New Mexico, and driving stolen Texas cattle south to sell to corrupt _patrones_ in Coahuila.

The border cattle thief was a specific breed: bilingual, possessing an encyclopedic knowledge of the desert river crossings, and usually operating under the protection of corrupt local officials on at least one side of the river (such as the south Texas empire run by John King Fisher).

The legal fiction of the border provided the ultimate laundering mechanism. Cattle stolen in Texas were driven across the river, their brands altered with a running iron. They were sold to a Mexican rancher, who might then legally sell those exact same cattle back to an American buyer, driving them north through U.S. Customs with a clean bill of sale. A single steer might cross the Rio Grande three times in its life.

## 4. Mexican Law Enforcement — The Rurales

The Mexican federal rural police force—the _Cuerpo de Rurales_—was originally established by Benito Juárez in 1861 and aggressively expanded under Porfirio Díaz to crush banditry and maintain brutal order in the countryside. By 1880, approximately 2,500 _rurales_ operated across Mexico, concentrated in the central states and along the violent northern border. They were mounted, heavily armed (initially with lever-action rifles and revolvers, becoming more standardized as Díaz consolidated power), and wore a distinctive gray-and-silver uniform topped with a wide sombrero.

The _rurales_ carried a terrifying reputation for summary execution under the _ley fuga_—the "law of flight." Suspected bandits caught in the brush were routinely shot in the back. The official report always stated the prisoner was shot while attempting escape; the reality was execution on the spot, without trial, judge, or jury. They were brutally effective at suppressing rural banditry, entirely at the expense of anything resembling due process.

The _rurale_ officer is the exact parallel to the Texas Ranger or the U.S. Deputy Marshal—the hard-bitten, mounted federal lawman operating in impossibly vast, difficult country.

## 5. The Texas Ranger

The Texas Rangers were officially reconstituted in 1874 as the "Frontier Battalion," tasked with ending Comanche raids, breaking up the massive border rustling rings, and bringing basic law enforcement to entirely unsettled country.

The Battalion never numbered more than a few hundred men, divided into six companies assigned to massive geographic regions. A company was rarely forty men, commanded by a Captain. They were mounted on their own horses, armed with their own weapons (almost universally a lever-action Winchester rifle and a heavy revolver), un-uniformed, and dismally paid.

The Rangers operated aggressively and entirely informally. They pursued fugitives into the brush, engaged them violently, and arrested them or killed them. The Rangers' reputation for uncompromising brutality on the border—specifically in their treatment of Tejano populations and their repeated, unauthorized military incursions across the Rio Grande into Mexico (such as Captain Leander McNelly’s infamous 1875 Las Cuevas raid)—is thoroughly documented history.

_(For campaign writers: While the classic image is entirely Anglo, numerous Tejano men actively served as Texas Rangers during this exact period, operating as scouts, interpreters, and full privates—a much more complex historical reality than usually portrayed)._

## 6. The Corrido

The border tells its own history through the _corrido_—the Mexican folk ballad. A _corrido_ is an acoustic, guitar-driven narrative ballad, sung in strict octosyllabic quatrains. It recorded the events the Anglo newspapers ignored or distorted: the famous killings, the flooding of the Rio Grande, and the careers of the men who defied the _estado anglo_.

A standard _corrido_ opens with a formal salute to the men in the bar (_“Voy a cantar esta historia, señores, escuchen bien”_—“I am going to sing this story, gentlemen, listen well”), establishes the protagonist and the date, drives through the narrative action using remembered dialogue, delivers a moral commentary, and closes with a formal farewell.

For the people living along the river, the _corrido_ was the sole historical record. A character hearing a _corrido_ sung in a border cantina detailing the death of his own father, or the burning of his own town, is engaging directly with the living memory of his people.

## 7. The Social Bandit

The border outlaw was not a simple highwayman. He operated along the razor-thin boundary dividing petty criminality from armed political resistance against the Anglo establishment. The historian Eric Hobsbawm defined the _social bandit_—the outlaw actively supported by the rural poor, who robs the powerful, shares the proceeds with his community, and embodies a direct resistance to a hostile foreign institution.

Not every cattle rustler was a social bandit, but the successful border figure consistently fit this pattern. A Mexican rustler who drove cattle exclusively from American cattle barons south across the river, who never robbed a Mexican _campesino_, and who rode through massive _Tejano_ communities unharmed because the locals hid his horses and fed his men, was operating in a totally separate moral universe than Billy the Kid.

- **Juan N. Cortina:** The defining border patriot-bandit. A wealthy Brownsville rancher who led an explosive armed uprising—the "Cortina War"—in 1859, explicitly protesting the systemic U.S. legal abuses against Mexicans in south Texas. He spent decades operating a massive political-military-rustling empire entirely unbothered by borders, celebrated as a hero south of the Rio Grande, and hunted as a murderer to the north.
- **Joaquín Murrieta:** The legendary California Gold Rush outlaw, allegedly decapitated by California Rangers in 1853. Whether he was a singular historical man or an amalgamation of five different Mexican outlaws operating simultaneously, Murrieta became the foundational symbol of Hispanic resistance to American occupation.
- **Tiburcio Vásquez:** The last major Californio outlaw, finally captured and hanged in a public California execution in 1875.

## 8. The Big Bend and the Trans-Pecos

The Trans-Pecos is the massive wedge of West Texas extending west of the Pecos River toward El Paso. It is a desert basin ruptured by towering, forested mountain ranges: the Davis, the Guadalupe, and the Chisos. In the 1870s, it remained an entirely unsettled void, dominated absolutely by Apache and Comanche raiding parties sweeping down from the High Plains toward the Mexican interior. The U.S. Army countered by anchoring isolated forts (Fort Davis, Fort Stockton, Fort Quitman) strictly along the southern Overland stageline, while heavily armed Texas Rangers hunted outlaws in the deep canyons.

The Big Bend is the monstrous southward bow of the Rio Grande. In the 1870s, it was scorching in the summer, freezing in the winter, and virtually empty of human settlement save for a handful of isolated Mexican farming villages clinging to the river mud at Boquillas del Carmen and San Vicente. To reach those villages, the river carved sheer, blind limestone slot canyons—Santa Elena, Mariscal, Boquillas—hundreds of feet deep.

Because of the terrifying terrain and absolute lack of federal oversight on either side of the line, Big Bend was, and remained for a hundred years, smugglers' country. In the 1870s, it was livestock moving north and Winchesters moving south.

## 9. El Paso and Ciudad Juárez

The sprawling, combined urban unit spanning the "Pass of the North" formed the singular major western crossing on the entire U.S.-Mexico border.

The original Spanish settlement, El Paso del Norte (formally renamed Ciudad Juárez in 1888), commanded the southern bank. It was the older, established, vastly larger city, firmly anchored to a massive, centuries-old mission complex and a sprawling valley agricultural economy based on grapes and onions.

The American side, El Paso, was significantly smaller and violently rougher. Until the Southern Pacific Railroad slammed into the town from the west in 1881, El Paso was a minor, isolated cluster of adobe saloons, freight offices, and gambling hells.

In the 1870s, crossing the border simply meant walking an American horse across the shallow river ford to the Mexican side, or paying the ferryman. Customs inspections were nominal to non-existent. Men lived in El Paso, drank in Juárez, and maintained entirely localized loyalties; the boundary was merely the water in the river.

A crossing scene at El Paso–Juárez can render the mixed character of border life: the customs man's desk, the Mexican family with their possessions on a wagon, the Anglo trader checking his bill of lading, the peddler with his pack, the soldier on detached duty going to or coming from Mexico for purposes officially obscure.

## 10. The Intermarried Families

The border's social fabric was knit by intermarriage. Anglo men married Tejana, Californio, and Sonoran women throughout the period. The Anglo–Hispanic families produced significant western figures: the Pico, Vallejo, Bandini, and other Californio families that allied with Anglo immigrants; the Otero family of New Mexico; many ranching families of south Texas.

A Hispanic–Anglo family's experience is one of the border's central stories. The children of such a marriage navigated two cultures, often being neither fully accepted in the Anglo-Protestant sphere of the nineteenth-century U.S. nor fully part of the Mexican-Catholic society. Their story is the border's story.

---

## 11. The Hacienda — the Mexican Rural Estate

A hacienda was a large estate in northern Mexico, typically including cattle ranching, agriculture, and a fortified compound — the casco — housing the owner (the patrón or hacendado), his family, the resident workers (peones acasillados), the chapel, the store (tienda de raya), and the workshops.

The hacienda system was the basis of rural Mexican economy through the nineteenth century. Workers were tied to the hacienda by debt — the tienda de raya extended credit at inflated prices, ensuring the worker could never pay off his debt and could not legally leave — and by tradition. The relationship between patrón and peón was paternalistic, exploitative, and stable.

A scene at a Mexican hacienda has specific furniture: the casco's adobe walls, the central courtyard with its well, the chapel where the family worshipped, the open kitchen where the women cooked, the stables for the patrón's horses, the corrals for the cattle, the small jacales — huts — where the peones lived beyond the casco walls. The hacendado's voice is formal Spanish, the voice of educated landowners. The peón's voice is the voice of working rural Mexicans, often with regional and Native-language influences. A scene at the hacienda can render the social distance through the linguistic distance.
