# Chapter 20 — Outlaw Craft

> _"THE PISTOL HE HAD NOT YET DRAWN WAS STILL UNDER HIS COAT."_

Tales of the Old West treats the outlaw as one of its central character types. The dime novels lied. The outlaw was not a romantic figure on horseback. He was a working criminal in a specific economy with specific procedures, specific risks, and a specific life expectancy that was not long. This chapter is the corrective: the mechanics of train robbery, bank robbery, the road agent's stage hold-up, horse theft, cattle rustling, the fence, the hideout, the posse, and the discipline of writing the outlaw without glorifying him or flattening him.

For the band's internal procedure as a game system, see Chapter 11 of the corebook on outlaw bands. For the law's side, see Chapter 14 of this supplement.

---

## 1. The Economic Reality

Most outlaws made far less than the dime novels claimed. Most died young. Most went to prison. The numbers matter to the writer.

A successful train robbery in the 1870s netted between $1,000 and $5,000 for the gang. Sometimes as much as $30,000. The great exception was the 1873 Adair, Iowa robbery. The James-Younger gang took $2,300 in cash and $30,000 in bonds they could not negotiate without fences. The bonds were mostly lost. A successful bank robbery netted similar amounts. The Northfield, Minnesota raid was September 1876. The gang took $26.70 in cash because the cashier locked the vault and was killed for it. The raid cost four members shot dead in the street and three captured. A stage robbery netted $50 to $500. Passengers carried personal cash. The express box carried the real money. Most stages did not carry large express on most days. A single good horse fenced for $30–$50. A herd driven to Mexico netted $20–$30 per head wholesale. A stolen steer brought $10–$20 depending on the buyer's questions. A small operation rustling fifty head a season made $500–$1,000, less feed, men, and the fence's cut.

Gang shares were not equal. The leader took a larger cut. The man who went into the express car or the vault took more than the man holding horses. Inside men took ten to twenty percent off the top. A six-man gang clearing $3,000 split $600 to the leader, $400 each to the inside men, $300 each to the hands-on robbers, $150 to the horse holder. Less than a year's wages for a cowhand in many cases. And the cowhand did not face hanging.

The Wild Bunch lived into the 1900s. Cassidy, Longabaugh, Logan, and others. The James-Younger gang lost half its members at Northfield. The Daltons lost four out of five at Coffeyville in 1892. Most named outlaws of the 1870s were dead, in prison, or in hiding by 1880. The career outlaw was a man who beat actuarial odds for as long as he beat them.

## 2. The Hideout

The outlaw camp was not a romantic canyon retreat. It was a cramped, cold, hostile space where a gang waited between jobs.

A hideout was a remote line shack, a played-out mine cabin, a brushy box canyon with one entrance, or an isolated ranch owned by a sympathizer. None were comfortable. Hideouts were chosen for remoteness. Wind cut through the walls. Snow drifted in. A fire was risky because smoke could be seen for miles. Six to ten men lived in a shack built for two. Bedrolls touched. Body smell, constant friction. Food was what men could carry in or shoot in the immediate vicinity. Beans, bacon, coffee, and whatever game had not been hunted out. After a month at a hideout the men were sick of each other and sick of the food. A few hideouts had a woman cooking and doing washing. Usually a sympathizer's wife or the leader's wife. Most did not. The lack of women is noted in every honest account. And then there was the boredom. Cards, whisky if it could be brought in, cleaning weapons, repairing tack, talking the same talk for the third week. The boredom of the hideout was the worst part of the outlaw life.

- **Hole-in-the-Wall.** Powder River country, Wyoming. A box canyon with one approach defended by a single rifleman. The Wild Bunch used it. And others. **Brown's Hole.** Later called Brown's Park. Northwest Colorado at the corner of three states. The jurisdictional confusion was its defense.
- **Robbers Roost.** Southeastern Utah, slickrock country. Almost impossible to track a man through.
- **The Indian Territory.** The whole region was a hideout for fugitives from the surrounding states until 1907. Federal jurisdiction only. Few marshals. Vast distances.

A working hideout had a lookout. The lookout sat on a high point with a glass and watched the approach for hours. The lookout was rotated. The lookout who fell asleep was the man who got the gang killed. The lookout system is the writer's friend. A scene at the lookout post is a scene of dread without action.

## 3. The Train Robbery

The classic late-1870s western crime had specific procedure.

- **Pre-robbery.** Inside information came from a railroad employee or a station agent. He told the gang which train, which date, which express car carried what. The inside man took a cut. The site was a remote stretch of track. Often a watering tank or a curve. Far from the next town. The party was five to eight men. Two on the locomotive, two at the express car, one or two guarding the passenger cars, one or two with the horses.
- **The stop.** The signal was a red lantern at night, a flag in daylight, a crosstie laid across the rails, a track obstruction. The engineer stopped because the alternative was a derailment. Two men climbed the locomotive with pistols on the engineer and fireman. The engineer was ordered to uncouple the express car from the passenger cars and pull it forward a quarter mile. This separated the messenger from rescue and from the passengers.
- **The express car.** The express messenger worked for Wells Fargo or for the railroad's express. His job was to defend the safe. Some fought. Most did not. The famous fighters were exceptions. The messenger barricaded the door from inside. The gang demanded entry. The messenger refused. The gang threatened to kill the engineer. If the messenger held out, the gang blew the door with a charge. The charge blew the door, the safe, the messenger, and a good part of the car's interior. Anything paper inside burned. Two safe types: the through safe, with combination known only to the destination clerk (the gang could not open it without explosives), and the local safe, which the messenger could open. The gang hoped for the local safe. They blew the through safe with care because the contents could be destroyed.
- **The split and scatter.** The take was split at the site or at a pre-arranged rendezvous a few miles off. Each man rode a different direction. The gang did not ride out together. A pursuit had to follow seven different trails. The leader took the largest share for himself and a second share to deliver to the inside man later.
- **The pursuit.** The express messenger or the conductor cut for the next station. A telegram went up the line. By morning every sheriff in three counties had the description. The Pinkerton Detective Agency or the railroad's own detectives took the case. They had the budget for long pursuit. The local sheriffs did not. A posse formed in the nearest town. Posses were effective for two to three days. After that they ran out of food, money, and patience and turned back.

## 4. The Bank Robbery

A bank robbery had different constraints than a train robbery. The bank was in a town. The town had citizens who could shoot. The timing was measured in minutes.

- **Pre-robbery.** Two to three men rode into town a day or two before the job. They posed as cattle buyers or drifters. They mapped the bank's layout, the street, the route in and out, the location of the marshal's office, the saloon where the men gathered. The horses were rested and watered the morning of the job. Each man's horse was tied to a hitching rail in a specific spot on the street near the bank.
- **The job.** Five to seven men. Two or three went inside. One or two stood outside the door as guards. One or two stayed mounted as horse holders and street watchers. The lead man covered the cashier and any customers. The second man went over the counter to the cash drawer and the vault. The third man watched the door. The vault was the question. A small-town bank vault might be a brick room with an iron door. A city bank had a true safe. The cashier was ordered to open it. If he refused, he was beaten or shot. A locked vault that could not be opened ended the job. The whole inside job took four to seven minutes. Longer than that and the town was alerted and the citizens had rifles.
- **The street.** The town learned of the robbery within two minutes. Usually because a citizen saw the guards at the bank door and shouted. Citizens with rifles took positions in second-floor windows and on rooftops. The gang's mounted men returned fire to keep the citizens' heads down while the men inside finished. The exit was the dangerous moment. **Northfield 1876.** The textbook disaster. The citizens of Northfield, Minnesota fired from second-floor windows. They killed two of the gang in the street and wounded several others. The gang fled without the money. Three Younger brothers were captured a few days later.

The pursuit was the same as a train robbery, with the addition that the town's citizens often joined the posse. The posse was therefore larger and angrier than the typical professional posse.

## 5. The Stage Robbery

The road agent's crime. A stage robbery brought a smaller take and smaller risk than a train, and a smaller crew. Often one or two men. Three at most. The road agent worked alone or with a partner.

The agent chose the site. A grade where the stage slowed—an uphill or a sharp curve—or a known watering stop. The road agent knew the route and the timing. He stepped from cover with a shotgun. The shotgun was the road agent's weapon of choice, the range short, the effect immediate, a threat the driver could not refuse. He stood twenty paces from the lead horses. _Throw down the box._ The express box was a wooden chest bolted to the front boot of the stage. The driver kicked it off or the messenger unbolted it and dropped it. Some stages carried an armed messenger riding shotgun. The messenger could fight, and many famous gunfights were stage messenger against road agent. The agent ordered the passengers out and lined them on the stage side. He took watches, cash, jewelry. He did not generally take women's wedding rings. Some did. The practice was considered low even among outlaws.

A variation, the box-off-the-road method, left the stage moving. The agent arranged for the box to be thrown from the stage at a set location by a confederate riding inside, and the agent collected it later. This avoided the confrontation but required an inside man.

Pursuit of a stage robbery was less keen than pursuit of a train or bank job—the take was smaller and the company's resources were less. Wells Fargo hired detectives. James B. Hume was chief detective for Wells Fargo for decades, and he pursued road agents without rest. A road agent who took a Wells Fargo box had Hume on his trail.

## 6. Horse Theft

The most common western crime by frequency. The most absolutely punished.

The horse thief came in the dark. He scouted the corral or the picket line in advance. He cut the picket rope or opened the corral gate without sound. He led the horses out by the lead rope, walking. He did not mount until well clear of the camp. Once mounted, he ran the horses hard for the first ten miles to put distance between himself and the discovery. The horses went to a known buyer who would not ask questions. Fences in border country drove the horses across the line into Mexico or up to the next territory.

The community enforced the death penalty for horse theft because a man without a horse on the frontier could die. Stealing a man's horse was attempting his life. The vigilance committee that hanged the horse thief acted on the understanding that the thief had endangered the owner's life. The cowhand caught with another man's horse received no trial in many cases. The rope was on hand. The nearest tree did the work.

## 7. Cattle Rustling

The crime built into the cattle trade. Different mechanics from horse theft.

The operator cut a few head out of a large herd's edge at night and drove them off. The cut was small enough to go unnoticed in a herd of several thousand. The stolen brand had to be altered. Skilled rustlers used a hot iron to add to the existing brand and turn it into something else. A Bar-S became a Box-S. A Lazy-K became a Lazy-KK. The art was to make the alteration look like the original. The rustled cattle were driven to a remote pasture and held until the brands healed. Then they were driven to a buyer who would not look too close—a small slaughterhouse, a corrupt rancher, a packer who paid less and asked nothing. Or, in border country, a drive across the line.

Some cattlemen built their herds through rustling. The Lincoln County and Tularosa cattle wars were disputes about who was rustling whom. The distinction between a rustler and a small rancher building a herd by appropriating mavericks (unbranded cattle) was sometimes a matter of who was making the accusation.

Cattlemen's associations hired range detectives. The detective's job was to ride the country, watch the brands, and report. He was paid by the association. He had wide latitude. Some detectives shot rustlers on sight and answered no questions later. Tom Horn was the most known. A complicated case. Convicted and hanged in 1903.

## 8. The Fence

Most outlaw operations depended on a fence—a man who bought stolen goods and moved them into legitimate markets.

- **The horse fence.** A horse trader or remount contractor who bought stolen stock and re-sold it through legitimate channels in distant markets.
- **The cattle fence.** A small slaughterhouse or packer who paid less and asked nothing.
- **The general fence.** A storekeeper, often in a border town, who bought watches, jewelry, paper money, cash. He paid ten to thirty cents on the dollar.
- **The bond fence.** A specialist who could move stolen bonds and securities. Rare and well-paid. The reason the James-Younger Adair bonds (1873) were largely lost was that the gang did not have the connections to move them.

The fence and the gang were a working relationship of years. The fence trusted the gang not to bring trouble to his door. The gang trusted the fence to pay promised amounts and not turn informant. Either could betray the other. Both knew it.

## 9. The Posse

How posses worked, versus the dime novel version.

A posse was deputized by the sheriff (or, in the Indian Territory, by a U.S. marshal). The men were local citizens who could be spared from work. The sheriff promised them per-day pay (rarely fulfilled in practice). A posse was usually six to fifteen men—local cowboys, townsmen, sometimes a tracker (often a freedman or a Native scout—the work paid). The marshal or sheriff led. Each man brought his own horse and rifle.

A posse rode hard for two days, slower for the third. By the fourth day the men were tired, hungry, and out of grain for the horses. Most posses turned back after three days because they could not be supplied in the field. The Pinkerton Agency and the Wells Fargo detectives did the long pursuit work the local posse could not. A Pinkerton man might follow a fugitive for months, across multiple states, hiring local help as needed. The fugitive who beat a posse still had the Pinkertons to deal with.

A posse that caught its man sometimes did not bring him back for trial. The lynch posse hanged him on the spot. The justification: he was caught with the goods, the trial was a foregone conclusion, the trip back was expensive. This was illegal but widely practiced and almost never prosecuted.
