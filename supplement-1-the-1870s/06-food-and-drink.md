# Chapter 6 — Food and Drink

> _“THE FIRE WAS A LOW BED OF COALS AND THE COFFEE WAS ALREADY ON. EVERYTHING ELSE THE COUNTRY HAD GIVEN US WAS ON THAT FIRE OR ABOUT TO BE.”_

Five distinct food economies operated in the 1870s West: the trail herd, the homestead, the mining camp, the Chinese cookhouse, and the army post. Each required specific iron tools, rigid scheduling, and raw physical labor to convert heavy bulk staples into calories for working men and women.

---

## 1. The Trail Cook

The Texas cattleman Charles Goodnight built the first chuckwagon in 1866. He bolted a heavy wooden cabinet—the _chuck box_—to the rear of a surplus Studebaker army wagon. The hinged lid dropped on chains to form a flat working surface. The heavy iron Dutch ovens rode in the _boot_ slung below the box. The flour, beans, and bedrolls rode in the wagon bed. A dried cowhide—the _cooney_—hung beneath the axles to catch dry cow chips or dead sagebrush for fuel.

The trail cook drew forty to sixty dollars a month, double the wages of a riding hand. He was often an older man crippled by a horse wreck. His authority at the wagon was absolute. No rider tied a horse near the wagon to stir up dust. No man stepped between the cook and the fire. No man opened the chuck box.

He woke at three in the morning, laid the fire, and boiled the coffee. He baked sourdough biscuits in the cast-iron Dutch ovens, regulating the heat by shoveling live coals onto the flanged lids. He fried thick slabs of salt pork or fresh beef from a butchered stray.

He fed the crew at dawn. The riders stepped to the lowered lid, took what he offered on tin plates, ate sitting in the dirt, scraped the plates into the wreck-pan, and rode out. The cook washed the heavy iron, packed the wagon, drove the team ten miles past the slow-moving herd, and built a new fire to cook the noon meal. He fed the crew three times a day for three solid months on the trail.

## Coosie at the Fire

> Coosie had been up since three. The fire was a low bed of cherry-red coals and the three-gallon enamel coffeepot was boiling hard. The sourdough was in the cast-iron Dutch oven. He shoveled three spades of ash and coals onto the flanged lid and left it to bake in the dark.
>
> He went to the back of the wagon and pulled down the wet slab of cured bacon. He laid out a dozen thick strips on the cold side of the iron spider-skillet. The skillet went on the coals beside the coffeepot. The bacon hissed softly in the grease.
>
> The horizon to the east was going gray. Coosie poured a tin cup of the black coffee and drank it standing beside the fire. The riders' bedrolls lay scattered around the mesquite like pale stones. They would be moving in ten minutes when he started calling.
>
> He bent down, jammed a hook under the hot bail of the oven lid, lifted it a fraction, and checked the dough. The biscuits were rising tall and browning at the edges. He set the lid back and turned the bacon strips with a long fork.
>
> The first man of the crew sat up in the gray light and reached for his hat.
>
> “Coffee,” Coosie said.

## 2. The Homestead Kitchen

The physical layout of the frontier kitchen dictated a crushing daily labor.

It was often just a single room in a dirt-floored sod house where the family cooked, ate, and slept. A second, adjacent room with a heavy workbench and meal chest existed only on established, wealthier claims. The cast-iron cookstove stood at the center, requiring constant, heavy feeding. The woman chopped kindling in the dark, built the fire up to a roar for boiling water, backed it down to a steady heat for baking heavy loaves of bread, and banked it with ashes at night. Where the timber ran out, she burned braided grass, dried buffalo chips, or hauled coal.

The kitchen burned twenty to forty gallons of water a day for boiling potatoes, scrubbing clothes, and washing children. She hauled every drop from a creek, spring, or dug well in wooden buckets.

### The Food

The homesteader pulled a sparse, heavy starch diet from the land. She fed the men corn mush, thick flour biscuits, salt-cured pork from the smokehouse, and black coffee. A heavy midday dinner featured boiled beef or pork, dense potatoes from the root cellar, and cornbread baked straight in the iron skillet. A poor harvest, a collapsed cellar, or a dry well broke the family outright.

### The Preservation

The homestead woman preserved food in absolute volume to survive the freeze. She dried apples on cloth frames in the August sun, packed salted beef into wooden barrels, and processed tomatoes into heavy glass Mason jars. She worked the dasher in the wooden churn to separate the butter from the cream of the milk cow. The kitchen was an industrial processing plant run on raw muscle.

### The Bread

The homestead kitchen demanded a daily output of heavy bread. The woman maintained a live sourdough starter in a crock, or mixed chemical baking soda (saleratus) into quick-breads, or relied on the Southern standard of cornmeal. A loaf baked twice a week, biscuits baked every morning, and cornbread stretched the evening supper. The rhythm was constant, measured in fifty-pound sacks of raw flour.

## 3. The Mining-Camp Mess

The mining camp fed men through a raw, high-inflation cash economy. The solitary prospector frying bacon in a pan vanished rapidly when the heavy machinery arrived.

### The Boarding House

The boarding house operator—usually a widow or an entrepreneurial unmarried woman—anchored the camp's logistics. She extracted five to fifteen dollars a week from thirty or forty hard-rock miners. In exchange, she provided three massive, high-calorie meals a day: beef stew, boiled potatoes, heavy beans, fresh oven bread, and dried-apple pie. Her cooks slung food onto long timber tables, and the men ate fast and in silence before changing shifts.

A competent boarding-house woman built raw financial muscle. If she served bad or thin food, the miners walked to the next house. If she fed them heavily, her business exploded.

### The Hash House and the Boom

A chop house in an established camp like Virginia City charged fifty cents to a dollar for a single meal—five times the price in an eastern city. In the first chaotic months of a raw strike, freight charges spiked the cost of basic survival to absurd extremes. Eggs carried over the passes by mule fetched a dollar apiece. Butter cost two dollars a pound. A standard plate of hash and beans ran two dollars.

### The Miner's Bucket

The man working ten hours at the rock face hauled his lunch down the cage in a tin bucket: cold hardboiled eggs, a thick meat sandwich, and an apple. The Cornish hard-rock miners (the "Cousin Jacks") imported the _pasty_—a heavy meat, potato, and onion pie baked in a dense crust that a man could hold in his dirt-caked hand. By 1875, the pasty fed men from Wales, Ireland, and Missouri indiscriminately.

## 4. The Chinese Cookhouse

The Chinese immigrants who hammered the rail grades and washed the gold tailings imported a distinct food culture that operated parallel to the Anglo beef-and-bean economy.

The Chinese cook was a fixture in western hotels, wealthy ranch houses, and major mining messes. He drew lower wages than his white counterpart, worked brutally long hours, and flawlessly executed the heavy Anglo demands of biscuits, steaks, and boiled coffee.

When he cooked for the tight Chinese quarter in a place like Sacramento or Deadwood, the reality shifted. Most 1870s Chinese men hailed from the Pearl River delta of Guangdong province. The Chinese cookhouse imported dried mushrooms, salted fish, and heavy soy sauce through complex San Francisco merchant networks, combining them with rice and thin-sliced pork or chicken.

The accompanying agricultural apparatus was the Chinese truck garden. A Chinese gardener trenched the soil outside an Anglo town, hauled water constantly, and raised fresh greens, melons, and root vegetables that he sold from a horse-cart directly into the town's kitchens. The Chinese gardener was frequently the only reliable source of fresh vegetables in a hundred-mile radius.

## The Bread at Three O'Clock

> At three in the afternoon, the cook let the fire burn down to a flat bed of ash. He set the heavy cast-iron Dutch oven in the coals and shoveled three heavy scoops of gray ash onto the flanged lid to bring the heat even. He dragged the wooden keg of sourdough starter from the shadow of the wagon and ladled a pint of it into a wooden bowl. He dumped in hard white flour, a fistful of salt, a heavy thumb of rendered lard, and a tin cup of spring water. He beat it with the heel of his right hand until it went stiff and pale.
>
> He pinched off pieces the size of duck eggs, dropped them into the hot bacon grease smoking in the bottom of the Dutch oven, and jammed the lid shut.
>
> He turned to the keg and repaired the mother: a pint of flour, a pint of water, and half a teaspoon of salt. He hammered the wooden lid down and wrapped the keg in a piece of dense canvas to hold the heat for the night.
>
> At three-thirty, the lid of the Dutch oven rose an inch as the sour gas swelled the dough in the heat.

## 5. The Army Ration

The Quartermaster Department operated an entirely rigid logistical system (see Chapter 19). The enlisted soldier consumed a massive daily ration of salt-cured pork or tough beef, hard white flour or yellow cornmeal, dried navy beans, issue coffee, and issue sugar. In the field, the standard compacted to dense flour hardtack, salt pork, and coffee. Coffee drove the field army.

## 6. The Hotel and the Urban Dining Room

The western hotel mirrored eastern mechanics in the heavy rail towns.

### The Harvey House

The Fred Harvey Company built a highly systematized food empire along the Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railway starting in 1876. The Harvey House delivered an exact standard at the edge of the desert. White linen tablecloths, heavy silver, heavy china, and a four-course dinner executed in twenty-five minutes while the heavy steam engines took on water. The waitresses—the Harvey Girls—were single women contracted from the East, housed in locked dormitories under a severe matron, and fired immediately for marriage or misconduct.

### The Hotel Dining Room

The Palace Hotel in San Francisco opened in 1875 serving fresh raw oysters, prime beef, iced French pastries, and imported French wines on heavy silver platters. In a middle-class hotel in Denver or Cheyenne, a man handed over one dollar, sat at a long timber table, and ate the fixed fare carried by sweating waiters: heavy stews, dense vegetables, and baked pie.

### The Free Lunch

The aggressive western saloon ran a heavy loss-leader to force liquor sales. A man buying a ten-cent glass of raw whiskey at noon could eat a massive spread of salty cold cuts, hard cheese, sharp pickles, and hot beef chowder left uncovered on a side table. The salt drove his thirst. The working mechanic ate his noon meal at the bar.

## 7. Heavy Staples of the Country

- **Coffee.** The absolute fuel of the West. Men bought green coffee beans in heavy burlap sacks, roasted them dry in an iron skillet over an open fire, crushed them with a rock or the butt of a revolver, and boiled them straight in unlined tin pots. The man drank it scalding hot, thick with grounds, and cut with black molasses.
- **Sourdough.** A heavy, live yeast culture maintained in a sealed crock and fed daily with raw flour and creek water. It lifted the heavy bread, the biscuits, and the camp flapjacks. A good starter lasted twenty years and possessed the distinct tang of the specific air it pulled yeast from.
- **Pemmican.** Massive slabs of raw buffalo or deer meat laid in the sun until brittle, pounded into heavy powder between two stones, and mixed with rendered fat and dried chokecherries. It poured into skin bags and kept for a decade without spoiling. (See Chapter 2.)
- **Dried Fruit.** Slices of tart apples, heavy peaches, and stringy apricots dried black in the sun on cloth racks. A woman soaked them for twelve hours in creek water to bake the heavy winter cobblers. Frieghters hauled dried fruit from Utah and Oregon in five-hundred-pound wagon loads.
- **Mush.** Coarse yellow cornmeal boiled down in a kettle of salted water until thick. A man ate it hot at breakfast with heavy molasses, or sliced it cold and fried it in hot pork grease at the noon halt.
- **Side Meat (Salt Pork).** The heavy abdominal fat of a slaughtered hog, soaked in a heavy salt brine and packed tight in a wooden barrel. Sliced thin and dropped in a hot skillet, it yielded the universal cooking grease of the frontier.
- **Hardtack.** The dense army cracker. A square brick of flour and water baked twice to eliminate every ounce of vapor. Extremely durable, routinely infested with small weevils. A man forced to eat it soaked it in boiling coffee or smashed it to powder with his rifle butt to thicken a thin stew.
- **Fresh Meat.** _Beef_ fed the trails and the ranches. _Pork_ fed the dirt farmers. _Game_ kept men alive in the timber. A man with a rifle dropped mule deer, pronghorn antelope, elk, and the rapidly vanishing buffalo on the plains; he took wild turkey, prairie chicken, and heavy sage grouse from the brush; he pulled trout and heavy catfish from the fast rivers. When he missed, he went back to salt pork.
