# Chapter 6 — Food and Drink

> _“THE FIRE WAS A LOW BED OF COALS AND THE COFFEE WAS ALREADY ON. EVERYTHING ELSE THE COUNTRY HAD GIVEN US WAS ON THAT FIRE OR ABOUT TO BE.”_

The popular West has rendered food as a recurring gag — beans, beans, beans — or as a cinematic shorthand: coffee on a tin cup beside a fire. The historical record is richer. Five distinct food cultures operated in the West, each with its own ingredients, techniques, vocabulary, and meanings: the trail cook, the homestead kitchen, the mining-camp mess, the Chinese cookhouse, and the army ration. This chapter documents each.

The scene that renders food well renders the day. A meal is a chapter's anchor. Get the meal right and the country comes through it.

---

## 1. The Trail Cook — the Wagon and the Herd

The dominant western food image is the trail-drive chuckwagon. The reality was specific.

### The chuckwagon

Invented or popularized by Charles Goodnight, the Texas cattleman, about 1866, the chuckwagon was a modified army-surplus wagon with a cabinet — the _chuck box_ — at the back. The chuck box had drawers and shelves and a hinged lid that lowered to form a work surface.

Below the chuck box was the _boot,_ holding the heavier items: the Dutch ovens, the pots, the coffeepot. Above the chuck box, in the wagon bed, were the cook's tools, the staple foods (flour, beans, salt pork, dried apples), and the bedrolls of the men. Slung beneath the wagon was the _cooney_ or _possum belly_ — a stretched cowhide carrying firewood and kindling. At the side was the water barrel.

### The cook (Cookie, Coosie, the Old Man)

The trail cook was a specific figure on the drive. He was paid more than the cowhands — about forty to sixty dollars a month versus thirty for a hand. He was often older than the hands, sometimes a former hand who could no longer ride hard. He was respected and feared. The crew did not cross him because he controlled their food.

The cook woke at three or four in the morning, started the fire, made the coffee, made the breakfast of biscuits and salt pork or beef. He fed the crew, packed the wagon, drove the wagon ahead of the herd to the next camp, set up there, started the noon meal (often just coffee and the morning's leftovers), then prepared the evening meal proper.

His authority extended to small matters: the wagon was his domain, no one slept in it without permission, no one helped himself from the chuck box. The cook decided when supper was ready and the crew came when called.

### The Dutch oven

The Dutch oven was the trail cook's principal appliance: a heavy cast-iron pot with a flat lid flanged to hold coals on top. Set in the coals of a fire with coals also on the lid, it baked. Biscuits, cobblers, beans, stew, fresh bread — all came out of the Dutch oven.

A skilled trail cook could produce, in a single night, a meal that would have been respectable in a small-town hotel. The bias of the literature against trail food (just beans and coffee) underrates what the good cook could do.

### The food

A typical trail-drive meal began with coffee — always — strong and boiled. Sourdough biscuits came from a sourdough starter the cook kept and protected; the biscuits were the bread of the drive. A good cook's biscuits were tall, brown, and tender; a bad cook's were flat, pale, and chewed. Salt pork came from the wagon's supply; beef from the herd, killed every few days, often a stray or a _limpy_ that could not keep up. Beans — pinto or red — were soaked overnight and cooked all day with salt pork in a Dutch oven. Dried fruit pie or cobbler made of dried apples or peaches was the crew's treat. Sometimes there was fresh game — a deer or antelope shot during the day, butchered and cooked. Sometimes fresh vegetables — wild onions, mushrooms, watercress, picked along the way and added to the stew.

A trail cook's meal was not haute cuisine but it was substantial and it had to feed working men. The description of the meal can render the country it crossed: where the wagon found water, what game was available, what kind of wood for the fire.

### Chuckwagon etiquette

Specific protocols of the trail mess. The wagon faced forward in camp; the lowered lid of the chuck box faced the fire. The cook called out the meal: _Come and get it._ The crew lined up in no particular order, took what was offered. Plates and cups were tin, not china. The senior man — the trail boss — ate first or ate apart, depending on the outfit. After eating, each man scraped his plate into the wreck pan (the dishwashing tub) and stacked his plate beside it. No man rode his horse close to the wagon (dust in the food). No man passed between the cook and the fire.

## The Trail Breakfast

> Coosie had been up since three. The fire was a low bed of coals and the coffee was already on. The sourdough was in the Dutch oven and the lid had its coals piled on it and would have its biscuits done in fifteen minutes more.
>
> He went to the back of the wagon and took down the bacon and laid out a dozen strips on the cold side of the iron skillet. The skillet went on the coals beside the coffeepot. The bacon began to talk in a small voice.
>
> The horizon to the east was paling. Coosie poured himself a cup of the coffee and drank it standing beside the fire. The riders' bedrolls were scattered around the camp like dark stones. They would be moving in ten minutes when he started calling.
>
> He bent down and lifted the lid of the Dutch oven a quarter-inch and looked. The biscuits were rising and beginning to brown. He set the lid back and turned the bacon.
>
> The first man of the crew stirred and sat up and reached for his hat.
>
> “Coffee,” Coosie said, in the dawn quiet.

## 2. The Homestead Kitchen — the Woman's Domain

The homestead or farm kitchen was the woman's working space and represented an enormous daily labor.

### The room

A homestead kitchen in the 1870s might be a single room in a soddy or a log cabin — the whole house, where the cooking, eating, and sleeping happened together. Or a separate kitchen in a frame house — a second room with a stove, a worktable, a dish dresser, a meal chest, and a door to the dining room. Or, in better-established places, a summer kitchen — a separate small building used in hot months to keep the heat out of the main house.

### The stove

The cookstove was the kitchen's center. By the 1870s the cast-iron range, with multiple burners on top, a firebox, an oven, and often a water reservoir, was the standard in established homes. The poorer homestead might have only a sheet-metal stove or a fireplace with hooks.

The stove had to be tended constantly. The woman laid the fire (kindling at dawn), let it burn down to coals for baking, fed it small wood for boiling, banked it at night. The wood supply was a major concern. On the treeless plains the woman burned buffalo chips, twisted hay, or coal where it could be got.

### The water

The homestead kitchen needed water for cooking, washing dishes, washing clothes, washing the family. The water came from a well (the lucky), a spring (the luckier), a creek (a hike), or a barrel filled by hauling (the unlucky). A typical homestead required twenty to forty gallons of water a day. Hauling water was often the children's chore.

### The food

A homestead's food came from the garden (vegetables in season, preserves and dried produce out of season), the smokehouse (cured pork), the henhouse (eggs and an occasional chicken), the milk cow (milk, cream, butter, buttermilk), the field (wheat or corn ground at the local mill), and the storeroom (bought staples: flour, sugar, coffee, salt).

A typical day's food. **Breakfast:** mush (cornmeal cooked in milk or water), or biscuits and gravy, or pancakes; coffee; sometimes eggs; sometimes side meat. **Dinner**, the midday and main meal: stew or boiled meat; potatoes or other vegetable; bread or biscuits; sometimes a pie or pudding. **Supper:** the day's leftovers, perhaps stretched with cornbread or biscuits.

### The preservation

A homestead woman's year was organized around preserving food for winter. _Smoking_ — pork hung in the smokehouse with a low smoldering fire of hickory or apple wood: hams, bacon, sausage. _Salting_ — pork and beef packed in barrels with salt and brine. _Drying_ — apples and peaches sliced and dried in the sun, beans dried on the vine, beef jerked, sweet corn dried on a board. _Canning_ — by the 1870s the Mason jar (1858) and pressure-cooker techniques were available to the better-off homestead: tomatoes, beans, preserves, pickles. _Cellar storage_ — root vegetables (potatoes, turnips, carrots, beets), apples, cabbage in a cool dry cellar.

The provisioning of the family for the year was a major intellectual and physical labor. A failed harvest, a damaged cellar, a barn fire could threaten the family's survival.

### The bread

The homestead kitchen's daily bread was usually one of these. _Salt-rising bread_ — risen with a starter of cornmeal and warm water, used in households without yeast. _Sourdough bread_ — risen with a kept sourdough starter. _Saleratus or baking-soda bread_ — a quick bread risen with chemical leavening (baking soda was common from the 1860s; baking powder was emerging). _Cornbread_ — cornmeal, salt, water (or milk), and perhaps eggs, baked in a skillet or in the oven. The Southerners' standard.

A loaf of bread baked twice a week, biscuits made daily, cornbread for the supper if the morning's biscuits were eaten — the rhythm was constant.

### The woman's working day

The homestead woman's day was longer than the man's. She rose first to start the fire and the breakfast, worked through the morning at washing or churning or sewing or canning, prepared the noon dinner, worked through the afternoon at more of the same, prepared supper, cleaned up, mended in the evening, and went to bed last. The work was constant and physically heavy. A woman's lifespan in the homestead conditions was significantly shorter than a man's; childbirth, overwork, and the absence of medical care wore the women down. (For the deeper rendering of the woman's experience, see Chapter 5.)

## 3. The Mining-Camp Mess

The mining camp's food was distinctive: a cash economy where men paid for prepared meals, often at extraordinarily inflated prices in the boomtown phase.

### The boarding house

A mining camp's principal food institution was the boarding house. Run by a woman — a widow, a married woman whose husband worked the mines, an entrepreneurial single woman — the boarding house fed twenty or fifty men two or three meals a day at a fixed weekly rate, typically five to fifteen dollars a week in the 1870s.

The boarding-house meals were substantial. Miners worked twelve-hour shifts and ate big. A typical supper was beef stew or roast, boiled potatoes, beans, bread (from the boarding house's own oven), pie or pudding, coffee, and sometimes vegetables — canned tomatoes, dried beans, occasional fresh in season.

The boarding-house woman was often the mining camp's most respected woman. Her reputation depended on the quality and quantity of her food. A woman who could feed thirty miners well grew her business; a woman who served bad food lost it.

### The chop house and restaurant

In larger camps and in established mining towns, a chop house or restaurant offered meals to non-boarders. A chop-house meal in a 1870s mining town might cost fifty cents to a dollar (compare to ten cents for a meal in a city).

### The booming food prices

In the early days of a strike, food prices ran wild. At Virginia City, Nevada (the Comstock), in the 1860s and 1870s, eggs sold for a dollar apiece, butter for two dollars a pound, fresh fruit (when available) for fifty cents an apple. A meal in a hash house might cost two dollars. The freight charges to bring food into a remote camp drove the prices.

Established camps with railroad or stage access ate better and cheaper. A mature mining town — Leadville by 1880, Virginia City by 1875 — had multiple restaurants, a hotel dining room, and produce delivered weekly.

### The miner's lunch

The man underground carried his lunch in a tin bucket: bread-and-meat sandwich, hardboiled eggs, fruit, a pie. The _pasty_ — a meat-and-vegetable hand pie of Cornish origin — became identified with mining camps because so many of the early hard-rock miners were Cornishmen who brought the pasty tradition. By the 1870s the pasty was eaten by miners of all backgrounds in many camps.

A mining-camp dining-room scene can render the camp's economy and demographics. The men eating — miners in their work clothes, mine engineers in suits, freighters passing through, gamblers, women of the town — make the camp visible at a single table.

## 4. The Chinese Cookhouse

Chinese workers came to the West in the 1850s (California gold rush) and through the 1860s and 1870s in numbers (railroad construction, mining camps, agricultural labor). They brought a food tradition that operated alongside but separate from the dominant Anglo and Hispanic food cultures.

### The Chinese cook

Many western kitchens were staffed by Chinese cooks: hotel kitchens, ranch kitchens, mining-camp boarding houses. A Chinese cook was reputed to be skilled, hard-working, and reliable. The wages were lower than a comparable white cook would command, which is why many establishments hired Chinese.

The Chinese cook in an Anglo establishment cooked Anglo food — beef, biscuits, beans, coffee. His own food, when he had time and ingredients, was Chinese.

### The Chinese restaurant and cookhouse

In larger towns with Chinese communities — San Francisco, Sacramento, Virginia City, the larger mining towns — Chinese restaurants and cookhouses served the Chinese community and an increasing Anglo clientele.

The food was Cantonese; most Chinese immigrants in the period came from the Pearl River delta region of Guangdong province. Rice was the staple. Stir-fried dishes (vegetables, pork, chicken, beef, fish in season). Noodles. Soups. Tea. The cooking used ingredients imported from China — soy sauce, dried mushrooms, dried fish, specific vegetables — supplied by Chinese-American merchant networks.

Chop suey, which became identified with American Chinese restaurants in the late nineteenth century, was emerging in the period. Its origins are disputed; the dish was widely available in California by the 1880s.

### The Chinese laundry and the Chinese garden

Adjacent to the cookhouse: the Chinese laundry was a near-universal western institution by the 1870s, and the Chinese vegetable garden supplied the Chinese restaurants and often the surrounding community with fresh vegetables that nobody else was growing. The truck garden of Chinese gardeners outside many California towns was a significant agricultural enterprise.

### The character

The Chinese character in western fiction has been written badly more often than any character except the Native. The dime-novel _John Chinaman_ was a caricature. The honest Chinese character is a man — or woman, less commonly written — of a specific village in a specific province with a specific dialect, who came west for specific reasons (often family remittance, debt repayment, escape from war or famine in China), with specific skills, and with a specific relationship to the surrounding Anglo population and to the Chinese community he is part of.

A few sources: Iris Chang, _The Chinese in America_; Sucheng Chan, _This Bittersweet Soil_; Mark Twain on the Chinese in _Roughing It_ (sympathetic but limited).

## Biscuit at Three O'Clock

> Three o'clock and the cook had the fire down to a bed of coals. He set the Dutch oven in the coals and shoveled coals onto the lid. He took the keg of starter from where it had ridden warm against the chuck box and ladled a cup of it into the bowl. He poured in flour, salt, lard, water; worked it up with the heel of his hand until it was a stiff dough. He pinched off pieces the size of a hen's egg and laid them in the oven on top of one another in the bacon grease.
>
> The lid went back. He fed the keg of starter back: a cup of flour, a cup of water, the spoon. He covered the keg and set it in the chuck box where the warmth would keep it alive through the night.
>
> The biscuit would be twenty minutes. He set the coffee pot to one side of the fire and threw in three handfuls of Arbuckles' ground in the sack. The water was already hot. He let it come to a boil and stay there a minute and threw a dipper of cold water in to settle the grounds.
>
> He stood up. The horizon was red along the line where the sun had gone. The herd was bedded a quarter mile off and he could hear the hand on first watch riding the perimeter singing low. He could not make out the song.
>
> “Come and get it,” he said toward the camp. “Bread is coming up.”

## 5. The Army Ration

The army ration was its own food culture, treated in detail in Chapter 19. Brief reference: the standard ration — salt pork or beef, flour or cornmeal, beans or rice, coffee, sugar, salt. The field ration — hardtack, salt pork, coffee. Coffee as the army's fuel. The soldier-cook detail and its variable competence.

## 6. The Hotel and the Urban Dining Room

In the larger western towns, the better hotels offered dining rooms that compared to Eastern hotels.

### The Harvey House

The Fred Harvey Company began operating its dining rooms along the Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railway in 1876. The Harvey Houses standardized a high level of food service at railroad stops: linen tablecloths, china, silver, trained Harvey Girls (the famous waitresses recruited from the East), and four-course meals served in the twenty-five minutes a train stopped.

The Harvey House was an institution unique to the period. The Harvey Girls — single young women from the East, hired on contract, housed in supervised dormitories — were a significant labor migration. A scene in a Harvey House dining room is a scene in a specific institution that the audience can recognize.

### The hotel dining room

A first-class hotel in San Francisco, Denver, or Cheyenne in the 1870s offered a multi-course French or American menu of considerable sophistication. The Palace Hotel of San Francisco (opened 1875) served oysters, fresh seafood, prime beef, French pastries, and a wine list of imported French and California wines.

The middle-class hotel dining room in a smaller town offered a fixed menu — one meal at one price — served at long tables in two or three sittings, with the food carried on platters by waiters and served by the diners themselves. A typical fixed menu: soup, beef or chicken, two or three vegetables, bread, pie, coffee.

### The free lunch

Many saloons offered a free lunch with the purchase of a drink: cold cuts, cheese, bread, pickles, sometimes hot stew or chowder. The free lunch was loss-leader marketing — the saloon hoped the man would drink more after eating salty food. Many a working man's noon meal was the saloon's free lunch.

## 7. Specific Foods Worth Knowing

- **Coffee.** The West's universal beverage. Drunk by everyone, all day. Bought green (unroasted), roasted at home or at the camp, ground (often crushed in a sock with a stone if no grinder available), brewed strong by boiling. The grounds were left in the pot or strained through cheesecloth. Sweetened with sugar or molasses if available; black if not. A cup at every halt, every meal, every conversation.
- **Sourdough.** A flour-and-water mixture left to ferment with wild yeasts. The starter was kept and refreshed; a good starter could be decades old. Used for bread, biscuits, pancakes. Every trail cook, every Alaskan prospector (_sourdough_ became a term for the veteran prospector), and every careful homestead woman kept a sourdough.
- **Pemmican.** Treated in Chapter 2. Adopted by some Anglo trappers and traders. Compact, portable, kept for years.
- **Dried fruit.** Apples, peaches, apricots, plums dried in the sun. Reconstituted in water for pies, cobblers, compotes. The standard winter fruit. Dried fruit was traded by the wagonload between regions: Utah fruit traveled to mining camps, Oregon fruit traveled south.
- **Mush.** Cornmeal cooked in water or milk to a porridge. Eaten hot at breakfast. Leftover mush sliced and fried for a later meal. The poor man's daily bread.
- **Side meat (salt pork).** Pork belly cured in salt brine, packed in barrels. Kept for months. Sliced and fried, the grease used for cooking. The trail-drive and homestead fat source.
- **Hardtack.** The army cracker. Flour, water, salt, baked dry, dried further. Lasted indefinitely if kept dry. Eaten softened in coffee, fried in pork grease, or crumbled into stew. Often weevily after long storage; the soldiers tapped them on the table to shake out the weevils before eating.
- **Fresh meat sources.** _Beef_ — common where available; trail-drive crews ate beef from the herd, ranches ate beef regularly, towns had butcher shops. _Pork_ — common in farming areas; salt pork from the smokehouse year-round, fresh pork at hog-killing time in fall. _Game_ — deer, antelope, elk where available; buffalo where the herds remained (declining rapidly through the 1870s); wild turkey in the southern plains and Texas; prairie chicken on the plains; sage hen in the Great Basin. _Fish_ — trout in mountain streams, catfish in the Mississippi-Missouri-Arkansas-Red drainages, salmon in the Pacific Northwest. Less universal than meat, but present.
