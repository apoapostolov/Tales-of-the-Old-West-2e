# Chapter 14 — Towns, Economy, and Law

> _"FRONT STREET IN DODGE WAS FIVE HUNDRED YARDS OF SALOON AND DANCE HALL AND CRIB. THE DEADLINE RAN DOWN THE TRACKS AND ARMED MEN DID NOT CROSS IT TO THE NORTH SIDE. THIS WAS LAW IN A CATTLE TOWN."_

The frontier town is the second setting of the West, after the open country. Get the geography of a town right — what it has, who runs what, where the law sits and where it does not — and a town scene grounds itself. This chapter covers the cattle town, the mining camp, the railroad town, the army-post town, the county seat, and the Mexican town. It covers money, prices, businesses, lawmen, courts, and how violence ended, legally and otherwise.

---

## 1. The Town Types

### The cattle town

Abilene 1867 to 1871. Then Ellsworth, Newton, Wichita, Dodge City, Caldwell — the Kansas trailhead towns followed each other as the railroad pushed west and as quarantine lines pushed the cattle trade from the ranges. Population ran five hundred to two thousand permanent, swelling to five thousand and more in shipping season (June through October).

The layout: Front Street, the main street, faced the railroad, with the saloons and the gambling halls and the dance halls. A red-light district sat across the tracks. In Dodge it was the _south side,_ across the _deadline_ — a line at the railroad track that armed men were not to cross to the north side. The cattle town died after the cattle trade moved on. Many became farm towns; many vanished.

### The mining camp

Virginia City, Nevada (Comstock silver, 1859 onward). Leadville, Colorado (silver and lead, 1877). Cripple Creek, Colorado (gold, 1891). Tombstone, Arizona (silver, 1877). Deadwood, South Dakota (gold, 1875). Bodie, California (gold, 1859). Butte, Montana (copper, 1880s). Population ran from a few hundred to twenty-five thousand (Leadville in 1880).

The town climbed the slope above the mine. Saloons and cribs and gambling halls outnumbered every other building. A red-light district sat in every camp. A church stood if one existed. Schools came late or not at all. Wages ran high — a hardrock miner in Leadville made three to four dollars a day, triple a cowhand. Prices ran high; mules packed everything in before the railroad reached the camp. Money came and went with the metal's price.

### The railroad town

The terminus or the divisional point. Cheyenne, Laramie, North Platte, Ogallala. The railroad founded many. All depended on it. The layout ran parallel to the tracks: the depot, the section house, the roundhouse, the company hotel. Railroad workers, freight handlers, drovers, and the trade that followed filled the town.

### The army-post town

Hog ranches and sutler's stores grew up just outside Army posts, off the reservation, where the post commander could not enforce regulation. Whiskey and women for the soldiers. Often violent. The post itself: officers' quarters, enlisted barracks, parade ground, sutler's store, headquarters, magazine, guardhouse.

### The county seat

The town that had the courthouse. Often the largest town in the county. Lawyers, judges, the land office, the bank, the newspaper, the courthouse square. Stable. The cattle towns and mining camps came and went; the county seats endured.

### The Mexican town

Older than any Anglo town in the Southwest. A plaza in the center, a church on one side, a government building on another. Adobe construction. Markets in the plaza. Bilingual through the Anglo period. Santa Fe, Las Vegas in New Mexico, Tucson, Mesilla — each absorbed an Anglo overlay after the Mexican-American War and the Gadsden Purchase.

## 2. The Buildings, Building by Building

### The saloon

The dominant business. A cattle town of fifteen hundred might have twenty saloons. Dodge City in 1878 had nineteen licensed. The room ran on a long bar of milled wood with a brass rail at the foot, a mirror behind the bar, bottles on the back wall, a painting of a reclining nude (the _fancy painting_) above the bar. Tables along the walls and in the back. A faro layout, a chuck-a-luck wheel, a poker table. Sawdust on the floor, swept and replaced daily. Spittoons at intervals.

Drinks: beer (when ice and breweries permitted) at five cents the schooner. Whiskey at twenty-five cents the shot in the cattle towns, ten in the rougher places. Champagne and imported wines in the parlor saloons of the towns with money — Tombstone's Crystal Palace, Denver's Windsor Hotel bar.

The saloon was the meeting hall, the post office (mail held behind the bar), the unofficial courthouse, the male parlor. It was open all hours.

### The brothel and its hierarchy

The trade ran a clear hierarchy.

- **The parlor house** stood at the top. A two-story Victorian house with a parlor, a piano, a madame, six to twelve women. A gentleman caller bought drinks (champagne five dollars the bottle), spent time in the parlor, and went upstairs. Five to twenty dollars the visit. The madame paid the women well. They lived without the misery the rest of the trade knew.
- **The dance hall** charged customers ten to twenty-five cents the dance, after which the customer bought the woman a drink (the bar cut her in on the drink). Many dance-hall women prostituted as well.
- **The saloon girl** worked the floor of a saloon, pushing drinks. Often a prostitute as well. Worked on commission from the bar.
- **The crib** was a single-room shack, often in a row of identical cribs along a back alley — Dodge's Tin Can Alley, Leadville's Stillborn Alley. The lowest rung. The woman might see twenty or thirty men a night at fifty cents to a dollar. Disease, addiction, violence, and suicide ended them. Most crib girls were dead or gone by thirty.
- **The hog ranch** was a roadhouse-brothel outside an Army post. Whiskey, gambling, women. Famously violent.
- **The married-out** was a prostitute who married a customer or a saloon man and left the trade. A path out for the lucky.

The reformer distorted the trade as uniformly degraded; the celebrant distorted it the other way as _soiled doves with hearts of gold_. The real population ran from highly paid madames to children dying of syphilis in back alleys. Write any specific woman by her position. Do not generalize.

### The bank

A wood-frame or brick building. A small lobby with a teller's window. A back office. A safe — or a vault where money sat. Banks ran small; a county-seat bank might have twenty thousand dollars in deposits. A five-thousand-dollar robbery made a major event. Banks did not fall often to robbery — the James-Younger gang's Northfield, Minnesota raid in 1876 ended in disaster (the townspeople shot the gang to pieces) and showed the danger. More money was taken from trains and from stagecoaches.

### The mercantile / general store

The center of trade for everything not gun, drink, or woman. Flour, sugar, salt, coffee, beans, salt pork, canned goods (Underwood deviled ham, Borden's condensed milk, canned peaches), bolts of cloth, ready-made clothes, boots, hats, hardware, harness, saddles, ammunition, tobacco, patent medicine, books, newspapers, school slates, hair tonic, lamp chimneys, kerosene. Often the post office. Often the unofficial bank — the merchant carried accounts on the books for the local ranchers (paid up at shipping time) and paid out cash from the till. A long counter. Glass cases for small valuable goods. Barrels of crackers and pickles. Hams hung from the ceiling. The smell of coffee, leather, kerosene, and tobacco.

### The hotel — three classes

The boarding house: a room in a frame building, twenty-five cents to a dollar a night, meals extra. The hotel: a two-story frame building, one to three dollars a night with meals. The grand hotel — the Driskill in Austin, the Brown Palace in Denver, the Crystal Palace in Tombstone — three to ten dollars a night, with electric lights and indoor plumbing in the rooms after the 1880s.

### Stables, smith, paper, church, school, court, jail, and undertaker

The boarding stable held the traveler's horse for fifty cents to a dollar a day for hay, oats, and a stall. The town's blacksmith often worked at or next to the livery. The smith had the open-front building with the forge, the anvil, the bellows; he shod horses, made and mended hardware, mended wagons. He charged a dollar to shoe a horse all around. Prosperity and respect came to him.

The newspaper came out weekly, often four pages. Editorials, local gossip, telegraph news from the East, advertisements, legal notices. The editor mattered and took bullets for it. The press was an iron Washington hand-press.

A church in any decent town. The Methodist circuit rider preceded permanent churches. Catholics where Mexicans or Irish made the population. Episcopalians, Presbyterians, Baptists. The rougher cattle towns got churches and preachers late if at all.

A one-room schoolhouse with eight grades together. The schoolmarm — often a single Eastern woman who came west on contract — took poor pay and lived watched. The schoolhouse held church, meetings, and voting.

The courthouse stood in the county seat — brick or stone if the county was rich, frame if not, with the courtroom on the upper floor and the offices below, the town square or plaza around it. The jail was one-story brick or stone in a rich county; a log or frame building in a poor one. Two to six cells. An iron bunk and a slop bucket. Often no separate cell for women, who stayed in the jailer's house under guard.

The undertaker was usually the town carpenter. Photographers posed the dead in their coffins and took pictures.

### The other businesses

A doctor (often above the drugstore). A lawyer (often above the bank). A drugstore (patent medicine, tobacco, soda fountain in the towns with money). A telegraph office (in any town on the railroad). An express office (Wells Fargo, American Express). A railroad depot. A land office (in any settlement zone). A barber shop (haircut twenty-five cents, shave a dime, bath twenty-five cents). Restaurants, chop houses, and oyster houses (yes — canned and later iced oysters reached the West, where they were a delicacy). A Chinese laundry in any town with a Chinese community. Tinkers, harness makers, gunsmiths, jewelers, booksellers.

## 4. Law on the Frontier — Who Did What

### The federal layer

- **The deputy U.S. marshal** held federal authority over federal cases — mail robbery, land disputes, crimes in federal territories, crimes in Indian Territory. The respected lawmen of the frontier. Bass Reeves, Heck Thomas, Bill Tilghman, Chris Madsen — the _Three Guardsmen_ of Indian Territory — worked as deputy U.S. marshals. They took pay by the arrest, mileage, and return.
- **The U.S. marshal** held the administrative post, appointed by the President, ran the deputies, kept the courthouse.
- **The federal court:** Judge Isaac Parker at Fort Smith, Arkansas held jurisdiction over the Indian Territory through the 1880s — the _Hanging Judge_ — and sent seventy-nine men to the gallows.

### The territorial / state layer

- **The sheriff** held the elected county post, served warrants, ran the jail, collected taxes, served civil documents, appointed deputies. The most powerful local law in most Western counties.
- **The Texas Rangers** formed a state mounted force of approximately five hundred men in companies of thirty to eighty, with statewide authority. They suppressed Comanche raiding, hunted outlaws, fought Mexican raiders along the Rio Grande, and carried out extralegal violence against Tejanos.
- **Arizona Rangers** and
- **New Mexico Mounted Police** were similar state forces. Later — 1900s.

### The town / municipal layer

- **The town marshal** worked for the town council with authority inside town limits. In cattle towns like Dodge and Tombstone the marshal enforced the no-firearms ordinance (every cattle town had one), broke fights, walked rounds at night, took fines.
- **The constable** held a lower municipal post.
- **The night watchman** worked for businesses on the beat — often for Wells Fargo to guard the bank or the express office.

### The private layer

- **Pinkerton agents** worked for the national detective agency. Banks, railroads, and mine owners hired them. They strikebreaked. They hunted bounties. They infiltrated. Working-class country hated them.
- **Stock detectives and range detectives** worked for cattlemen's associations in Wyoming, Montana, and Texas. Some killed. Tom Horn. The Johnson County War (Wyoming, 1892) came between the large ranchers' detectives and the small ranchers and homesteaders.
- **Wells Fargo detectives** investigated stage and express robberies. James B. Hume was the most known.
- **Private guards** worked for companies, mines, and ranches.

### The extralegal layer

- **Vigilance committees** formed from citizens. They arrested, tried, and hanged suspects when (or before) the law moved. Notable ones: the San Francisco Committee of Vigilance (1851 and 1856), the Montana Vigilantes (1864–65, who hanged Sheriff Henry Plummer's gang in Bannack and Virginia City), the Ku Klux Klan and the White Leagues in the Reconstruction South.
- **Lynch mobs** ran less organized than vigilance committees — a crowd, some drunk, that took a prisoner from the jail and hanged him. They came often. Jails in the West lost prisoners regularly.
- **Posses** — a sheriff or marshal could deputize the _posse comitatus,_ any able-bodied man in his jurisdiction, to chase a fugitive. A posse was the line between law and mob, and the line moved.

## Saturday Night in Front Street

> The piano in the Long Branch was working over a tune nobody could place. The cowhands from the Slaughter outfit had come into town that afternoon, their horses tied three deep at the rail, and were down to silver dollars and shouting. Wyatt walked the boardwalk on the north side with the eight-pointed star on his vest and a sawed-off Belgian gun under his arm. The deadline ran along the tracks. The boys knew the deadline. None of them had crossed. One of them — Cleveland, the youngest, no older than seventeen — was in the alley between the saloon and the dance hall with a girl named May, and his pay for four months on the trail was in May's silk garter inside half an hour. Wyatt did not look down the alley. The piano kept on. The night went forward. A pistol shot from the south side a little after midnight; nobody dead, somebody's hat ruined. Wyatt walked back the other way and the lamps in the front windows of the saloons threw long yellow blocks on the dust of the street.

## 5. The Court

The justice of the peace held the local elected post. He heard small civil cases and minor criminal matters, married people, issued warrants, fined drunks. Most jurisdictions required no legal training. The police court or mayor's court heard ordinance violations in town. The county or district court heard the major cases — often a circuit judge who rode the circuit of several counties, holding court in each in season.

A trial: twelve-man jury. The accused had counsel if he could pay. Court-appointed counsel for capital cases by the 1880s in most jurisdictions. Witness testimony, cross-examination, closing arguments. Verdict.

The sentence: hanging for murder in most jurisdictions, replaced by long prison sentences in the 1890s in some states. Penitentiary for serious felonies — Yuma in Arizona, Cañon City in Colorado, Deer Lodge in Montana, Huntsville in Texas, Leavenworth federal. County jail for misdemeanors. Fines.

A frontier trial ran quick — a murder trial lasted often one or two days. Crowded; the town turned out. The press came; the newspaper printed the testimony in full. The hanging came public, well attended (in some places by ticketed entry), and followed by a picnic. State law largely abolished public hangings in the late nineteenth century, but private ones (with witnesses in the jail yard) continued.

## 6. The Reality of Frontier Crime

Two corrections to the popular myth.

- **Most western towns were not violent.** Dodge City in its peak year had five killings. Tombstone in 1881 had five (one was the OK Corral). A modern American city of the same size has more. Most arrests in cattle towns came for drunkenness, disorderly conduct, and prostitution — the last licensed and taxed as revenue.
- **The big violence was in the country, not the town.** Range wars, Indian Wars, vigilante actions in the unsettled country, the lynching of Mexicans and Black men in the Texas borderlands and the Reconstruction South, the killing of homesteaders by hired guns of the cattle barons — the West's real body counts were here. The _gunfighter walking the dusty street at high noon_ was one tradition out of many. Use it sparingly. Use the rest.

## 7. The Big Picture of the Economy

The West was a colonial economy supplying raw materials to the industrial East and to Europe. **Cattle:** beef on the hoof shipped east; hides shipped to Europe; tallow for candles and soap. **Mining:** gold, silver, copper, lead, zinc shipped east and to Europe. **Hides and furs:** buffalo hides (1872–83) shipped east for industrial belting and robes; beaver had been the trade of mountain men a generation before. **Lumber:** Sierra and Pacific Northwest timber. **Wheat:** California, Kansas, the Dakotas — shipped east and to Europe. **Wool:** the New Mexico, Wyoming, and Montana sheep flocks.

The frontier was capital-poor. Eastern and European money financed cattle barons (Scottish syndicates owned ranches in Wyoming and the Texas Panhandle). British and Eastern money financed the railroads. Local currency was scarce. Credit ran the small economy.

The screen-cowboy western misses this. The cattle baron in the page version is not a man on a horse — he is a syndicate, a New York or Edinburgh director who has never been west of Pittsburgh. The drover who works for him is the bottom of a long financial chain whose top sits in a London office. Write this and the West gets larger and stranger.

## 8. What the Town Smells Like, Sounds Like

- **Smell.** Horse manure in the street, constant — a town of two thousand had perhaps a thousand horses. Wood smoke from cookstoves and forges. Bread smell from the bakery in the morning. Tobacco. Whiskey smell through saloon doors. Coal smoke from the railroad. Outhouse stench in summer. Flowers in spring along the yards of the respectable houses.
- **Sound.** Wagon wheels on dirt or on plank sidewalks. Iron shoes on the same. Hammers in the smith's shop. Piano in the saloon at night, out of tune. The locomotive whistle three times a day. The court bell. The school bell. The church bell on Sunday. Drunks shouting in the late hours. Pistol shots now and then, often at the moon.
- **Sight.** Mud or dust in the streets, depending on the weather. False-front frame buildings with the storefront one story taller than the building behind. Boardwalks with hitching rails in front. Horses tied at the rails. Loose dogs. Children. Few trees in most western towns; planted ones showed status.

A town scene with two or three of these on the page reads true. A town scene with none reads like a movie set, which is what too many published westerns amount to.
