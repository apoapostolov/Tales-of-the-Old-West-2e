# Chapter 17 — Horse Culture

> "BOWMAN THREW THE BLANKET ON. HE SET THE SADDLE ON TOP AND SLID IT BACK ALONG THE BAY'S SPINE UNTIL IT SAT RIGHT. THE BAY HAD LEARNED TO PUFF AND HELD HIS BREATH AGAINST THE RIG. BOWMAN STOOD WITH HIS SHOULDER TO THE BAY'S SHOULDER AND WAITED HIM OUT."

The horse moved the West. He carried men and freight across country. He was the instrument of war and work. A man without a horse had nothing. The horse was wealth. Men stole him and traded him and fought over him. Chapter 15 covers saddling and riding and trail care. Chapter 16 covers the string. This chapter covers breeds, mustanging, the cavalry remount, racing, theft, the trade, gear, ceremony, the talk, and the man and his horse.

## 1. The Breeds and Types

The 1870s West ran specific horses. Not breeds in the modern sense with papers — most horses were grade animals with no registry — but types with their own conformation and work.

### The mustang

Spanish brought horses to the Americas from the sixteenth century onward. They went wild on the open ranges of Texas, the Southwest, and the Great Basin. By the 1870s herds numbered in the millions across the West.

The mustang stood thirteen to fourteen hands, sometimes fourteen to fifteen at the larger end. Tough. Sure-footed. Intelligent. Often stubborn. He ran every color. Paint, dun, buckskin, grulla — the Spanish lines were common. Broken, the mustang made a trail horse, a cow horse, a war horse. The breaking was serious work. The Plains Indian's horse was mustang stock mixed with what the herd had taken in. The mustang was too small for heavy work, too slow for the formal track. The thoroughbred and the draft horse took those jobs.

### The quarter horse in formation

The quarter horse type — short-distance racer with quarter-mile sprinting speed — came from colonial Virginia and moved West with the cattle trade. By the 1870s the type concentrated in Texas. The working cow horse drew on this stock. The Steeldust line came from a stallion named Steel Dust brought to Texas in the 1840s. The Copperbottom line was another foundation. A "Steeldust" or "Copperbottom" cow horse meant a thing: compact, heavy-quartered, fast out of a stand, intelligent on a cow, easily trained. The 1870s cowhand on a top horse rode a Steeldust or a Steeldust cross.

### The Morgan

A New England type from the stallion Justin Morgan, foaled 1789. Compact. Muscular. Willing. Versatile. The type moved West from the 1840s onward as a saddle horse, a light harness horse, a cavalry horse, a family horse. The U.S. Cavalry remount service prized the Morgan and Morgan-cross horses.

### The thoroughbred

The English race horse. In the West, used for racing and as a bloodline improver — the thoroughbred stallion bred to grade mares to add height and speed and refinement. Not a working horse — too leggy, too thin-skinned, too fragile for cow work or rough country. The Kentucky-bred thoroughbred came West for the race tracks of San Francisco, Denver, and the mining camps. It was a trade.

### The draft horse

The heavy work horse. Percheron. Belgian. Clydesdale. Crosses thereof. Brought West for plowing, freight hauling, mine work, heavy team work. The freight wagons ran heavy teams — six to twelve animals, horses or mules — pulling loaded wagons across country. The draft horse was not common on the open range but was substantial in the agricultural districts and on the freight roads.

### The mule

Not a horse but central to western transportation. The mule — horse-donkey hybrid, sterile — combined the horse's size and strength with the donkey's sure-footedness, hardiness, and intelligence. The mule was the Army's pack animal. The freighter's team animal in rough country. The miner's mine animal. The Missouri mule was the type — tall, strong, well-conformed. A Missouri mule team was the freight industry's standard.

### The criollo and the Spanish horse

In the Southwest the Hispanic ranches ran horses of direct Spanish descent — the criollo type. Tough. Small. Fit to rough country and the vaquero's work. Many of the West's best cow horses carried criollo blood through Texas stock that had absorbed Mexican animals over generations.

### The Indian pony

Not a breed but a type: the Plains Indian's horse, mustang in foundation, often well-cared-for within what the band's resources allowed, valued for what it could do. The buffalo runner — trained to ride into a buffalo herd — was a specialist animal of high value. The war horse was another specialist. The travois horse and the camp horse had less prestige. A Comanche or Cheyenne war horse was chosen and trained with the care a man gave to a thoroughbred race horse. White memoirists spoke of "Indian ponies" with contempt. They missed the horsemanship and selection involved.

## 2. Mustanging

The capture and breaking of wild horses — a trade through the late nineteenth century.

### Capture

- **The walk-down.** A team of mustangers — typically three to six men, multiple horses each — located a wild herd and pursued it on relay. Each rider pushed the herd until his horse tired, then dropped back and a fresh rider took over. The wild herd could not rest and exhausted itself. The mustangers drove it into a corral or roped the animals one by one. A walk-down took days.
- **The trap corral.** A corral built in a canyon or draw with wing fences leading into it. The herd was driven into the wings and into the corral, the gate shut behind. Required scouting to find herds that used predictable water or travel routes.
- **The water trap.** A corral built around a water source the herd used. The mustangers waited until the herd entered to drink, then closed the gate. Effective in dry country where water was scarce.
- **Roping individuals.** A mounted rider on a fast, tough horse cut individual animals from the herd and roped them. The roped horse was thrown, hobbled, and held until it could be led back to camp.

### Breaking

The captured mustang was broken to the saddle by a procedure that varied by region and by breaker. The work ran in three stages. First, **hold and handle**: the wild horse was held in a small corral and handled — touched, talked to, halter-broken — over days or weeks. Some breakers skipped this and went straight to the next step. Second, **saddle and ride**: the horse was saddled, often with considerable bucking the first times, then mounted. The first rides were rough — the horse bucked until exhausted, then was ridden in a circle until it would respond to the reins. Third, **refine**: subsequent rides taught the horse to neck-rein, to stop, to back, to side-pass. A well-broken horse was the result of weeks or months of patient work.

The "buckaroo" tradition in the Northwest, derived from California vaquero practice, emphasized slow, careful breaking with the hackamore and spade bit. This produced a highly responsive horse over years. The Texas tradition was rougher and faster. Both produced working horses. The buckaroo horse was more finished.

### Trade

A broken mustang sold for $10 to $25 in the 1870s, depending on quality and region. A top cow horse might bring $50 to $100. The mustanging trade supplied the cavalry remount service, the cattle ranches, and the general horse market.

## 3. The Cavalry Remount

The U.S. Cavalry needed a constant supply of horses. The 1870s remount service operated through contracts with regional suppliers who delivered horses meeting Army specifications: height fifteen to sixteen hands; weight 950 to 1,150 pounds; age four to eight years; sound — no major conformation faults, sound feet, legs, eyes, wind; color solid — bay, sorrel, brown, black, gray — no paints, pintos, or appaloosas in the regular cavalry, though an officer might claim exception; temperament trainable, not vicious or spoiled.

Contractors — often substantial ranchers with horse operations — bought from many smaller producers and delivered consolidated remounts to the Army at a contracted price of $100 to $150 per horse in the 1870s. A failed delivery — sick horses, horses that did not meet specifications — was rejected on Army inspection.

Each trooper was issued a horse on enlistment or arrival at his unit. The trooper was responsible for the horse's care: feeding, watering, grooming, shoeing (assisted by the company farrier), veterinary attention. A trooper who abused his horse was disciplined. A trooper whose horse died of neglect or injury might be fined the horse's value. The cavalry horse worked hard. A campaign meant weeks of long marches, short rations, no shelter. Horse mortality on hard campaigns was substantial — twenty to forty percent of the cavalry's horses might be lost on a major expedition through disease, exhaustion, accident, or combat. For the trooper's life around the horse, see Chapter 19.

## 4. Horse Racing

A major western entertainment, especially in Texas, the Southwest, and California.

**The match race** was the classic form. Two horses, one race, a short distance — a quarter-mile to a half-mile in Texas; longer distances in California. The horses ran from a standing start on a straight track: sometimes a graded race track at a town, sometimes a straight stretch of road, sometimes a measured course on the prairie. The match race was the occasion for substantial betting. The Texas "quarter race" — quarter-mile sprint — was a particular form, drawing on the short-distance racing tradition the Anglo-American settlers brought from the South. The Steeldust horses excelled at this distance.

- **The Mexican carrera.** The Hispanic racing tradition. The carrera de caballos was a community event with substantial betting, run on Sunday after Mass on a track at the village edge. The Mexican gambler at the carrera was a recognizable figure. The prize horse was the village's pride.
- **The track meet.** In the larger western towns, formal race tracks operated with multiple races per day, organized betting — parimutuel and bookmaker — and thoroughbred racing on the Eastern model. San Francisco, Sacramento, and later Denver were the principal centers.

## 5. Horse Theft

Horse theft was a major western crime, distinct from cattle rustling though often practiced by the same people. The horse was more portable than cattle, more valuable per head, easier to steal in small numbers. Horse thieves were despised in a way cattle rustlers sometimes were not — the horse thief took a man's specific working partner, sometimes left him afoot in hard country.

### Methods

- **The bunch theft.** A group of horses driven off a range and taken cross-country to a distant market. Required getting the horses to a place where they would not be known. Cross-border theft was common — Texas horses driven into Mexico and vice versa, Wyoming horses driven into Colorado.
- **The single-horse theft.** A specific horse taken from a corral, hitching rail, or pasture. Typically a riding horse the thief intended to use himself. The thief might leave his own worn-out horse in trade.
- **The remuda theft.** The theft of a working ranch's or trail outfit's horse herd. Catastrophic for the victim — the outfit could not work without horses.
- **The Indian raid.** Plains tribes practiced horse raiding as a major economic and prestige activity. Raids took horses from other tribes, from Hispanic ranches, and from white settlements. The cultural framework was different from white horse theft, but the economic effect on the victim was the same.

### Brand alteration and penalty

A stolen horse with a recognizable brand had to be re-branded to be salable. The methods were the same as for cattle (see Chapter 20): adding lines to convert one brand into another, hot iron over the old brand, killing and skinning if the brand could not be altered (uncommon for horses, as the meat had little value).

Horse thieves were among the most likely western criminals to be lynched. The horse thief who fell into the hands of a vigilance committee or a ranchers' posse was apt to hang from the nearest tree. The legal penalty for horse theft was a long prison term. The extra-legal penalty was death.

A horse-theft scene carries substantial dramatic weight: the dawn discovery of an empty corral; the trail followed for two days; the thief overtaken at the bend of the river; the hanging tree. A scene of being left afoot is its own subject — the horse stolen out from under the rider in remote country could be a death sentence. The rider on foot in waterless country, walking out. A Cormac McCarthy scene, often.

## 6. The Horse Trade

The 1870s western horse trade was substantial and informal. A man might buy and sell horses at the local livery, at the stockyards, at horse fairs, or in private deals. The horse trader — a type — traveled from town to town buying and selling.

A buyer evaluating a horse looked at **age**, determined by the teeth — a process called _mouthing_. The 1870s buyer expected to be told the horse's age and verified by mouthing. He looked at **conformation** — build, balance, legs. **Soundness** — no lameness, no broken wind, no blind eye, no tender feet. The buyer led the horse out, watched it walk and trot, picked up the feet, examined the eyes. **Disposition** — the buyer wanted to see the horse saddled and ridden, preferred to ride it himself. **Brand and ownership** — the buyer wanted a bill of sale and a clear brand.

In the 1870s, horse prices varied by type and quality:

| Animal                             | Price        |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------ |
| Plug or worn-out horse             | $5–$15       |
| Ordinary saddle horse              | $25–$50      |
| Good cow horse                     | $50–$100     |
| Top cow horse or matched team      | $100–$200    |
| Race horse or trotter              | $300–$1,000+ |
| Cavalry remount (contractor price) | $100–$150    |
| Mustang (newly broken)             | $10–$25      |

Rough; market and region varied.

The horse trader's pitch was the period's salesmanship. _A four-year-old, sound of wind and limb, no vice, gentle as a lamb to ride, will work in any harness, has been on the trail to Kansas twice. I'll let him go for sixty dollars, and I'm losing money._ The buyer's counter-pitch — finding the flaws to drive down the price — was the conversation's other half.

## 7. Equipment Beyond the Basics

Chapter 15 covers the saddle, the bit, and the basic gear. This section expands on equipment with cultural and regional dimensions.

### Bits

**The snaffle** is a jointed bit working on the corners of the mouth. Green horses wore the snaffle. English riders wore the snaffle. Some western riders wore the snaffle for trail work. **The curb** had a port—the curved central section that fit over the tongue—and shanks that produced leverage when the reins were pulled. It was the standard western working bit. **The spade bit** had a high port and long shanks. The buckaroo used the spade bit on trained horses. It required a light hand or it would injure the animal. **The hackamore** is a bridle without a bit. It worked on the horse's nose with a rawhide noseband called the bosal. Buckaroos used the hackamore to start a horse. **The jaquima** is the Spanish form of the hackamore, used in the vaquero tradition. **The war bridle** was used by Plains Indians. It was a bit and bridle, sometimes only a thong tied around the lower jaw. It permitted close control with little equipment.

### Saddles beyond the basic stock saddle

- **The Mexican saddle (silla)** was the parent of the western stock saddle. It had a high pommel, high cantle, and big horn. It was ornately tooled.
- **The Texas saddle** was developed from the Mexican saddle for cattle work in Texas. It was lower than the Mexican and had a horn for roping.
- **The California saddle** was developed from the Mexican saddle for vaquero work in California. It had a round skirt and different rigging from the Texas saddle.
- **The McClellan saddle** was the U.S. Cavalry's service saddle, adopted in 1859 and used through World War II. It was an open-tree saddle designed to protect the horse's back on long rides but was hard on the rider.
- **The English saddle** was used for race horses and by some eastern riders. It was rarely seen on the working range.
- **The pack saddle** came in two types: the sawbuck and the aparejo. The sawbuck was a wooden cross-frame strapped to the pack animal. The aparejo was a Mexican-derived padded cushion saddle. The aparejo distributed the load across the animal's back. Packers chose the aparejo.

### Boots, spurs, chaps

- **Boots** were high-heeled, pointed-toed, and underslung-heeled. The high heel kept the foot from slipping through the stirrup. The pointed toe slid in easily. The underslung heel caught on the stirrup if the horse fell, helping the rider pull free. The boots were pulled on with no laces and reached the calf.
- **Spurs** were worn on the boots. Types included the Texas spur with a small rowel and short shank, the California spur with a large rowel and long shank and often jingles, and the Mexican spur with a large rowel and ornate styling. Spurs signaled the horse more than punished it. The rider used the spur lightly.
- **Chaps** were leather coverings worn over the trousers to protect against brush, weather, rope burn, and falls. Two types existed: shotgun chaps, full-length tubes pulled on like trousers, and batwing chaps, open at the back and buckled around the leg. The Mexican chaparreras were the parent type. The woollies were winter chaps with the hair left on.

### The rope

The American cowhand used a braided rawhide reata in California and the Spanish tradition or a hemp or grass rope in Texas and the south. The rope was thirty to sixty feet long. The roping technique varied by region. The Texas "hard and fast" style tied the rope to the horn with no slip. The California "dally" style wrapped the rope around the horn after the catch and could slip to absorb the shock. The vaquero or buckaroo dallied. The Texas hand tied hard and fast. Both styles caused accidents. The dally rider could lose fingers in a slipping wrap. The hard-and-fast rider could be jerked from the saddle by a caught animal.

## 8. Horse, Ceremony, Relationship

The horse was labor and transport and also status, gift, ceremony, partner.

### The Plains Indian horse culture

The horse transformed Plains Indian life from the seventeenth century onward. By the 1870s, mounted horse cultures—the Comanche, Cheyenne, Lakota, Crow, Blackfeet, Ute, Shoshone—had developed horse-handling traditions. Horses were **wealth**—a man's wealth was counted in horses. **Bride price**—marriage involved horses given to the bride's family. **Gift**—the giving of horses was a diplomatic act. **War booty**—captured horses were an economic gain. **Ceremony**—horses figured in dances, in burials where a man's best horse was killed at his grave, in regalia. Comanche and Cheyenne men knew their horses by name. The buffalo runner and the war horse were known by name and were partners.

### The cowhand's horse

The American cowhand was issued a string of horses, typically four to ten, on a working outfit. He did not own them; the ranch owned them. He rode them daily. A cowhand's "top horse"—his best horse in the string—was treated with care. A cowhand whose top horse was killed or seriously injured felt the loss. The horse-and-rider partnership was the cow work's unit.

### The Hispanic vaquero's horse

The vaquero tradition valued the finished horse—the trained saddle horse developed over years of work. The vaquero's horse was an expression of the vaquero's training and the hacienda's standing. A famous bridle horse was known in the region.

### The army horse

The cavalry trooper's relationship with his issued horse varied. Some troopers became attached; others did not. The horse was the trooper's working partner under fire.

### Funerals and gifts

Horse culture had ceremonial dimensions. Funeral practices included the killing of a horse at the grave in some Plains traditions and the riderless horse with reversed boots in cavalry funerals. The horse was a gift in many cultures. Races and contests used the horse as the medium of competition.

## 9. Voice — the Horseman's Vocabulary

Render the horseman through his vocabulary. The specific terms carry the period.

- **Mouth** is a verb—to examine a horse's teeth to judge its age. _He mouthed the gelding and pronounced him eight years old._ A **mouthy** horse has a hard mouth that does not respond to the bit. **Wind** is the horse's breathing. _Sound of wind_ meant healthy lungs. _Broken wind_ was heaves, a chronic respiratory condition. **Hand** is the unit of horse height—four inches. _Fifteen and a half hands_ was about 5'2" at the withers. **Withers** is the point at the base of the horse's neck where it joins the back. **Hocks** is the joint of the horse's hind leg.
- **Cinch** or **girth** is the strap holding the saddle on—_cinch_ in Western usage, _girth_ in English. **Latigo** is the leather strap that fastened the cinch. **Off side** and **near side** are the right and left sides of the horse. The horse was mounted from the near (left) side. **Up** means mounted—_he was up by daylight._
- **Bunch up** is a horse drawing his legs together and rounding his back, often before bucking. **Crow-hop** is a buck—the horse hopping with all four legs. **Sun-fish** is a buck—the horse twisting the body in mid-air, presenting the belly to the sun. A sun-fishing horse threw most riders. **Rear** is the horse standing on his hind legs, dangerous. A rearing horse can fall over backwards on the rider. **Bog down** is a horse trapped in mud or a bog. **Founder** is laminitis, an inflammation of the horse's feet from overfeeding or overwork. **Galled** is a horse with sore patches under the saddle or harness from rubbing. **Pulled up lame** is a horse that became lame on the trail. **Saddle-sore** is either the rider after riding or the horse with sore patches. **Throw a shoe** is a horseshoe coming off in the field—a horse without a shoe might become lame on stony ground.

These terms came to the horseman's mouth naturally.
