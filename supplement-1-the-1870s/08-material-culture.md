# Chapter 8 — Material Culture: Iron, Leather, Cloth

> _“THE GUN HAD RIDDEN IN HIS BELT THROUGH FIVE WET WINTERS AND THE BLUING WAS ALL OFF THE MUZZLE AND THE CYLINDER FACE AND THE GRIP WAS WORN TO HIS HAND.”_

The frontier was, beneath everything else, a material culture: iron, leather, and cloth, made and remade by hand and by small machine, traded across the country in wagons and sold across the counters of mercantiles. Each object had its weight, smell, sound, cost, and failure mode. What follows is the inventory of the things a person of the period actually held.

---

## 1. Firearms

### Revolvers

- **Colt 1851 Navy.** A .36-caliber percussion cap-and-ball revolver. Six-shot. It carried an octagonal barrel and a brass trigger guard. It was light, accurate, and slow to reload—a man needed thirty to sixty seconds in good light to seat the powder, ball, and caps. Wild Bill Hickok carried a pair backward in a silk sash until his death.
- **Colt 1860 Army.** A .44-caliber percussion revolver. Six-shot. It carried a round barrel and a heavier frame. It was the standard Union cavalry sidearm and remained common on the frontier through the 1870s. Gunsmiths frequently converted them to fire self-contained metallic cartridges.
- **Remington 1858 New Model Army.** A .44-caliber percussion revolver. Six-shot. It featured a solid top-strap frame that made it stronger than the open-top Colt. A man carrying a spare, pre-loaded cylinder could drop the empty cylinder out and lock the fresh one in under ten seconds.
- **Colt Single Action Army.** Introduced in 1873. Initially chambered in .45 Colt and later in .44-40. Six-shot cartridge revolver. The defining sidearm of the period. Single-action: the shooter cocked the heavy iron hammer manually for each shot. The user loaded one brass cartridge at a time through a hinged loading gate on the right side of the frame, and pushed the empty brass cases out one at a time with a spring-loaded ejector rod. It retailed for seventeen dollars in 1873. It weighed over two pounds unloaded.
- **Smith & Wesson Model 3 (Schofield, Russian).** A top-break design. The shooter unlatched the barrel and hinged it downward, which automatically ejected all six empty cases simultaneously. It was significantly faster to reload than the Colt. The U.S. Army issued the Schofield variant. Jesse James and John Wesley Hardin carried them.
- **Derringer.** A small, large-bore pocket pistol. The Remington Double Derringer in .41 rimfire was the standard. Gamblers carried them in vest pockets. They were lethal across a card table and useless past ten feet.

### Rifles

- **Sharps Model 1874.** Chambered in massive cartridges like .45-70, .50-70, and .50-90. A single-shot, breech-loading, falling-block rifle. It weighed ten to twelve pounds. The buffalo hunters used it to exterminate the herds from a distance of five hundred yards.
- **Henry Rifle (Model 1860).** A .44 rimfire lever-action rifle featuring a sixteen-round brass tube magazine under the barrel. The brass frame was distinctive. Men on the frontier carried them heavily through the early 1870s.
- **Winchester Model 1866 (Yellow Boy).** A brass-framed lever-action rifle in .44 rimfire, refining the Henry's design with a wooden forearm and a side-loading gate.
- **Winchester Model 1873.** Built with an iron, and later steel, frame. Lever-action. Handled centerfire cartridges. Initially chambered in .44-40—allowing a man to carry a single caliber of ammunition for both his Winchester rifle and his Colt revolver.
- **Springfield Model 1873 (Trapdoor).** A .45-70 single-shot breech-loading rifle. The standard U.S. Army infantry and cavalry weapon through the 1880s. Custer's troopers carried the carbine variant at the Little Bighorn, where the soft copper cartridge cases occasionally jammed and swelled in the hot breeches.

### Shotguns

- **Coach Gun.** A double-barreled 10- or 12-gauge shotgun with the barrels cut down to eighteen or twenty inches. Express guards rode the stagecoach box with these weapons. They were violently lethal at twenty feet.
- **Long-Barrel Double.** A 12-gauge hunting gun. Men loaded brass or paper shells with heavy double-aught buckshot for close-quarters fighting.

### The Realities of Period Firearms

- **Reloading was slow.** A man needed at least thirty seconds to fully eject and reload a Single Action Army. In a gunfight, men reloaded behind heavy cover, or they did not reload at all. Most gunfights ended before the first cylinder was empty.
- **Ranges were short.** A pistol fight at thirty feet was considered long-range. Hitting a moving man with a heavy one-handed revolver past fifty feet was rare luck.
- **Black powder generated heavy smoke.** A man firing three rounds inside a saloon filled the room with dense, blinding white sulfur smoke. After the second shot, he frequently could not clearly see his opponent or his own iron front sight.
- **Misfires occurred frequently.** Percussion caps drew moisture. The copper primers on cheap cartridges failed to detonate. A revolver riding in a leather holster through a three-day rainstorm might simply click when the hammer fell.
- **Accidental discharges killed men.** The firing pin on a Colt Single Action Army rested directly on the primer of the cartridge under the hammer. If a man dropped a fully loaded revolver on the floor, or if a horse fell on a saddlebag holding a loaded gun, the impact drove the pin into the primer and fired the weapon. Experienced men loaded five rounds and rested the hammer on the empty sixth chamber.
- **The body did not fly backward.** A man hit with a .45-caliber lead slug folded, knelt, dropped straight down, or simply stood in shock for a moment before collapsing.

### The Fast Draw

It was largely myth. The formalized, face-to-face duel in the street occurred rarely. The vast majority of killings were ambushes, men shot in the back with shotguns, or close-quarters, drunken struggles where men grabbed for the same revolver inside a saloon.

Men who survived multiple gunfights, like Wyatt Earp and Bat Masterson, stated the fight was typically over before either gun completely cleared the leather. The man who drew first and fired first usually lived.

## 2. Edged Weapons

- **The Bowie Knife.** A heavy, single-edged, nine-to-twelve-inch carbon steel blade featuring a clip point. Designed for killing and heavy camp work. Many men carried it in a leather sheath on the belt.
- **The Sheath Knife.** A smaller, rigid knife with a five-to-eight-inch steel blade. Men carried it for skinning game, butchering cattle, and eating cooked meat off the blade.
- **The Jackknife.** A folding pocketknife. A man used it for cutting plug tobacco, whittling wood, cleaning mud from his fingernails, or prying open a tin can.
- **The Hatchet.** A short-handled iron camp tool. Used for splitting firewood and splitting heavy animal bones.
- **The Cavalry Saber.** A curved, three-foot steel blade with a brass handguard. The U.S. Army cavalry carried it frequently through the Civil War but largely relegated it to ceremonial use by the 1870s. The Plains tribes occasionally carried captured sabers. It was entirely absent from the working cattle ranches.

## 3. Saddles and Tack

### Saddle Anatomy

The **tree** formed the rigid internal frame, carved from wood and stretched tightly over with wet rawhide that dried like iron. The **cantle** was the raised back of the seat. The **horn** anchored the pommel, allowing a man to lock his heavy hemp rope around it to stop a twelve-hundred-pound steer. The leather **skirts** rode directly against the horse’s flanks. The wooden **stirrups** frequently carried heavy leather covers, called tapaderos in the Southwest, to block heavy brush. The **cinch**, woven from mohair or horsehair, pulled brutally tight underneath the horse’s belly. The leather **latigo** strap looped through the heavy iron saddle ring to tie the cinch off. Silver or brass **conchos** anchored the long leather saddle strings used to tie bedrolls and canvas slickers behind the cantle.

### Saddle Types

The **California Saddle** was heavily tooled, ornately stamped leather over a center-fire rigging, utilized heavily on the Pacific Coast. The **Texas Saddle** was a lighter, plainer, double-rigged working tool that dominated the arid plains brush. The **McClellan Saddle** was the severe, skeletal U.S. Cavalry issue, hard on a soldier's spine over fifty miles but noticeably lighter for the horse.

### Bridles and Bits

The **hackamore** functioned as a bitless bridle of braided rawhide, utilizing nose pressure to train raw, unbroken horses. The **snaffle bit** carried a mild, jointed iron mouthpiece. The **spade bit** forced a heavy, high-rising iron port violently against the roof of the horse's mouth. A rider with a heavy hand on a spade bit could completely shatter a horse’s jaw.

### Other Tack

The **blanket** rode folded four times beneath the heavy saddle. Many were woven wool purchased or traded from Diné (Navajo) weavers. **Hobbles** were heavy leather cuffs buckled around the horse's front fetlocks to strictly limit its stride grazing at night. A heavy iron **picket pin** drove deep into the dirt to hold the night-horse securely on a rope near the sleeping men.

## 4. Clothing

### The Townsman

A businessman or banker wore a dark gray, rough-woven wool sack-suit over a white cotton shirt with a stiff, detachable collar. He wore a dark wool vest, a heavy pocket watch secured by a gold chain, low black leather shoes, and a rigid bowler hat that held heat in the summer.

### The Gambler

The professional wore a long, dark wool frock coat fitted tightly at the waist. His vest frequently carried heavily brocaded silk. He pinned a diamond stickpin through a silk cravat and wore soft, thin-soled leather boots designed specifically not to creak on the floorboards behind a poker table. A heavy, short-barreled Remington derringer rode in the left vest pocket.

### The Lawman

Depending on the rain and wind, he wore a short wool coat or a long canvas duster over a heavy vest. The tin or silver-plated star pinned straight through the vest fabric. He wore a single-action revolver shoved tightly into a slim leather belt-holster. He frequently carried a heavy twelve-gauge double-barreled shotgun openly when crossing the street to make an arrest.

### The Soldier

A private in the U.S. Cavalry wore a dark blue wool blouse with heavy brass buttons, light blue wool trousers with a yellow stripe down the outer seam, and heavy, knee-length black leather boots. He wore a wide-brimmed felt campaign hat or the shorter, flat-topped forage cap. A wide black leather belt held brass cartridges directly over the wool trousers.

### The Native Man

Dress varied radically by nation, season, and occasion. By the 1870s, the plains trade networks had heavily integrated white manufactured goods. A Cheyenne man standing in a reservation agency in 1880 might wear a red print calico shirt, U.S. Army-issue blue wool trousers, traditional beaded moccasins, and a heavy red wool blanket draped completely over his shoulders against the wind. It was not a static culture; they took what functioned.

### The Woman

The wife of a dryland homesteader wore a heavy linsey-woolsey or fading print calico dress, a long canvas apron, heavy black leather brogan shoes, and a deep, stiff canvas sunbonnet to block the brutal plains sun. The wealthy town woman wore a heavily corseted dress over a tightly laced wire bustle that extended sharply backward, layered heavy petticoats, and tall leather button boots. The working girl operating in a railhead parlor house wore cheap, bright silk imitating eastern fashion, cut significantly shorter to deliberately show the high black leather boot and the ankle.

## The Mercantile

> Wesley came up the wooden steps and the stray dog moved to the corner of the porch and lay down to watch the street. Inside the door, the air held the brown, heavy smell of all mercantiles—roasted coffee decaying in a wooden barrel, leaked kerosene, the thin sugary scent of cured salt pork hanging from a steel hook in the back, and the bitter cut of new harness oil on raw leather.
>
> Mr. Pruitt stood at the plank counter, weighing iron nails into a brown paper funnel for a man Wesley did not know. Wesley stood at the door and waited in the dim light. The wall behind the counter held goods stacked to the ceiling—heavy tin cans labeled in printed red and black, thick bolts of stiff calico in five dyed colors, heavy square pasteboard boxes of brass-cased cartridges, dark glass bottles of patent medicine, and rows of thick glass jars holding penny candy. A pair of stiff black leather boots hung from the rafters by dark cotton laces. A Winchester lever-action rifle leaned darkly against the back wall with a white paper tag tied by string to the iron trigger guard.
>
> Mr. Pruitt finished and folded the paper over the nails. The man took the heavy package and went out past Wesley without speaking. Mr. Pruitt looked over the counter and waited.
>
> "A pound of the green coffee beans," Wesley said. "And a round tin of the sulfur matches. And a pasteboard box of the .44-40s, if you stock them."
>
> Mr. Pruitt turned to the shelf to reach for the heavy cartridge box. "On your account?"
>
> "Cash," Wesley said. He laid two heavy silver cartwheels flat on the scarred wood.

## 5. Tools

### The Cow Country

The cowboy's working tools beyond the gun existed solely to move heavy stock. The branding iron—a long iron rod carrying the forged shape of the brand—heated entirely red in an open mesquite fire to burn the hide. After 1875, men carried heavy iron wire-stretchers and heavy iron fence pliers to string and cut the brutal new barbed wire. They carried short, flat-bladed iron shovels and heavy iron pickaxes strapped to the chuckwagon to dig mud from stagnant water-holes. They packed block-and-tackle rigging—heavy wooden pulleys and thick Manila rope—to drag a submerged twelve-hundred-pound steer bodily out of a quicksand bog.

### The Trades

- **The Blacksmith:** He stood before a brick forge holding heavy iron tongs, drawing forced air through the deep glowing coke fire with a wide leather bellows, and swinging a four-pound iron hammer against a two-hundred-pound steel anvil.
- **The Carpenter:** He built towns with a heavy iron handsaw, a flat iron drawknife, an iron block plane, a set of thick iron chisels, and a heavy brass plumb bob swinging on a thin cotton string.
- **The Wheelwright:** He shaped hardwood spokes with a sharp iron spokeshave, measured the rim with a flat iron dish-cone, forced the outer iron tire through a heavy iron tire-bender, and heated the completed iron rim in a circular trench forge.
- **The Saddler:** He drove heavy steel awls through half an inch of cured saddle leather and pulled thick linen harness-needles through the punched holes by raw hand-strength, beveling the raw edges with a flat steel knife.
- **The Doctor:** He bled patients by opening a vein with a sharp steel lancet or a spring-loaded steel fleam. He extracted crushed lead bullets with heavy steel forceps and amputated shattered legs with a wide-toothed steel bone-saw. He dosed syphilis with heavy mercury, malaria with bitter quinine powder, and brutal pain with dark liquid opium tincture.
- **The Surveyor:** He dragged the heavy, sixty-six-foot iron Gunter's chain through the dirt and sighted the exact grid-lines through a heavy brass transit mounted on a wooden tripod.

### The Household

The cast-iron Dutch oven sat directly in the red coals to bake heavy bread and boil tough meat. The heavy black cast-iron skillet fried fat salt pork. A heavy cast-iron hand-cranked mill ground roasted coffee beans. The heavy copper boiler sat on the cast-iron woodstove to heat well-water for the galvanized iron washboard. The heavy iron treadle-operated Singer sewing machine anchored the room, a massive status symbol after the war. A wooden dash churn beat raw milk into pale yellow butter. Heavy glass lanterns burning pungent coal-oil or kerosene lit the cabin walls at night.

## 6. The Wagons

- **The Prairie Schooner.** The massive, canvas-covered emigrant wagon. Extremely slow, incredibly heavy. A man hitched six massive oxen to the front to drag his family, his plow, and his seed two thousand miles across the rutted dirt.
- **The Studebaker Farm Wagon.** The standard frontier workhorse. Driven on heavy iron tires circling heavy wooden wheels, utilizing a standardized wooden box. Men quickly converted them into chuckwagons or open hay-rakes by stripping or modifying the box.
- **The Freighter.** A massive, high-sided Murphy or Studebaker wagon, frequently hauled in trains of two or three wagons linked rigidly together and dragged by a fourteen-head team of heavy oxen.
- **The Stagecoach.** The heavily slung Concord coach, built specifically by Abbot-Downing of New Hampshire. It rocked violently on heavy leather thorough-braces above heavy iron axles. Six fast horses dragged six passengers wedged tightly inside the dark box, with an armed guard riding the high exposed box.
- **The Buckboard.** A light, open, spring-mounted wooden wagon with a single flat iron seat, pulled rapidly by a single horse or a light team for short trips to the mercantile.

## 7. Money

- **Gold Coin.** The twenty-dollar double eagle, ten-dollar eagle, five-dollar half-eagle, and the very small one-dollar gold piece. Pure, heavy, yellow currency. It rang distinctly on a wooden bar.
- **Silver Coin.** The heavy silver dollar (the cartwheel), the half-dollar, the quarter, the dime, and the small silver half-dime.
- **The Mexican Peso.** A massive silver dollar minted heavily in Mexico and trading at exact par with U.S. silver across the entire Southwest.
- **Paper Greenbacks.** The fragile U.S. paper money issued to finance the Civil War. Men generally distrusted it, and merchants frequently discounted its face value heavily against hard gold or silver coin.
- **The Bit.** A fraction equal to twelve-and-a-half cents. Two bits made a silver quarter. Four bits equaled a heavy silver half-dollar.
- **Coppers.** The heavy copper penny. Men used it exclusively to buy penny candy for children or in very cheap saloons.

A working cowboy did not use a bank. He carried his heavy gold and silver coins directly on his body, frequently sealing them inside a tied leather poke hidden deep against his chest inside his wool shirt, or burying the bag solidly inside his rolled canvas bedroll on the ground.

## 8. Wear and Tear

The objects showed exactly how they lived.

A man's **revolver** smelled sharply of burnt sulfur powder and sweet gun oil. The dark blue finish rubbed completely off the iron barrel, the cylinder face, and the sharp frame corners where coarse leather and his bare hand gripped it hundreds of times, leaving gray polished steel. The sharp checkering on the dark wood grip wore completely smooth.

His **saddle** smelled heavily of old, sweet leather, dried white horse sweat, and sharp saddle soap. The high saddle fork and the back cantle rubbed pale yellow. The center seat dished deeply from the rider's heavy weight dropping into it. The long leather stirrup straps severely twisted permanently to match the angle of the man's heavy boots.

His **boots** cracked deeply straight across the leather instep. The thick leather sole wore completely through to the inner lining at the ball of the foot. Thick white salt crusted permanently around the high leather heel. The tall leather shaft bowed heavily forward, locked exactly to the shape of the calf muscle that rode inside it.

His **hat** darkened with heavy, rigid sweat stains circling the inner leather band. He punched and folded the high crown to precisely his own specific crease. Fine gray trail dust packed deeply into the felt. A working cowhand's hat settled instantly on his head and fit exactly no other man on earth.

The **bedroll** is stiff with grease, smells of wood smoke, has a brown ring on the canvas where the bottle of laudanum the man used to keep against his back has slowly leaked.

The **chuckwagon** is gray-weathered, the iron tires worn thin, the box battered by tools and feet. The starter crock is sour-sweet on the lid. The coffee pot is black inside and out.

The **bunkhouse** smells of unwashed wool, leather, sweat, tobacco, coal-oil, the boot-grease in a dish on the table, the sourdough starter bubbling in its jar.

These are the textures the prose puts on the page and the table.
