# Chapter 23 — Medicine and Death

> _"THE SMELL CAME ON THE SECOND DAY. BY THE THIRD THE DOCTOR WOULD NOT GO IN THE ROOM."_

In the 19th century wounds killed. A wound treated in an hour in this time killed in 1875. A child's fever killed the child by morning. A woman who survived three deliveries was fortunate. Many did not survive one. Death stood at every door. The people knew it. What follows is the medical reality of the period.

---

## 1. The Doctor

By the 1870s a frontier town might have one. He was a graduate of a two-year proprietary medical college with no internship, no residency, no licensing in most states. Or a Civil War surgeon with battlefield experience and outdated training. Or a man who had read Gray's _Anatomy_ and practiced. Or an itinerant who came through with a wagon of patent medicine and pulled teeth.

His instruments fit in a black leather bag. **Lancet** for bleeding. Bloodletting remained common practice through the 1880s for fevers and _plethora_. **Bone saw.** **Bullet probe** — a long thin probe with a porcelain tip. When it touched lead it left a gray mark. **Forceps.** **Trephine** — skull saw to relieve pressure on the brain. **Scalpels.** **Catgut and silk** for sutures. **Tincture of opium** — laudanum. **Calomel** — mercurous chloride, given for nearly everything, poisonous, caused tooth loss, ulcers, salivation. **Whiskey or chloroform** for anesthesia. **Quinine** for malaria. **Mercury** for syphilis — the famous _one night with Venus, a lifetime with Mercury._

His fee in a working town was $1–$2 for a visit, $5 for setting a bone, $10–$25 for a major operation, $5–$15 for delivering a baby. A country doctor rode 30 miles to a homestead birth, often for nothing or for a chicken and a cured ham.

He knew germ theory was being discussed in the East after the late 1860s. He may have washed his hands and his instruments. He may not have. Joseph Lister's antiseptic surgery with carbolic acid spray reached the U.S. through the 1870s. Many frontier doctors never adopted it.

## 2. Gunshot Wounds

A pistol or rifle wound was a death sentence. Black-powder firearms drove fragments of cloth from the man's clothing into the wound. Antibiotics did not exist. Sterile technique did not exist on the frontier.

- **Head.** Almost always fatal. A man shot through the brain died before he hit the ground. A man shot in the face who survived the first hour usually survived. The jaws and cheekbones absorbed the ball. A graze along the temple sometimes left a man unconscious for hours and concussed for weeks.
- **Chest, lungs.** Frequently fatal. The lung collapsed. The man drowned in his own blood over hours. Sucking chest wounds were sealed with a wet cloth or a flap of leather so the man could breathe. Many such men died in three to twelve hours. Some survived if the bullet missed the major vessels, but tuberculosis and chronic infection were common after.
- **Heart, great vessels.** Death in seconds to minutes.
- **Abdomen.** The worst. Almost certainly fatal. Perforated bowel spilled feces into the abdominal cavity. Peritonitis set in over 24 to 72 hours. The man died of sepsis in three to seven days, in agony, with high fever, distended belly, vomiting, and finally coma. Frontier doctors did not open the abdomen. A gut-shot man received laudanum and was made comfortable.
- **Limb.** Survivable if the bullet missed the major vessels and bone. Often a compound fracture — bone broken and broken through the skin. The treatment was usually amputation if the wound was severe. A bone saw was used, with whiskey or chloroform if available, with the man held down by friends if not. Mortality after amputation: 30–50% from infection, depending on the surgeon's practice.
- **Spine.** Paralysis below the wound. Most such men died of infection from pressure sores within weeks.

A pistol ball at twenty feet does not knock a man down. He staggers, sits, sometimes stands a moment looking surprised. The blood is not bright at first. It darkens. A lung-shot man breathes with a wet rattle and a faint hiss through the wound. A gut-shot man pales gray-green within an hour. Fever sets in in twelve hours. By day three he is delirious. A man shot in the abdomen asks to be cold. He asks for water. The doctor refuses it. A perforated bowel cannot tolerate it. The smell of a gut wound by day three is unmistakable.

Treatment: probe for the ball with a bullet probe. Extract it if reachable. Pack the wound with lint or charcoal. If a limb wound, splint or amputate. Laudanum for pain. Whiskey for shock. Wait. If the man survived the first 48 hours and did not develop fever, he had a chance. If he developed fever and a foul discharge, he would probably die.

## 3. Infection — The Real Killer

More men died of infection in the 19th century than of the wounds themselves. Sepsis, pyemia, gangrene, tetanus.

- **Sepsis (blood poisoning).** Onset 2–7 days after wound. Symptoms: high fever, racing pulse, chills, confusion, falling blood pressure. Mortality before antibiotics: 70–90%. Treatment in 1875: nothing worked. Cool cloths, whiskey, prayer.
- **Gangrene.** Wet gangrene — wound turns gray-green, swells, stinks like rotting meat. The smell was so foul attendants could not stay in the room. Amputation upstream of the rotting tissue was the only chance. Often it was already too late. Dry gangrene — tissue turns black and shriveled from frostbite, ergotism, vascular disease. Less acutely lethal but still required amputation.
- **Tetanus (lockjaw).** Onset 7–14 days after a puncture wound — a horse manure-fouled cut, a barbed wire scratch, a nail puncture. Symptoms: stiffness of the jaw progressing to total muscular rigidity, fever, convulsions. Mortality 80–90%. Treatment in 1875: none. Vaccine developed 1924.
- **Erysipelas (St. Anthony's fire).** Streptococcal infection of the skin. A red rash advanced across the skin. High fever. Sometimes men survived. Often they died, especially the very young or the old.

## 4. Disease

- **Cholera.** Bacterial gut infection from contaminated water — Vibrio cholerae. Massive watery diarrhea, called _rice-water stool_. Violent vomiting. Dehydration. Death by cardiovascular collapse in hours to days. Multiple cholera waves swept through the West: 1832–34, 1849–54 (devastated the wagon trains and the gold rush), 1866–67. Whole emigrant trains died on the trail. Graves marked every spring. Treatment: nothing worked until rehydration was understood.
- **Typhoid.** Salmonella typhi. A fever that rose daily for a week. Rose spots appeared on the abdomen. Bowel perforation came in the third week. Mortality 10–20%. Endemic across the frontier. Bad water caused it.
- **Typhus.** Rickettsial. Spread by lice. Camp fever. Common in soldiers, prisoners, the very poor. High fever, rash, delirium. 10–60% mortality.
- **Smallpox.** Variola virus. Highly contagious. Fever came first, then the characteristic pustular rash. Mortality 30%. Scarring was almost universal in survivors. Vaccination came available since 1796 and was practiced widely in the East but spotty on the frontier. Native populations who had not been exposed had mortality approaching 90% in some outbreaks — the 1837 Mandan epidemic killed approximately 90% of the tribe.
- **Tuberculosis (consumption, the white plague).** Mycobacterium tuberculosis. The leading cause of death in the United States in the 19th century. A cough began slowly. The man lost weight, ran fever, sweated at night, and coughed blood. Many tubercular men from the East moved West for the dry climate — Doc Holliday among them. Many died there anyway. A tubercular character is period-correct and gives the writer the cough, the handkerchief, the slow wasting, the second-act collapse.
- **Diphtheria.** Bacterial infection of the throat. Children especially. A gray membrane formed across the throat and suffocated the child. Mortality in the unimmunized 30–50%. The child died gasping. Antitoxin was developed 1894.
- **Dysentery.** The bloody flux. Endemic. Common in soldiers, drovers, emigrants. Often fatal in the very young or weak.
- **Malaria (ague).** Common in the Mississippi valley and the Southeast, less in the dry West. Quinine treated it when it was available.
- **Syphilis.** Common in the West, given the demography. Three stages: primary — chancre. Secondary — rash, fever, then years of latency. Tertiary — gummas, neurosyphilis, paresis. Mercury treated it. Salvarsan came after 1910. Penicillin not until 1940s. A common cause of madness, blindness, paralysis in the old.
- **Gonorrhea.** The clap. Endemic among soldiers and drovers. Silver nitrate treated it, with little success.
- **Childhood diseases.** Measles, mumps, whooping cough, scarlet fever — common, often fatal. Many homesteads had a small graveyard with three or four children's stones.

## 5. Childbirth

A woman in 1875 had roughly a 1-in-100 chance of dying in any given childbirth. She had eight to twelve childbirths over a lifetime. Maternal mortality across a reproductive lifetime approached 1 in 8.

Midwives or neighbor women attended most births, not doctors. Puerperal fever (_childbed fever_ — streptococcal infection of the uterus from unwashed hands) killed most. Onset came 2–10 days after delivery. High fever. Foul discharge. Death within a week. Hemorrhage killed. Eclampsia killed. Obstructed labor killed. Ruptured uterus killed.

The infant: roughly 1 in 5 died in the first year, roughly 1 in 3 by age five. A homestead family of ten pregnancies raised five or six children to adulthood and buried four or five.

## 6. Medicines, Period-Correct

- **Laudanum** (tincture of opium in alcohol) — the painkiller; in every kitchen; addiction took hold.
- **Paregoric** (camphorated tincture of opium) — milder, given to children for colic and diarrhea; many infants died from overdose.
- **Calomel** (mercury chloride) — given for everything; it poisoned with mercury.
- **Quinine** for malaria.
- **Salts** (Epsom) — laxative.
- **Castor oil** — laxative.
- **Cod-liver oil** — tonic.
- **Whiskey or brandy** — anesthesia, painkiller, stimulant for shock, treatment for snakebite (useless), drink.
- **Patent medicines** — Hostetter's Bitters, Lydia Pinkham's Vegetable Compound (high alcohol content), Hamlin's Wizard Oil; the medicine show; many were 30–60% alcohol or laced with opium or cocaine; they sold because they worked, in a fashion: the patient felt better.
- **Snake oil** — originally Chinese laborer's medicine made from Chinese water snake (with effects that reduced inflammation); white peddlers appropriated and faked it by selling mineral oil with no relation to the original; hence the term.
- **Carbolic acid (phenol)** — as an antiseptic after the 1870s in advanced practice.

## 7. Surgery

A frontier doctor performed amputations, set fractures, sutured wounds, lanced abscesses, pulled teeth, delivered babies, and occasionally trephined the skull or removed a bullet from a limb. He almost never opened the chest or abdomen — the mortality rate was so near total it was not attempted.

Anesthesia: **whiskey** straight (not a true anesthetic; reduced inhibition and dulled sensation; the patient screamed); **chloroform** on a cloth held over the face after 1847 (risk of overdose; the patient occasionally died on the table from the chloroform itself); **ether** likewise; **local anesthesia** with cocaine after 1884. Many surgeries took place with the patient awake and held down.

## 8. The Dying — What It Actually Looks Like

- **Cholera death.** Onset within hours of the first watery stool. Patient ages decades in a day. Eyes sink. Skin gray-blue (the _cholera face_). Hands and feet ice-cold while the body burns with fever. Voice fails to a whisper. The man asks for water and water will not stay in him. Death in 12–48 hours. The body is shrunken, the eyes still open.
- **Pneumonia death.** The _old man's friend._ High fever, productive cough with blood or rust-colored sputum. The breath grows shallow. By the third or fourth day a rattle sounds across the room (the _death rattle_ — fluid in the airways the patient can no longer clear). The patient becomes confused, then unconscious. Death comes quiet.
- **Gut-shot death.** Day 1: the man complains of the wound, of cold, of thirst; the doctor refuses water; laudanum is given. Day 2: fever; the belly distends; vomiting begins, dark and foul. Day 3: delirium; the man calls for his mother, for his wife, for men dead twenty years; the smell from the wound is foul. Day 4–7: coma. Death.
- **Tuberculosis death.** Years of wasting. The patient is light as a child by the end. The cheek flushes (the _hectic flush_) in the afternoon, pale by morning. The cough, the bloody handkerchief, the night sweats. Death often comes by sudden hemorrhage — the patient coughs and blood fills the mouth, and he drowns in it in minutes. Or the patient slips away in the morning after a night.
- **Hanging death.** The drop. The neck breaks if the drop is calculated right (4–6 feet for a man of average weight). Death in seconds. The slow strangle — if the drop is too short or the rope misplaced: fifteen to twenty minutes of strangulation, the man's face purple, his tongue out, his bowels and bladder voiding. Witnesses said it was the worst thing they ever saw. The vigilante hanging from a tree branch with a horse pulled out from under: usually the slow strangle.
- **Frozen death.** Hypothermia. Confusion. Then warmth and a feeling of well-being (terminal burrowing — the man undresses, paradoxical warming). Then unconsciousness, then death. Found in the spring melt with his shirt unbuttoned, his hat off.
- **Thirst death.** 24 hours: thirst. 36: weakness, headache. 48: confusion. 60: the tongue swells, the lips crack and bleed, the man cannot swallow. 72: hallucination, fever, coma. 96: death. In the sun the timeline shortens to two or three days.
- **Old age death.** Pneumonia, often. Stroke. Heart failure, slow. A man of seventy in 1875 was old. A woman of sixty was old. Life expectancy from birth was around 40 due to infant mortality; a man who reached 20 might reach 60 or 65.

## 9. Burial

- **Graveyards.** Town graveyards on a hill outside town, fenced if the town could afford it. Wooden markers usually, stone for the affluent. Many graves unmarked within twenty years of burial.
- **Trail graves.** A rock cairn on the site, sometimes a marker. Many graves dug across the wagon trails.
- **Boot Hill.** The graveyard for the violent dead — those who died with their boots on. Tombstone, Dodge City, Deadwood, many others had one. **Cremation** was almost unknown among Anglos in this period; practiced by some nations. **Sky burial / scaffold burial** by Lakota and other plains tribes — the body wrapped in a blanket and lashed to a platform on poles or in a tree, exposed to the elements and scavengers.
- **Coffins.** Pine box, six boards, made by the town carpenter for $5–$15. The undertaker did not exist as a profession in many towns; the carpenter built the coffin and the family or the dead man's friends dug the grave.
- **Embalming.** Civil War practice spread; common by the 1880s for shipping a body home. Uncommon in working towns where the body went into the ground the next day.
