## Phase 6 — References (craft)

New craft modules to create in `references/`.

- [ ] **P1** `references/pov-and-distance.md`
  Point of view as a strategic craft decision. Close-third
  vs. omniscient vs. first-person in western prose. How to
  stay inside a character's perceptual range without slipping
  into therapy-speak interiority. The omniscient pull-back
  to landscape (McCarthy's move) vs. the close-third camera
  (McMurtry). Unreliable narrators. How to signal POV shift
  cleanly. When interior is earned and when it is a tell.
  Before/after rewrites for POV drift. Length target: ~500
  lines.

- [ ] **P1** `references/story-structure.md`
  How westerns are built above the sentence level. The
  canonical structural patterns: arrival-in-country opening,
  the mid-story reversal, the long-trail middle, the town
  confrontation, the departure or the burial ending. How
  McMurtry compresses months into paragraphs; how McCarthy
  works in geological time; how Portis uses the picaresque;
  how Hall builds a moral case over chapters. Scene function
  (what each scene must do to earn its place). Chapter
  openings and closings. The ending problem (what makes a
  western novel end vs. just stop). Before/after rewrites.
  Length target: ~500 lines.

- [ ] **P1** `references/landscape-as-prose.md`
  Landscape writing as craft, not as fact dump. How to move
  from frontier-survival.md's facts into actual prose.
  The distinction between landscape as backdrop vs. landscape
  as moral weather. The specific palette approach: three or
  four physical facts chosen for emotional resonance, not
  for completeness. The McCarthy method (landscape as
  indifferent; time as geological). The McMurtry method
  (landscape as known and familiar). How to write light at
  different times of day in desert, plains, mountains. The
  rule of one: one landscape sentence per scene opening does
  more work than five. Before/after rewrites. Worked passages
  for: Llano Estacado, the Rio Grande, the Colorado high
  country, the Montana plains. Length target: ~500 lines.

- [ ] **P2** `references/violence-as-craft.md`
  A dedicated craft module. The grammar of a gunfight (the
  silence before; the speed of the event; the silence after).
  The difference between a duel, a brawl, a massacre, an
  ambush — each has a different structure and a different
  moral weight. Violence as weather vs. violence as event.
  How the canonical writers handle it: McCarthy (violence
  as weather, no slowdown); Hall (moral reckoning); Lansdale
  (grotesque and sometimes absurd); Hansen (documentary
  precision). The aftermath as more important than the act.
  The author's position: who gets to feel the violence and
  how. The wounds that do not kill and the wounds that do.
  Hard rules: no slow-motion; no choreography; no hero
  invulnerability. Before/after rewrites. Length: ~500 lines.

- [ ] **P2** `references/humor-and-irony.md`
  The complete absence of humor in current skill is a
  production defect. McMurtry is frequently funny (Call and
  Augustus, Gus's cooking, Newt's bewilderment). Portis's
  Mattie is ironically comic in her deadly seriousness.
  Leonard's dialogue turns on wit. Lansdale's dark comedy
  defines his Texas work. The tall tale is a genuine western
  literary form. The laconic deadpan is a voice mode.
  Cover: the tall tale structure (the setup, the escalation,
  the straight-faced delivery); the laconic wit (understatement
  as comedy); the ironic narrator; the comedy of competence
  gap (the dude among cowhands); bunkhouse humor. Period
  humor forms (the stump speech, the newspaper squib, the
  practical joke). What makes period humor funny vs. what
  makes it cruel. Before/after rewrites. Length: ~400 lines.

- [ ] **P3** `references/journey-narrative.md`
  The journey is the fundamental western form (the cattle
  drive, the emigrant trek, the cavalry patrol, the
  prospector's route). How to structure a journey narrative:
  the departure (what is being left behind defines what the
  journey costs), the middle country (time compression vs.
  the specific day that stands for many), the arrival (which
  is often a failure, a transformation, or both). The
  problem of the journey's middle: keeping motion interesting
  over many pages. The technique of the landmark (water,
  river, pass, town as structural markers). How Lonesome
  Dove structures the drive. How Hansen structures the
  outlaw flight. How Portis structures the journey to
  Cheesman. Before/after exemplar passages. Length: ~400
  lines.

## Phase 7 — Knowledge (historical/factual)

New knowledge modules to create in `knowledge/`.

- [ ] **P1** `knowledge/outlaw-craft.md`
  The practical criminal knowledge the corebook requires.
  How outlaw gangs actually operated between jobs (the
  hideout's physical reality — not a romantic canyon camp
  but a cramped, cold, hostile space; the safe house; the
  look-out system; the coded message). The mechanics of each
  major crime type: train robbery (the stop procedure, the
  express car, the messenger, the safe, the dynamite, the
  split, the scatter); bank robbery (the front man, the town
  read, the horses, the vault's limitations, the clock, the
  pursuit); stage robbery (the road agent's halt, the
  messenger and his shotgun, the express box, the box-off-
  the-road method). Horse theft operations. Cattle rustling
  procedure. The fence and the receipt and movement of
  stolen goods. How posses actually worked (who formed them,
  how fast, how far they rode, their limitations). The
  economics of the outlaw trade (most made far less than the
  dime novels claimed; the James-Younger gang's Northfield
  disaster; the Wild Bunch's actuarial problem). Writing the
  sympathetic outlaw without lies. Length: ~600 lines.

- [ ] **P1** `knowledge/native-material-culture.md`
  What peoples-and-conflict.md deliberately omits: the
  physical, daily, material world of the major nations
  encountered in 1870s fiction. Section per nation grouping:
  Southern Plains (Comanche: the tipi, the horse, the
  raiding party, the winter camp, Comanche material culture
  in detail — weapons, dress, food, economy); Northern
  Plains (Lakota: the camp circle, the warrior society,
  the Sun Dance, the winter count, the specific material
  objects a Lakota man carried); Southwest (Apache: the
  wickiup vs. the tipi, the ranchería, the raiding economy,
  the specific material culture); Diné (the hogan, the
  sheep economy, the weaving, the silver work, the clan
  system); Pueblo (the multi-story pueblo, the ceremonial
  calendar, the agricultural economy). Plains Indian Sign
  Language basics. How to write a Native character in scene
  procedure parallel to competence-procedures.md — the
  specific verbs and nouns for their work. What to read
  (Hämäläinen, Gwynne, Welch, the primary sources). Length:
  ~600 lines.

- [ ] **P1** `knowledge/gambling.md`
  Faro: the bank, the layout, the casebook, the copper,
  the call, the sucker's game. Monte: the Spanish origin,
  the layout, the dominant game of the Southwest and of
  Mexican gamblers, how to play and write it. Poker in its
  period form (five-card draw; five-card stud after 1864;
  the vocabulary — pot, ante, call, raise, fold, full house,
  dead man's hand). The professional gambler as a character
  type (how he dresses, how he moves, what he does with his
  hands, what his tells are, what he does when cheated).
  The cheating methods: the holdout device, marked cards
  (sand-tell, luminous marks), shaved cards, cold decks,
  partners at the table. The faro bank's house edge and why
  it was beatable (barely). The specific vocabulary of each
  game. How to write a gambling scene without stopping the
  story to explain the rules. Wrote exemplars for faro and
  poker scenes. Length: ~500 lines.

- [ ] **P2** `knowledge/army-life.md`
  The physical reality of service in the 1870s Army of the
  West. The post: layout (officer's row, enlisted barracks,
  parade ground, guard house, magazine, hospital, sutler's
  store, stables); daily routine (reveille at 0545, guard
  mount, drill, fatigue details, stable call, retreat, tattoo,
  taps); the social divide between officers and enlisted; the
  sutler's store and its prices and its credit; pay day and
  the hog ranch outside the post. The field campaign:
  breaking camp, column of march, the pack train, the
  Indian scouts, the bivouac (no tents on campaign), rations
  on the march (hardtack, bacon, coffee), the fight (the
  cavalry charge vs. the Apache hit-and-run), the pursuit,
  the return to post. The Buffalo Soldiers' specific
  experience (best NCOs in the Army; worst posts; the racial
  dynamics with white officers; pride). Courts-martial.
  Desertion. Army music (the calls and the regimental bands).
  Length: ~500 lines.

- [ ] **P2** `knowledge/religion-and-faith.md`
  Religion was the center of most people's understanding of
  the world and it is nearly absent from the skill. The
  circuit-riding Methodist preacher (his itinerary — how
  many miles, how many congregations, what he was paid);
  the frontier Baptist revival meeting (the camp meeting,
  the mourners' bench, conversion, speaking in tongues,
  the physical reality of a revival); Catholic missions
  and parish life in the Southwest (the *padre*, the
  *ranchos* community, the feast day, the saint's image);
  the Mormon settlement (Sunday service, tithing, the
  bishop's storehouse, plural marriage as domestic reality,
  the specific vocabulary of Latter-day Saint practice);
  the Black church (the AME church, the prayer meeting, the
  sermon register); the role of faith in birth and death
  (the bedside prayer, the deathbed conversion, the
  scripture at a frontier burial); specific Bible passages
  that appear in period literature (Psalms 23, 91; Ecclesiastes;
  Revelation); what a man said when he prayed and what he
  did not say. How the canonical writers use religion without
  condescension or mockery. Length: ~400 lines.

- [ ] **P2** `knowledge/food-culture.md`
  A dedicated module pulling together what is scattered
  across multiple files and adding what is missing. The
  trail-drive cook's full equipment list and what each
  item was for. The recipes that appear in period fiction:
  son-of-a-gun stew (specific ingredients and why it was
  made, including the parts of the animal that went into it),
  sourdough biscuit (the starter, the process, the cook's
  devotion to the starter), beans (the three-day cook, the
  soaking, the salting timing, the period of service), Texas
  chili (the period preparation, no beans in the pot). The
  homestead wife's kitchen: the wood stove, the baking
  schedule, the preserving calendar (what was canned by
  month, what was smoked, what was dried). The mining camp
  mess: the miner's boarding house table (the Cornish
  pasty, the Irish stew, the Scandinavian board). Chinese
  restaurant cooking on the frontier (the chop suey myth,
  the actual Chinese camp cook's work, what he could and
  could not source). The Army ration (hardtack, salt pork,
  beans, the desiccated vegetable disc the soldiers called
  *baled hay*). Feast vs. famine as a prose tool: what it
  meant when a character had enough to eat. Length: ~500
  lines.

- [ ] **P2** `knowledge/regional-landscapes.md`
  The specific sensory palettes of the eight or nine major
  sub-regions of the 1870s American West. For each: the
  ground cover and the specific plants a character would
  know and name; the specific rock and soil colors; the
  water sources (or their absence); the wildlife that appears
  in a correctly-set scene; the weather patterns and the
  specific sounds; the specific light at different hours;
  the human settlement character. Regions: the Texas Hill
  Country and Edwards Plateau; the Llano Estacado and the
  Caprock; the Chihuahuan Desert and the Trans-Pecos; the
  Rio Grande bosque and the New Mexico territory; the
  Colorado Rockies (range by elevation zone); the Great
  Basin; the Montana and Wyoming high plains; the Black
  Hills and Dakota country; the Sonoran Desert of Arizona.
  Specific plant and animal names for each region — the
  nouns that place the reader in the country. Length:
  ~600 lines.

- [ ] **P3** `knowledge/borderlands.md`
  The U.S.-Mexico border region as a specific literary
  environment. The physical landscape: the Trans-Pecos,
  the Rio Grande crossings, the Chihuahuan desert, the
  specific border settlements (El Paso del Norte / El Paso,
  Presidio, Eagle Pass, Laredo, Brownsville). Mexico in the
  1870s: Juárez's republic, Lerdo de Tejada, the *tuxtepecazo*
  and Porfirio Díaz coming to power in 1876. The *rurales*
  as lawmen and their limits. The cross-border economy
  (legal: cattle, silver, wool; illegal: stolen cattle,
  contraband silver, weapons). How legal authority ended at
  the river (or didn't — the Texas Rangers and the Mackenzie
  Raid, 1873). The *corrido* as a literary form: the border
  ballad, the outlaw hero, the structure of the corrido (the
  naming, the deed, the flight). The specific culture of the
  border town (the *presidio* origin, the *plaza* center,
  the two-language commercial zone). Length: ~400 lines.

- [ ] **P3** `knowledge/women-domestic-world.md`
  A dedicated module for the women's interior world that is
  currently scattered across three files without a home.
  The domestic material culture of the frontier woman: the
  kitchen inventory, the sewing basket, the garden in its
  season. The specific rhythms of the domestic day on a
  homestead vs. a ranch vs. a mining town vs. a cattle town.
  The friendship networks between women (the quilting bee,
  the childbirth support, the church social, the visiting
  by horse and buggy). The specific pressures unique to
  women: the homestead isolation, the fear of childbirth,
  the management of a sick household without a doctor, the
  dressmaking calendar (when a new dress was made, from
  what, at what cost). The women writers of the period who
  describe this from the inside (Elinore Pruitt Stewart's
  *Letters of a Woman Homesteader*; Mollie Dorsey Sanford's
  journals; Elizabeth Custer; Frances Roe; Martha
  Summerhayes). How to write a scene from inside a woman's
  experience vs. from outside it. Length: ~400 lines.

- [ ] **P3** `knowledge/documents-and-print.md`
  Letters, legal documents, newspapers, and official papers
  as scene props and as voices. Period newspaper content and
  voice: boosterism (the frontier paper's relentless advocacy
  for its town's growth), political partisanship (the
  Republican and the Democratic papers fighting through print),
  crime reporting (the lynching reported as community service;
  the Indian raid reported as atrocity), the specific
  vocabulary of the period editor (*our esteemed subscriber,
  a miscreant of the most depraved stripe, we are reliably
  informed*). Wanted posters: format, typical language, reward
  scale by crime. Legal documents: the deed, the bill of sale,
  the promissory note, the indictment, the coroner's verdict.
  Commercial documents: the mercantile account, the consignment
  note, the bill of lading. The private letter: formats by
  class and education. How to use a document as a scene device
  without making the scene about explaining the document.
  Length: ~400 lines.

- [ ] **P3** `knowledge/horse-culture.md`
  The horse beyond cowboy work. Horse racing as social event
  (the quarter-mile sprint down a main street, the crowd, the
  betting, the specific vocabulary of the race). Mustang
  running and catching (the *mustanger's* work: the trap, the
  walk-down, the gentling). The Army remount system (how the
  cavalry sourced horses, what they paid, what they selected
  for). The social meaning of specific breeds and colorings
  (a blue roan as working-class, a Thoroughbred as officer's
  class, a *caballo* as borderland culture). Horse theft:
  the procedure (the night approach, the quiet removal, the
  run across the border or to the next county), the
  consequences (the hanging that awaited a caught horse thief
  and why the community enforced it so absolutely), the
  difference between horse stealing and borrowing without
  permission. The specific vocabulary of horse color, gait,
  and conformation that marks a knowledgeable character.
  Length: ~400 lines.

## Phase 8 — Examples (new exemplars for scenes.md)

Append to the existing 20 exemplars in `examples/scenes.md`.
Each needs: the worked passage, then the critique that names
what makes it work and what it refuses.

- [ ] **P1** `#21 The mine — underground`
  A hardrock miner's shift underground. The dark (only
  candles on the helmet, the tallow smell, the specific
  dimness), the sound (the drill and the hammer, the drip,
  the creak of the square-set timbers), the physical labor
  (single-jack or double-jack drilling), the moment of
  blasting (the count, the fuse, the run, the wait, the
  smell of the spent powder). Demonstrates the competence
  register applied to mining work per `work-and-trades.md`
  and the forthcoming `knowledge/army-life.md` section on
  the working mine.

- [ ] **P1** `#22 The Army at the post — a soldier's morning`
  A private (or corporal) at a frontier post, from reveille
  to stable call. Demonstrates: the specific vocabulary of
  Army life (the calls, the formation, the fatigue detail,
  the stables), the Buffalo Soldier in his specific situation,
  the officer/enlisted dynamic shown through behavior not
  description. To be written parallel to competence-
  procedures.md — the soldier's work in the terse register.

- [ ] **P1** `#23 Deadpan humor — the dude and the cook`
  A scene of laconic western wit. The subject: an Eastern
  visitor trying to eat trail-drive food while the cook
  watches. Demonstrates: the humor of competence gap, the
  deadpan delivery, the tall-tale structure, the one-line
  payoff. Shows that the skill produces comedy as well as
  weight. Critique names the McMurtry and Portis registers.

- [ ] **P2** `#24 A woman's scene from the inside`
  A homestead wife or widow in her domestic space, written
  from the inside of her experience and her perceptual range.
  Not a scene *about* a woman — a scene *from* a woman. Her
  specific work (preserving or sewing or the garden in one
  task), her specific interior geography (what she looks at,
  what the house contains), a moment of crisis or choice
  delivered in the western register: action as interiority,
  per `references/masculinity-and-code.md`. Critique notes
  what existing western fiction does with women and what it
  refuses.

- [ ] **P2** `#25 Journey compression — the middle of the trail`
  A passage that compresses ten or fourteen days of travel
  into a paragraph (the long ride, the weather, the
  accumulation of country) and then zooms in to one specific
  moment on one day. Demonstrates the time-compression craft
  per the forthcoming `references/story-structure.md`, and
  contrasts it with the single-day detail. Should feel like
  a chapter of Lonesome Dove in miniature.

- [ ] **P3** `#26 A gambling scene — faro`
  A scene at a faro bank. Three characters: the dealer, the
  lookout, the player. The specific mechanics of faro in the
  prose (not explained, shown). The specific vocabulary
  (the case board, the copper, the call, the split). The
  table as a social space: what men do at a faro table that
  is not playing faro. Critique names the knowledge in the
  forthcoming `knowledge/gambling.md`.

- [ ] **P3** `#27 A Native character's scene`
  A scene written inside a Native character's perceptual
  range. Specific nation (Comanche or Lakota — whichever
  the writer chooses for the period). The scene should
  ground itself in the material culture from the forthcoming
  `knowledge/native-material-culture.md`: specific objects
  owned and used, specific verbs for specific work. The
  critique must address the challenge of writing a character
  whose world does not use English as its primary conceptual
  language.

## Standing rules for new modules

Each new file must:
1. Open with a one-paragraph statement of purpose and its
   relationship to the other files it complements.
2. Have concrete worked passages, not just lists.
3. End on the most important rule or the hardest truth,
   not on a summary.
4. Stay within the anti-AI-patterns rules — no em-dash
   clusters, no rule-of-three conclusions, no synonym
   cycling, no positive uplift endings.
5. Cross-reference any file it overlaps with by name.
