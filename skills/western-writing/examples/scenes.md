# Worked Examples — Ten Scenes With Critique

Theory is cheap. Examples are the test. Each section below is a
worked passage in the western register the rest of this skill
teaches. Each is followed by a critique that points to the rules
in `references/` and the knowledge in `knowledge/`.

These are originals, written for this skill. They are not
imitations of any specific living writer's prose. They model
the principles. Use them as benchmarks; do not copy them.

---

## 1. The chapter opener

> The herd had been four days on the Cimarron with no water
> and the lead steer had begun to bawl. McNair sat his horse
> at the point and watched the gray morning come up over the
> grass. Behind him a thousand head moved slow in the dust
> their own hooves had raised. Mendez rode up out of the swing
> and reined in alongside.
>
> _They will smell water by noon,_ Mendez said.
>
> _They will smell something._
>
> They sat a while. The wind moved in the grass and a bird
> called once and went on. McNair pulled his hat down against
> the sun coming.
>
> _Ride back and tell the boy to bring the wagon up,_ he said.
> _We will not make the river by dark and I want him close._
>
> Mendez turned the horse and rode. McNair watched the steer
> bawl again and the herd lift its hundred heads and lower
> them. He spat into the dust and put his hand to the rein.
> The day was already old.

### Critique

- Opens with motion and a problem (no water, four days, the
  bawling steer). The reader is in the country and in the work
  before the second sentence.
- Period detail is concrete: the Cimarron, the lead steer's
  bawling, the swing position, the dust. See `cowboy-life.md`.
- Two characters introduced by surname only — McNair and
  Mendez, an Anglo and a Tejano — by their position in the
  drive. Their relationship is shown through the brief
  exchange (Mendez rides up, makes a guess; McNair corrects
  with a half-joke; gives an order). No exposition.
- Dialogue is plain and without tags-of-feeling. The italics
  for dialogue follow the McCarthy-influenced western style
  (no quotation marks). This is a stylistic choice; the
  alternative (standard quotation) is also period and works.
- _The day was already old_ — ends on weight, see
  `references/anti-ai-patterns.md` rule on positive uplift
  endings. The line carries the threat of the day to come
  without explaining it.
- Sensory: the wind in the grass, the bird, the dust. Sparse
  but specific.
- No anachronisms. _Sat his horse, point, swing, lead steer,
  remuda_ (implied), _the wagon_ — period vocabulary. See
  `knowledge/language-and-diction.md`.

## 2. Landscape

> The country east of the Pecos lay flat and pale to a
> horizon that did not seem to come any closer for the riding.
> A man could see a mounted figure ten miles off and not know
> whether it was an hour away or four. There was no shade.
> There was no water for thirty miles in any direction the
> riders cared to name. A coyote had crossed their track in
> the night. They saw its prints in the morning, fresh, going
> south.

### Critique

- Land first, then human meaning, then evidence of life.
- The middle sentence (_A man could see... not know..._) is
  the one that does the work. It tells the reader what kind
  of country this is by what it does to the eye, not by
  describing the eye.
- _There was no shade. There was no water for thirty miles_ —
  parallel structure used sparingly, not as the AI rule of
  three. Two declaratives, not three. See `references/
anti-ai-patterns.md` on rule-of-three overuse.
- The coyote print is the closing detail. Not an explanation,
  not a metaphor, just a thing in the world. The reader
  registers that something else moves in this country, and
  that the riders saw it.
- No emotional language at all. The dread is in the country
  itself.
- See `knowledge/frontier-survival.md` on water and distance.

## 3. Dialogue, Leonard register

> _You ride for him?_
>
> _I do._
>
> _You been with him long?_
>
> _Long enough._
>
> The bartender set the bottle down between them. The man on
> the right side of the bar took the bottle by the neck and
> poured for himself and set it back. He did not pour for the
> other man.
>
> _I knew his brother,_ he said.
>
> _Did you._
>
> _In Brownsville. Year before last._
>
> _Then you know more about him than I do._
>
> The man on the left took the bottle and poured. He filled
> the glass past the line a careful drinker would have
> stopped at and he watched the other man's face while he did
> it. The other man did not look up.
>
> _I am not looking for anything,_ the first man said.
>
> _No man is, until he finds it._

### Critique

- Pure Elmore Leonard register. See `references/voices.md` on
  Leonard.
- No tags of feeling. _He said_ and the action between lines
  carry everything.
- The information delivered is minimal: one man rides for
  someone, the other knew the someone's brother, somewhere in
  Texas, recently enough to remember. The reader feels the
  pressure without being told what is at stake.
- The pour-past-the-line is the showing detail. The reader
  knows what it means.
- _No man is, until he finds it_ — a line that does several
  things. Folk-philosophical period register. Closes the
  exchange on weight.
- The piece is short. It earns no resolution and gives none.
  The next paragraph would land hard whatever it carried.

## 4. Violence

> Ramsey came through the door fast and Bell was already
> standing. Bell shot him once and Ramsey kept coming and
> Bell shot him a second time and Ramsey took two more steps
> and sat down at the table where the hands of the dead man
> from the first round of cards were still face down on the
> green cloth. He sat there a moment looking at the table and
> the cards. Then he laid his head down on the table beside
> them. The pistol he had not yet drawn was still under his
> coat.
>
> Bell stood holding the gun. Smoke hung in the room and the
> bartender was gone behind the bar. A clock on the wall went
> on counting the seconds it had been counting before either
> man came in.

### Critique

- Two shots, not five. A man shot twice does not fly back; he
  takes two more steps and sits. See `knowledge/material-
culture.md` on what a gunshot wound actually does.
- The undrawn pistol under the coat is the period detail. The
  reader has just learned that Ramsey came in to kill Bell
  and was killed first. This is the most period-accurate kind
  of frontier killing — see `knowledge/towns-economy-law.md`
  on the rarity of the choreographed quick-draw.
- The hands of the dead man from the first round — a piece
  of context the reader fills in (someone died here earlier;
  the cards are still on the table; this is the second
  killing in this room).
- The smoke hanging is period (black powder; see
  `knowledge/material-culture.md`).
- The clock that goes on counting — closes the scene on weight
  without commentary. The world does not stop for the killing.
  This is the McCarthy / Hansen note. See `references/
voices.md`.
- No adjectives of horror. No _suddenly._ No _and then._ The
  prose does the work by showing.

## 5. Death

> The fever came up the second night. By the third he could
> not be got to drink. The doctor pulled the dressing back
> and looked at the wound and put it back the way it had been.
> He did not say anything to the woman who sat by the bed but
> he stood a moment longer than he needed to before he picked
> up his bag and went out.
>
> She sat by him through the fourth day. He spoke twice. The
> first time he asked for his mother. The second time he
> spoke a name she did not know and after that he did not
> speak again. The smell that had come into the room on the
> third day stayed in her hair after he was buried. She would
> wash and wash and at night smell it still on the pillow.

### Critique

- Gut wound. See `knowledge/medicine-and-death.md` on the
  three-to-seven-day course, the smell, the refused water.
- The doctor's silence is the entire diagnosis. He does not
  tell her the man is dying; he stands a moment longer than
  he needs to.
- Two spoken moments, both small, both the kind of thing a
  dying man actually does (a man calling for his mother is
  ancient and true; the unknown name is the heart-twist).
- The smell in her hair is the closing image. It does the
  grief work without naming grief. The reader has the wife,
  the loss, and the residue all in two lines.
- Past simple throughout. No interior reflection. No _she
  felt_ or _she could not believe._
- The McMurtry note (the unsentimental tracking of human
  feeling) and the Hansen note (the documentary precision)
  meet here. See `references/voices.md`.

## 6. Work — branding

> The fire was hot enough by midmorning. Curtis pulled the
> first iron and held it up and the metal glowed dull red
> and went to orange where the air took it. He turned the
> letter to look at it and nodded at Wilkins, who was sat on
> the calf with his knee on the calf's neck.
>
> _Bring her,_ Wilkins said.
>
> Curtis came down the line and laid the iron on the calf's
> hip and held it. The hair smoked first and then the hide.
> The calf bawled and lurched and Wilkins kept her down. Hop
> the boy stood with the bucket of axle grease and when
> Curtis took the iron away Hop dipped his finger and laid
> the grease on the brand. The smell of burned hair and burned
> hide came up off the work. The other calves at the rope
> waited their turn.
>
> Curtis walked the iron back to the fire and stood it in the
> coals to take the heat back up. He looked across the corral
> to where the cook had his pot on. The cook lifted a hand
> without looking up.
>
> _That's eight,_ Curtis said.
>
> _We got forty more,_ Wilkins said.
>
> They went on.

### Critique

- A working scene with the rhythm of work. Nothing happens
  beyond the work, but the reader is in the corral and feels
  the fire and the smell.
- Period detail: the iron heating, the brand applied to the
  hip, the axle grease (period treatment to keep the burn
  from getting fly-blown), the boy at the bucket, the rope
  with calves waiting. See `cowboy-life.md`, `knowledge/work-
and-trades.md`.
- The cook's lifted hand is a small humanizing beat without
  comment.
- The closing exchange — _That's eight. We got forty more_ —
  is the western work register. The job is large, the men
  are tired, the day is not over. _They went on_ closes the
  scene with weight.
- No adjectives of effort. The work is the prose.
- Three named characters by surname. One named by his role
  (Hop the boy). The cook is unnamed. This is the period
  hierarchy on the page.

## 7. Town — saloon

> Marlow came in out of the cold and stood a moment letting
> his eyes find the room. The lamps were turned low. A man at
> the faro layout was losing slowly and not minding it. Two
> drovers at a table by the wall watched him come in and went
> back to their cards. The bartender, a German named Kessel
> whom Marlow had known in another country, set a glass on
> the bar and looked at him.
>
> _Whiskey,_ Marlow said.
>
> _I figured._
>
> Kessel poured. Marlow put a coin on the bar and Kessel slid
> it back.
>
> _I have not seen you in eight years,_ Kessel said. _I will
> stand the first one._
>
> Marlow took the glass and drank. Behind him the piano started
> up, the player drunk and not minding it either. The two
> drovers had gone back to their game with the kind of careful
> attention to it that meant they had stopped paying attention.
> A man at the back of the room watched Marlow in the bar's
> mirror.
>
> _He has been here three days,_ Kessel said. _He asked of
> you the second night._
>
> Marlow set the glass down empty.
>
> _Pour another, then,_ he said.

### Critique

- Town economy and population on the page. Lamps low (oil
  lamps; see `knowledge/towns-economy-law.md`), faro layout
  (the period gambling game), drovers (the period word for
  cowhands), German bartender (the period demographic; see
  `knowledge/peoples-and-conflict.md`), piano (every saloon
  with money had one), bar mirror (the period feature).
- Three layers of attention: Kessel and Marlow's reunion;
  the watching drovers and the watched stranger; the piano
  drunk in the back. The scene moves by the layering.
- _I will stand the first one_ — period idiom for _I'll buy
  this round._ See `knowledge/language-and-diction.md`.
- The man in the mirror at the back of the room. The reader
  knows what is coming. Marlow knows. The order of the next
  drink is the only thing on the page.
- Closing on the order of another whiskey is the kind of
  weight the period earns. Marlow is not afraid; he is going
  to drink before the work, and the reader feels the cool of
  it.

## 8. Camp

> They had made the camp on the lee side of a fold of ground
> where the wind off the high country broke partway. Cookie
> had a fire in a pit dug to the height of his knee and a
> reflector of stones built behind it. The coffee pot was
> already on. The men sat in their bedrolls with their boots
> still on and ate beans out of tin plates and did not talk.
> The horses were strung on a picket line at the edge of the
> firelight.
>
> The boy who handled the remuda came in and got his plate
> last and sat with the men and ate. Nobody spoke to him.
> Nobody had to. He had the work down now and the men knew it.
> When he had finished his beans he set the plate down and
> took his coffee in both hands the way a man does in the
> cold. The cup steamed against his face.
>
> Out beyond the firelight a coyote called and after a
> moment another one answered.

### Critique

- Working drover camp. The fold of ground (period plains-
  craft of pitching camp out of the wind), the pit fire and
  the reflector (period camp technique; see `knowledge/
frontier-survival.md`), the coffee pot perpetually on
  (period and central; see `cowboy-life.md` on the chuck
  wagon), the picket line (period horse handling).
- The boy who has earned his place without being told he
  has — a small character moment with no commentary. The
  men's silence is the praise.
- The coyote call at the end — closes on the country itself.
  Not a metaphor, just a fact. The land is around them.
- No adjectives of comfort or discomfort. The reader feels
  the cold by the boy's two hands on the cup, the sit-with-
  boots-on, the wind broken by the fold of ground.

## 9. Weather catastrophe — the blue norther

> The sky in the north went black at noon. McNair saw it
> first because he was riding the point and had nothing else
> in front of him. He sat the horse a moment. Then he turned
> in the saddle and called to Mendez and Mendez looked north
> and saw it and started riding back along the herd without
> waiting for orders. The boy with the wagon was already
> turning the team.
>
> The temperature began to drop while McNair was still
> giving the order. The wind came twenty minutes after the
> first sight of the cloud and it came hard. The herd had
> begun to drift south as the cattle felt it. McNair pulled
> his slicker from behind the cantle and got it on with one
> hand on the rein. The slicker was stiff with cold by the
> time he had it tied.
>
> The first snow came not in flakes but in pellets that
> stung the face. The light went out of the day. McNair
> could no longer see the wagon. He could no longer see
> Mendez. He could see his horse's ears and the gray that
> was the herd in front of him and that was all.
>
> By dark he had lost the count and was riding by the
> sound of the cattle. By midnight he could no longer hear
> them and rode by the wind on his face and what he
> remembered of the country. The cold had got under the
> slicker and he could not feel his feet in the stirrups.
> He thought of the boy in the wagon and could not think
> of him long because his mind would not stay where he put
> it. He rode.

### Critique

- A blue norther scene. See `knowledge/frontier-survival.md`
  on what a norther actually does — the temperature crash,
  the speed of arrival, the killing capacity. The 50°F drop
  in two hours is real.
- Period gear — the slicker behind the cantle, the team
  with the wagon, the picket-line system off-page.
- The drop in observable detail as the storm closes in
  (he could no longer see the wagon, then Mendez, then any-
  thing past his horse's ears) is the period weather scene
  done by sense-narrowing.
- _His mind would not stay where he put it_ — the period
  hypothermia interior, simple and accurate. See `knowledge/
medicine-and-death.md` on the cognitive sequence in the
  cold.
- The closing one-word paragraph — _He rode_ — is the kind
  of weighted ending the western register earns. The work
  goes on while the man cannot feel his feet.
- Notice no chapter-break or rescue. The scene ends with the
  ride continuing. The reader holds the unresolved cold.

## 10. The mixed rules-and-fiction bridge — for the corebook

This last example is the bridge between fiction prose and rule
text in a roleplaying corebook. The skill teaches western prose;
this corebook puts that prose against the mechanics. The
example shows how the two modes can sit together without
breaking either.

> The wound is a hard fact in this country. A man shot in the
> belly will live two days, three at the most, and he will
> not live well. The doctor will not open the abdomen. The
> family will not be told what they already know. The man
> himself will know it before any of them, and what he does
> with the time is the thing the table will play out.
>
> When a character takes a Critical Injury to the Torso, roll
> on the Torso table. On a roll of 7 or higher, the wound is
> a gut wound. The character is at −2 on all rolls and may
> not heal naturally. He may receive Medical Care, but the
> Medicine roll is at Hard difficulty. He has 2d6 days. The
> Game Master should describe the symptoms by day, drawing
> on the medical reference in the corebook (see _Medicine
> and Death,_ page XX). The player may take a final action on
> any of those days; the action does not have to be heroic
> and is not graded by the Game Master. It is only what the
> character does. After the action the character dies.
>
> A gut wound is the western table at its most honest. The
> rule will not save the character; the table will not save
> the character; the player has only the time and the choice
> of what to do with it. Use it sparingly. When you use it,
> do not soften.

### Critique

- First paragraph: pure prose, period in voice, period in
  fact. Sets the world the rule will operate in.
- Second paragraph: rule text, plain and procedural. Note the
  vocabulary shift — the prose voice steps back and the
  designer voice takes over. _Critical Injury, Torso table,
  Medicine roll, Hard difficulty._ The rule is unambiguous.
  Note also the integration: the rule sends the player back
  to the medical reference (which is the actual prose of the
  game's knowledge base).
- Third paragraph: design commentary. Returns to the prose
  voice but as the designer addressing the reader at the table.
  Names the rule's purpose (_the western table at its most
  honest_) and gives use guidance (_sparingly_ and _do not
  soften_).
- This three-beat structure — fiction frame, rule, design
  commentary — works for any hard rule a western corebook
  carries (gunshot wounds, child mortality, lynching, race
  violence, addiction, suicide). The frame earns the rule;
  the rule does its work; the commentary tells the GM what
  to do with it.
- The voice never slips. The fiction passages do not turn
  into rule text. The rule passage does not get poetic. The
  commentary does not preach.

## How to use these examples

When you write a passage in this skill's register, compare it
against the closest example here. Ask:

1. Does my opening do what the example opening does — put the
   reader in the country and the work?
2. Does my dialogue earn its lines as in #3 — no tags of
   feeling, the work in the action between lines?
3. Does my violence land as in #4 — period-accurate gunshot
   physics, the small detail (the undrawn pistol) carrying the
   meaning?
4. Does my death scene work as in #5 — two or three sensory
   details, a refused word, a ringing ending?
5. Does my work scene have the rhythm of the trade as in #6 —
   the right tools, the right verbs, the men talking the way
   men working talk?

If your passage falls short on any of these against its model,
you have a target for revision. Rewrite. Compare again.

The examples are not the ceiling. They are the floor. A good
western passage does what these do and more — surprises with
specificity, lands an image the reader will remember, opens
a window into a man or a country the reader did not know was
there.

Write up. The shelf is full of writers (see `references/
voices.md`) who set the bar high and reward the writer who
tries to clear it. Try.
