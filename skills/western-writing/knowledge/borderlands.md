# Borderlands — The US-Mexico Frontier

This file is the knowledge module for the US-Mexico
borderlands of the 1870s. It complements
`knowledge/peoples-and-conflict.md` (the wider
political frame), `knowledge/native-material-culture.md`
(the Apache and Comanche raid economies that crossed
the border in both directions), and
`knowledge/regional-landscapes.md` (the specific
country of the Trans-Pecos, the Sonoran Desert, and
the lower Rio Grande). For Hispanic voice and
register, see `references/speech-patterns.md`.

The popular West has been written almost entirely
from the Anglo side of the border. The historical
West does not work that way. The border was a porous,
violent, commercial, intermarried, and contested
zone. The same families lived on both sides. The same
cattle and horses crossed in both directions, often
multiple times in their lives, often without anyone's
permission. The same languages — Spanish, English,
the various Native languages, the trade pidgin —
were heard on both sides.

A western set on the border that does not render the
Mexican side, the Mexican characters, the Mexican
institutions, and the Mexican-American population is
writing the wrong country. This file is the reference
for getting it right.

---

## 1. The geography of the border

The 1870s US-Mexico border ran from the mouth of the
Rio Grande on the Gulf of Mexico to San Diego on the
Pacific. It was established by the Treaty of
Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848) and the Gadsden Purchase
(1854). Most of it was unfenced, unmarked except for
occasional surveyor monuments, and unguarded.

### The eastern border

The Rio Grande from El Paso del Norte (now Ciudad
Juárez) to the Gulf. The river itself was the border;
the river was crossed at fords and ferries. The
river shifted course (Rio Grande meanders are
characteristic), creating disputes about which side
of the new channel a piece of land was on (the
Chamizal dispute, not resolved until 1963).

Major crossings: Brownsville/Matamoros, Rio Grande
City/Camargo, Laredo/Nuevo Laredo, Eagle Pass/
Piedras Negras, Del Rio/Ciudad Acuña, El Paso/Ciudad
Juárez.

### The western border

A surveyor's line west from El Paso to the Pacific.
This was the desert border, crossing the Sonoran and
Chihuahuan deserts. Few settlements. The border was
crossed where there was water: at the few springs
and at the small towns near them.

### The country on both sides

The Mexican states bordering the US in 1870 were:
Tamaulipas, Nuevo León, Coahuila, Chihuahua, Sonora,
and (until the Gadsden Purchase had taken its
northern part) Baja California. The northern Mexican
states were thinly populated, dominated by large
ranching estates (haciendas) and small farming
villages, and as embattled by Apache raids as the
American Southwest was.

## 2. Population and language

### Hispanic populations on the U.S. side

The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo guaranteed citizenship
and property rights to Mexicans in the ceded
territory. Hispanic populations remained throughout
the Southwest — in northern New Mexico (the
Hispano villages and the great families like the
Oteros, Lunas, Chaves), in southern Texas (the
Tejano ranchos and towns), in southern Arizona (the
Sonoran population around Tucson), in California
(the Californio ranchos, declining rapidly under
American pressure).

In 1870, much of the Southwest was Spanish-speaking.
Court proceedings, town councils, newspapers, and
social life were conducted in Spanish in many areas.
The Anglo immigrant adapted or did not function. By
1900, the demographic and language balance had
shifted decisively toward English, but in 1870 the
shift was incomplete.

### The new Mexican migration

The 1870s saw beginning northward migration of
working Mexicans seeking employment in the
expanding U.S. Southwest economy: railroad
construction, mining, agriculture (especially the
cotton fields of south Texas and the citrus and
vegetable fields of California). The labor migration
that would define the twentieth-century border was
just starting in our period.

### The languages of the border

- **Spanish.** The dominant language south of the
  border and a major language north of it.
- **English.** Spreading rapidly north of the border,
  rare south of it.
- **Native languages.** Yaqui, Mayo, Tarahumara,
  Comanche, Apache, and others.
- **Border Spanish.** A specific dialect with regional
  vocabulary, English loanwords, and idioms not
  found in Spain or central Mexico.
- **Caló.** The argot of the border — a mixed Spanish-
  English-Native vocabulary used in some specific
  communities (the pachuco caló was a later
  development).

### A scene's language

A scene set on the border should render the language
mix honestly. A few practices that work:

- Render Spanish dialogue in English with occasional
  Spanish words (specifically the words for which
  there is no English equivalent — _mesa, arroyo,
  rebozo, alcalde, jefe, vaquero_).
- For longer Spanish utterances, render in Spanish
  with a clean translation following or implicit in
  context.
- Avoid ungrammatical English as a marker of
  Spanish-speaker (the Speedy Gonzales register).
  Render the Spanish-speaker's English as the English
  of an educated bilingual when appropriate, and as
  hesitant or limited only when the character would
  actually have hesitant or limited English.
- Use _usted/tú_ distinctions where they matter to
  the scene. The English _you_ flattens a social
  signal that the Spanish-speaking character would
  hear.

## 3. The cattle and horse trade

The border's principal economic activity, legal and
illegal, was the movement of cattle and horses.

### The legal trade

Mexican cattle were imported into Texas and the
Southwest legally through customs at the major
crossings. The traffic was substantial: tens of
thousands of head per year through the 1870s, paying
duties, herded north to U.S. markets.

### The illegal trade

The illegal traffic was perhaps as large. Mexican
cattle stolen by U.S. cattle thieves and driven
north for sale at U.S. markets. U.S. cattle stolen
by Mexican thieves and driven south. Indian raiding
parties (especially Apache and Comanche through the
1870s) drove stolen stock from one side to the other
and sold it through fences on the receiving side.

The cattle thief on the border was a specific type:
operating on both sides, knowing the country, often
allied with corrupt officials. The "Cattle Kingdom"
of King Fisher, the operations of John King Fisher
and others in south Texas, are documentable cases.

### The remount trade

A major specific trade was horses for the U.S.
remount service. Mexican horses (the small, tough,
hardy criollo stock descended from the Spanish
horses) were valued by the U.S. cavalry. Whether
purchased honestly or stolen and laundered, they
moved north in numbers.

### The fence

The fence on the border was different from the fence
in the U.S. interior because the border itself
provided the laundering. Cattle stolen in Texas
were driven across the river, brands altered or
left to grow over, sold to a Mexican rancher who
might or might not know they were stolen, then
perhaps sold north as Mexican cattle into the legal
trade. The cycle could run several times for a
single animal.

## 4. Mexican law enforcement — the rurales

The Mexican federal rural police force (the _Cuerpo
de Rurales_) was established by Benito Juárez in
1861 and built up under Porfirio Díaz (president
from 1876). The rurales were a mounted national
police force charged with maintaining order in the
countryside and pursuing bandits.

### The force

By 1880, the rurales numbered about 2,500 men in
several corps. They were concentrated in the central
Mexican states and the border states. They were
mounted, armed (initially with rifles and revolvers,
later more standardized), and uniformed (a
distinctive gray-and-silver uniform with a wide
sombrero).

### The methods

The rurales had a fearsome reputation for summary
justice. Suspected bandits were often shot on
capture under the _ley fuga_ (the "law of flight" —
the official explanation was that the prisoner was
shot while attempting escape; the actual practice
was execution without trial). The rurales were
effective at suppressing banditry but at high cost
in due process.

### The character

The rurale in fiction is rarer than he should be.
He is a parallel to the Texas Ranger or the U.S.
marshal — the federal mounted lawman in difficult
country. A scene with a rurale officer crossing
paths with a Texas Ranger is a scene the canon has
not used enough.

For source: Paul Vanderwood, _Disorder and
Progress: Bandits, Police, and Mexican Development_
(the academic standard).

## 5. The Texas Ranger

The Anglo counterpart, broadly. The Texas Rangers
were re-established in 1874 (the Frontier Battalion)
after the post-Civil-War period of dormancy. Their
brief was Indian fighting, anti-banditry, and
general law enforcement in the unsettled country.

### The force

The Frontier Battalion at full strength was several
hundred men in six companies, each company assigned
a region. Each company was about thirty to seventy
men under a captain. They were mounted, armed
(typically Winchester or Sharps rifle plus revolver),
unuiformed (no standard uniform; each Ranger
provided his own clothes and equipment), and paid
modestly.

### The methods

The Texas Ranger's methods were aggressive and
informally lawful. He pursued, he engaged, he
arrested or killed. The Ranger reputation for
violence on the border (especially against Tejano
populations during the late nineteenth and early
twentieth centuries) is documented and has been the
subject of significant historical reassessment.

The famous Rangers of the period — Leander McNelly
(whose company conducted the controversial 1875 Las
Cuevas raid into Mexico), Lee Hall, John Armstrong,
Sergeant Ira Aten, John Hughes — provide character
models. McNelly is a particular case: brilliant,
brutal, dying of tuberculosis, willing to violate
the border for what he understood as Texan
interests.

### The Mexican-American Ranger

A complicating fact: some Tejano men served as
Texas Rangers (notably during the period). The
Hispanic Ranger is a figure rarely written and
worth writing.

## 6. The corrido

The Mexican border ballad. The musical form that
recorded the border's events from the Mexican
perspective.

### The form

A corrido is a narrative folk ballad, typically in
quatrains, with a regular meter (octosyllabic
lines), a guitar accompaniment, and a sung
narrative. The corrido tells a story — a famous
killing, a folk hero's exploits, a notable event,
an outlaw's career and capture, a flood or a fire.

### The structure

A typical corrido has:

- An opening salute to the listener (_Voy a cantar
  esta historia, señores, escuchen bien_ — _I am
  going to sing this story, gentlemen, listen
  well_).
- A statement of the protagonist and the situation.
- The narrative — a sequence of events, often with
  remembered dialogue.
- A moral or commentary.
- A farewell to the listener.

### The famous corridos of the border

The corrido tradition produced specific famous
ballads about specific events:

- **El Corrido de Joaquín Murrieta.** The California
  bandit of the 1850s; the corrido tradition
  preserved his memory long after Anglo accounts
  forgot or distorted him.
- **El Corrido de Gregorio Cortez.** A 1901 event
  (just past our period but the cultural
  formation is older) — Cortez killed a Texas
  sheriff in a misunderstanding and was pursued
  for hundreds of miles. The corrido became one of
  the most important Mexican-American folk songs.
  Américo Paredes's _With His Pistol in His Hand_
  (1958) is the scholarly reference and one of
  the most important works of Mexican-American
  cultural studies.
- **The corridos of the Mexican Revolution.** A
  later development (1910s) but the tradition's
  high point.

### The corrido in fiction

A scene with a corrido sung in a Mexican border town
cantina can carry historical and emotional weight.
The corrido is the people's history, the version
the Anglo records did not preserve. A character who
hears the corrido of his own father, his own town,
his own cause has been placed in a specific cultural
context.

For sources: Américo Paredes, _With His Pistol in
His Hand_ and _A Texas-Mexican Cancionero_; Manuel
Peña, _Música Tejana_.

## 7. The bandit and the social bandit

The border bandit was a complicated figure. Some
were criminals by any measure. Some were
Robin-Hood-figures in their communities, robbing
the Anglo establishment and sharing with Hispanic
poor. Some were political — the boundary between
banditry and revolutionary politics on the
nineteenth-century border was thin.

### The famous border bandits

- **Juan Cortina (Juan Nepomuceno Cortina).** A
  Mexican rancher of Brownsville who led an armed
  uprising in 1859 (the Cortina War) protesting
  Anglo abuses against Mexicans in south Texas.
  Continued to operate as a political-military figure
  on both sides of the border for decades. Both
  bandit and patriot, depending on perspective.
- **Joaquín Murrieta.** California gold-rush-era
  outlaw, killed (or perhaps not — the head shown
  was disputed) by California Rangers in 1853. The
  legend grew enormous on both sides.
- **Tiburcio Vásquez.** California outlaw, captured
  and hanged in 1875. Last of the major Hispanic
  outlaws of California.

### The social bandit framework

The historian Eric Hobsbawm's concept of the
"social bandit" applies to many border figures:
the outlaw who is supported by his community, who
robs the powerful and shares with the poor, who
embodies a resistance to dominant institutions. Not
all bandits were social bandits, but many border
figures fit the pattern. The Mexican bandit who
robbed only Anglo ranches and never Mexican ones,
and who could ride through Mexican villages
unmolested, was operating in a different moral
economy than the random highwayman.

## 8. The Trans-Pecos — the specific country

The country of west Texas west of the Pecos River
through Big Bend to El Paso. Specific landscape,
specific border culture.

### The country

Mountain ranges (the Davis, the Guadalupe, the
Chisos) rising from desert basins. The Rio Grande
running through the Big Bend in deep canyons (Santa
Elena, Mariscal, Boquillas). Sparse settlement.
Difficult access. The country was Apache and
Comanche raiding ground through the 1870s; the Army
established posts (Fort Davis, Fort Stockton, Fort
Quitman) along the southern overland route.

### The Big Bend

A particular country: the great loop where the Rio
Grande swings south and then north around the Chisos
Mountains. Hot in summer, cool in winter. Almost
no settlement in 1870s. A few Mexican villages along
the river (Boquillas del Carmen, San Vicente,
Santa Elena). Cattle ranching beginning by the
1880s. The country became Big Bend National Park
in 1944.

### The smugglers

The Big Bend was, and is, smugglers' country. The
unguarded border, the difficult terrain, the few
crossings — perfect for moving goods that needed to
move quietly. In the 1870s the principal contraband
was livestock and ammunition; later it became
liquor (during U.S. Prohibition) and other goods.

### Worked passage

> The trail came down out of the Chisos in the late
> afternoon and the river was visible from the divide
> as a thin green line in the brown country. Carlson
> sat his horse and looked at it.
>
> _Three more hours,_ Romero said. \_The crossing is
> at Boquillas. The water will be low this time of
> year.\_\_
>
> _And then?_
>
> \_Then we are in Coahuila, and the man we want is
> at his ranch ten miles south. If he is there.\_\_
>
> Carlson nodded and started his horse down the
> trail. The country between him and the river was
> ocotillo and creosote and the sotol stalks
> standing dry from last year's bloom. A roadrunner
> crossed the trail ahead of them and was gone into
> the brush.

## 9. The El Paso–Juárez crossing

El Paso and its sister city across the river, Ciudad
Juárez (then called El Paso del Norte, renamed for
Juárez in 1888), formed the major western border
crossing. The combined urban area was a single
economic unit.

### The setting

The Pass of the North (the Paso del Norte) is a
natural break in the southern Rockies, with the Rio
Grande flowing through. The Spanish crossing was
established in 1659. The U.S. side was a small
village until the railroad's arrival in 1881
(Southern Pacific from the west, AT&SF from the
north, just past our period); by 1880 El Paso was
booming.

### The 1870s crossing

In the 1870s, El Paso del Norte (the Mexican side)
was the larger settlement. The U.S. side was
smaller, rougher, with a reputation for violence. A
man crossed the border by walking across the
river's shallow ford or by ferry. Customs were
nominal. The two cities operated as one for daily
purposes.

### The voice

A crossing scene at El Paso–Juárez can render the
mixed character of border life: the customs man's
desk, the Mexican family with their possessions on
a wagon, the Anglo trader checking his bill of
lading, the peddler with his pack, the soldier on
detached duty going to or coming from Mexico for
purposes officially obscure.

## 10. The intermarried families

The border's social fabric was knit by intermarriage.
Anglo men married Tejana, Californio, and Sonoran
women throughout the period. The Anglo-Hispanic
families produced significant western figures: the
Pico, Vallejo, Bandini, and other Californio
families that allied with Anglo immigrants; the
Otero family of New Mexico; many ranching families
of south Texas.

A Hispanic-Anglo family's experience is one of the
border's central stories. The children of such a
marriage navigated two cultures, often being neither
fully accepted in the Anglo-Protestant sphere of
the nineteenth-century U.S. nor fully part of the
Mexican-Catholic society. Their story is the
border's story.

## 11. The hacienda — the Mexican rural estate

A hacienda was a large estate in northern Mexico,
typically including cattle ranching, agriculture, and
a fortified compound (the casco) housing the owner
(the patrón or hacendado), his family, the resident
workers (peones acasillados), the chapel, the store
(tienda de raya), and the workshops.

### The system

The hacienda system was the basis of rural Mexican
economy through the nineteenth century. Workers
were tied to the hacienda by debt (the tienda de
raya extended credit at inflated prices, ensuring
the worker could never pay off his debt and could
not legally leave) and by tradition. The relationship
between patrón and peón was paternalistic,
exploitative, and stable.

### The setting

A scene at a Mexican hacienda has specific furniture:
the casco's adobe walls, the central courtyard with
its well, the chapel where the family worshipped,
the open kitchen where the women cooked, the stables
for the patrón's horses, the corrals for the cattle,
the small jacales (huts) where the peones lived
beyond the casco walls.

### The voice

The hacendado's voice is formal Spanish, the voice of
educated landowners. The peón's voice is the voice
of working rural Mexicans, often with regional and
Native-language influences. A scene at the hacienda
can render the social distance through the
linguistic distance.

### Worked passage

> They came up the long dusty road to the casco of
> Hacienda San Felipe in the late afternoon and the
> bell at the chapel was ringing for vespers. The
> walls of the casco were ten feet high and the
> gate stood open. Inside the courtyard the well
> was working — a woman in a black rebozo was
> drawing water and pouring it into the trough for
> the patron's horses.
>
> Don Eulalio Vargas came out of the front door of
> the main house in a clean white shirt and a dark
> jacket. He was an old man with white hair and a
> face the color of saddle leather and eyes that did
> not give anything away.
>
> _Caballeros,_ he said, _bienvenidos. He estado
> esperando._
>
> Romero answered him in the formal Spanish that
> Vargas would expect. Carlson stood behind, his hat
> in his hand.

## 12. The cheat-sheet

- **Render both sides of the border.** The border was
  not a wall; it was a zone.
- **Render the language mix honestly.** Spanish words
  for Spanish things. Don't use bad English as
  shorthand for Spanish-speaker.
- **Know the institutions.** The rurales, the Texas
  Rangers, the customs officers, the alcaldes, the
  consulates.
- **Know the trade.** Cattle and horses moving in
  both directions, legal and illegal.
- **The corrido is the border's other history.** A
  scene with a corrido can carry the people's
  perspective.
- **The bandit is often more than a bandit.** Render
  the political, economic, and moral dimensions.
- **The Trans-Pecos and Big Bend are specific
  country.** Use the regional landscape module.
- **The hacienda is the rural Mexican setting.** It
  has specific architecture, social organization,
  voice.
- **Intermarried families are the border's social
  fabric.** Render them.

## 13. The rule the others serve

The border is not the edge of the West. It is the
West's other half. A novel that treats Mexico as
backdrop, Mexicans as supporting cast, and Spanish
as decoration is a novel that has not done its work.

The honest border novel renders the river crossings,
the ranching estates, the cantinas with corridos,
the rurales and the Rangers, the Anglo-Hispanic
families, the smugglers and the patriots and the
exiles. It uses Spanish where Spanish belongs. It
treats the Mexican character as a person with
politics and a history. It refuses the cartel of
bad clichés that has dominated the border in
American fiction since the dime novels.

The border is the West's most fertile setting for
the writer who can do the work. The work is real.
