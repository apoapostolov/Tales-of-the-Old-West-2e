# Army Life — The Frontier Post and the Field

This file is the knowledge module for U.S. Army life
on the frontier in the 1870s. It complements
`knowledge/peoples-and-conflict.md` (the wider context
of the Indian Wars and federal policy) and
`knowledge/competence-procedures.md` (general procedural
craft for any working scene). For the soldier as
character, see also `references/speech-patterns.md`
(the army's distinct register) and
`references/violence-as-craft.md` (the military
engagement).

The frontier army of the 1870s was small (about
twenty-five thousand men), scattered across hundreds
of posts, undermanned, underpaid, and engaged in
small-unit operations against highly mobile
adversaries. The romantic cavalry charge of the
movies was not the army's daily work. The daily work
was garrison drudgery, escort duty, scouting, and
the long, slow, grinding business of campaign that
might never produce a battle.

The writer who renders army life faithfully needs to
know the post, the routine, the field column, the
rations, the hierarchy, and the speech. This file is
the reference.

---

## 1. The frontier post

### Layout

The standard frontier post was an open square (the
parade ground) surrounded by buildings:

- **Officers' row.** One side of the square. Wood-frame
  or adobe houses for the officers and their
  families. The post commander's house was usually
  the largest. Officers' row was the post's social
  center.
- **Enlisted barracks.** Opposite officers' row.
  Long single-story buildings (wood, adobe, or
  stone), each housing a company (about fifty men
  in the 1870s). A single open bay with bunks along
  both walls; small attached kitchen and mess; the
  first sergeant's room at one end.
- **Headquarters / adjutant's office.** A small
  building, often near the flagpole at the parade
  ground's center.
- **Quartermaster and commissary storehouses.** Heavy
  buildings containing the post's supplies. Guarded.
- **Stables.** For cavalry posts. Long open barns,
  with the saddler's shop and farrier's shop nearby.
- **Hospital.** Small (one or two wards), staffed by
  a contract surgeon.
- **Guardhouse.** The post's jail.
- **Sutler's store / post trader's.** A civilian
  business licensed to operate at the post, selling
  goods (tobacco, canned food, whisky, clothing) to
  officers and enlisted at posted prices. The
  sutler's was the post's only commercial space.
- **Laundresses' quarters (Soapsuds Row).** Often a
  separate row of shacks where the post's laundresses
  (frequently soldiers' wives) lived and worked.

Most posts had no walls. The stockade fort of the
movies was largely a fiction by the 1870s; the open
plan was standard. A few exceptions (Fort Apache, some
older posts) had walls.

### The flagpole

The center of the parade ground. The garrison's
formal heart. Reveille and retreat were called by
the bugler at the flagpole. The flag went up at
reveille and down at retreat, with the garrison at
attention.

### The post hierarchy

- **Post commander.** A senior officer (lieutenant
  colonel or colonel). Responsible for everything.
- **Adjutant.** A junior officer handling
  administration. The post's day-to-day manager.
- **Quartermaster.** Officer responsible for supply.
- **Surgeon.** A contract or regular medical officer.
- **Officers commanding companies.** Captains, with
  lieutenants under them. The company was the basic
  unit; a post might have two to six companies.
- **First sergeants.** The senior enlisted of each
  company. The actual day-to-day authority over the
  enlisted men. The first sergeant ran the company.
- **Other NCOs.** Sergeants, corporals.
- **Privates.** The enlisted bulk.

The relationship between the company commander and
the first sergeant is one of the great relationships
in army fiction. The captain is the formal authority;
the first sergeant is the practical authority. A good
pair worked together; a bad pair tore the company
apart.

## 2. The daily routine

The army day was structured by bugle calls. The bugler
was a critical figure; he announced every transition
of the day to the entire post.

### A garrison day

- **Reveille (about sunrise).** Bugle call, formation
  in front of barracks, roll call.
- **Stable call (cavalry).** Men go to the stables to
  feed, water, groom, and clean stalls. Cavalry was
  forty percent horse-care.
- **Mess call (breakfast).** Coffee, hardtack or
  bread, salt pork or beef, occasional eggs.
- **Sick call.** Men reporting illness present at the
  hospital.
- **Fatigue call.** Work details — wood-cutting,
  hay-cutting, repairing buildings, carrying supplies,
  digging latrines. The soldier's daily work was
  largely manual labor, not military exercise.
- **Drill (when held).** Formation drill, weapons
  drill, mounted drill (cavalry). Frequency varied;
  some posts drilled daily, some rarely.
- **Mess call (dinner, midday).** The main meal. Beef
  stew, beans, bread, coffee. Vegetables were rare.
- **Fatigue continues.**
- **Stable call.** Evening care of horses.
- **Mess call (supper).** Light meal. Often leftovers.
- **Retreat (sunset).** Formation, lowering of the
  flag.
- **Tattoo (about 9 PM).** Final formation, roll call.
- **Taps.** Lights out.

The day was punctuated by guard mount (about 9 AM),
when the new guard detail relieved the old. Guard
duty was twenty-four hours and was rotated through
the company.

### What the soldier did

The frontier soldier's primary occupations, in order
of time spent:

1. **Manual labor.** Wood-cutting, hay-cutting, road
   work, building repair, latrine digging.
2. **Horse care (cavalry).** Two formal stable calls
   plus continuous attention.
3. **Guard duty.** A regular rotation.
4. **Drill.** Variable.
5. **Escort duty.** Escorting paymasters, supply
   trains, surveyors, civilians.
6. **Scouting.** Small patrols.
7. **Combat.** The rarest activity. Years could pass
   between actions for many soldiers.

The romantic image of the cavalry trooper as a man
who rode and fought is misleading. He was a manual
laborer who occasionally rode and occasionally
fought.

## 3. The pay and the discharge

### Pay

A private earned thirteen dollars a month in the 1870s
(reduced from sixteen after 1871). Sergeants earned
about twenty. Officers earned considerably more (a
lieutenant about a hundred and twenty, a captain
about a hundred and fifty).

Pay came from the paymaster (a special officer) on
his rounds, every two months at most posts. The
paymaster carried the pay in cash, escorted by a
detachment.

The thirteen dollars went largely to the sutler (where
the soldier had run a tab), to the laundresses (about
a dollar a month), to gambling, and to drink. The
soldier rarely had money long after payday.

### Enlistment

Five years (cavalry) or three years (some periods,
infantry). Re-enlistment bonuses were modest. Many
soldiers re-enlisted because they had nowhere else to
go; the army was a refuge for men with no other
prospects.

### Discharge

Honorable discharge produced a paper that documented
service. Dishonorable discharge produced disgrace and
a record. Desertion was the army's epidemic problem;
about one in three enlistees deserted at some point.
Penalties (when caught) ran from forfeiture of pay to
imprisonment to (rarely) execution.

### The deserter

A common western figure. The man who has slipped
away from the army, often headed for the gold camps
or for Mexico. The deserter was hunted by the army's
provost marshals when worth the trouble. Many
deserters made new lives under new names.

## 4. The ration

Army rations were standardized. The soldier ate what
the regulations specified.

### The regulation ration

Per man per day:

- Twelve ounces of pork or bacon, OR
- One pound, four ounces of fresh or salt beef.
- Eighteen ounces of flour or bread, OR
- One pound, four ounces of cornmeal.
- One and a half ounces of beans or peas, OR
- One ounce of rice or hominy.
- One and three-quarter ounces of coffee (green
  unroasted), OR one and a half ounces of tea.
- Two and four-tenths ounces of sugar.
- Salt, vinegar, candles, soap (small quantities).

A small quantity of dried fruit, molasses, or
potatoes might be substituted. Fresh vegetables were
rarely available; scurvy was a real threat at remote
posts.

### Coffee

The army's true fuel. The soldier roasted his own
green coffee on a hot pan, ground it (often by
crushing in a sock with a stone), and brewed it
strong. Coffee was made at every halt. The phrase
_boil the coffee_ defined the day's structure.

### The mess

Each company had a mess kitchen, with a soldier-cook
detailed to the duty. The cook was rarely trained.
Food was monotonous: stew, beans, bread, coffee,
fried meat. A good cook was treasured; a bad cook
made life worse.

### Field rations

In the field, the soldier carried hardtack (a flour-
and-water cracker, sometimes weevily), salt pork,
and coffee. The hardtack soaked in coffee or fried
in pork grease was the day's substantial meal.

## 5. The cavalry — the field column

The cavalry column on campaign is the iconic frontier
army image. The reality was less romantic and more
exhausting.

### Composition

A typical column for an Indian-country campaign:

- Several troops of cavalry (a troop was about fifty
  men).
- A pack train (mules) or a wagon train (depending on
  country). Mules carried more across difficult
  terrain; wagons carried more along good roads.
- A small detachment of infantry (sometimes), to
  garrison supply camps or support fixed positions.
- Indian scouts (Crow, Pawnee, Apache, depending on
  the country and the campaign). The scouts were
  essential — the cavalry could not find the enemy
  without them.
- A surgeon and his assistants.
- A small staff (adjutant, quartermaster, signal
  officer if available).

### Pace and distance

A cavalry column on campaign moved fifteen to thirty
miles a day depending on terrain, weather, and
condition of horses. Pushing harder broke down the
horses. The Indian opposition could move faster
because the Indians had fresh horses (taken or
spared) and the soldiers did not.

The pursuit was the cavalry's recurrent failure. The
column would find a trail, follow it, and lose it as
the Indian band scattered into smaller groups across
broken country. After weeks of pursuit, the column
returned to base without engagement.

### The bivouac

The end of the day's march. The column halted at
water (the only consideration that mattered). Horses
were unsaddled, watered, picketed (tied to a picket
rope), grain rations issued. Men cooked their suppers
over small fires. Sentries posted around the
perimeter. The column slept in their boots with
weapons at hand.

A camp on a campaign was not pitched as a fixed
camp. Tents were rare in the field; men slept in
their blankets under their saddles. The cold camp
(no fire, when concealment mattered) was the most
miserable form. The hot camp with coffee and bacon
was the better night.

### The march

The column moved at a walk most of the time. The
trot was used for short distances; the gallop was
used for the rare charge. A man and a horse could
walk all day; trotting and galloping broke the horse
in hours.

The column rode in column of twos or fours, on a
trail or open country. Officers rode at the head and
flanks. The pack train followed in the rear with
its own escort.

## 6. The Buffalo Soldier regiments

After the Civil War, four regiments of Black enlisted
men were established under white officers: the 9th
and 10th Cavalry, the 24th and 25th Infantry. They
were stationed primarily on the southern plains and
the Southwest.

### The name

_Buffalo Soldier_ was a name given by Plains Indians,
its exact origin disputed (perhaps the men's hair, or
their tenacity, or their winter coats). The men
adopted the name as an honor.

### The service

The Buffalo Soldier regiments served in the Indian
Wars from the late 1860s through the 1890s. They
fought in the southern plains campaigns, the Apache
campaigns, the Red River War, the Geronimo
campaigns. Their service record was exceptional;
their combat record was strong. Eighteen Medals of
Honor were awarded to Buffalo Soldiers for action
during the Indian Wars.

### The conditions

Buffalo Soldier regiments received the worst horses
(remounted last from the army's central depots), the
oldest equipment, the least desirable posts. They
faced civilian hostility at most posts in the
South and Southwest. Their officers were exclusively
white through the period. Their re-enlistment rate
was very high — the army was, despite the prejudice
within it, a better life than what was available to
Black men in the post-Reconstruction South.

### Writing the Buffalo Soldier

The Buffalo Soldier is one of the western canon's
underwritten figures. The cavalry novels of the
mid-twentieth century were almost exclusively about
white regiments. A serious western from a Buffalo
Soldier perspective remains an opportunity.

A few sources: William Leckie, _The Buffalo
Soldiers_; Frank Schubert, _Black Valor_. The 1989
novel _Captain Newman, M.D._ by Leo Rosten and the
research surrounding the 9th and 10th Cavalries
provides material.

## 7. The officer's wife

The frontier post's social world was substantially
shaped by the officers' wives who lived on the post.

### Living conditions

Officers' quarters were small, often poorly built,
shared with cooks (typically the wives of enlisted
men) and sometimes with junior officers. The house
might be a four-room adobe with a kitchen at the
back, infested with mice and crawling insects, hot
in summer and cold in winter. The wife's life was
domestic management under difficult conditions.

### Social structure

Rank determined social precedence. The post
commander's wife outranked all others. Calls were
made and returned. Dinner parties were held. Children
were schooled (often by an officer's wife who took
on the duty). Books and newspapers arrived months
late.

### The literature

The frontier officer's wife is one of the period's
significant memoirists. Read Elizabeth Custer (_Boots
and Saddles, Following the Guidon, Tenting on the
Plains_), Frances Roe (_Army Letters from an Officer's
Wife_), Martha Summerhayes (_Vanished Arizona_).
These books are the best primary source on post life.

A scene from inside an officer's wife's life is a
scene the reader rarely gets. Worth writing.

### The enlisted man's wife

The enlisted man's wife (the laundress, often) lived
in Soapsuds Row, did the post's washing, raised
children in shacks, and was generally beneath the
notice of the officers' families. Her life was
harder than the officer's wife's life. Less
documented, less written, equally real.

## 8. Discipline and punishment

Army discipline in the 1870s was severe by modern
standards.

### Minor offenses

- **Extra duty.** Additional fatigue assignments.
- **Loss of pay.** Forfeiture of part of a month's
  pay.
- **Confinement to barracks or the post.**

### Serious offenses

- **The guardhouse.** The post jail. A man could be
  confined for days, weeks, or months.
- **Hard labor.** Wood-cutting, ditch-digging, latrine
  work, with a chain on the leg.
- **The bucking and gagging.** A punishment of being
  tied in a uncomfortable position with a stick gag
  in the mouth. Officially banned but unofficially
  used.
- **The wooden horse.** A man made to sit astride a
  narrow plank for hours, weighted at the ankles.
- **Court-martial.** For desertion, theft, striking
  a superior, mutiny. Penalties up to dishonorable
  discharge with prison or, rarely, death.

### Drinking

The army drank. The post commander drank. The general
drank (Grant's reputation was earned). Drunkenness
on duty was an offense; drunkenness off duty was
expected. The sutler's whisky and the smuggled rotgut
were constant.

The temperance soldier was rare and was suspected by
his comrades. The soldier who could hold his drink
without becoming a problem was respected.

## 9. The army's voice

See `references/speech-patterns.md` for the broader
period speech reference. Specific army features:

- **Rank in address.** Officers were addressed by
  rank: _Captain, Lieutenant, Colonel._ Enlisted by
  _Sergeant, Corporal._ A private was addressed by
  surname or as _Soldier_ or _Trooper._
- **The colonel's voice.** Officers from West Point
  had a Latin-saturated formal English. _I should be
  obliged, I am inclined to think, sir._
- **The first sergeant's voice.** Old-army NCOs were
  often Irish or German immigrants. The voice was
  practical, profane (in the field, less in earshot
  of officers), specific. _Move it, you sons of
  bitches. Mount up. Mount up._
- **The recruit's voice.** The new soldier in 1875 was
  often a recent immigrant (Irish, German, English,
  Italian) or an Eastern man with no other prospect.
  Multiple accents in the ranks.
- **The reports.** Written reports used a formal
  bureaucratic register: _I have the honor to
  report, in compliance with instructions, the
  command marched at sunrise._ A field report had
  its own form. A letter of complaint had its own
  form. A request for transfer had its own form.

A scene of an officer dictating a report is a scene
in which the formal voice can be heard against the
informal voice of the surrounding day.

## 10. A worked scene — morning at the post

> The bugle call came as it always came, before the
> first light, and Sergeant Doherty was up and shouting
> before the second note had ended.
>
> _Up. Up. Up. On your feet, you sons of bitches._
>
> The barracks of B Company, 6th Cavalry, came awake.
> Forty men rolled out of their bunks and into their
> uniforms in the cold dark. Doherty paced the
> center aisle with his lantern.
>
> _Reveille in five minutes. Five minutes._
>
> Outside, the parade ground was a square of frozen
> mud under a sky just turning gray. The men formed up
> in two ranks in front of the barracks, jackets
> half-buttoned, breath standing in the air. Lieutenant
> Hatch came out of officers' row in his greatcoat and
> took the report.
>
> _All present, sir,_ Doherty said, _excepting Hannigan
> who is in the guardhouse and Murphy who is at the
> hospital._
>
> _Carry on, Sergeant,_ Hatch said, and went back into
> his quarters where his wife had coffee waiting on
> the stove.
>
> Doherty turned the company toward the stables.
>
> The horses were waiting and the horses had to be
> watered and grained and groomed and the stalls
> mucked before the men could think about their own
> breakfasts. This was the cavalry. The horses came
> first. The horses came first every morning of the
> world.
>
> Doherty walked the line of stalls behind the men,
> looking. Trooper Reilly's horse had a swelling at
> the right fetlock that had not been there the day
> before.
>
> _Reilly,_ Doherty said. \_Take that animal to the
> farrier before the troop forms.\_\_
>
> _Yes, Sergeant._
>
> \_And if it is what it looks like, you are walking
> today.\_\_
>
> Reilly said nothing. Doherty moved on.

The scene's specifics — the bugle, the formation, the
first sergeant's voice, the stable detail, the horse
care that comes before the man's breakfast, the
lieutenant returning to his quarters and the wife's
coffee, the swelling at the fetlock — render the
army's day. The exposition is in the verbs.

## 11. The campaign — what could go wrong

The army campaign in the West offered specific
failure modes that the writer should know.

- **Heat exhaustion.** Summer marches in the southern
  plains and the Southwest killed horses and men. A
  forced march in July might mean a third of the
  horses dead by the end.
- **Frostbite.** Winter campaigns (the army made many
  in the late 1860s and 1870s, on the theory that
  Indians were less mobile in winter) produced
  amputations from frostbite. Custer's 1868 winter
  campaign and the 1876-77 northern campaign were
  brutal.
- **Sickness.** Dysentery was the army's killer.
  Bad water, bad food, bad latrine discipline. A
  campaign could lose more men to sickness than to
  combat.
- **Desertion.** Men slipped away from the column,
  especially when the pursuit was futile and the
  column had been out for weeks.
- **Failure to find the enemy.** The most common
  campaign result. The column marched, marched,
  marched, and saw no Indians at all.
- **Ambush.** When the column did find the enemy, it
  was often on the enemy's terms. The Fetterman
  fight (1866), the Wagon Box fight (1867), the
  Little Bighorn (1876), the Wounded Knee aftermath
  (1890) — each is a study in the army's
  vulnerabilities.

A campaign novel that ends without a battle is
historically more accurate than one that ends with
glorious victory. The army's frustration is the
campaign's real subject.

## 12. The cheat-sheet

- **The post is a working space.** Render the layout,
  the bugle calls, the daily routine. The drudgery is
  the truth.
- **The officer-NCO relationship is the key.** Render
  it.
- **Coffee is the fuel.** The bivouac is built around
  it.
- **Pay was thirteen dollars and went fast.** The
  sutler, the laundress, the gambler, the bottle.
- **The cavalry column moved at a walk.** The horse
  came first.
- **The Buffalo Soldier regiments existed and served
  with distinction.** Write them.
- **The officer's wife is a major character type with
  a strong literature.** Read it.
- **The campaign often ended without a battle.** The
  pursuit's failure is the campaign's truth.
- **The army drank.** Render the drinking honestly.
- **The voice is layered: officer formal, NCO
  immigrant, recruit Eastern or European.**

## 13. The rule the others serve

The frontier army was an institution of poor men under
the command of an officer class, doing hard manual
labor in difficult country, with occasional outbreaks
of violence that the popular memory has elevated into
the whole story. The writer's job is to render the
labor and the boredom and the bugle and the coffee
and the swollen fetlock, and to let the violence be
the rare and shocking thing it actually was.

The cavalry charge, when it comes, has the weight of
all the months of work that preceded it. Without
those months on the page, the charge is a cartoon.
With them, it is the chapter the reader will not
forget.
