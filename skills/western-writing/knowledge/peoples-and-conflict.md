# Peoples and Conflict — Who Was in the West

The American West of 1865–1890 was the most demographically
mixed region of the country. The screen tradition shrank it to
white men and Indians. The actual country had Mexicans whose
families had been there for two centuries before the United
States existed, Black freedmen and Buffalo Soldiers, Chinese
laborers, German farmers, Irish railroad navvies, Cornish hard-
rock miners, Bohemian and Czech homesteaders, French Canadians
in the trapping country, and dozens of Native nations each with
its own language, law, and history.

A western that flattens this mix into _cowboys and Indians_ is
not a western about the West. It is a western about a movie.

This file gives the writer the working population.

---

## 1. Anglo-Americans

A category that flattens what was never one people.

### a. Southerners

The largest group on the cattle frontier. Many were Confederate
veterans or sons of veterans. Texas was a Confederate state.
The Texas-to-Kansas drives carried south Texas culture (with
its Mexican vaquero techniques and its Gulf-coast Anglo
culture) north onto the open range. The southern accent was
the dominant one in cow country.

A southerner in 1875 came from a defeated country, an occupied
country (Reconstruction lasted 1865–1877), an impoverished
country. He had often lost family in the war. He resented
Federal authority. Some wore Confederate gray hats deliberately
into the 1890s. The unreconstructed Southerner is a real
character.

### b. Yankees

New Englanders, Mid-Atlantic men. Often the merchants, the
bankers, the editors, the schoolteachers, the army officers.
Disliked by the southerners and tolerated for the cash they
brought.

### c. Midwesterners

Iowans, Illinoisans, Ohioans, Missourians, Kansans. Many
homesteaders. Many small ranchers. Often Methodist or Baptist.
The bulk of the wagon-train emigration of the 1840s–60s.

### d. Mountain men and old timers

A vanishing class by 1875. The trapping era ended in the 1840s.
A few old beaver men were still around: Jim Bridger died 1881,
Kit Carson 1868. They had often married Native women, lived
between cultures, spoke half a dozen Native languages and
Spanish and broken French. Replaced by buffalo hunters, then by
cowmen.

### e. Easterners on the move

The remittance man (a younger son of a wealthy family sent
West with a quarterly check), the Eastern dude on a hunting
trip (Owen Wister, Theodore Roosevelt), the failed lawyer or
clerk starting over, the consumptive sent for the dry climate.
A regular feature of the Western town. Often the source of
condescension and amusement.

## 2. Mexicans / Tejanos / Hispanos / Californios

The pre-existing Spanish-speaking population of the territories
acquired in 1848 (the Mexican Cession) and 1853 (the Gadsden
Purchase). Distinct local communities.

- **Tejanos** — Mexican-Texans. Some families went back to the
  18th-century Spanish settlements at San Antonio and along the
  Rio Grande. Many were dispossessed of land grants under the
  Anglo legal system after annexation. Provided the foundational
  ranching culture of Texas (the rope, the saddle, the brand,
  the round-up, half the working vocabulary — _lariat, lasso,
  ranch, rodeo, corral, remuda, mustang, bronco, chaps, sombrero,
  buckaroo (vaquero)_).
- **Hispanos** — the Spanish-speaking population of New Mexico
  and southern Colorado. Older settlement than any Anglo
  presence west of the Mississippi (Santa Fe founded 1610).
  Largely rural, sheep-raising, deeply Catholic. The Penitente
  brotherhood — a lay religious order famous for Holy Week
  flagellation and crucifixion processions — was a major rural
  institution.
- **Californios** — pre-1848 Spanish-speaking Californians.
  Largely dispossessed in the gold rush and after. The grand
  rancho era ended quickly.
- **Mexican immigrants** — laborers, vaqueros, miners. Crossed
  the border for work and frequently across it.

The Anglo-Mexican relationship was poisoned by the Mexican-
American War, by the Texas Revolution, and by Anglo land theft.
Anglo violence against Tejanos and Mexicans was endemic and
largely unpunished. Texas Rangers killed hundreds of Mexicans in
the borderland through the period (the _La Matanza_ of
1915–1919 was a peak but the violence ran for half a century
before). Lynching of Mexicans in California, Arizona, New Mexico,
Texas was common — the historian William Carrigan estimates 597
lynchings of Mexicans in the United States 1848–1928, a per-
capita rate similar to Black lynchings of the same period.

A Mexican character in a western should not be a colorful sidekick
or a bandit type. He has a community, a faith, a language, often
old grievance and a long memory. Write him so.

## 3. African Americans

Massively under-represented in the screen tradition. Real and
substantial in the West.

- **Freedmen.** After Emancipation thousands of formerly
  enslaved people moved west, particularly to Kansas (the
  _Exodusters_ of 1879) and to all-Black towns: Nicodemus
  Kansas (1877), Boley Oklahoma (1903), Mound Bayou
  Mississippi (1887, just outside the West but the same
  movement).
- **Black cowboys.** Estimates: about one in four working
  cowhands on the major drives was Black. Famous: Bose Ikard
  (rode for Charles Goodnight, kept his money for him,
  inscribed on his tombstone _Splendid behavior, never failed
  a duty_), Nat Love (autobiography 1907), Bill Pickett
  (rodeo bulldogger).
- **Bass Reeves.** Deputy U.S. Marshal in the Indian Territory.
  Born enslaved in Arkansas, escaped during the Civil War,
  recruited as a deputy by Judge Parker. Made over 3,000
  arrests in his career. Often cited as a possible inspiration
  for the Lone Ranger. The most consequential Black lawman of
  the West.
- **Buffalo Soldiers.** The 9th and 10th U.S. Cavalry and the
  24th and 25th U.S. Infantry — the Army's Black regiments,
  formed 1866. Stationed across the Southwest and Plains.
  Fought in the Apache campaigns, the Red River War, against
  the Comanche, on the Mexican border. Named _Buffalo Soldiers_
  by the Plains Indians (the precise reason is debated:
  comparable courage; the texture of their hair). Suffered the
  worst posts, the worst supplies, lower desertion rates than
  white units, and a high incidence of distinguished service.
- **Black women in the West.** Worked as cooks, laundresses,
  domestic servants, prostitutes (often in segregated houses
  or as the lowest-status workers in mixed houses), school-
  teachers in Black communities, sometimes ranchers and
  homesteaders.

The 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments were federal law. Their
enforcement on the western frontier varied. Some states (Kansas,
in particular) had relatively open Black communities. Others
(Texas, Oklahoma after statehood) imposed Jim Crow with full
violence. A Black character's experience varied entirely with
where he was. Get the geography right.

## 4. Chinese

The Chinese came first to the California gold fields in the
1850s, then in great numbers to build the Central Pacific Railroad
(1865–69, perhaps 12,000 to 20,000 Chinese laborers blasted and
graded the line over the Sierras). After the railroad they
spread across the West as miners, laundrymen, cooks, vegetable
gardeners, fishermen on the California coast, domestic servants.

Most were men — the population was deliberately kept male by
Chinese custom and by U.S. law (the Page Act of 1875 effectively
barred Chinese women, and the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882
barred most Chinese immigration outright). The men sent money
home, intended to return, often did not.

Anti-Chinese violence was extreme:

- The Los Angeles massacre of 1871 (18 Chinese killed by a
  white mob).
- The Rock Springs massacre, Wyoming, 1885 (28 Chinese miners
  killed).
- The Hells Canyon massacre, Oregon, 1887 (34 Chinese miners
  killed and robbed).
- Anti-Chinese expulsions from Tacoma, Seattle, Eureka, dozens
  of mining towns.

A Chinese character on the page is rare in published westerns
and should not be drawn as comic relief or cipher. He is a man
in a country whose laws and customs are arrayed against his
existence; he has a story and likely a family ten thousand miles
away. Use his actual conditions and not the caricature.

## 5. Native nations

Not _Indians_ as a single people. Hundreds of nations across the
West, each with its own language, government, religion, law,
economy. A few of the major ones a writer encounters:

### a. Southern Plains

- **Comanche.** The horseback empire of the southern plains
  (Texas, eastern New Mexico, the Llano Estacado). Six bands
  (Quahadi, Yamparika, Penateka, Nokoni, Kotsoteka, Kwahadi —
  spellings vary). The dominant power on the southern plains
  for 150 years. Quanah Parker (mixed Comanche and Anglo) was
  the last war chief; his band surrendered at Fort Sill 1875
  after Mackenzie's destruction of their winter camp at Palo
  Duro Canyon in 1874.
- **Kiowa.** Allied with the Comanche through the period.
  Plains horsemen, raiders into Texas and Mexico. Surrendered 1875.
- **Apache.** Multiple peoples (Chiricahua, Mescalero, Jicarilla,
  Lipan, Western Apache) across the Southwest. Irregular
  warfare specialists. The Chiricahua under Cochise (until
  1872), then under Victorio (until 1880), then Geronimo
  (until 1886) fought the U.S. Army to a stalemate for a
  generation.
- **Cheyenne and Arapaho.** Originally northern plains; some
  bands moved south to the Arkansas River. The Sand Creek
  Massacre (1864 — Colonel Chivington's Colorado militia
  attacked Black Kettle's peaceful camp, killing 150–200 mostly
  women and children) and the Washita Massacre (1868 — Custer's
  7th Cavalry attacked Black Kettle's camp again, killing him
  this time) were southern Cheyenne tragedies.

### b. Northern Plains

- **Lakota Sioux.** Seven divisions (Oglala, Hunkpapa, Sicangu,
  Mnikowoju, Sihasapa, Itazipco, Oohenupa). The dominant
  power of the northern plains. Sitting Bull (Hunkpapa), Crazy
  Horse (Oglala), Red Cloud (Oglala), Spotted Tail (Sicangu).
  Fought the U.S. through the 1860s (Red Cloud's War, ending
  in the Treaty of Fort Laramie 1868) and through the Great
  Sioux War of 1876–77 (the Little Bighorn, the surrender of
  Crazy Horse, the flight of Sitting Bull to Canada and his
  return). The Wounded Knee Massacre (1890) ended the era.
- **Northern Cheyenne and Arapaho.** Allies of the Lakota in
  most fights.
- **Crow.** Traditional enemies of the Lakota; allied with the
  Army as scouts.
- **Blackfoot.** Northern Montana and into Canada. Smallpox
  epidemics (1837, 1869) destroyed huge fractions.
- **Pawnee.** Nebraska. Traditional enemies of the Lakota;
  Pawnee scouts served the Army.

### c. Southwest

- **Diné (Navajo).** Largest Native nation in the U.S. The
  Long Walk (1864 — forced march from their homeland to Bosque
  Redondo in eastern New Mexico, mass starvation, return in
  1868 to a reduced reservation). Sheep-raising, weaving (the
  famous Navajo blankets), silverwork. Continuing presence on
  the Colorado Plateau.
- **Hopi.** Pueblo people on the high mesas of northeast Arizona.
  Twelve villages, agricultural, ancient. Largely peaceful
  through the period.
- **Pueblo nations.** Tewa, Tiwa, Towa, Keres, Zuni speakers.
  Nineteen pueblos along the Rio Grande and to the west. The
  oldest continuously inhabited communities in the United
  States. Largely Catholic by 1875 (Spanish conversion since
  the 16th century, with the Pueblo Revolt of 1680 a major
  interruption).

### d. Northwest and Great Basin

- **Nez Perce.** Of the Wallowa Valley in Oregon. Chief Joseph's
  retreat — 1,170 miles across Idaho, Wyoming, Montana, pursued
  by the U.S. Army, ending 40 miles from the Canadian border
  with Joseph's surrender (_From where the sun now stands, I
  will fight no more forever_). 1877.
- **Modoc.** Northern California / southern Oregon. The Modoc
  War (1872–73) — Captain Jack and 50 warriors held off the
  Army for months in the lava beds, killed General Canby (the
  only U.S. general killed in the Indian Wars), and were
  eventually overcome.
- **Bannock and Shoshone.** Great Basin and Snake River.
  Sacagawea (Shoshone) had guided Lewis and Clark seventy
  years before.
- **Ute.** Colorado and Utah.

### e. Indian Territory

The five _civilized tribes_ — Cherokee, Choctaw, Creek
(Muscogee), Chickasaw, Seminole — removed from the Southeast in
the 1830s (the Trail of Tears) and resettled in the Indian
Territory (modern Oklahoma). By 1875 they had constitutional
governments, courts, schools, newspapers (the _Cherokee
Phoenix_). Many were slaveholders before the Civil War; many
fought on each side; the Reconstruction treaties of 1866 imposed
Federal terms. The Allotment Acts of the 1880s and 1890s broke
up their tribal lands. Statehood (Oklahoma) 1907.

The Indian Territory also held the resettled Plains tribes after
their wars (Comanche, Cheyenne, etc.) on reservations carved out
of the western half. The Territory was federal jurisdiction;
Judge Parker's court at Fort Smith had jurisdiction over Anglo
crimes in it. Outlaws hid in the Territory because state law did
not reach them.

### f. How to write Native characters

- **Specific nation.** Not _Indians_ but _Comanche,_ _Lakota,_
  _Diné._ Use the people's own name where you know it.
- **Specific religion.** A Lakota in 1875 may follow Lakota
  traditional religion (the Sun Dance, the seven sacred rites)
  or be a Christian (some were) or both.
- **Specific language.** Lakota, Comanche, Diné Bizaad, Apache
  — different language families. A Comanche and a Cheyenne
  do not understand one another's tongue. They use Plains
  Indian Sign Language for cross-tribal communication.
- **Specific period.** A Comanche in 1840 is at the height of
  Comanche power. In 1875 he is on a reservation, his horse
  herd shot, his bands broken. Period-correct.
- **Specific person.** Names from the actual record (cited
  carefully — these are not mascots) or invented within the
  naming conventions of the people.
- **Avoid the noble-savage cliché AND the murderous-savage
  cliché.** Both are inventions of Anglo writers, neither the
  Native person.
- **Read the actual sources.** _Black Elk Speaks_ (Black Elk and
  Neihardt). _The Comanche Empire_ (Hämäläinen). _Empire of the
  Summer Moon_ (Gwynne — popular history of the Comanche). N.
  Scott Momaday's _House Made of Dawn_ and _The Way to Rainy
  Mountain._ James Welch's _Fools Crow_ (Blackfoot novel set in
  the 1870s — perhaps the best historical novel of Native life
  by a Native writer). Leslie Marmon Silko's _Ceremony._ Joy
  Harjo. Louise Erdrich.

A good writer of westerns reads at least two of those before
writing a Native character.

## 6. European immigrants

The frontier was a continuing destination for European
immigration through the period.

- **Germans.** Texas Hill Country (Fredericksburg, New Braunfels,
  settled 1840s by the Adelsverein). Wisconsin, Minnesota, the
  Dakotas. Brewers (Adolphus Busch, Frederick Pabst, Jacob
  Schlitz, Adolph Coors), farmers, freighters, gunsmiths
  (the firm of Daly in Liege, the Lefaucheux of Paris, but the
  individual gunmaker in San Antonio or Dodge often German).
  Lutheran or Catholic.
- **Irish.** Built the Union Pacific from Omaha west.
  Catholics, often. Soldiers in the Army (a third or more of
  enlisted men through the period were Irish-born). Workers in
  the mines (Butte was an Irish mining town). Saloon-keepers.
- **Scandinavians.** Norwegians, Swedes, Danes. The northern
  prairies — Minnesota, the Dakotas, eastern Montana. Wheat
  farming. Lutheran. Ole Rölvaag's _Giants in the Earth_ is
  the great novel of this immigration.
- **Cornish.** Hardrock miners (the _Cousin Jacks_) — from the
  played-out tin mines of Cornwall to the silver and copper
  mines of Butte, Leadville, the Comstock. Wesleyan Methodist
  often. Brought specific mining techniques (the Cornish
  square-set timbering, the Cornish pump).
- **Italians.** Stoneworkers, miners (especially in the coal
  fields of Colorado and Wyoming after the 1880s), railroad
  laborers, farmers (Napa Valley vineyards).
- **Welsh.** Coal miners, particularly in Pennsylvania but
  also in the western coalfields.
- **Bohemian and Czech.** Nebraska and the Plains. Catholic.
  Willa Cather wrote them: _My Ántonia,_ _O Pioneers!_
- **French Canadians.** Older trapping country (the Rockies,
  the upper Missouri). The mountain man Jim Beckwourth (mixed
  Black and white American), the trapper Toussaint Charbonneau
  (French Canadian, Sacagawea's husband). By 1875 a fading
  presence.
- **Mormons.** Religious refugees from the Eastern United
  States after the persecutions of the 1830s and 40s. The Salt
  Lake settlement (1847), the colonization of much of Utah,
  southern Idaho, southern Nevada, northern Arizona. Plural
  marriage practiced openly until 1890. A distinct society
  with its own theocratic governance under Brigham Young
  through 1877.
- **Russians and Eastern Europeans.** Volga Germans (Russian-
  Germans expelled by Russification policies after 1871) settled
  Kansas and the Dakotas, brought Turkey Red wheat that became
  the Plains' staple crop. Russian Jews settled urban centers.

## 7. Women in this world

Cross-reference `references/uncomfortable-truths.md` section on
women's lives. Add here:

The frontier was statistically male. The 1880 census of Wyoming
showed 75 men for every 25 women in the working-age population.
Mining camps could be 95 men to 5 women. This shaped everything:

- The economic and social value of any woman.
- The high rate of marriage and rapid remarriage of widows.
- The size of the prostitution economy.
- The acute isolation of homestead women.
- The relative power of women in territorial politics — Wyoming
  granted women's suffrage 1869, the first U.S. jurisdiction to
  do so. Utah followed in 1870. Statehood in those states kept
  the suffrage.

Specific groups:

- **Anglo wives.** Of homesteaders, ranchers, townsmen,
  soldiers (army wives at the western posts kept letters and
  diaries that are major sources — Elizabeth Bacon Custer,
  Frances Roe, Martha Summerhayes).
- **Anglo single women.** Schoolteachers, nurses, stenographers
  (after the typewriter spread in the 1880s), milliners,
  dressmakers, prostitutes. A few prospectors, ranchers, and
  homesteaders in their own right.
- **Mexican women.** Wives, mothers, widows, washerwomen,
  cooks, laundresses, prostitutes. Older Catholic culture,
  often less restricted than the Anglo Protestant model in
  social mixing.
- **Black women.** Cooks, laundresses, domestic servants,
  midwives, prostitutes, schoolteachers in Black schools.
  Often the economic anchor of a Black family because Black
  men's wages were lower.
- **Native women.** A wide range. A Diné woman owned the family's
  sheep flock and the hogan; descent and inheritance were
  matrilineal. A Lakota woman owned the tipi and its contents.
  An Apache woman ran her household and shared in band
  decisions. The Anglo assumption of Native women as drudges
  was a misunderstanding of different gender economies.
- **Chinese women.** Few. Largely concentrated in Chinatowns.
  Many were brought as indentured prostitutes (the _yellow
  slave trade_). Donaldina Cameron's mission rescues in San
  Francisco from 1895 onward.

## 8. The conflicts

A short ledger. Each is a possible setting; each is a backdrop
for character.

### a. The Indian Wars

- The Plains War (1854–1890) — innumerable engagements. Major:
  - The Sand Creek Massacre (1864).
  - The Fetterman Fight (1866).
  - The Washita Massacre (1868).
  - Red River War (1874–75) — broke the southern plains tribes.
  - The Great Sioux War (1876–77) — the Little Bighorn, Crazy
    Horse's surrender.
  - The Nez Perce War (1877).
  - The Apache Wars (1849–1886) — Cochise, Victorio, Geronimo.
  - Wounded Knee (1890) — last major engagement. 250–300 Lakota
    killed, mostly women and children, by the 7th Cavalry.

### b. The range wars

- The Lincoln County War, New Mexico (1878). Mercantile factions
  fought a small war; Billy the Kid's name was made.
- The Johnson County War, Wyoming (1892). The big cattlemen's
  hired guns invaded a county to kill suspected rustlers and
  small ranchers. The town of Buffalo and the small ranchers
  besieged the invaders; only Federal cavalry intervention
  saved the cattlemen from being killed.
- The Mason County War, Texas (1875). Anglo and German factions.
- Many smaller fence-cutting wars after barbed wire.

### c. The labor wars

- The Great Railroad Strike of 1877.
- The Haymarket Affair (1886, Chicago — but the Western
  reverberations were strong).
- The Coeur d'Alene mining war (Idaho, 1892, 1899).
- The Cripple Creek strikes (Colorado, 1894 and 1903–04).
- The Western Federation of Miners and the Industrial Workers
  of the World (the Wobblies) organized the hardrock miners
  and the timber camps. Brutally repressed.

### d. The borderlands

- The Texas–Mexico border was its own war zone. Cattle theft
  ran both ways. Anglo violence against Tejanos. Mexican
  raiding into Texas. The Texas Rangers operated as a
  paramilitary force. Juan Cortina's raids (1859–1875). The
  Salt War of 1877 in San Elizario.

### e. Reconstruction violence

- Texas in particular saw heavy Reconstruction-era violence.
  The Texas State Police (1870–73), then the reorganized
  Rangers, fought freedmen and Republican lawmen as much as
  outlaws. White-supremacist organizations (Ku Klux Klan,
  later the White Man's Union) intimidated and killed
  freedmen and Republican voters. This is part of the
  Western story; it is rarely written.

## 9. The mistake to avoid

The mistake is to write the West as a costume drama in which
contemporary American types ride horses. The corrective is to
remember that the West was not a homogeneous Anglo-Protestant
country with cosmetic minorities. It was a contested zone of
peoples with grievances against one another going back
generations, layered onto recent grievances of the most violent
kind. Every character carries a specific history. A Comanche in
1875 has just lost his world. A Tejano in 1875 has watched his
family's land grant pass into Anglo hands by lawyer's papers he
could not read. A Black freedman in 1875 has lived through
slavery and the war. A Mormon in 1875 has been driven across
half a continent and now watches the federal government move to
prosecute his marriages. An Irish soldier in 1875 has crossed
the ocean from a famine country. A Chinese laborer in 1875 has
buried friends in the Sierra snowdrifts and is months from a
mob's torches.

Write each of them as the carrier of his own history. The
West gets larger and stranger and truer as you do.
