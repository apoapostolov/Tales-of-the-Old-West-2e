# Horse Culture — Breeds, Trade, Theft, Ceremony

This file is the knowledge module for the horse in
the 1870s West beyond what `knowledge/competence-procedures.md`
covers (saddling, riding, trail care). It complements
`knowledge/outlaw-craft.md` (horse theft as
business), `knowledge/native-material-culture.md`
(the horse in Plains Indian life), and
`knowledge/army-life.md` (the cavalry remount). For
horse-related action and travel scenes, see
`references/journey-narrative.md`.

The horse in the West was the principal means of
transportation, the principal beast of burden, the
principal instrument of war, the principal status
object, the principal subject of pride and theft and
ceremony. A western that does not get the horse
right is a western that has not done its work. This
file is the reference for getting the horse on the
page in its full economic, cultural, and physical
reality.

---

## 1. The breeds

The 1870s West contained a specific population of
horse types. Not breed in the modern registered
sense — most horses were unregistered grade animals
— but type, with characteristic conformation,
ancestry, and work.

### The mustang

The descendant of horses brought to the Americas by
the Spanish (sixteenth century onward) and gone wild
on the open ranges of Texas, the Southwest, and the
Great Basin. By the 1870s, mustang herds numbered in
the millions across the West.

Conformation: small (13 to 14 hands typical, 14 to
15 at the larger end), tough, sure-footed,
intelligent, often stubborn. Coat colors: every
color, with the strong Spanish lines (paint, dun,
buckskin, grulla) common.

Use: when broken (a serious undertaking; see
mustanging below), the mustang made a tough trail
horse, cow horse, and Indian war horse. Plains
Indians' horses were predominantly mustang stock
crossed with whatever the herd had absorbed.

Limits: the mustang was not large or strong enough
for heavy farm work or fast enough for the formal
race meet. The thoroughbred and the draft horse
filled those roles.

### The quarter horse (in formation)

The American quarter horse as a recognized breed
dates from the 1940s, but the type — short
distance racer with quarter-mile sprinting speed —
was established in colonial Virginia and carried
West with the cattle trade. By the 1870s the type
was concentrated in Texas, where the working cow
horse drew on this stock heavily.

The Steeldust line (descended from a stallion named
Steel Dust brought to Texas in the 1840s) and the
Copperbottom line were the principal foundations.
A "Steeldust" or "Copperbottom" cow horse meant a
specific quality: compact, heavy-quartered, fast
out of a stand, intelligent on a cow, easily
trained.

The 1870s cowhand on a top horse was on a Steeldust
or a Steeldust cross. The horse was the cowhand's
working partner and his pride.

### The Morgan

A New England type traced to the stallion Justin
Morgan (foaled 1789). Compact, muscular, willing,
versatile. Brought West in numbers from the 1840s
onward. Used as a saddle horse, light harness horse,
cavalry horse, and family horse. The U.S. Cavalry
remount service prized the Morgan and Morgan-cross
horses.

### The thoroughbred

The English race horse line. In the West, used
primarily for racing and as a bloodline improver
(the thoroughbred stallion bred to grade mares to
add height, speed, and refinement). Not common as a
working horse — too leggy, too thin-skinned, too
fragile for cow work or rough country.

The Kentucky-bred thoroughbred coming West for the
race tracks of San Francisco, Denver, and the
mining camps was a recognizable type and a
substantial trade.

### The draft horse

The heavy work horse. Percheron (French), Belgian,
Clydesdale (Scots), and crosses thereof. Brought
West for plowing, freight hauling, mine work, and
heavy team work. The freight wagons of the period
ran on heavy teams (six to twelve animals — horses
or mules — pulling loaded wagons across country).

The draft horse was not common on the open range
but was substantial in the agricultural districts
and on the freight roads.

### The mule

Not a horse but central to western transportation.
The mule (horse-donkey hybrid, sterile) combined
the horse's size and strength with the donkey's
sure-footedness, hardiness, and intelligence. The
mule was the Army's principal pack animal, the
freighter's principal team animal in rough
country, and the miner's principal mine animal.

The Missouri mule was the famous American type:
tall, strong, well-conformed. A Missouri mule
team was the freight industry's standard.

### The criollo / Spanish horse

In the Southwest, the Hispanic ranches ran horses of
direct Spanish descent — the criollo type. Tough,
small, well-suited to rough country and to the
vaquero's working style. Many of the West's best
cow horses had criollo blood through Texas stock
that had absorbed Mexican animals over generations.

### The Indian pony

Not a separate breed but a type: the Plains Indian's
horse, mustang in foundation, often well-cared-for
within the limits of the band's resources, valued
for specific qualities. The buffalo runner (the
horse trained to ride into a buffalo herd) was a
specialist animal of high value. The war horse was
another specialist. The travois horse and the
ordinary camp horse were less prestigious.

A Comanche or Cheyenne war horse was as carefully
chosen and trained as a thoroughbred race horse.
The contempt with which white memoirists sometimes
speak of "Indian ponies" misses the level of
horsemanship and selection involved.

## 2. Mustanging

The capture and breaking of wild horses — a
substantial industry in the West through the late
nineteenth century.

### The capture methods

- **The walk-down.** A team of mustangers (typically
  three to six men, multiple horses each) located a
  wild herd and pursued it on a relay basis. Each
  rider would push the herd until his horse tired,
  then drop back and a fresh rider took over. The
  wild herd, unable to rest, eventually exhausted
  itself and could be driven into a corral or
  caught individually. A walk-down might take days.
- **The trap corral.** A corral built in a canyon or
  draw with wing fences leading into it. The herd
  was driven into the wings and on into the corral,
  the gate slammed shut behind. Required scouting
  to find herds that used predictable water or
  travel routes.
- **The water trap.** A corral built around a water
  source the herd used. The mustangers waited until
  the herd entered to drink, then closed the gate.
  Effective in dry country where water was scarce.
- **Roping individuals.** A mounted rider on a fast,
  tough horse cut individual animals from the herd
  and roped them. The roped horse was thrown,
  hobbled, and held until it could be led back to
  camp.

### The breaking

The captured mustang was broken to the saddle by a
specific procedure varying by region and by
breaker. The general sequence:

1. **Hold and handle.** The wild horse was held in a
   small corral and handled (touched, talked to,
   eventually halter-broken) over days or weeks.
   Some breakers skipped this and went straight to
   step 2.
2. **Saddle and ride.** The horse was saddled
   (often with considerable bucking the first
   times), then mounted. The first rides were rough
   — the horse bucked until exhausted, then was
   ridden in a circle until it would respond to
   the reins.
3. **Refine.** Subsequent rides taught the horse to
   neck-rein, to stop, to back, to side-pass. A
   well-broken horse was the result of weeks or
   months of patient work.

The "buckaroo" tradition (Northwest, derived from
California vaquero practice) emphasized slow,
careful breaking with the hackamore (bosal) and
spade bit, producing a highly responsive horse over
years. The Texas tradition was rougher and faster.
Both produced working horses; the buckaroo horse
was more finished.

### The trade

A broken mustang sold for $10 to $25 in the 1870s,
depending on quality and region. A top cow horse
might bring $50 to $100. The mustanging trade
supplied the cavalry remount service, the cattle
ranches, and the general horse market.

### Reading

J. Frank Dobie's _The Mustangs_ is the classic
study. Walt Coburn's memoirs and J. Evetts Haley's
_Charles Goodnight_ contain mustanging passages.
The Bureau of Land Management's modern wild horse
program is a continuation.

## 3. The cavalry remount

The U.S. Cavalry needed a constant supply of
horses. The 1870s remount service operated through
contracts with regional suppliers who delivered
horses meeting Army specifications.

### The specifications

- **Height.** 15 to 16 hands.
- **Weight.** 950 to 1,150 pounds.
- **Age.** 4 to 8 years.
- **Soundness.** No major conformation faults; sound
  feet, legs, eyes, wind.
- **Color.** No paints, pintos, or appaloosas in the
  regular cavalry (officer's privilege might allow
  exceptions); the standard was solid colors —
  bay, sorrel, brown, black, gray.
- **Temperament.** Trainable; not vicious or
  spoiled.

### The procurement

Contractors (often substantial ranchers with horse
operations) bought from many smaller producers and
delivered consolidated remounts to the Army at a
contracted price (typically $100 to $150 per horse
in the 1870s). A failed delivery (sick horses,
horses that did not meet specifications) was
rejected on Army inspection.

### The horse in cavalry service

Each trooper was issued a horse on enlistment or
arrival at his unit. The trooper was responsible
for the horse's care: feeding, watering, grooming,
shoeing (assisted by the company farrier),
veterinary attention. A trooper who abused his
horse was disciplined; a trooper whose horse died
of neglect or injury might be fined the horse's
value.

The cavalry horse worked hard. A campaign might
mean weeks of long marches, short rations, no
shelter. Horse mortality on hard campaigns was
substantial — twenty to forty percent of the
cavalry's horses might be lost on a major
expedition through disease, exhaustion, accident,
or combat.

### Reading

See `knowledge/army-life.md` for the cavalry
generally. For the horse specifically: Don Rickey's
_Forty Miles a Day on Beans and Hay_ has substantial
horse material; the Army manuals of the period
(Cavalry Tactics, the Quartermaster regulations)
detail the horse's care and use.

## 4. Horse racing

A major western entertainment, especially in Texas,
the Southwest, and California.

### The match race

The classic western race. Two horses, one race, a
short distance (a quarter-mile to a half-mile in
Texas; longer distances in California). The horses
ran from a standing start on a straight track —
sometimes a graded race track at a town, sometimes a
straight stretch of road, sometimes a measured
course on the prairie.

The match race was the occasion for substantial
betting. The Texas "quarter race" (quarter-mile
sprint) was a particular form, drawing on the
short-distance racing tradition the Anglo-American
settlers brought from the South. The "Steeldust"
horses excelled at this distance.

### The Mexican carrera

The Hispanic horse racing tradition. The carrera de
caballos was a community event with substantial
betting, often run on Sunday after Mass on a
designated track at the village edge. The Mexican
gambler at the carrera was a recognizable figure;
the prize horse was the village's pride.

### The track meet

In the larger western towns, formal race tracks
operated with multiple races per day, organized
betting (parimutuel and bookmaker), and
thoroughbred racing on the Eastern model. San
Francisco, Sacramento, and (later) Denver were the
principal centers.

### Reading

For racing: Wayne Gard's _The Chisholm Trail_
contains some racing material; J. Frank Dobie's
_The Longhorns_ touches on it; Tom Lea's _The
Brave Bulls_ is set in Mexico but renders the
horse-and-betting culture.

## 5. Horse theft

Horse theft was a major western crime, distinct from
cattle rustling though often practiced by the same
people. The horse was more portable than cattle, more
valuable per head, easier to steal in small numbers.
Horse thieves were despised in a way cattle rustlers
sometimes were not — the horse thief took a man's
specific working partner, sometimes left him afoot in
hard country.

### The methods

- **The bunch theft.** A group of horses driven off
  a range and taken cross-country to a distant
  market. Required getting the horses to a place
  where they would not be recognized. Cross-border
  theft was common — Texas horses driven into
  Mexico (and vice versa), Wyoming horses driven
  into Colorado.
- **The single-horse theft.** A specific horse taken
  from a corral, hitching rail, or pasture.
  Typically a riding horse the thief intended to
  use himself. The thief might leave his own
  worn-out horse in trade.
- **The remuda theft.** The theft of a working
  ranch's or trail outfit's horse herd. Catastrophic
  for the victim — the outfit could not work
  without horses.
- **The Indian raid.** Plains tribes practiced horse
  raiding as a major economic and prestige
  activity. Raids took horses from other tribes,
  from Hispanic ranches, and from white
  settlements. The cultural framework was different
  from white horse theft, but the economic effect
  on the victim was similar.

### The brand alteration

A stolen horse with a recognizable brand had to be
re-branded to be salable. The methods were the same
as for cattle (see `knowledge/outlaw-craft.md`):
adding lines to convert one brand into another, hot
iron over the old brand, killing and skinning if the
brand could not be altered (uncommon for horses, as
the meat had little value).

### The penalty

Horse thieves were among the most likely western
criminals to be lynched. The "horse thief" who fell
into the hands of a vigilance committee or a
ranchers' posse was apt to be hanged from the
nearest tree. The legal penalty for horse theft was
typically a long prison term; the extra-legal
penalty was death.

### Use in scenes

A horse-theft scene can carry substantial dramatic
weight. The dawn discovery of an empty corral. The
trail followed for two days. The thief overtaken at
the bend of the river. The hanging tree.

A scene of being left afoot is its own subject.
The horse stolen out from under the rider in remote
country could be a death sentence. The rider on
foot in waterless country, walking out — a Cormac
McCarthy scene, often.

## 6. The horse trade — buying and selling

The 1870s western horse trade was substantial and
informal. A man might buy and sell horses at the
local livery, at the stockyards, at horse fairs, or
in private deals. The horse trader (a recognized
character type — see Conrad Richter's _The Sea of
Grass_, where the horse trader appears) traveled
from town to town buying and selling.

### Evaluating a horse

A buyer evaluating a horse looked at:

- **Age.** Determined by the teeth (a process called
  "mouthing"). The 1870s buyer expected to be told
  the horse's age and to verify by mouthing.
  "Mouthing a horse" was a recognized skill.
- **Conformation.** Build, balance, legs.
- **Soundness.** No lameness, no broken wind, no
  blind eye, no tender feet. The buyer led the
  horse out, watched it walk and trot, picked up
  the feet, examined the eyes.
- **Disposition.** The buyer wanted to see the horse
  saddled and ridden; preferred to ride it himself.
- **Brand and ownership.** The buyer wanted a bill
  of sale and a clear brand.

### The price

In the 1870s, horse prices varied enormously by type
and quality:

- **Plug or worn-out horse.** $5 to $15.
- **Ordinary saddle horse.** $25 to $50.
- **Good cow horse.** $50 to $100.
- **Top cow horse or matched team.** $100 to $200.
- **Race horse or trotter.** $300 to $1,000+.
- **Cavalry remount (contractor price).** $100 to
  $150.
- **Mustang (newly broken).** $10 to $25.

These are rough; market and region varied.

### The trader's voice

The horse trader's pitch was the period's
characteristic salesmanship. _A four-year-old, sound
of wind and limb, no vice, gentle as a lamb to
ride, will work in any harness, has been on the
trail to Kansas twice. I'll let him go for sixty
dollars, and I'm losing money._ The buyer's
counter-pitch — finding the flaws to drive down the
price — was the conversation's other half.

A scene at a livery or trade is rich material for
voice and character.

## 7. The horse equipment beyond the basics

`knowledge/competence-procedures.md` covers the
saddle, the bit, and the basic gear. This section
expands on specific equipment with cultural and
regional dimensions.

### The bit varieties

- **The snaffle.** A jointed bit working on the
  corners of the mouth. The mildest standard bit.
  Used for green horses, for English riding, and
  by some western riders for trail work.
- **The curb.** A bit with a port (the curved
  central section that fits over the tongue) and
  shanks that produce leverage when the reins are
  pulled. The standard western working bit.
- **The spade bit.** A severe bit with a high port
  and long shanks. Used in the buckaroo tradition
  on highly trained horses; required a soft hand to
  use without injury.
- **The hackamore.** A bridle without a bit, working
  on the horse's nose with a heavy rawhide noseband
  (the bosal). Used in the buckaroo tradition for
  starting a horse.
- **The jaquima.** The Spanish form of the
  hackamore. Used in the vaquero tradition.
- **The war bridle.** A simple bit and bridle used
  by Plains Indians, sometimes only a thong tied
  around the lower jaw. Permitted close control with
  minimal equipment.

### The saddle types beyond the basic stock saddle

- **The Mexican saddle (silla).** The original
  parent of the western stock saddle. High pommel,
  high cantle, big horn, ornately tooled. Heavy
  and durable.
- **The Texas saddle.** A development of the
  Mexican saddle for Texas cattle work. Lower than
  the Mexican, with the strong horn for roping.
- **The California saddle.** A development of the
  Mexican saddle for California vaquero work. More
  ornate than the Texas saddle, with specific
  details (the round skirt, the differing rigging).
- **The McClellan saddle.** The U.S. Cavalry's
  service saddle (adopted 1859, used through World
  War II). A spare, open-tree saddle designed to
  spare the horse's back on long rides. Hard on the
  rider's seat.
- **The English saddle.** Used for race horses and
  by some Eastern-trained riders. Rare on the
  working range.
- **The pack saddle.** The sawbuck or aparejo. The
  sawbuck was a wooden cross-frame strapped to the
  pack animal; the aparejo was a Mexican-derived
  padded cushion saddle that distributed load
  better. The aparejo was preferred by serious
  packers.

### The boots, the spurs, the chaps

The cowhand's working dress had functional and
cultural dimensions.

- **The boots.** High-heeled, pointed-toed,
  underslung-heeled. The high heel kept the foot
  from slipping through the stirrup; the pointed
  toe slid in easily; the underslung heel caught on
  the stirrup if the horse fell, helping the rider
  pull free. The boots were typically pulled on (no
  laces) and reached well up the calf.
- **The spurs.** Worn on the boots. Various types:
  the Texas spur (small rowel, short shank), the
  California spur (large rowel, long shank, often
  with jingles), the Mexican spur (the largest,
  most ornate). Spurs were used to signal the horse
  more than to punish; the experienced rider used
  the spur lightly.
- **The chaps.** Leather leg coverings worn over
  the trousers to protect against brush, weather,
  rope burn, and accidental falls. Two main types:
  shotgun chaps (full-length tubes pulled on like
  trousers) and batwing chaps (open at the back,
  buckled around the leg). Plus the Mexican
  chaparreras (the parent type) and the woollies
  (winter chaps with the hair left on).

### The lariat and reata

The rope. The American cowhand typically used a
braided rawhide reata (in California and the Spanish
tradition) or a hemp or grass rope (in Texas and
the south). The rope was 30 to 60 feet long. The
roping technique varied by region — the Texas
"hard and fast" style (rope tied to the horn, no
slip) versus the California "dally" style (rope
wrapped around the horn after the catch, can slip
to absorb shock).

The roping style was a source of regional pride and
rivalry. The vaquero/buckaroo dallied; the Texas
hand tied hard and fast. Both styles produced
substantial accidents — the dally rider could lose
fingers in a slipping wrap; the hard-and-fast
rider could be jerked from the saddle by a heavy
catch.

## 8. The horse in ceremony and relationship

The horse was not only labor and transport. It was
status, gift, ceremony, partner.

### The Plains Indian horse culture

The horse transformed Plains Indian life from the
seventeenth century onward. By the 1870s, mounted
horse cultures (Comanche, Cheyenne, Lakota, Crow,
Blackfeet, Ute, Shoshone) had developed sophisticated
horse-handling traditions.

Horses were:

- **Wealth.** A man's wealth was counted in horses.
- **Bride price.** Marriage involved horses given to
  the bride's family.
- **Gift.** The giving of horses was a major
  diplomatic and social act.
- **War booty.** Captured horses were a war's
  principal economic gain.
- **Ceremony.** Horses figured in dances, in
  burials (a man's favorite horse might be killed
  at his grave), in regalia.

A Comanche or Cheyenne man's relationship with his
favorite horses was substantial and recognized
within the culture. The buffalo runner, the war
horse, were specific animals known by name and
treated as partners.

### The cowhand's horse

The American cowhand was issued a string of horses
(typically four to ten) on a working outfit. He did
not own them; the ranch owned them. But he rode them
daily and developed substantial relationships. A
cowhand's "top horse" (his best animal in the
string) was treated with care and pride.

The relationship was working — not sentimental in
the modern sense — but real. A cowhand whose top
horse was killed or seriously injured felt the loss.
The horse-and-rider partnership was the cow work's
basic unit.

### The Hispanic vaquero's horse

The vaquero tradition placed substantial weight on
the finished horse — the highly trained,
beautifully responsive saddle horse developed over
years of patient work. The vaquero's horse was an
expression of the vaquero's skill and the
hacienda's prestige. A famous bridle horse was a
local celebrity.

### The army horse

The cavalry trooper's relationship with his issued
horse varied. Some troopers became attached;
others did not. The horse was the trooper's
working partner under fire — a relationship that
created strong bonds in some cases, indifference in
others.

### Funerals and gifts

The ceremonial dimensions of horse culture appear
in funeral practices (the killing of the favorite
horse at the grave in some Plains traditions; the
riderless horse with reversed boots in cavalry
funerals), in gifts (the horse as the major gift in
many cultures), and in races and contests (the
horse as the medium of competition and prestige).

## 9. Voice — the horseman's vocabulary

Render the horseman through his vocabulary. The
specific terms carry the period:

- **Mouth.** Verb. To examine a horse's teeth to
  judge its age. _He mouthed the gelding and
  pronounced him eight years old._
- **Mouthy.** A horse with a hard mouth that does
  not respond to the bit.
- **Wind.** The horse's breathing capacity. _Sound
  of wind_ meant healthy lungs. _Broken wind_ was
  heaves (a chronic respiratory condition).
- **Hand.** The unit of horse height. Four inches.
  _Fifteen and a half hands_ was about 5'2" at the
  withers.
- **Withers.** The high point at the base of the
  horse's neck where it joins the back.
- **Hocks.** The major joint of the horse's hind
  leg.
- **Cinch / girth.** The strap holding the saddle
  on. _Cinch_ in Western usage; _girth_ in English.
- **Latigo.** The leather strap by which the cinch
  was fastened.
- **Off side / near side.** The right side and the
  left side of the horse. The horse was traditionally
  mounted from the near (left) side.
- **Up.** Mounted. _He was up by daylight._
- **Bunch up.** A horse drawing his legs together
  and rounding his back, often as a prelude to
  bucking.
- **Crow-hop.** A mild buck — the horse hopping
  with all four legs, not violently.
- **Sun-fish.** A serious buck — the horse twisting
  the body in mid-air, presenting the belly to the
  sun. A sun-fishing horse threw most riders.
- **Rear.** The horse standing on his hind legs.
  Dangerous; a rearing horse can fall over backwards
  on the rider.
- **Bog down.** A horse trapped in mud or a bog.
- **Founder.** Laminitis — a severe inflammation of
  the horse's feet, often from overfeeding or
  overwork.
- **Galled.** A horse with sore patches under the
  saddle or harness from rubbing.
- **Pulled up lame.** A horse that became lame on
  the trail and could not continue at speed.
- **Saddle-sore.** Either the rider after long
  riding, or the horse with sore patches.
- **Throw a shoe.** A horseshoe coming off in the
  field. A horse without a shoe might quickly
  become lame on stony ground.

A scene with a horse will deploy these terms
naturally; the writer who reaches for them gives
the period weight.

## 10. Worked passage — the horse trade

> The trader had four head in the corral when Colter
> rode up. Colter looked them over from the saddle
> for a minute and then climbed down and tied his
> own horse to the rail.
>
> _What you got?_
>
> _Three saddle horses and a pack mule. The bay
> mare is six years old, sound as a dollar, broken
> to anything you want her broken to. The sorrel
> gelding's eight, pretty good cow horse, been on
> two trail drives. The little dun is green-broke,
> only five, but he'll make somebody a hand. The
> mule's old, fifteen years, but he'll pack two
> hundred pounds all day._
>
> Colter went into the corral. He went to the bay
> first and ran his hand down her near foreleg and
> picked up the foot and looked at it. He set the
> foot down and walked around her and ran his hand
> down each leg in turn. He stood at her shoulder
> and pulled her lips back and looked at her teeth.
>
> _Six?_ he said.
>
> _Six this spring._
>
> _She's eight._
>
> _Well. Maybe seven._
>
> _Eight. The cups are gone out of these
> incisors._
>
> The trader did not argue further. Colter went to
> the sorrel and mouthed him too and then put a
> halter on him and led him out into the open and
> jogged him in a circle. The sorrel was sound. The
> dun he handled briefly and passed by; he did not
> want a green horse. The mule he checked the feet
> and the back.
>
> _Forty for the bay,_ he said. _Sixty-five for the
> sorrel. Twenty for the mule. I'll give you a
> hundred and twenty-five for the three._
>
> _A hundred and fifty._
>
> _A hundred and thirty._
>
> _A hundred and forty._
>
> Colter took out his wallet and counted out a
> hundred and forty dollars in greenbacks and laid
> them on the corral rail.

The scene's specifics — the mouthing, the leg
checking, the jogging in a circle, the bargaining,
the greenbacks — render the horse trade
procedurally. The verbs carry it. The dialogue
carries the period voice.

## 11. The cheat-sheet

- **Pick the horse type for the scene.** Mustang,
  Steeldust cow horse, Morgan, thoroughbred, draft,
  mule, criollo, Indian pony.
- **Render the equipment specifically.** Saddle
  type, bit type, chaps type, rope type. Region-
  specific.
- **The vocabulary is the period's voice.** Mouth,
  wind, hands, withers, cinch, near side, off side,
  sun-fish, bog down. Use them.
- **Horse theft is a major plot device.** Use it
  with awareness of the cultural penalty (lynching).
- **The horse trade has its own scene mechanics.**
  Mouthing, leg checking, demonstration ride,
  bargaining.
- **Mustanging and breaking are substantial
  procedures.** A scene set in mustanging is a
  scene the canon underuses.
- **Racing is a major social event.** Match race,
  carrera, track meet — each has its own scene.
- **The horse-and-rider relationship is real.** The
  cowhand's top horse, the vaquero's bridle horse,
  the Plains warrior's buffalo runner — render the
  partnership.
- **The cavalry horse is a specific type.** See
  `knowledge/army-life.md` for the trooper's

## 12. The rule the others serve

The horse on the page is not transportation — not in
the western. The horse is a partner, a status, a
working tool, a target of theft, a subject of
ceremony, a measure of wealth, a measure of skill.
The character with a horse is in a relationship the
writer must render with as much specificity as the
character's relationships with other people.

A western that gets the horse right has done its
fundamental work. A western that does not — that
treats the horse as a generic mount or as set
dressing — has missed the period's central
animal. The horse made the West possible. The
western that forgets this is a western that has
not been written.
