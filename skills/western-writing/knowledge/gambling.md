# Gambling — The Tables of the West

This file is the knowledge module for gambling in the
1870s American West. It complements
`knowledge/towns-economy-law.md` (the saloon and the
town economy generally), `knowledge/prices-and-anchors.md`
(stakes and the dollar values that make a stake
meaningful), and `references/speech-patterns.md` (the
gambler's voice fingerprint). For the gambler as a
character type and the dramatic possibilities of the
table, see also `references/violence-as-craft.md`
(many western confrontations begin at the table).

Gambling was the central social activity of the
working West. Cowhands gambled their wages on payday.
Soldiers gambled garrison pay. Miners gambled the
strike. Women in the towns ran some of the most
respected games. The professional gambler — well-
dressed, soft-spoken, with a clean hand and a quick
eye — was a specific character type, neither outlaw
nor citizen but his own thing.

The writer who renders a gambling scene must know the
games well enough to write them without explaining
them. The reader feels the rhythm of the table because
the prose moves with the rhythm. This file is the
reference.

---

## 1. The four major games

### Faro

The dominant bank game of the western saloon through
the 1870s and into the 1890s. A house game, played
against the dealer (the "bank"). Easy to play, fast,
visible, and the house edge was small — making faro
the most respectable bank game by the standards of
the period.

### Monte (three-card monte / Spanish monte)

Two distinct games sharing a name.

**Spanish monte:** A bank game with a Spanish-deck
heritage, dominant in the Southwest and in border
country. A regional standard alongside faro.

**Three-card monte:** A confidence game (the dealer
wins because the game is rigged). Played by sharps
on stages, in railroad cars, in saloons. Properly a
swindle, not a game.

The writer must distinguish.

### Poker

The dominant private game of the West. Played
between players, with the house taking a small rake or
a table fee. Multiple variants in the period, evolving
fast. The dominant western variants:

- **Five-card draw.** The classic. Each player dealt
  five cards, may discard and draw replacements once,
  betting in two rounds.
- **Five-card stud.** First card down, four up. Betting
  after each up card. Took hold from the late 1860s.

Texas hold 'em did not exist in the 1870s. Stud and
draw were the games.

### Other games

**Chuck-a-luck (a.k.a. birdcage).** A dice game played
with three dice in a wire cage. Players bet on which
numbers will come up. House game, large house edge.
Common in cheap saloons.

**Roulette.** Present in the better saloons. The
American double-zero wheel (38 numbers) became
standard in the West. Played but never as common as
faro.

**Keno.** A lottery-style game with numbered balls or
beans. Common in the larger saloons.

**Brag, all-fours, euchre.** Card games of regional or
class distribution. Brag was a poker ancestor; euchre
was popular in the army.

## 2. Faro — the procedure

The most-played game in the West deserves the deepest
treatment. The writer who can render a faro scene can
render any gambling scene.

### The layout

A green baize cloth or a printed leather mat showing
the thirteen ranks (Ace through King) of one suit
laid out in two rows. Players bet by placing chips
(or coin) on the rank they predict.

### The dealer's box

A small metal or wooden box with one open side, sized
to hold a deck of cards face-up. The cards are
inserted face-up. The top card is visible through the
window.

### The deal

- The top card (the "soda") is removed and set aside.
  It does not figure in the betting.
- The dealer pulls the next card and lays it to one
  side of the box. This is the "loser" or "banker's
  card." All bets on cards of that rank lose.
- The dealer pulls the next card and lays it on the
  other side. This is the "winner" or "player's
  card." All bets on cards of that rank win.
- The two cards together are a "turn." The dealer
  pays winning bets and rakes losing ones.
- The deck is dealt down in turns until two cards
  remain (with the soda set aside). This is the
  "hock," and the last turn is dealt for double or
  triple stakes.

### The casebook (case keeper)

A separate device tracking which cards have been
played. A wooden frame with thirteen rows of four
beads, one row per rank. As each card is dealt, the
case keeper moves a bead. Players can see at any
moment which cards remain in the deck. This is the
faro player's analytical instrument.

### Copper

A player can "copper" his bet — placing a small
hexagonal token (a copper) on top of his chips —
which reverses the bet. Now he wins if the rank
loses and loses if it wins. This is a major
flexibility of the game.

### The split

If both winner and loser cards in a turn are the
same rank, it is a "split." The dealer takes half of
all bets on that rank. The split was the bank's
principal advantage.

### The call

On the last turn (the "hock"), if a player has
correctly tracked the casebook, he may "call the
turn" — predict the order of the last three cards.
Pays four to one. The faro player's edge case.

### The vocabulary

- **Bank.** The dealer (he holds the bank).
- **Lay.** To place a bet (_he laid ten on the queen_).
- **Soda.** The top burned card.
- **Hock.** The last few cards.
- **Casekeeper.** The man tracking the deck.
- **Copper.** The reversing token.
- **Heel.** A bet specifying that one of two cards
  paired in a turn wins.

### The house edge

A faithfully dealt faro game had a house edge of about
1.5% — among the lowest of any house game ever
offered. This is why faro dominated. A player who
tracked the casebook well could in theory beat the
game; in practice, dishonest dealing (rigged boxes,
marked cards, dealing seconds) returned the edge to
the house.

### A faro scene's rhythm

A faro game has its own pulse. The dealer pulls; pause;
he pulls; pause. The two cards land and the bets are
settled. The case keeper moves a bead. The next bets
go down. The pause between turns is where the prose
lives — the faces around the table, the sip of whisky,
the look at the casebook.

## 3. Faro — a worked scene

> The Tilghman bank had been running an hour and the
> stack of chips at Bowman's right was less than the
> stack he had brought. He laid five on the queen and
> coppered ten on the trey.
>
> Tilghman pulled the next card without speaking. The
> trey came on the loser's side and Tilghman pushed
> twenty across to Bowman. The queen came on the
> winner's side a turn later and Tilghman pushed ten
> more.
>
> The case keeper moved his beads. There were two
> queens left and one trey. The deck was thinning.
>
> Mendez at the next chair laid a stack on the king. He
> had been laying on the king all night and the king
> had been good to him. Tilghman did not look up.
>
> Two turns later the king came on the loser's side and
> Tilghman raked the stack. Mendez looked at the
> empty cloth in front of him for a moment and then
> he looked at the casebook. Then he reached into his
> vest and brought out his last roll and laid it on
> the seven.

The reader does not need the rules explained. The
casebook, the copper, the loser and winner sides, the
case keeper's beads — they are nouns and verbs. The
reader registers the rhythm. The drama is in the
changing piles and Mendez's last roll.

## 4. Spanish monte — the procedure

The dominant card game of the Southwest and of border
country. Played with a Spanish 40-card deck (no 8s,
9s, 10s).

### The layout

The dealer holds the deck face-down. He removes the
top two cards and places them face-up in a row — the
"top layout." He removes the bottom two cards and
places them face-up in another row — the "bottom
layout."

Players bet that the next card turned from the deck
will match (in rank) one of the four exposed cards.

### The bet

Players place chips on one of the four cards. The
dealer turns the top card of the deck. If it matches
in rank one of the four exposed cards, players who
bet on that card win.

### The bank

The dealer (the banker) pays even money on most bets.
The house edge comes from the four-of-a-kind problem
(if more than one card of a rank has already been
dealt, the bet's mathematics shift).

### The vocabulary

Mostly Spanish: _banco_ (the bank), _puerta_ (the door
— the top layout), _albur_ (the bottom layout), _gallo_
(the rooster — a winning card).

### The cultural register

Monte was the Mexican gambler's game. A monte table
in El Paso, Tucson, Las Vegas (New Mexico Territory),
or any border town was a Spanish-language space. The
Anglo who sat at a monte table was the outsider in
his own country.

## 5. Poker — the procedure

Poker is a private game between players. The house
provides the table, the cards, sometimes the dealer,
and takes a rake (a small percentage of each pot) or
a table fee.

### Five-card draw — the dominant 1870s game

- Each player puts up an ante (a small forced bet).
- Each player is dealt five cards face-down.
- A round of betting. Each player in turn may check
  (if no bet has been made), bet, call (match the
  current bet), raise, or fold.
- After betting, each player may discard up to three
  cards and draw replacements. (Some house rules
  allowed four if the player held an Ace.)
- A second round of betting.
- Showdown. Best hand wins.

### Five-card stud

- Each player put up an ante.
- One card dealt face-down (the hole card), one face-
  up.
- Betting round, opened by the high card showing.
- Another card up. Betting round.
- Another card up. Betting round.
- Last card up. Final betting round. Showdown.

### The hand rankings (1870s standard)

From low to high:

1. High card.
2. One pair.
3. Two pair.
4. Three of a kind.
5. Straight.
6. Flush.
7. Full house.
8. Four of a kind.
9. Straight flush.

The straight flush ranking was settled in the 1860s.
The royal flush as a separate name came later. A wild
card was not standard. Lowball variants existed but
were rare.

### The vocabulary

- **Ante.** Forced opening bet.
- **Pot.** The accumulated bets.
- **Call.** Match the current bet.
- **Raise.** Match and increase.
- **Fold.** Surrender the hand.
- **Check.** Decline to bet without folding.
- **Showdown.** Reveal hands.
- **Tells.** Unconscious habits revealing the hand.
- **Bluff.** Bet strongly with a weak hand to drive
  out callers.
- **The dead man's hand.** Aces and eights, the hand
  Wild Bill Hickok was reputedly holding when shot
  in Deadwood, August 1876. The phrase entered
  western lore quickly.

### The professional player at the table

The professional player has specific behaviors. He
stacks his chips neatly. He does not look at his hole
card more than once. He plays slowly when he intends
to play and quickly when he intends to fold. He does
not drink at the table (or drinks colored water from
a bottle that looks like whisky). He sits with his
back to a wall.

### The amateur at the table

The amateur drinks. He is loud or silent depending on
the strength of his hand. He counts his money during
the play. He looks at his cards too often. He makes
his bet with the wrong number of chips and corrects
it. The professional reads him in three hands.

## 6. Poker — a worked scene

> The pot stood at sixty dollars. There were three
> men in the hand and two of them had folded. The
> dealer dealt the cards and the cards came around
> and Hatch took up his five.
>
> A pair of jacks. He set them down face-down and
> pushed in a five-dollar chip without looking at
> Caldwell.
>
> _Five,_ he said.
>
> Caldwell across the table looked at his hand and
> looked at Hatch and pushed in the call and a ten on
> top of it.
>
> _Fifteen,_ Caldwell said.
>
> The third man, a buyer up from the railhead, looked
> at his cards a long time and folded.
>
> _Cards,_ the dealer said.
>
> Hatch threw three down and took three. He did not
> look at them.
>
> Caldwell threw one down and took one. He looked at
> it and put it in the back of his hand and set the
> hand down.
>
> Hatch picked up the new cards. The first one was
> a jack. The second one was a deuce. The third one
> was a jack. He had four jacks and the deuce. He set
> the cards down face-down and looked at Caldwell.
>
> _Twenty,_ Hatch said.
>
> Caldwell took a long sip of his whisky and put the
> glass down. _Forty,_ he said.
>
> Hatch let the silence run. The buyer behind him was
> watching the pot. The dealer was watching Hatch.
> Hatch counted out the call and the raise.
>
> _Forty and forty,_ he said.
>
> Caldwell looked at his hand and looked at the pot
> and shook his head once and pushed in the call.
> _What you got, then,_ he said.
>
> Hatch turned over the four jacks.
>
> Caldwell turned over a queen-high straight. He sat
> looking at it for a moment. Then he stood up and
> said _Tell Sam I will be back_ and walked out of
> the saloon.

The hand is rendered without the rules being explained.
The reader knows what a pair, a draw, four of a kind,
and a straight are because the prose treats them as
nouns. The drama is in Caldwell's quiet exit.

## 7. Cheating — the methods

The skilled cheat had a repertoire. A scene that
includes cheating must show the right method for the
game, and the prose should not over-explain.

### The holdout

A device worn under the coat (a "Kepplinger" or a
strap holdout) that could deliver a card into the
hand or take one away. The player palmed the card
between the holdout and the play. Detection: a player
whose hand somehow always had what he needed.

### Marked cards

Cards marked on the back so the cheat could read them
without seeing the face.

- **Sand-tell.** Cards that have been touched with a
  fine sandpaper at specific positions. The cheat
  feels the marks; an unsuspecting player does not.
- **Luminous reader.** A faint mark visible only
  through colored glasses or a specific light. Mostly
  later than the 1870s, but appearing.
- **Edge work.** Cards trimmed slightly so a stripper
  deck can be cut to a desired card.
- **Daub.** A paste applied to specific positions on
  the card backs, visible to the cheat who knows where
  to look.

### Dealing seconds

The dealer deals not the top card but the one
underneath, holding the top for himself or his
confederate. A skill that takes years; a true second-
deal is invisible.

### The cold deck

A deck that has been pre-arranged and is switched in
for the legitimate deck during play. The switch
happens in a moment of distraction and the hand that
follows is rigged.

### The signaler / the partner

Two cheats at the table communicating with signals
(a touch of the ear, a cough, a particular phrasing).
The partner knows the cheat's hand and bets
accordingly. The partner is often unsuspected because
he plays the role of an ordinary player.

### The detection

A player who suspected cheating could call out the
cheat. The traditional response was to flip the
table, draw a derringer or a knife from a vest, and
fight. Many famous gunfights began at the gambling
table.

The clean cheat — the one who could not be caught —
often went his whole career undetected. The famous
gambler Soapy Smith built a town-running operation
around organized cheating.

## 8. The professional gambler — the character

The professional gambler is a distinct character type
with specific markers.

### Dress

Black broadcloth coat (frequently a frock coat),
boiled white shirt, black string tie or a small bow,
black wool trousers, polished boots, often a vest of
patterned silk or brocade. The hat: a low-crown
black wool. The watch chain across the vest. A
diamond ring or a gold pin if successful.

The dress signals: this man does not work outdoors,
this man has standing, this man has money. The
working dress and the appearance of money are part of
the bank he brings to the table.

### Manner

Soft-spoken. Slow-moving. Long looks. Never visibly
angry. The gambler who shows emotion at the table
has lost his edge.

### Hands

The gambler's hands are clean. The nails trimmed. The
hands move precisely. He does not gesture broadly.
He does not slap the table. The hands are his
instrument.

### The voice

Period-formal. The gambler speaks like a man who has
read books. _I do believe, I am inclined to think,
sir, ma'am._ The vocabulary is broader than a
cowhand's. See `references/speech-patterns.md`.

### The famous

- **Doc Holliday.** Tubercular dentist turned
  gambler, friend of Wyatt Earp. Educated, deadly,
  sick. The model for the educated gambler with a
  pistol.
- **Wild Bill Hickok.** Former scout and lawman,
  turned to professional gambling in his last years.
  Killed at the table.
- **Poker Alice (Alice Ivers).** Female professional
  poker player. Operated in Deadwood and elsewhere.
  The skilled woman gambler was rarer than the man
  but real.
- **Bat Masterson.** Lawman, gambler, eventually a
  New York sportswriter.
- **Luke Short.** Dapper, deadly, quiet. The model
  for the small, well-dressed gambler.

### The gambler's death

Most successful gamblers died of disease (consumption
was epidemic in the trade) or of violence at the
table. A few retired and died in beds. The gambler
who lived past forty was unusual.

## 9. Stakes — what the dollar amounts meant

The dramatic weight of a gambling scene depends on
the stakes mattering to the reader. See
`knowledge/prices-and-anchors.md` for the full anchor
sheet. Brief reference:

- **A nickel ante.** The penny-ante game. Cowhands
  killing time in the bunkhouse.
- **A quarter ante.** Standard saloon poker. A pot
  might run to ten or twenty dollars.
- **A dollar ante.** A serious game. A pot might run
  to a hundred dollars. A cowhand's monthly wage was
  about thirty.
- **A five-dollar ante.** A high game. The
  professional gambler's table. Pots could run to
  several hundred dollars. A scene at this table
  carries the weight of months of a working man's
  wages on each hand.
- **A bank bet at faro.** Could be anything. Players
  laid five cents up to a hundred dollars depending
  on the house. The famous Bird Cage in Tombstone had
  no limit.

### The losing-it-all scene

The gambler who loses his last roll is a recurring
western figure. The scene's mathematics: the man
came in with three hundred dollars (a year's saved
wages, or the proceeds of a small cattle sale), he
leaves with nothing. The reader who knows what three
hundred dollars meant in 1875 feels the weight.

## 10. The table as social space

A faro or poker table was not only a place to gamble.
It was a social center, an information exchange, and
a place where deals other than the game's were made.

- **The cowhand on payday.** The drover's wages went
  to the saloon, and a portion went to the table. The
  scene of the cowhand losing his roll is a
  fundamental western figure.
- **The cattle deal at the table.** Two ranchmen at
  the same table might be negotiating a cattle sale
  between hands. The poker game was the cover for the
  business.
- **The rendezvous.** A man met another man at a
  specific table at a specific hour. Information
  changed hands. Money changed hands. The gambling
  was incidental.
- **The watcher.** A man sat at a table to watch. The
  watcher might be a Pinkerton detective tracking a
  fugitive, a sheriff watching a suspect, a relative
  watching a son. The watcher does not play seriously;
  he loses small amounts as the cost of the seat.

A scene at the table can carry any of these
sub-currents. The game is the visible activity. The
real business is what happens between the hands.

## 11. Women at the gambling table

The respectable woman did not gamble. The disreputable
woman did. The third category — the professional
woman gambler — was rare but real.

### The saloon girl

Many saloons employed women whose job was to entice
men to play and to drink. They were not professional
gamblers; they steered customers to the games. They
took a cut of the house's win and often a cut of the
drinks.

### The brothel madam

Some madams ran gambling tables in their houses (the
sporting house was often part-saloon, part-brothel).
The madam might deal faro or run the games herself.
Some madams were known for fair dealing; others for
the opposite.

### The professional

Poker Alice. Lottie Deno (an alias; her real name was
Carlotta Thompkins). Madame Mustache (Eleanore Dumont,
a French-born faro dealer who worked the gold camps
of California, Nevada, and Montana). These women were
known by name and by reputation. Their scenes are
worth writing because they have not been written
enough.

The professional female gambler dressed well, played
her own bank or sat at a private game, and was treated
with the respect a successful gambler commanded. She
was not, by virtue of being a gambler, a prostitute;
the two trades overlapped but were not the same.

## 12. Before / after rewrites

### Rules-explanation poker → played poker

**Before:**

> Hatch was playing five-card draw poker, a popular
> game in which each player is dealt five cards and
> may then discard and draw replacements. He looked at
> his hand and saw that he had a pair of jacks, which
> in poker is a moderately good hand.

**After:**

> Hatch took up his five and saw the pair of jacks and
> set them down. He pushed in a five-dollar chip
> without looking at Caldwell.

### Generic gambling → specific game

**Before:**

> The men were gambling in the saloon, betting their
> hard-earned wages on games of chance.

**After:**

> The faro table was full at six o'clock and the
> dealer had pulled the soda twice already. Mendez had
> laid his last roll on the seven and was watching the
> case keeper.

### Cinematic cheat → procedural cheat

**Before:**

> Suddenly, Hatch realized that the dealer was
> cheating! He must have been using marked cards or
> some kind of trick.

**After:**

> Hatch had been losing for an hour and he had not
> seen Caldwell deal a top card the whole night. He
> watched Caldwell's right hand on the next deal and
> the second knuckle of the index finger moved a
> fraction before the card came off. Caldwell was
> dealing seconds.

## 13. The gambling cheat-sheet

- **Pick the game.** Faro, monte, poker (draw or
  stud), chuck-a-luck. Each has its own grammar.
- **Render the rules through play, not exposition.**
  The reader infers from the verbs.
- **Get the vocabulary right.** Casebook, copper,
  hock, ante, call, fold, soda, hole card.
- **The pause is the rhythm.** The pulse of the table
  is in the moments between actions.
- **Stakes must matter.** Use the anchor sheet to make
  the dollar amounts feel like the weeks or months of
  labor they represent.
- **The professional gambler is a specific type.**
  Dress, manner, voice, hands. See
  `references/speech-patterns.md`.
- **Cheating is procedural.** The method is specific
  to the game. The detection is specific to the
  method.
- **The table is more than the game.** Information,
  deals, watching, courting. The visible activity
  hides the real business.

## 14. The rule the others serve

The gambling table is the West's stage. Every rank,
every nation, every trade gathered around it. The
prose that renders a table well opens the world to
the reader because everything came through it: money,
news, women, deals, deaths. The writer who masters the
table can write any scene the West has to offer.

The rules of faro are the rules of faro. The rhythm
of the deal is the rhythm of the prose. The man who
is losing and the man who is cheating and the woman
who is watching are the chapter's center.
