# Language and Diction

The single fastest way to break the western dream is the wrong
word in a character's mouth. _Guy._ _Okay._ _Hopefully._ _Pre-
existing condition._ The reader hears the year wrong and the
spell drops. This file is the working dictionary.

Cross-reference `references/anti-ai-patterns.md` for the
contemporary-vocabulary kill list and `references/voices.md`
for register by writer. Cross-reference
`references/prose-craft.md` for dialogue mechanics.

---

## 1. The big rule

A 19th-century westerner spoke a 19th-century English. The
period English was:

- More formal in writing than ours.
- More biblical in cadence (the King James was the universal
  literary model).
- Heavily Latinate among the educated.
- Heavily idiomatic and regional among the working.
- Profane among men in male company; constrained around women
  and children, in church, in print.
- Slow to take new words; the modern habit of fast slang
  turnover was not yet established.

Get this background right and the period speaks itself.

## 3. The slurs — what to do with them

A grim section. Read carefully and decide deliberately, scene by
scene.

The 19th-century West used racial slurs that are now correctly
treated as harm. They were spoken in casual conversation by
ordinary people; they appeared in newspapers, in court records,
in the patter of saloon characters and the fond letters of
otherwise gentle men.

The writer's choice:

1. **Period-accurate use.** Some characters say what they
   would have said. The reader is shown the casual cruelty
   of the period as it was. Cormac McCarthy does this. Larry
   McMurtry does it (_Lonesome Dove_'s Bolivar uses Spanish
   slurs; the cowboys use racial epithets). The risk is that
   the contemporary reader takes the slur as authorial
   endorsement.
2. **Restricted use.** Slurs appear only in moments where
   they carry narrative weight (a man revealing his cruelty,
   a town's casual exclusion). Avoided in narration, used
   only in dialogue, used only when the moment demands it.
3. **Soft-pedaled.** The writer leaves slurs implied, written
   as _the word he used,_ avoided on the page. This is a
   defensible choice for many books and is the choice most
   contemporary literary western novels make.

The N-word in particular is the hardest. Period dialogue from
white characters about Black characters used it routinely. A
Confederate veteran in Texas in 1875 used it without thought.
A reader in the present cannot read it without thought. The
gap between _period accuracy_ and _current harm_ is real.

The author's stance — see `references/uncomfortable-truths.md`
section 12 — is _this was so._ The author does not endorse
the slur or the men who used it. The author shows the world.

Practical guidance:

- If you use a slur, use it because the moment requires it.
  Do not use it for color or for shock.
- Show the harm. The slur is not a piece of period flavor like
  a saddle horn. It is a wound being inflicted. Write the wound.
- The slur is more powerful when it is rare. A page that has
  the word four times has lost weight by the fifth. A page that
  has the word once and the word lands.
- Some books need the word. Some books work without it. Decide
  early and follow through.
- Slurs against Mexicans (_greaser, spik_), against Chinese
  (_chink, John, Celestial, Mongolian_), against Native people
  (_savage, redskin, buck, squaw_) are period-correct in
  white speech and require the same deliberate choice.
- Slurs spoken between members of a group (a Black character
  using the N-word with another Black character; a Mexican
  character using _gringo_ of an Anglo) follow different rules
  than across-group speech. Get a sensitivity reader if you are
  working outside your own background.

The bottom line: slurs are not decoration. They are a load-
bearing element of the period and require deliberate handling.

## 4. Period slang — by trade and by region

A working selection. Add as you read.

### a. Cowhand / drover

- _Aboard_ — in the saddle.
- _Bell mare_ — the lead mare with the bell on her, around
  whom the remuda gathered.
- _Bog rider_ — a hand whose job was to pull cows out of mud
  holes.
- _Brand-blotcher_ — a rustler who altered brands.
- _Brush-popper_ — a south Texas hand who worked cattle out of
  thick brush.
- _Bronc_ / _bronco_ — an unbroken horse.
- _Buckaroo_ — California-Nevada term for cowhand (from
  _vaquero_).
- _Chaps / chinks_ — leather leggings.
- _Chuck_ — food. _Chuck wagon, chuck box, chuck up_ (eat).
- _Cinch up_ — tighten the cinch; metaphorically, settle a
  matter.
- _Cookie / coosie / dough-puncher / biscuit-shooter_ — the cook.
- _Cowpoke_ — period-correct from the practice of poking
  cattle through the slats of a stockcar.
- _Cut_ — to separate from the herd.
- _Dally_ — to wrap the rope around the saddle horn (Spanish
  _dale vuelta,_ give it a turn).
- _Dogie_ — an orphan calf.
- _Drag_ — the rear of the moving herd.
- _Drift_ — to wander, as cattle do.
- _Fill his hand_ (with a pistol) — draw.
- _Fork a horse_ — mount and ride.
- _Forty-rod_ — cheap whiskey (kills you at forty rods).
- _Greenhorn_ — newcomer.
- _Grub_ — food.
- _Hog-tie_ — bind the legs of a calf or a captive.
- _Honda_ — the loop at the end of a rope through which the
  rope runs to make a noose.
- _Jingle_ (the remuda) — bring it in.
- _Kick over the traces_ — break out of restraint.
- _Maverick_ — an unbranded calf.
- _Mossy horn_ — an old steer.
- _Nester_ — a homesteader (cattleman's pejorative).
- _Outfit_ — a ranch and crew, or a man's gear.
- _Point_ — the lead position on a moving herd.
- _Powder-burner_ — a gunfighter.
- _Pull leather_ — grab the saddle horn (a sign of poor
  riding).
- _Quirly_ — a roll-your-own cigarette.
- _Range hog_ — a man who claimed too much range.
- _Remuda_ — the herd of saddle horses.
- _Rope corral_ — a corral made by stretching a rope around
  the horse herd.
- _Rustler_ — a thief of stock.
- _Saddle bum_ — a drifting cowhand.
- _Savvy_ — to understand (from _sabe_).
- _Segundo_ — the second in command on a drive.
- _Sky pilot_ — a preacher.
- _Slick / slick-eared_ — unbranded.
- _Snake-eyes_ — two ones on the dice; bad luck.
- _Sourdough_ — the cook, or a long-term hand.

- _Pull the pin_ — leave (a job, a place).
- _Rustler_ — stock thief.
- _Sneak (verb)_ — burgle.

## 5. Spanish in the Anglo West

The Anglo cowman of Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, southern
Colorado, southern California spoke a working English heavily
salted with Spanish loans and pidgin Spanish for trade and stock
work.

Period loans (use freely in southwestern setting):

- _adios, amigo, andale, bandido, bronco, buckaroo (vaquero),
  caballero, calaboose (calabozo), canyon (cañón), cañon,
  chaparral, chaps (chaparreras), corral, coyote, dally (dale
  vuelta), fandango, frijole, hacienda, hombre, hoosegow
  (juzgado), lariat (la reata), lasso (lazo), llano, loco,
  mesa, mesquite, mustang (mesteño), padre, palomino, peon,
  pinto, plaza, poncho, pronto, ranch (rancho), remuda, rodeo,
  rurales, sabe, savvy (sabe), señor, señora, señorita, sierra,
  sombrero, stampede (estampida), tortilla, vaquero_

A working cowhand spoke perhaps a hundred words of Spanish for
his trade. Very few Anglos were fluent. The Tejano vaqueros and
the Mexican peons spoke Spanish among themselves and adapted
English with their employers.

For dialogue: an English-only Anglo character does not switch
into formal Spanish phrases. He uses the words he learned at
work, often mispronounced (the Anglo _lar-yet_ for _la reata_).

A Spanish-speaking character in English narration: write the
English clean, indicate Spanish through occasional untranslated
words and through syntax (_you have not eaten today, no?_).
Resist the urge to write phonetic accents (_Si, señor, the man
who is who you say is here, but he is not the man_) — it
condescends. Trust the reader.

## 6. Registers — how a character sounds at his rank

### a. The illiterate working man

- Short sentences.
- Plain Anglo-Saxon vocabulary (no Latinate words; _got_ not
  _received_).
- Idioms heavy.
- Profane in male company; constrained around women, children,
  preachers.
- Drops grammar (_I seen him; I done it; he don't; we ain't
  got none_).

Example:

> _Well, I seen the man down at the corral and I told him I
> reckoned the bay had thrown a shoe. He didn't say nothing.
> Just went out there and looked._

### b. The literate working man

- Slightly fuller sentences.
- Some Latinate vocabulary from school.
- Less grammar-dropping but some.
- Newspaper-influenced phrasing (men read the _Police Gazette_
  and the local weekly).

Example:

> _I observed the man at the corral and informed him the bay
> had cast a shoe. He made no reply, but went out and looked
> her over._

### c. The educated man

- Latinate, formal, periodic sentences when he chooses.
- Code-switches into the working register depending on company.
- Quotes scripture, sometimes Shakespeare or Cicero.
- Speaks differently in court, in church, in the saloon, at
  home.

Example:

> _I encountered the gentleman at the corral and brought to
> his attention that the bay had thrown a shoe. He neither
> spoke nor acknowledged me, but proceeded to inspect the
> animal._

### d. The immigrant

- Imports syntax from his first language. The German speaker
  puts the verb at the end (_the bay her shoe had thrown_).
  The Mexican speaker uses Spanish word order (_the bay, she
  has thrown a shoe_). The Chinese speaker (in pidgin English)
  drops articles and conjugates loosely (_bay horse lose
  shoe_).
- Use lightly. A heavy hand makes the character a cartoon.
  One marker per long speech is enough.

### e. The military man

- Brisk, abrupt, declarative.
- Military jargon (_detachment, company, troop, picket,
  vidette, skirmish line, double time_).
- Profane in the field, formal in HQ.
- _Sir_ and _Captain_ and _Colonel_ used liberally.

### f. The preacher

- Biblical cadence (the King James was the model).
- Long sentences, rolling parallel structure.
- Latinate when reading; plainer when extemporizing.

Example:

> _Brethren, the Lord did not bring us out of bondage, did
> not lead us through the wilderness, did not deliver us upon
> this land of milk and honey, that we should forget Him in
> the prosperity of our hand._

### g. The Eastern dude

- Latinate, formal, slightly stiff.
- Uses Eastern slang the locals do not.
- Pronounces foreign loans as foreign (the _vah-ker-oh_ of the
  Boston schoolboy versus the _back-ah-ro_ of the cowhand).
- Often overformal where the situation calls for plain speech.

## 7. Profanity

The 19th century was rich and fluent in profanity, but the
profanity differed from ours.

### a. The rough male register

- _God damn, goddamned, by God, damnation, hell, hell-fire,
  the very devil, son of a bitch, bastard, bastard's bastard,
  whoreson_
- _Shit_ and _piss_ were old words and used in male company.
  _Fuck_ existed and was used (American working-class profanity
  has been continuous since at least the 17th century) but
  written record is poor because of print law and editorial
  bowdlerization.
- _Cock, prick_ — period-correct.
- The cunt-word — period; used; rare in mixed company.

### b. Constrained registers

- Around women, in church, in print: euphemism. _Dad-burn,
  dad-blame, by gum, by jingo, dagnabbit, dang, darn, gosh,
  golly, gee whillikers, gee whiz, jiminy, jeez, blame me,
  doggone, blasted, confound it._
- _Heck, dang, gosh_ are post-1850s euphemisms and are
  period-correct.
- A rough character switching into the polite register in front
  of a lady is a common period beat. The reader watches the
  effort.

### c. The rule

If you write profanity, write it because the moment requires
it. A man under fire swears. A man in church does not. A man
at a card table swears in proportion to his hand. A man
addressing a strange woman in the street does not swear at all.

Underwriting profanity is more period-accurate than over-
writing it. Most published 19th-century writing scrubbed the
profanity out entirely. The reader who has read the period
knows the prose was cleaner than the speech. A modern western
that lays the profanity on thick can read like a 1970s parody.
The trick is selective use — make every _goddamn_ land.

  Vega, Flores. (Compound: García y López, Spanish naming
  convention.)

### e. Black names

- Many freedmen took new names on emancipation. Common: Lincoln,
  Washington, Jefferson, Freeman, Liberty, January / February
  / March (born in those months). Also biblical (Moses, Caleb,
  Joshua). Surnames: often the former owner's surname; some-
  times an entirely new name.

### f. Native names

- A complex matter. Many Native people had multiple names: a
  birth name, a youth name, an adult name (taken on a deed or
  vision), sometimes a name taken on conversion or by Anglo
  imposition.
- _English_ names given by Anglos (Sitting Bull, Crazy Horse,
  Red Cloud, Spotted Tail, Black Kettle, Roman Nose, Geronimo,
  Cochise, Sequoyah) are translations of (or in Geronimo's
  case, mispronunciations of) the original.
- Use the Native name where you can, in the language of the
  people. _Tȟašúŋke Witkó_ for Crazy Horse (Lakota). _Quanah
  Parker_ (Comanche, mixed Anglo). Quote names accurately;
  do not invent generic Native names.

### g. Chinese names

- Chinese surname first, given name second. Chen, Wong, Lee,
  Liu, Zhang, Wang, Lin, Huang, Yang, Wu. Given names two
  syllables typically.
- Anglos in the period often called Chinese men _John_ generic-
  ally or used a single given-name segment with no surname.

### h. Saloon names, ranch names, town names

- Saloons: the Long Branch (Dodge), the Crystal Palace
  (Tombstone), the Bird Cage Theatre (Tombstone), the
  Oriental, the Alhambra, the Bull's Head, the Lady Gay, the
  Brown Jug, the Bucket of Blood (multiple). Often colorful;
  often named for an animal, a precious thing, a play, a
  proper name. Often _the_ + adjective + noun.
- Ranches: the brand became the name (the JA, the XIT, the
  Three-D, the Quarter Circle Box, the Lazy S). Or geography
  (the Goodnight, the Pitchfork, the Matador, the Spur).
- Towns: incorporated under a name proposed by the founder
  (often himself), the railroad superintendent, a politician
  whom the founders wished to flatter, an Eastern hometown,
  or geography. Spanish names for older Mexican towns. Native-
  derived names mediated through Anglo mishearing (Wichita,
  Cheyenne, Omaha, Pawnee).

## 9. The diction test

Before you accept a line of dialogue, run it through:

1. Could this character have said this in this year?
2. Does the syntax fit his rank, his region, his trade?
3. Is the vocabulary period-correct?
4. Is the profanity calibrated to the company?
5. Does it sound like speech, not like a written sentence?
6. Read it aloud. Does it ring?

If yes to all six, keep it. If no to any, rewrite.

## 10. The single example to keep in mind

Here is Charles Portis, _True Grit,_ Mattie Ross narrating in
1928 the events of 1878:

> _People do not give it credence that a fourteen-year-old
> girl could leave home and go off in the wintertime to avenge
> her father's blood but it did not seem so strange then,
> although I will say it did not happen every day._

Read it carefully. Notice:

- _give it credence_ — Latinate, formal.
- _did not give it credence that_ — period syntax (we would
  say _believe that today_).
- _avenge her father's blood_ — biblical / period diction
  (the modern phrasing would be _get revenge for her father's
  killing_).
- _I will say_ — period qualifier.
- _it did not happen every day_ — colloquial.
- _I will say it did not happen every day, although_ — the
  whole movement, formal then colloquial then qualified, is
  pure 19th-century narrator on the page.

Portis got every word right. Not a syllable is anachronistic.
The line could have been written in 1880 and read as well.

Aim there. The diction is everything.
