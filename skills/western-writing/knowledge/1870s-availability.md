# 1870s Availability — What Exists and What Does Not

This file is the strict catalog of what was available in the
1870s American West and what was not. The wrong-decade object
is one of the most reliable AI tells: the cowboy with a
zipper, the sheriff with a flashlight, the saloon with a
jukebox, the cowman drinking a Coca-Cola, the homesteader
loading her .30-30. Each one snaps the spell.

The decade in question runs January 1, 1870 to December 31, 1879. Many references narrow further (early 1870s = 1870-1873,
mid = 1874-1876, late = 1877-1879). Where a technology arrived
mid-decade it is dated. Where it was widespread by the end of
the decade it is so noted.

This file pairs with `knowledge/material-culture.md` (gear
specs) and `knowledge/towns-economy-law.md` (institutional
context). Cross-reference both.

---

## 1. The default rule

If you are not certain a thing existed in 1875, do not put it
in 1875. The book the author you are imitating wrote was
written by a man who _knew_; the AI who guesses gives itself
away in one wrong noun.

The bias should always be toward _not yet._ The West of fiction
arrives later than the West of history because the technology
took a year to a decade to reach a Texas ranch from a New York
factory. A telephone existed in Boston in 1876; it did not
reach the west Texas range for another twenty years.

## 2. The technology timeline (decade-by-decade snapshot)

### a. By 1870 (the start of the decade)

- Repeating rifles: yes (Henry, Spencer, Winchester '66).
- Cartridge revolvers: rare; conversions of percussion arms
  appearing.
- Telegraph: yes, in major cities and along railroads.
  Transcontinental telegraph completed 1861. Messages cost
  $0.50 to $1.00 for the first ten words plus per-word
  charges; not casual.
- Railroad: transcontinental completed May 10, 1869. The
  Union Pacific runs Omaha to Promontory; the Central
  Pacific Sacramento to Promontory. Branch lines push out
  through the decade.
- Steamboat: yes, on the Missouri, the Mississippi, the Ohio,
  the Sacramento, parts of the Columbia and the Snake. Major
  freight and passenger artery.
- Stagecoach: yes; the great transcontinental stage lines
  (Butterfield, Wells Fargo) dwindle as railroads extend
  but local stages run everywhere.
- Photography: yes (wet-plate collodion process, 1851-1880s).
  Daguerreotypes (1840s-1850s) and tintypes (1856 onward)
  predate the period; tintype portraits common in the West
  through the period. Glass-plate negatives standard for
  studio portraits; flexible film not until 1885.
- Sewing machine: yes (Singer 1851, by 1870 widespread).
- Canned food: yes. Tin cans since the 1810s; canned milk
  (Borden, 1856), canned condensed milk widespread by 1870;
  canned meat (Underwood deviled ham 1868); canned peaches,
  tomatoes, oysters available in mercantile stores. Expensive
  on the frontier.
- Refined sugar: yes.
- Coffee: yes. Green beans shipped, parched at home or in
  camp until pre-roasted Arbuckles' Ariosa coffee (introduced
  1864 and shipped widely from 1865) becomes the camp staple.
  Arbuckles' is the cowboy's coffee through the period.
- Whiskey: yes. Bonded brands (Old Crow, Old Forester arrives
  1870), unbonded rotgut everywhere.

### b. Arriving 1870-1873

- Colt Single Action Army (Peacemaker): introduced 1873. The
  iconic western pistol. Not in 1872. Available 1873 onward,
  widespread by 1875.
- Winchester Model 1873: introduced 1873 in .44-40. The
  iconic western lever-action. Common from 1874.
- .44-40 cartridge: introduced 1873 with the Winchester '73,
  and adopted by the Colt SAA in 1878 (the _Frontier Six-
  Shooter_) — so a man could carry one cartridge for both
  rifle and pistol.
- Levi's denim jeans: Jacob Davis and Levi Strauss patent the
  copper-riveted overalls May 20, 1873. Levi's is from 1873
  onward. _Not_ in 1872. The 501 designation comes later.
- Barbed wire: Joseph Glidden patents the type that succeeds
  in 1874. Sold widely from 1875. Transforms the open range
  through the late 1870s and 1880s. _Not_ in 1873.
- Sharps Model 1874: introduced 1874. The buffalo rifle of
  the great hide hunts.
- Typewriter: Sholes and Glidden Type-Writer manufactured by
  Remington from 1874. Office machine, slowly adopted; rare
  in the West before the 1880s.
- Steel windmill: Halladay self-governing windmill (1854) and
  others; cheap steel windmills for stock water from late
  1870s; widespread on the Plains 1880s onward.

### c. Arriving 1874-1876

- Telephone: Bell patents March 7, 1876. First commercial
  telephone exchange (New Haven) January 28, 1878. _Not_ in 1875. _Not_ in 1876 except in Boston laboratories. Reaches
  major Eastern and Western cities in the 1880s. A telephone
  in a Texas ranch house in 1878 is wrong by twenty years.
- Carpet sweeper: Bissell, 1876.
- Electric arc lighting: Yablochkov candle 1876, used in
  street lighting in major cities late 1870s. _Not_ in homes
  or saloons.
- Edison's incandescent lamp: October 1879 lab; commercial
  installations from 1882. _No_ electric incandescent light
  in any 1870s setting.

### d. Arriving 1877-1879

- Phonograph: Edison patents December 1877, public
  demonstration 1878. Tinfoil cylinder. A novelty curiosity
  in cities; not a practical music device. The cowboy in 1879
  does not hear recorded music.
- Microphone (carbon): 1878. Improves the telephone but does
  not put one in his bunkhouse.
- Cash register: Ritty patents 1879. Diffuses through the
  1880s.

### e. Not yet anywhere in the 1870s

- Smokeless powder: French Poudre B 1884. _All_ 1870s firearms
  use black powder. The smoke is part of every gunfight.
- Bolt-action smokeless rifles: 1880s and later. The hunter
  in 1875 does not have a Mauser.
- Lever-action with .30-30: Winchester Model 1894 in
  smokeless .30 WCF (.30-30) introduced 1894. _Not_ in any
  1870s scene.
- Semi-automatic pistol: Borchardt 1893. _Not_ in any 1870s
  scene.
- Modern double-action revolver: rudimentary versions exist
  (Adams, Tranter, Beaumont-Adams in earlier decades; Colt
  Lightning 1877 is double-action but mechanically poor and
  rare). The standard 1870s western revolver is single-action.
- Automobile: 1880s laboratory; 1890s commercial. _No_ car.
- Bicycle: Penny-farthing (high-wheeler) from 1870 in cities;
  rare in the West. Safety bicycle (the modern form) from 1885.
- Powered flight: Wright Brothers 1903.
- Radio: Marconi 1895. _No_ radio.
- Motion pictures: Edison Kinetograph 1891. _No_ film.
- Cassette tape, vinyl record, transistor radio, television,
  computer, mobile phone, Internet — obviously not.
- Refrigeration: mechanical refrigeration is in
  meatpacking plants by the 1870s (Swift refrigerator car
  1878). Domestic refrigerator: 1920s. The 1870s home and
  saloon use ice (cut from northern lakes, shipped south by
  rail in insulated cars, sold by the ice company in town).
- Electric refrigeration in saloons: no. The bar's beer and
  ice is cut, shipped, and stored in an icebox.
- Aspirin: Bayer 1899. _Not_ in any 1870s medicine chest.
- Penicillin: 1928. The antibiotic era is post-1940. The 1870s
  doctor has whiskey, opium, morphine (yes — by hypodermic
  syringe from the late 1850s), quinine, mercury (for
  syphilis, ineffective), bismuth (for diarrhea), camphor,
  ipecac, paregoric, calomel, lime water, laudanum, cocaine
  (from the late 1880s — _not_ 1870s), heroin (Bayer 1898 —
  _not_ 1870s).
- Vaccine: smallpox vaccination, yes (Jenner, 1796; routine
  in the 1870s). Other vaccines: rabies (Pasteur, 1885).
  Diphtheria antitoxin: 1890s. The 1870s doctor cannot
  cure diphtheria, scarlet fever, cholera, typhoid, or
  tuberculosis.
- Anesthetic: yes. Ether (1846) and chloroform (1847)
  available to surgeons. Nitrous oxide (1844). A frontier
  doctor may have ether or chloroform. A frontier dentist
  may; many do not.

## 3. Goods — what is in the mercantile

A 1875 general store carries the following. If your scene
calls for an item not on this list, verify before writing.

### a. Foodstuffs

- Flour (sack), cornmeal (sack), oats (sack), barley (sack).
- Sugar (loaf and granulated; brown sugar in some stores).
- Salt (block and granulated).
- Coffee (Arbuckles' Ariosa from late 1860s; green beans for
  home parching).
- Tea (black tea; some green tea in coastal towns).
- Beans (pinto, red, navy).
- Rice.
- Lard (in tin pails).
- Salt pork (in barrel).
- Bacon (sliced from the side, sold by the pound).
- Ham (whole or sliced).
- Hardtack (in barrel; military surplus and commercial).
- Crackers (in barrel; soda crackers).
- Cheese (wheel; cut by the pound).
- Butter (when local).
- Eggs (when local).
- Canned milk (Borden's from 1856; Eagle Brand from 1853).
- Canned tomatoes, peaches, oysters, sardines, deviled ham.
- Pickles (in barrel).
- Vinegar (in barrel).
- Molasses (in barrel; New Orleans molasses, Vermont sorghum).
- Honey (when local).
- Dried fruit (apples, peaches, raisins).
- Spices (pepper, cinnamon, nutmeg, cloves, allspice; ginger).
- Yeast (compressed yeast cakes from 1868; sourdough starter
  on the trail).

Not present (or not as we know them):

- Bananas (yes by 1880s in major cities; rare on Western
  frontier in 1870s).
- Citrus (oranges shipped from California or Florida; expensive
  in the interior).
- Frozen anything.
- Modern breakfast cereals (Granola 1863 and corn flakes
  1894 — _no flakes in 1870s_).
- Baking powder (yes — Royal Baking Powder from 1866,
  widespread).

### b. Drinks

- Whiskey: bonded (named brands at $3-5 the bottle) and
  rotgut ($1-2 the bottle).
- Beer: lager (German immigrant brewers), bottled and on
  draft. Refrigerated rail cars permit beer shipment by the
  late 1870s.
- Wine: domestic California wine from the late 1860s;
  imported wines in the parlor saloons and grand hotels.
- Champagne: imported French; expensive ($5+ the bottle in
  the parlor house).
- Soda water: yes (carbonated water from a soda fountain).
- Coca-Cola: introduced 1886. _Not_ in 1879.
- Pepsi: 1898. _Not_ in 1879.
- Root beer (Hires 1876 commercial). Available by mid-decade
  in cities; not necessarily on the frontier in 1875.
- Sarsaparilla: yes, available as a patent medicine syrup
  through the period.
- Lemonade: yes, when lemons available.

### c. Tobacco

- Plug (chewing tobacco): yes, dominant form. Bull Durham
  smoking tobacco from 1858; widespread.
- Loose (smoking, for the roll-your-own): yes.
- Cigars: yes (cigar-making is a major Cuban-American
  industry; cigars from $0.05 each to several dollars).
- Cigarettes: existed but uncommon. A cowman _rolls his own_
  with paper and tobacco. Pre-rolled cigarettes are an
  Eastern dandy's affectation in 1875; mass-market machine-
  rolled cigarettes (Bonsack machine) come in 1881-1884.
- Pipe: yes; corncob pipe (Missouri Meerschaum from 1869),
  briar, clay.

### d. Clothing

- Boots: custom-made by local bootmaker ($15-30) or ready-
  made ($5-10). The cowboy boot proper (high heel, narrow
  toe, tall shaft) develops through the period; in 1870 the
  _Wellington_ and the _military_ boot are dominant. By
  1880 a recognizable _cowboy_ boot exists.
- Hat: the John B. Stetson _Boss of the Plains_ (1865) is
  the standard; Stetson hats $5-10. Other felt and straw hats
  available. The _cowboy hat_ shape is set; brim widths and
  crown styles vary by region.
- Levi's jeans: 1873 onward. Copper-riveted overalls, $1.50.
- Wool trousers: standard for most occasions.
- Linen and cotton shirts: ready-made in the mercantile.
- Bandana: cotton, large, in red or blue paisley primarily.
- Vest: standard with the working suit and with the cowman's
  rig.
- Coat: working coat (canvas, wool), Sunday coat (broadcloth
  or wool serge), buffalo coat for winter.
- Long underwear: union suit (one-piece) from 1868; widespread
  in cold country.
- Dresses: women's; ready-made or sewn at home from store
  bolts of cloth. The bustle is fashionable through much of
  the decade. Working dress is calico or gingham; Sunday dress
  is finer cloth.
- Corset: yes, standard women's underwear. Stays of whalebone
  or steel.
- Hoop skirt: declining through the period; the bustle
  succeeds it.
- Gloves: leather (work) and kid (dress).
- Hosiery: knitted; cotton or wool; held by garters.

### e. Hardware and tools

- Iron tools: hammer, chisel, plane, saw, brace, bit, axe,
  hatchet, sledge, pick, shovel, spade.
- Nails: cut nails (until late 1880s wire nails take over);
  square-shanked, flat-tipped.
- Screws: yes, slotted; the Phillips head is 1933 — _not_
  in 1870s.
- Files, rasps, augers, draw knives, planes.
- Plow: cast-iron and steel (John Deere steel plow from 1837;
  by 1870 the standard). Sulky plow (riding plow) from 1875
  in some regions.
- Reaper, mower: yes, McCormick reaper from 1834; mower from
  1850s; widespread on the wheat frontier.
- Threshing machine: yes; horse-powered or steam-powered (a
  steam threshing rig is a major investment but exists from
  the 1860s).
- Steam tractor: rare and large; from the 1850s; widespread
  on the wheat frontier from the 1880s.
- Internal combustion engine: late 1870s laboratory; _not_ in
  any 1870s farm or shop.

### f. Household goods

- Kerosene lamp: standard. Kerosene (refined from petroleum,
  available from late 1850s) is the lighting fuel of the
  period. Whale oil declines.
- Candles: tallow (home-made) and stearin (manufactured).
- Matches: yes (friction matches from 1830s; safety matches
  from 1855). Lucifers, sulphur matches.
- Soap: lye soap (home-made) and bar soap (Procter & Gamble
  Ivory from 1879; _not_ in 1878). Pears' soap, Castile soap,
  Fels-Naptha (1894 — _not_ in 1870s).
- Washboard: standard.
- Wringer: yes (wringer washtubs from 1858; widespread).
- Sad iron (flatiron): heated on the stove.
- Cookstove: wood- or coal-burning; cast iron; standard in
  any settled home. Range of qualities from a sheet-iron camp
  stove to an elaborate Acme cast-iron range with reservoir.
- Refrigerator: an _icebox_ (an insulated wooden cabinet with
  a compartment for a block of ice). The mechanical refrigerator
  is 20th century.
- Sewing machine: standard (Singer 1851 onward).
- Phonograph, radio, television: no.
- Glass jar canning: yes (Mason jar 1858; widespread).
- Bottled patent medicine: yes, abundantly.

### g. Stationery and writing

- Steel-nib pen and inkwell: standard. Quill pens declining.
- Fountain pen: Lewis Waterman patents 1884; _not_ in 1870s
  (earlier reservoir pens existed and were unreliable).
- Pencils: yes (Faber 1761; widespread). Mechanical pencil
  (1830s onward). Eraser (rubber eraser 1770; combined
  pencil-and-eraser 1858 onward).
- Stationery: foolscap, letter paper, envelopes (gummed
  envelopes since 1840s; widespread).
- Typewriter: 1874 (Sholes-Glidden / Remington Number 1).
  Office adoption slow; rare in West before 1880s.
- Carbon paper: yes (carbon paper from the 1820s; standard
  office tool by 1870s).

### h. Patent medicine

- Common: Hostetter's Bitters (heavily alcoholic), Lydia
  Pinkham's (women's tonic), Dr. Williams' Pink Pills,
  paregoric (camphorated tincture of opium), laudanum (opium
  in alcohol), morphine and the hypodermic syringe (military
  legacy from the Civil War; widespread addiction), various
  cough syrups containing opiates, calomel (mercury),
  blue mass.
- Cocaine: not yet a household ingredient; cocaine isolated
  1859, in some patent medicines from late 1880s. _Not_ the
  Coca-Cola of 1886.
- Heroin: 1898. _Not_ in 1870s.
- Aspirin: 1899. _Not_ in 1870s.

## 4. Services — what a town has

A county-seat town of 1,500 in 1875 could be expected to have:

- General store / mercantile.
- Saloon (often several; a cattle town might have twenty).
- Hotel (one to several).
- Boarding house (one to several).
- Stable / livery / feed.
- Blacksmith / farrier.
- Wheelwright (often combined with the blacksmith).
- Doctor (sometimes; smaller towns shared a circuit doctor).
- Dentist (rarely; often the town doctor or the blacksmith).
- Lawyer (in any county seat).
- Bank (small, often).
- Newspaper (weekly broadsheet).
- Telegraph office (in any railroad town).
- Express office (Wells Fargo, American Express).
- Stage office (declining, but present).
- Post office (in the mercantile or in its own building).
- Land office (in any new settlement zone).
- Railroad depot (if on the railroad).
- Saddler / harness maker.
- Gunsmith (in larger towns).
- Tinsmith.
- Cobbler / bootmaker.
- Tailor (in larger towns).
- Barber.
- Bath house (often combined with the barber).
- Chinese laundry (in any town with a Chinese community).
- Restaurant / chop house.
- Church (one or several).
- School (one-room).
- Courthouse (in the county seat).
- Jail.
- Undertaker (often the town carpenter).
- Brothel / dance hall / parlor house (often).
- Photographer (in larger towns; in smaller, an itinerant
  photographer comes through).

What a town does _not_ have:

- Hospital. (Hospitals existed in cities; the frontier town
  treated the sick at home or in the doctor's parlor. The
  Sisters of Charity ran hospitals in some larger Western
  towns.)
- Public library (occasional; Carnegie libraries are 1890s+).
- Fire department (volunteer brigade in larger towns; bucket
  brigade in smaller).
- Sewer system (privies and outhouses; cesspools; the city
  water-and-sewer infrastructure is late-19th-century in
  larger towns and 20th in smaller).
- Indoor plumbing (rare; fancy hotels have it; private homes
  by the 1880s in cities and the 1890s in small towns).
- Electric lighting (commercial electric lighting from 1882
  and after; _no_ electric lights in 1870s buildings).
- Telephone exchange (1878 New Haven; reaches the West in
  the 1880s and 1890s).
- Movie theater, radio station, record store, etc.: no.

## 5. Transportation — what carries a man and what does not

### a. By horse

- Saddle horse: standard. A man rides forty to fifty miles
  in a long day, twenty-five to thirty in a comfortable day.
- Pack horse / mule: carries gear. A pack mule carries 200
  pounds; a horse less.
- Wagon: family transport, freight. Two miles per hour with
  oxen, three to four with horses.
- Buggy / buckboard / spring wagon: light passenger transport.

### b. By rail

- Passenger train: by 1879 a man can travel from New York to
  San Francisco in seven days on the transcontinental, with
  changes in Chicago and Omaha. Ticket: $100+ first class,
  $40 second class, $20 emigrant car.
- Freight train: yes; cattle by stockcar; grain by hopper.
- Pullman sleeper: yes (Pullman from 1864). $2-3 a berth the
  night.

The railroads in the 1870s West:

- Union Pacific (Omaha to Promontory).
- Central Pacific (Sacramento to Promontory).
- Kansas Pacific (reaches Denver 1870).
- Atchison Topeka & Santa Fe (Atchison KS to La Junta CO by
  1875; pushes south through New Mexico through 1879).
- Texas & Pacific (chartered 1871; eastern Texas through the
  decade).
- Northern Pacific (chartered 1864; bankrupted in the Panic
  of 1873; resumes construction late 1870s; Twin Cities to
  Bismarck by 1873; Pacific completion 1883).
- Southern Pacific (Los Angeles to Yuma by 1877).
- Denver and Rio Grande (Denver south to Pueblo and beyond
  through the late 1870s).

A character in 1875 cannot take the train from Dodge City to
Tombstone. The Atchison reaches Dodge in 1872 but Tombstone is
not on rail until later. A character in 1879 cannot fly. A
character in 1873 cannot use a Texas & Pacific line that has
not yet reached his town.

### c. By stage

- Operated on routes the railroad has not reached. A stagecoach
  carries six to nine passengers and travels four to six miles
  per hour with frequent changes of teams. Long-distance fares
  $0.10-0.15 per mile.
- The great cross-country lines (Butterfield, Overland) have
  been displaced by the railroad. Local lines remain
  important — Tucson to Phoenix, Cheyenne to Deadwood, El
  Paso to San Antonio.

### d. By foot

- Standard for the poor. A man walks three miles per hour and
  twenty-five miles in a long day.

### e. By steamboat

- The Missouri (Bismarck to St. Louis): yes through the
  decade.
- The Mississippi: yes.
- The Ohio: yes.
- The Sacramento: yes.
- The Columbia, the Snake (parts): yes.
- The Colorado (parts): yes, irregularly.
- A character in Wyoming Territory does not take a steamboat.

## 6. Communication — what reaches whom and how fast

### a. The mail

- The U.S. Postal Service runs to any town. Letter rate: 3
  cents the half-ounce within the U.S. (since 1863 — the
  3-cent rate runs through the 1880s). Postcard rate: 1 cent
  (postcards introduced 1873).
- Delivery in town: often picked up at the post office (which
  was usually in the mercantile). Free home delivery in cities
  from 1863, in towns of 10,000+ from 1887. The Western
  villager picks up his mail in person.
- Time: Eastern letter to Western town: a week to ten days
  by 1879 (depending on rail connection). Western town to
  another Western town in the same region: two to three days.
  Letter from England: two to four weeks (Cunard mail steamer
  to New York, then rail).

### b. The telegraph

- Yes, since 1844 nationally and 1861 transcontinental.
  Western Union has the dominant network. Standard rate
  $0.50-$1.00 the first ten words plus per-word charges.
  Reaches any town on the railroad and many off it.
- _Cable_ across the Atlantic (transatlantic telegraph cable
  laid 1858, reliably operating from 1866). News from
  Europe reaches New York within a day. From New York to
  San Francisco: minutes.
- Local telegraph from a small town: the operator is in the
  railroad depot or the express office.

### c. The newspaper

- Local weekly: in any town. National news arrives by
  telegraph and is republished.
- New York and St. Louis dailies arrive by rail in the West;
  a man in Cheyenne reads yesterday's _New York Times_ on
  the day after.
- The wire services (Associated Press, since 1846) supply
  national news to the local papers.

### d. The telephone

- _No._ Patent 1876. New Haven exchange 1878. Reaches the
  Western city in the 1880s and the small Western town by 1900. A telephone in a 1870s scene is wrong.

## 7. Food and drink — what is on a 1870s table

### a. Cattle-country breakfast (working cowman)

- Coffee (Arbuckles', boiled, strong).
- Sourdough biscuit or hardtack.
- Bacon or salt pork.
- Beans (warmed-over from the night before).
- Sometimes eggs (in settled country, when chickens are
  kept).
- Sometimes beef.

### b. Town hotel breakfast (1875 county seat)

- Coffee or tea.
- Eggs, ham or bacon, hash browns or fried potatoes.
- Biscuits or hot bread.
- Steak (yes, beefsteak for breakfast was common).
- Pancakes with syrup or molasses.
- Stewed fruit.
- Oysters (yes, canned or iced, depending on the town).

### c. Sunday dinner (1875 farmhouse)

- Roast meat (beef, ham, pork roast, fried chicken).
- Mashed potatoes with gravy.
- Boiled greens (spring), squash (fall), root vegetables
  (winter).
- Hot bread or biscuits.
- Stewed dried fruit, pie, cake.
- Coffee or buttermilk.

### d. The trail drive table (cook on a chuck wagon)

- Beans (sourdough beans cooked all day in the iron pot).
- Beef or bacon.
- Sourdough biscuit.
- Hot Arbuckles' coffee.
- Stewed dried fruit (peaches, apricots, raisins) on a good
  day.
- Molasses, sometimes.
- Onion or wild onion when foraged.

### e. The miner's camp table

- Heavier; better paid. Bacon, beans, biscuits, beef when
  available, coffee, more canned goods, more tobacco. The
  hardrock miner of Leadville eats better than the Texas
  cowhand on the trail.

### f. The Mexican / Tejano table

- Tortillas (corn or wheat).
- Beans (frijoles, often pinto).
- Chile (red and green; rehydrated dried chiles for sauces;
  _Hatch_ and the modern New Mexico chiles develop later).
- Carne asada (grilled beef), carnitas, machaca, menudo.
- Tamales (especially Christmas).
- Coffee (Mexican-style, often with cinnamon).
- Atole (corn-based drink), chocolate (Mexican chocolate,
  bittersweet, with cinnamon).

### g. The Chinese cook's table (in mining camps and railroad

camps)

- Rice (imported from China through San Francisco).
- Salt fish, dried fish, dried mushroom.
- Pork, when available; chicken.
- Bok choy and other Chinese vegetables grown in market
  gardens around the camps.
- Soy sauce, ginger, garlic.
- Tea (more than coffee).

## 8. Money and currency

### a. The coin

- Gold: $20 double eagle (one ounce), $10 eagle, $5 half
  eagle, $2.50 quarter eagle, $1 (gold dollar minted 1849-
  1889).
- Silver: $1 (Liberty Seated dollar to 1873; Trade dollar
  1873-1885; Morgan dollar 1878-1904), $0.50, $0.25, $0.10
  (dime), $0.05 (half dime to 1873).
- Nickel: $0.05 (Shield nickel 1866-1883; Liberty nickel from
  1883).
- Copper: $0.01 (Indian Head cent 1859-1909). Two-cent piece
  (1864-1873). Three-cent piece (1851-1873).

### b. The greenback

- Federal paper currency. The U.S. Notes (greenbacks) issued
  during the Civil War remain in circulation. National Bank
  Notes (issued by national banks against deposits of U.S.
  bonds). Specie payments resumed 1879 — the greenback
  becomes redeemable for gold and silver.
- The greenback fluctuated against gold through the 1860s
  and 1870s. A man could receive his pay in greenbacks worth
  90 cents on the gold dollar.

### c. The Mexican coin

- The Mexican silver peso circulated freely in the Southwest
  alongside the U.S. dollar. Roughly equivalent to one U.S.
  silver dollar but valued by weight in many trades.
- The _real_ (1/8 of a peso, the _bit_ of _two bits, four
  bits, six bits_) survived as an accounting unit.
  _Two bits_ = $0.25. _Four bits_ = $0.50. _Six bits_ =
  $0.75.

### d. Gold dust

- In gold-rush regions (California, Colorado, Black Hills,
  later Yukon), gold dust was weighed out at the bar and at
  the mercantile counter. The merchant kept a small balance
  scale.

### e. Credit

- Long credit was the rule on the frontier. Cowmen settled
  with the merchant at shipping time once a year. Miners
  ran tabs at the saloon. Doctors waited months for
  payment. Bankruptcy on the frontier was common because the
  credit chain was long.

### f. Bank checks

- Yes; in use in the period. A merchant or a cowman would
  pay by draft on a Kansas City bank or a San Francisco
  bank. Banks discounted checks from distant institutions.

### g. Paper $1 bill design

- The 1869 _Rainbow_ Series ($1 with George Washington and
  _Christopher Columbus in sight of land_). Through the
  decade, U.S. Notes and National Bank Notes circulate.
  Federal Reserve Notes (the modern dollar bill) come 1914 —
  _not_ in 1870s.

## 10. Common AI fingerprints by wrong availability

A short field guide to errors AI commonly makes in 1870s
prose.

- **The flashlight.** Battery-powered electric torch invented 1899. _Lantern_ in the 1870s.
- **The matchbook.** Book matches 1892. _Matches in a tin or
  a small wooden box,_ or single _lucifers_ in the pocket.
- **The flashbulb.** Magnesium flash powder from 1887. _No_
  photography flash before that. Studio portraits use
  natural light or skylights.
- **The wristwatch.** Pocket watch, yes. Wristwatch is post-
  1900 (military adoption WWI). A man in 1875 takes out his
  pocket watch, snaps the case open, and consults it.
- **The zipper.** Patented 1893; widespread post-1920. _No_
  zipper in 1870s clothing. Buttons, hooks-and-eyes, lacing,
  drawstrings.
- **The paper bag.** Square-bottom paper bag (Margaret
  Knight's machine 1879 — _just_ arriving). Earlier: cones
  and cylinders rolled up by the merchant.
- **The brown paper sack with handles.** Handled paper sack
  is later. The mercantile wraps purchases in brown paper
  and twine.
- **The Coca-Cola sign.** 1886 onward.
- **The Marlboro Man.** 1955.
- **The cowboy hat with a _ten-gallon_ shape.** The high-
  crown white cowboy hat is a 20th-century rodeo and movie
  shape. The 1870s working hat is a _Boss of the Plains_
  Stetson — flat-crowned, modest brim, practical.
- **The cowboy quick-drawing from a low-slung holster.**
  The low-slung tied-down holster is a 20th-century movie
  invention. Period holsters ride high on the belt; the
  pistol comes out by lifting up and forward, not by
  dropping the hand to the thigh.
- **The pearl-handled six-shooter.** Existed but rare. The
  standard SAA grip is walnut. Pearl, ivory, mother-of-pearl
  grips were the gambler's or the showman's affectation.
- **The cowboy with mirrored sunglasses.** Sunglasses for
  general wear arrive in the early 20th century. Eye
  protection in 1875 is a hat brim and a bandanna. Tinted
  glasses for medical use existed (Sam Colt wore tinted
  spectacles).
- **The cowboy with a chrome belt buckle and a tooled
  belt.** Heavy show buckles are 20th-century rodeo. The
  1870s working cowboy's belt is plain leather, a simple
  brass or steel buckle, two inches wide.
- **The cowboy listening to country music on the radio.**
  Radio is 20th century. _Singing_ is the music of the
  trail, by men around a fire and by riders on night-herd.
- **The whiskey served on the rocks.** Ice in a glass of
  whiskey is rare in the 1870s saloon. Whiskey is served
  neat or with water (and sometimes sugar — a _toddy_). Ice
  in the saloon is for the beer keg in the back.

## 11. The cross-check

When in doubt, run the object through this five-question
gate before putting it in your 1875 scene:

1. **When was it invented?** A patent date is the floor.
2. **When was it commercialized?** Often years after invention.
3. **When did it reach the West?** Often years after
   commercialization. Many Eastern goods take 5-10 years to
   become routine on the frontier.
4. **Could a man in your scene's town and trade afford it?**
   The Stetson at $5-10 was within a cowman's reach. The
   $200 spring carriage was not.
5. **Would the character know it by your name?** A cowman
   in 1875 does not call his pistol a _Peacemaker_ or a
   _.45 long Colt_ the way a 1990s hobbyist does. He calls
   it his pistol, his Colt, his six-shooter, his hogleg.

If the object passes the gate, it is in the scene. If not,
substitute, omit, or reset the year.

Use this file every time you write a scene that includes a
