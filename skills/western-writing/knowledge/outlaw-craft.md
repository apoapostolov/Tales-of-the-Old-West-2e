# Outlaw Craft — The Practical Criminal World

This file is the knowledge module for the actual mechanics
of outlaw work in the 1870s American West. It is the
counterpart to `knowledge/towns-economy-law.md` (the law's
side) and pairs with `references/uncomfortable-truths.md`
(the moral context) and `references/violence-as-craft.md`
(the craft of writing the violent moment when it comes).
For the band's internal procedure as a game system, see
`proposals/proposal-ch11-outlaw-band-procedures.md`.

This is a corebook game called Tales of the Old West and
the outlaw is one of its central character types. The
prior skill modules treated outlaws lightly. This file is
the corrective. The outlaw was not a romantic figure on
horseback. He was a working criminal in a specific
economy with specific procedures, specific risks, and a
specific average life expectancy that was not long.

---

## 1. The economic reality

Most outlaws made far less than the dime novels claimed
and most died young or went to prison. The numbers
matter to the writer.

### Average take

- A successful train robbery in the 1870s netted between
  $1,000 and $5,000 for the gang, occasionally as much
  as $30,000 (the great exception: the 1873 Adair, Iowa
  robbery, the James-Younger gang, $2,300 cash and
  $30,000 in bonds the gang could not negotiate without
  fences, and the bonds were largely lost).
- A successful bank robbery netted similar amounts. The
  Northfield, Minnesota raid (September 1876) netted
  $26.70 in cash because the cashier locked the vault
  and was killed for it. The raid cost the gang four
  members shot dead in the street and three captured.
- A stage robbery netted $50 to $500 typically. The
  passengers carried personal cash; the express box
  carried the real money but most stages did not carry
  large express on most days.
- Horse theft of a single good horse: the horse sold to
  a fence for $30-$50. A herd of stolen horses driven
  to Mexico might net $20-$30 per head wholesale.
- Cattle rustling: a stolen steer brought $10-$20
  depending on the buyer's questions. A small operation
  rustling fifty head a season made $500-$1,000, less
  the cost of feed, men, and the fence's cut.

### The split

Gang shares were not equal. The leader took a larger
cut. The man who went into the express car or the bank
vault took more than the man holding horses. Inside men
(railroad employees, bank clerks who tipped the gang)
took ten to twenty percent off the top.

A six-man gang clearing $3,000 might split $600 to the
leader, $400 each to the inside men, $300 each to the
hands-on robbers, $150 to the horse holder. Less than
a year's wages for a cowhand in many cases, and the
cowhand did not face hanging.

### Life expectancy

The Wild Bunch (Cassidy, Longabaugh, Logan, et al.)
was an unusual gang in that several members lived
into the 1900s. The James-Younger gang lost half its
members at Northfield. The Daltons lost four out of
five at Coffeyville (1892). Most named outlaws of the
1870s were dead, in prison, or in hiding by 1880. The
career outlaw was a man who beat actuarial odds for as
long as he beat them.

## 2. The hideout

The outlaw camp was not a romantic canyon retreat. It
was a cramped, cold, hostile space where a gang waited
between jobs.

### The physical reality

A hideout was usually a remote line shack, a played-out
mine cabin, a brushy box canyon with one entrance, or
an isolated ranch owned by a sympathizer. None of them
were comfortable.

- **Cold.** Hideouts were chosen for remoteness, not
  shelter. Wind cut through the walls. Snow drifted
  in. A fire was risky because smoke could be seen for
  miles.
- **Cramped.** Six to ten men in a shack built for two.
  Bedrolls touching. Body smell. Constant friction.
- **Hostile food.** What men could carry in or shoot
  in the immediate vicinity. Beans, bacon, coffee, and
  whatever game had not been hunted out. After a
  month at a hideout the men were sick of each other
  and sick of the food.
- **No women.** A few hideouts had a woman cooking and
  doing washing — usually a sympathizer's wife or the
  leader's wife. Most did not. The lack of women is
  noted in every honest account.
- **The boredom.** Cards. Whisky if it could be brought
  in. Cleaning weapons. Repairing tack. Talking the
  same talk for the third week. The boredom of the
  hideout was the worst part of the outlaw life.

### Famous hideouts

- **Hole-in-the-Wall.** Powder River country, Wyoming.
  A box canyon with one approach defended by a single
  rifleman. Used by the Wild Bunch and others. Cold
  and remote.
- **Brown's Hole** (later Brown's Park). Northwest
  Colorado at the corner of three states. The
  jurisdictional confusion was its defense.
- **Robbers Roost.** Southeastern Utah. Slickrock
  country. Almost impossible to track a man through.
- **The Indian Territory.** The whole region was a
  hideout for fugitives from the surrounding states
  until 1907. Federal jurisdiction only; few marshals;
  vast distances.

### The lookout system

A working hideout had a lookout. The lookout sat on a
high point with a glass and watched the approach for
hours at a time. The lookout was rotated. The lookout
who fell asleep was the man who got the gang killed.
The lookout system is the writer's friend: a scene at
the lookout post is a scene of dread without action.

## 3. The train robbery — procedure

The classic late-1870s western crime. Specific
procedure.

### Pre-robbery

- **Inside information.** A railroad employee or a
  station agent tells the gang which train, which
  date, which express car carried what. The inside man
  takes a cut.
- **The site.** A remote stretch of track, often a
  watering tank or a curve. Far from the next town.
- **The party.** Five to eight men. Two on the
  locomotive, two at the express car, one or two
  guarding the passenger cars, one or two with the
  horses.

### The stop

- **The signal.** A red lantern at night, a flag in
  daylight, a crosstie laid across the rails, a track
  obstruction. The engineer stops because the
  alternative is a derailment.
- **The boarding.** Two men climb the locomotive with
  pistols on the engineer and fireman. The engineer is
  ordered to uncouple the express car from the
  passenger cars and pull it forward a quarter mile or
  so. This separates the messenger from any rescue and
  from the passengers.

### The express car

- **The messenger.** The express messenger (Wells Fargo
  or the railroad's own express) was an armed employee
  whose job was to defend the safe. Some fought; most
  did not. The famous fighters were exceptions.
- **The door.** The messenger barricaded the door from
  inside. The gang demanded entry. The messenger
  refused. The gang threatened to kill the engineer.
- **The dynamite or the powder.** If the messenger held
  out, the gang blew the door with a charge. The
  charge often blew the safe, the messenger, and a
  good part of the car's interior. Anything paper
  inside burned.
- **The safe.** Two types: the through safe, with
  combination known only to the destination clerk
  (the gang could not open it without explosives); and
  the local safe, which the messenger could open. The
  gang hoped for the local safe. They blew the through
  safe with care because the contents could be
  destroyed.

### The split and scatter

- The take was split at the site or at a pre-arranged
  rendezvous a few miles off.
- Each man rode a different direction. The gang did not
  ride out together. A pursuit had to follow seven
  different trails.
- The leader often took the largest share for himself
  and a second share to deliver to the inside man
  later.

### The pursuit

- The express messenger or the conductor cut for the
  next station. A telegram went up the line. By
  morning, every sheriff in three counties had the
  description.
- The Pinkerton Detective Agency or the railroad's own
  detectives took the case. They had the budget for
  long pursuit; the local sheriffs did not.
- A posse formed in the nearest town. Posses were
  effective for two to three days; after that they
  ran out of food, money, and patience and turned
  back.

## 4. The bank robbery — procedure

A bank robbery had a different set of constraints than
a train robbery: the bank was in a town, the town had
citizens who could shoot, and the timing was measured
in minutes.

### Pre-robbery

- **The town read.** Two to three men rode into town a
  day or two before the job, posing as cattle buyers
  or drifters. They mapped the bank's layout, the
  street, the route in and out, the location of the
  marshal's office, the saloon where the men gathered.
- **The horses.** The horses were rested and watered
  the morning of the job. Each man's horse was tied
  to a hitching rail in a specific spot on the street
  near the bank.

### The job

- **The party.** Five to seven men. Two or three went
  inside the bank. One or two stood outside the door
  as guards. One or two stayed mounted as horse holders
  and street watchers.
- **The inside.** The lead man covered the cashier and
  any customers. The second man went over the counter
  to the cash drawer and the vault. The third man
  watched the door.
- **The vault.** The vault was the question. A small-
  town bank vault might be a brick room with a iron
  door; a city bank had a true safe. The cashier was
  ordered to open it. If he refused, he was beaten or
  shot. A locked vault that could not be opened ended
  the job.
- **The clock.** The whole inside job took four to
  seven minutes. Longer than that and the town was
  alerted and the citizens had rifles.

### The street

- **The alert.** The town learned of the robbery within
  two minutes — usually because a citizen saw the
  guards at the bank door and shouted. Citizens with
  rifles took positions in second-floor windows and
  on rooftops.
- **The crossfire.** The gang's mounted men returned
  fire to keep the citizens' heads down while the men
  inside finished. The exit was the dangerous moment.
- **Northfield 1876.** The textbook disaster. The
  citizens of Northfield, Minnesota, fired from second-
  floor windows and killed two of the gang in the
  street and wounded several others. The gang fled
  without the money and three Younger brothers were
  captured a few days later.

### The pursuit

Same as train robbery, with the addition that the
town's citizens often joined the posse and the
posse was therefore larger and angrier than the
typical professional posse.

## 5. The stage robbery — procedure

The road agent's classic crime. Smaller take, smaller
risk than a train, smaller crew.

### The party

Often one or two men. Three at most. The road agent
worked alone or with a partner.

### The site

A grade where the stage was slowed (an uphill or a
sharp curve) or a known watering stop. The road agent
knew the route and the timing.

### The halt

- **The position.** The agent stepped out from cover
  with a shotgun (the shotgun was the road agent's
  preferred weapon — short range, devastating, a
  threat the driver could not refuse). He stood
  twenty paces from the lead horses.
- **The order.** _Throw down the box._ The express box
  was a wooden chest bolted to the front boot of the
  stage. The driver kicked it off or the messenger
  unbolted it and dropped it.
- **The messenger.** Some stages carried an armed
  messenger riding shotgun. The messenger could fight
  back, and many famous gunfights were stage messenger
  vs. road agent encounters.
- **The passengers.** The agent ordered the passengers
  out and lined them up beside the stage. He took
  watches, cash, jewelry. He did not generally take
  women's wedding rings (some did; the practice was
  considered low even among outlaws).

### The box-off-the-road method

A variation: the agent did not stop the stage. He
arranged for the box to be thrown off the stage at a
specific location by a confederate riding inside, and
the agent picked it up later. This avoided the
confrontation but required an inside man.

### The pursuit

Stage robbery pursuit was less intense than train
or bank. The take was smaller and the company's
resources were less. Wells Fargo, however, hired
detectives (notably James B. Hume, chief detective for
Wells Fargo for decades) who pursued road agents
relentlessly. A road agent who hit Wells Fargo
boxes had Hume on his trail.

## 6. Horse theft

The most common western crime by frequency. The most
absolutely punished.

### The procedure

- **Night approach.** A horse thief came in the dark.
  He scouted the corral or the picket line in advance.
- **The quiet removal.** He cut the picket rope or
  opened the corral gate without sound. He led the
  horses out by the lead rope, walking. He did not
  mount until well clear of the camp.
- **The run.** Once mounted, he ran the horses fast for
  the first ten miles to put distance between himself
  and the discovery.
- **The fence.** The horses went to a known buyer who
  would not ask questions. Fences in border country
  drove the horses across the line into Mexico or up
  to the next territory.

### Why hanging

The community enforced the death penalty for horse
theft because a man without a horse on the frontier
could die. Stealing a man's horse was attempting his
life. The vigilance committee that hanged the horse
thief acted on the explicit understanding that the
thief had endangered the life of the owner.

The cowhand caught with another man's horse received
no trial in many cases. The rope was on hand. The
nearest tree did the work.

## 7. Cattle rustling

The crime built into the cattle trade. Different
mechanics from horse theft.

### The procedure

- **The cut.** A small operator cut a few head out of a
  large herd's edge at night and drove them off. The
  cut was small enough to go unnoticed in a herd of
  several thousand.
- **The brand.** The stolen brand had to be altered.
  Skilled rustlers used a hot iron to add to the
  existing brand and turn it into something else. A
  Bar-S could become a Box-S; a Lazy-K could become a
  Lazy-KK. The art was to make the alteration look like
  the original.
- **The drive.** The rustled cattle were driven to a
  remote pasture and held until the brands healed.
  Then they were driven to a buyer who would not look
  too closely.
- **The buyer.** A small slaughterhouse, a corrupt
  rancher, a packer who paid less and asked nothing.
  Or, in border country, a drive across the line.

### The big operations

Some cattlemen built their herds substantially through
rustling. The Lincoln County and Tularosa cattle wars
were partly disputes about who was rustling whom. The
distinction between a rustler and a small rancher
building a herd by appropriating mavericks (unbranded
cattle) was sometimes a matter of who was making the
accusation.

### The detection

Cattlemen's associations hired range detectives. The
detective's job was to ride the country, watch the
brands, and report. He was paid by the association.
He had wide latitude. Some detectives shot rustlers on
sight and answered no questions later. The most famous
was Tom Horn (a complicated case; convicted and hanged
in 1903).

## 8. The fence

Most outlaw operations depended on a fence — a man
who bought stolen goods and moved them into legitimate
markets.

### Types

- **The horse fence.** A horse trader or remount
  contractor who bought stolen stock and re-sold it
  through legitimate channels in distant markets.
- **The cattle fence.** A small slaughterhouse or
  packer who paid less and asked nothing.
- **The general fence.** A storekeeper, often in a
  border town, who bought watches, jewelry, paper money,
  cash. He paid ten to thirty cents on the dollar.
- **The bond fence.** A specialist who could move
  stolen bonds and securities. Rare and well-paid. The
  reason the James-Younger Adair bonds (1873) were
  largely lost: the gang did not have the connections
  to move them.

### The relationship

The fence and the gang were a working relationship of
years. The fence trusted the gang not to bring trouble
to his door. The gang trusted the fence to pay
promised amounts and not turn informant. Either could
betray the other; both knew it.

## 9. The posse

How posses actually worked, vs. the dime novel
version.

### Formation

A posse was deputized by the sheriff (or, in the
Indian Territory, by a U.S. marshal). The men were
local citizens who could be spared from work. The
sheriff promised them per-day pay (rarely fulfilled
in practice).

### Composition

A posse was usually six to fifteen men. Local cowboys,
townsmen, sometimes a tracker (often a freedman or a
Native scout — the work paid). The marshal or sheriff
led. Each man brought his own horse and rifle.

### Range and time

A posse rode hard for two days, slower for the third.
By the fourth day the men were tired, hungry, and out
of grain for the horses. Most posses turned back
after three days because they could not be supplied
in the field.

### The professional posse

The Pinkerton Agency and the Wells Fargo detectives
did the long pursuit work the local posse could not.
A Pinkerton man might follow a fugitive for months,
across multiple states, hiring local help as needed.
The fugitive who beat a posse usually still had the
Pinkertons to deal with.

### The lynch posse

A posse that caught its man sometimes did not bring
him back for trial. The lynch posse hanged him on the
spot. The justification: he was caught with the goods,
the trial was a foregone conclusion, the trip back
was expensive. This was illegal but widely
practiced and almost never prosecuted. See
`knowledge/towns-economy-law.md` and
`references/uncomfortable-truths.md`.

## 10. The sympathetic outlaw — writing without lies

The outlaw can be a sympathetic protagonist. The dime
novels lied about how. The writer's discipline.

### What to refuse

- **The Robin Hood lie.** Few outlaws gave to the poor.
  Most kept what they took. The James brothers cultivated
  the image; the reality was that the money went to
  themselves and their families.
- **The chivalry lie.** Outlaws shot men in the back,
  killed witnesses, and brutalized civilians. Some had
  codes; many did not. The chivalrous outlaw was
  largely a postwar Confederate myth applied to
  ex-guerrillas.
- **The political lie.** The James-Younger gang were
  not freedom fighters against Yankee oppression. They
  were ex-Confederate guerrillas who continued
  killing for money after the war ended.
- **The clean death.** Most outlaws died ugly. Shot in
  the street, hanged in a courthouse yard, dying of
  wounds in a friend's barn. The writer who gives the
  outlaw a noble death is lying.

### What to render

- **The skill.** The successful outlaw was a competent
  man. He rode hard, shot well, planned carefully,
  and kept his mouth shut. The competence is real and
  the prose can render it.
- **The fear.** The successful outlaw was afraid most
  of the time. Of being recognized. Of the man at the
  next table. Of the sound on the lookout's hill. The
  fear is part of the truth.
- **The love.** Many outlaws had wives, children, and
  parents who knew what they did and loved them anyway.
  The love is part of the truth.
- **The cost.** The outlaw who survived to old age
  carried the dead with him. The outlaw who did not
  survive left a wife and children to a bad life. The
  cost is the part the dime novels skipped.

### The model: Ron Hansen's \_The Assassination of Jesse

James\_

Hansen renders Jesse James without endorsement and
without condemnation. The man is small, vain, jumpy,
murderous, devoted to his family, and certain of his
own myth. The writing does not flatter him. It also
does not flatten him. The reader sees the man and
understands the man and is not asked to like the man.
This is the model.

## 11. The outlaw cheat-sheet

For the writer working a chapter that involves
criminal procedure.

- **Pick the crime type and learn the procedure.** Train,
  bank, stage, horse, rustling. Each has its own
  mechanics.
- **The numbers.** Most jobs paid less than the dime
  novels claimed. The cowhand's wages were often
  better than the outlaw's average.
- **The hideout is hostile.** Cold, cramped, boring,
  hungry. The romance is a lie.
- **The pursuit is real.** Posses for two days,
  Pinkertons for months. The fugitive does not relax.
- **The fence is the bottleneck.** Without a fence,
  the take cannot be moved.
- **Refuse the four lies.** Robin Hood, chivalry,
  politics, clean death.
- **Render the four truths.** Skill, fear, love, cost.
- **The end is usually ugly.** The outlaw who beats the
  odds is the exception. Write to the rule.

## 12. The rule the others serve

A western that includes outlaws must take the outlaw's
work seriously as work. The procedures are specific.
The economics are unfavorable. The lives are short.
The writer who renders this honestly produces an
outlaw character the reader believes in. The writer
who renders the dime-novel version produces a cardboard
hero the reader has met in a hundred bad books.

The outlaw is a working criminal in a brutal trade. The
prose meets him where he stands and does not look away
from the rope at the end of his road.
