# Music and Entertainment — What Filled the Evenings

This file is the knowledge module for the music,
performance, and entertainment cultures of the 1870s
and 1880s American West. It complements
`knowledge/towns-economy-law.md` (the saloon, the
opera house, the dance hall as institutions),
`knowledge/religion-and-faith.md` (the hymn, the
revival, the camp meeting song), `knowledge/cowboy-life.md`
(the trail song at the night herd), `knowledge/borderlands.md`
(the corrido, the fandango), and `knowledge/childhood.md`
(the play-party, the school song).

The popular West has rendered music as a tin cup of
coffee at the campfire and a lonesome harmonica at
sundown. The reality was louder, more various, and
more constant. The frontier ran on music. The trail
hand sang to settle the herd. The homestead family
sang at evening worship. The town fiddler played
the Friday-night dance until the lamps were turned
down at one in the morning. The German immigrant
brought his concertina and his choral society. The
Mexican household sang the corridos of the border
war. The Black soldier at Fort Davis sang the
spirituals his mother had taught him in Louisiana.
The Cornish miner sang the chapel hymns of his
village. The opera house in Leadville and the
opera house in Tombstone booked Edwin Booth and
Sarah Bernhardt and the Black Crook road company
and pulled them off the Atchison train.

The writer who renders frontier music well renders
the country's evenings. Most of what the reader
imagines as silence on the frontier was filled with
sound the period made for itself.

## 1. The fiddle and the dance

The fiddle was the West's principal instrument. It
was portable, it was loud, and one player could
furnish a whole evening's dance. A fiddler in any
frontier community of more than fifty people was
within riding distance.

### The instrument

A factory fiddle from a mail-order house cost three
to seven dollars in the 1870s. The traveling
peddler sold them. A homemade fiddle, often badly
made, often with a cigar box for a body, existed
in poorer households. The professional fiddle (a
German factory instrument or, rarely, an old
Italian) cost twenty to a hundred dollars and was
the fiddler's working capital.

Strings were gut (the lower three) and steel (the
top E). They broke. The fiddler carried spares
wrapped in oiled paper in a small tin in his case.
Rosin was a small block in the case; the bow was
horsehair on a wooden stick.

### The fiddler

A man, almost always. He learned the tunes by ear
from his father, his uncle, his neighbor. He
played without notation. His repertoire was a
hundred or two hundred tunes carried entirely in
his head and his fingers.

He was paid in cash, in drink, in a cooked meal,
in the privilege of dancing the last waltz with
the prettiest girl. A working dance fiddler in a
country county might earn five dollars a Saturday
night plus supper. A town fiddler at a regular
saloon engagement earned more, twenty to forty
dollars a month plus tips.

He was sometimes also a barber, a teamster, a
cobbler. He was often known by his fiddle name
(_Fiddlin' Bob, Fiddler Pete_) more than his proper
name.

### The repertoire

The fiddle tunes that travel through the western
record:

- **Reels and breakdowns.** _Soldier's Joy, Devil's
  Dream, Old Joe Clark, Turkey in the Straw,
  Arkansas Traveler, Sally Ann, Soldier's Return,
  Cripple Creek, Sally Goodin, Bonaparte's Retreat._
- **Hornpipes.** _Fisher's Hornpipe, Rickett's
  Hornpipe, Durang's Hornpipe._
- **Jigs.** _Irish Washerwoman, Garryowen, Haste to
  the Wedding._
- **Waltzes.** _Tennessee Waltz_ (much later; the
  period had _Wearing of the Green_ as a slow air,
  _Aura Lee_, _Lorena_, _When You and I Were Young,
  Maggie_).
- **Schottisches and polkas.** Imported by the
  German immigrants, picked up by the Anglo
  fiddlers in the 1860s and 1870s. _Heel and Toe
  Polka, Five-Step Schottische._
- **Two-steps.** Later in the period; the cakewalk
  rhythms of the 1890s belong to the next decade.
- **Trail and ballad airs.** _Streets of Laredo
  (The Cowboy's Lament), The Old Chisholm Trail,
  Sweet Betsy from Pike, Buffalo Gals, Whoopee Ti
  Yi Yo (The Old Chisholm Trail's relation), Doney
  Gal, The Dying Cowboy._

The fiddler called the tune by name and the dancers
knew the figures. The same tune might be played for
fifteen minutes for a long set of figures and then
the next tune called.

### The dance

The frontier dance was a square dance or a round
dance and was the principal social institution of
the rural West. It was held in a barn freshly swept
out, in a schoolhouse with the desks pushed against
the wall, in a settler's parlor with the rug rolled
up, in the dance hall of a town saloon, at the
ranch headquarters at the end of a roundup.

The form: the caller (often the fiddler doubling)
called the figures (_form your squares, allemande
left, do-si-do, ladies in the center, gents around,
swing your partner_). The dancers walked or
danced through the figures while the fiddle drove
the rhythm. The tunes ran twenty minutes or longer
for the long sets.

Round dances (waltz, polka, schottische, two-step)
were the European imports and were danced couple-
to-couple around the room. They were faster, more
intimate, and were what the young people preferred.
The Methodist parents sometimes objected; the
square dance and the play-party were the moral
substitute. (See `knowledge/religion-and-faith.md`
for the Methodist objection to dancing and the
compromises by region and congregation.)

The country dance went from sunset to one or two in
the morning. The ranch dance, when the
neighborhood gathered for a wedding or a barn-
raising, ran all night and breakfast was served at
dawn. The cowhands rode home Sunday morning and
slept in the saddle.

### The music at the night herd

The trail hand sang to the cattle on night watch.
The function was practical: the steady singing
voice settled the herd and prevented a sudden
sound from starting a stampede. The song could be
anything the hand knew — a hymn, a sentimental
ballad, a rough trail song, a Mexican song picked
up at a previous outfit, a song he had made up
himself.

The trail repertoire that has come down: _The Old
Chisholm Trail_ (its hundred-plus verses, most of
them obscene), _Streets of Laredo_ (an Anglo
adaptation of the Irish _Bard of Armagh_), _The
Dying Cowboy (Bury Me Not on the Lone Prairie),
Doney Gal, Goodbye Old Paint, The Cowboy's Lament,
The Strawberry Roan_ (later in the period), _Sam
Bass_ (the outlaw ballad), _Jesse James_ (the same).

The hand sang quietly, almost to himself, often in a
register higher than his speaking voice. The other
hands at their stations could hear him faintly
across the pasture. The songs were passed hand to
hand and outfit to outfit and were modified at
each passing. There was no fixed text.

## 2. The home instruments

### The harmonica (mouth organ, French harp)

The cheapest instrument. A small Hohner from
Germany cost a dollar in the 1870s and the
mercantile carried them. Every camp, every
bunkhouse, every line shack had at least one. The
hand played for himself and for the others around
the fire. The repertoire overlapped the fiddle's
but was simpler — single melody lines, the chord
work limited.

### The banjo

The banjo of the period was the four-string
instrument descended from the African gourd
banjo, by the 1870s standardized as a five-string
factory instrument. The banjo was originally the
Black instrument and was taken up by the white
minstrel troupes from the 1840s onward. By the
1870s it was widespread among Anglo musicians,
particularly in the Appalachian and Southern
homestead families that had carried it west.

A factory banjo cost five to fifteen dollars.
Strings were gut. The banjo was the second
instrument at a dance after the fiddle and was
often paired with it.

The technique was clawhammer or, by the late
1870s and 1880s, the new "guitar-style" picking
that would become the bluegrass technique. The
repertoire was the fiddle repertoire plus minstrel
songs (_Old Dan Tucker, Camptown Races, De
Boatmen's Dance_) and parlor songs.

### The guitar

Less common in the rural West before the late
1880s. The Spanish guitar was the dominant
instrument in Mexican households and at fandangos
in the borderlands and California. The Anglo
guitar arrived with the railroad and became
common in the 1880s mail-order catalogues. A
factory guitar cost six to fifteen dollars.

The Mexican guitar player at a fandango or a
serenade was a known frontier figure: the guitar
across his lap, the soft Spanish songs, the
corridos in his repertoire if he was political.

### The parlor organ (reed organ, melodeon)

The status instrument of the prosperous frontier
home. A small reed organ from a mail-order
catalogue cost forty to a hundred dollars by the
1880s and was found in the parlor of any merchant,
doctor, or successful rancher. The mother or the
elder daughter played it. The repertoire was
hymns, parlor songs (_Sweet Hour of Prayer, Home
Sweet Home, In the Sweet By and By, Beautiful
Dreamer, Listen to the Mockingbird, Aura Lee,
Lorena_), and simplified arrangements of the
operatic airs.

The Sunday evening at home gathered the family
around the organ. The mother played, the children
sang, the father sat and listened. The neighbors
sometimes joined.

### The piano

The piano was the town instrument. The merchant's
parlor in a substantial frontier town had one. The
hotel parlor had one. The opera house had one or
two grands.

The upright cabinet piano, the standard parlor
instrument from the 1870s onward, cost two to four
hundred dollars. The square piano (older, going out
of fashion in the 1870s) was cheaper used. The
shipping of a piano to a frontier town was a major
operation: by rail to the nearest depot, by
freight wagon the rest of the way, the instrument
crated and padded and unloaded by four men with
ropes and rollers.

The piano teacher (almost always a woman, often
the schoolmarm, often the wife of a professional)
gave lessons at fifty cents to a dollar a lesson.
The merchant's daughter, the doctor's daughter, the
banker's daughter took lessons. The repertoire was
the parlor songs above plus the simpler classical
pieces (Mendelssohn's _Songs Without Words_, the
easier Chopin nocturnes, simplified Beethoven and
Mozart).

### The accordion, the concertina

The German immigrant brought the accordion and the
concertina (different instruments — the accordion
larger and louder, the concertina smaller and used
for English country dance and the Royal Navy
sailor's instrument). Both became part of the
western repertoire in the German settlement areas
of Texas, Nebraska, the Dakotas, Wisconsin, and
Iowa, and from there spread.

The Mexican accordion came up from Mexico in the
1850s and 1860s and became the heart of what
would later be called Tejano and conjunto music.
The polka rhythms of the German Hill Country of
Texas crossed with the Mexican corrido tradition.
The vocabulary, the technique, the dance forms
all developed in the borderlands through the
1870s and 1880s.

## 3. The saloon and the dance hall

The town saloon's musical economy was specific.

### The saloon piano

The saloon piano was an upright in the corner near
the bar. It was usually a battered factory
instrument, often badly out of tune, often with
keys missing. The professional saloon pianist
(the "professor" or the "perfesser") played for
tips and a small wage and free drinks.

The repertoire was dictated by the customers'
requests and the period's popular songs. By 1880
it included the parlor songs (_Silver Threads
Among the Gold, In the Gloaming, Grandfather's
Clock, The Old Oaken Bucket_), the minstrel
songs, the sentimental ballads (_Lorena, Listen
to the Mockingbird, Aura Lee, The Vacant Chair,
Just Before the Battle Mother_), and the dance
tunes.

Ragtime is the next decade. _Maple Leaf Rag_ was
published in 1899. The 1870s and 1880s saloon
pianist did not play ragtime; he played what
preceded it. The earliest "coon songs" and
syncopated dance music were emerging in the
1880s but ragtime as a defined form belongs to
the 1890s and after.

The saloon pianist's social standing was low. He
was assumed to drink. He was often a man whose
respectability had broken down and who had
landed at the saloon piano because the saloon
piano was where he had landed. He was sometimes
also a card sharp, a procurer, a small-time crook.
He was sometimes a genuine artist whose
circumstances had brought him there. The customer
did not look closely.

### The dance hall

The dance hall was a separate establishment from
the saloon, though often connected. It contained
a polished floor, a small stage for the
musicians (typically a fiddle, a piano or banjo,
sometimes a small horn), a row of chairs along
the walls, and a bar.

The women employed at the dance hall (the "dance
hall girls," the "hurdy-gurdy girls") were not
necessarily prostitutes, though the line moved by
establishment and town. The respectable hurdy-gurdy
of the 1860s and early 1870s in the mining camps
employed German girls who danced with the miners
for fifty cents a dance and split the take with
the house. The customer paid for the dance and
the customer's drink at the bar; the girl's
companionship was the inducement.

By the late 1870s and 1880s the dance hall had in
many towns become a thinly disguised brothel; in
others it remained a dance hall. The term covered
both. The local reputation determined which.

### The music in the brothel

The parlor house at the upper end (San Francisco,
Denver, Leadville at its peak, Storyville in New
Orleans by the 1890s) employed a piano player, a
Black piano player by the 1880s as a frequent
matter, who played the parlor songs and the
sentimental ballads as the customers waited.

The crib at the lower end had no music. The crib
woman did her business and the customer left and
there was no entertainment.

## 4. The German singing societies, the Welsh chapel

choirs, the Czech bands

The European immigrant communities of the West
brought their organized musical institutions and
maintained them.

### The German Liederkranz, Sängerbund, Turnverein

The German singing society was a fixture of the
German communities of San Antonio, New Braunfels,
Fredericksburg, Denver, St. Louis (just east of the
West but a major influence on the West), Davenport,
Milwaukee. The society met weekly for choral
rehearsal, performed at concerts and festivals,
held an annual Sängerfest with neighboring
societies. The repertoire was German part-songs,
operatic choruses, the patriotic German repertoire.

The Turnverein (turners' society) added physical
culture to the music. The combined institution
provided the German immigrant community with
choir, gymnasium, social hall, lecture room, and
beer garden under one roof.

### The Welsh chapel choir

The Welsh miners of the western coal fields and
the slate quarries brought their chapel choral
tradition. The four-part chapel choir, rehearsed
weekly, performing at the chapel's services and at
the annual Eisteddfod competition, was the
center of Welsh community life.

### The Czech and Bohemian band

The Czech communities of central Texas and central
Nebraska brought the brass band and the polka
tradition. The town band (cornets, alto horns,
trombone, tuba, snare and bass drums) played at
the town's Fourth of July, at the picnic, at the
funeral, at the Saturday night dance.

### The Mexican community

The Mexican community of the borderlands and
California maintained the corrido tradition (the
narrative ballad — _El Corrido de Joaquín Murrieta,
El Corrido de Gregorio Cortez_ in the next decade,
the older Mexican romances of the colonial
period). The fandango (the traditional dance), the
serenata (the serenade under a young woman's
window), the wedding dance with the mariachi-style
ensemble (though "mariachi" as a labeled genre
came later), the Catholic feast day with its
processions and its singing — the calendar of
Mexican community music was full.

### The Chinese opera and the lion dance

The Chinese quarters of San Francisco, Portland,
and the larger camps maintained Cantonese opera
performances at the joss house or at a dedicated
theater. The instruments (the erhu, the moon-
guitar, the gongs, the cymbals, the wood block)
and the high-pitched Cantonese vocal style were
unfamiliar to Anglo ears and were generally
described in the Anglo press of the period as a
noise. The Chinese audience knew the operas and
the actors and held them in the regard the Anglo
audience held Edwin Booth.

## 5. The black musical traditions in the West

The Black population of the West was small in
absolute numbers (fewer than thirty thousand in
the western territories combined in the 1870 census)
but musically significant.

### The Black soldier

The Buffalo Soldier regiments (the 9th and 10th
Cavalry, the 24th and 25th Infantry) carried the
spirituals, the work songs, and the post-bellum
sacred song tradition with them. The regimental
chaplain ran a choir at the post chapel. The
soldiers sang on the march. The fiddlers and
banjo players among them played at the post
dances.

### The Black cowhand

About a quarter of the trail crews on the post-
war trail drives included Black hands. They
contributed to the trail song repertoire and
brought the Black musical sensibility to the
drive. Some specific trail songs (_Get Along Home,
Cindy_ in its trail variants) carry the Black
imprint.

### The Black homesteader

The Exoduster settlements of Kansas (Nicodemus
and the smaller settlements from 1879 onward)
maintained the Black church musical tradition: the
spirituals, the camp meeting songs, the sacred
harp shape-note tradition where it was carried.
The church was the center of the community's
music as it was of the community's life.

### The Black saloon pianist

By the 1880s the Black piano player was working in
the western saloons and dance halls of the larger
towns. He played the popular repertoire and added
the syncopated and the bluesy elements that would
within a decade emerge as ragtime and within two
as the early blues. The Black pianist's wage was
typically lower than the white pianist's; his
conditions were often worse; his musical
contribution to the period's saloon music was
foundational and was at the time uncredited.

## 6. The medicine show

The medicine show was the West's distinctive
traveling entertainment. A wagon, sometimes
elaborated into a small troupe with two or three
wagons, traveled from town to town selling patent
medicines under cover of a free show.

### The form

The wagon arrived in town in mid-afternoon and
parked on the main street or the public square.
The proprietor (the "doctor" or "professor")
unloaded a small platform stage from the wagon,
hung a banner advertising his medicine, and
announced that a free show would commence at sundown.

The show ran an hour or two: a banjo player, a
comic, a magician or escape artist, a small
"Indian" in feathered costume (often a non-Native
performer in costume), a singer, a dance act. The
acts were short. Between each act the proprietor
gave a five-minute pitch for his medicine.

The medicine was a bottled compound of alcohol,
opiates, herbal extracts, and water, sold for a
dollar or two a bottle. The label promised cure
for rheumatism, dyspepsia, female complaints,
catarrh, the consumption, the ague, the falling
hair. The medicine sometimes contained an active
ingredient that produced a temporary effect; it
sometimes contained nothing but alcohol. The
customers bought.

### The repertoire

The medicine show troupe was a small entertainment
unit and its members were proficient performers,
often having served apprenticeships in minstrel
companies or circuses. The music was banjo and
fiddle and singing. The comedy was minstrel-
inflected blackface and the standard frontier
humor (the hayseed, the dude, the city-slicker,
the henpecked husband, the strong-minded woman).

### The Kickapoo Indian Medicine Company

The largest of the medicine show operators by the
late 1870s, with multiple shows on the road across
the Midwest and West, employing white actors as
"Kickapoo Indians," selling Sagwa and other
nostrums. The Kickapoo shows were elaborate by
medicine-show standards and could put a dozen
performers on the platform.

### How to render

The medicine show is a frontier evening's
entertainment that has been rendered cinematically
many times and historically less often. The actual
show was lower and rougher than the cinematic
version. The customers were not fooled in the
modern sense; they bought the medicine because the
medicine contained alcohol and opium and worked
on the customer's symptoms in the short term and
because the show had been a free hour's pleasure
on a Wednesday night in a town with no other
amusement.

The proprietor was sometimes a genuine charlatan
of the old American type. He was sometimes a
former actor who had drifted into the medicine
trade because his stage career had failed. He was
sometimes both at once.

## 7. The touring theatrical and operatic companies

The West of the 1870s and 1880s was on the
national theatrical circuit. The opera houses of
San Francisco, Denver, Leadville, Tombstone,
Virginia City, Salt Lake, Portland, Seattle, and
the smaller mining towns at their peaks all
booked touring companies from the East.

### The actors who came

- **Edwin Booth.** The leading American tragedian,
  toured the West repeatedly in the 1870s and
  1880s. Played Hamlet, Lear, Iago, Richelieu,
  Brutus, the standard tragic repertoire.
- **Lawrence Barrett.** Booth's frequent partner.
- **Joseph Jefferson.** The popular comic actor,
  famous for his Rip Van Winkle, which he played
  for forty years.
- **Adelaide Neilson.** The English tragedienne,
  toured America in the late 1870s playing
  Juliet, Rosalind, Imogen.
- **Sarah Bernhardt.** Toured America in the
  1880s; reached the West Coast.
- **Lillian Russell, Lotta Crabtree, Maude
  Adams.** The popular American actresses; Crabtree
  had begun her career in the California mining
  camps in the 1850s and continued touring
  through the 1880s.
- **John McCullough.** The American tragedian, a
  rival to Booth, toured extensively in the West
  before his collapse in 1884.

### What they played

The Shakespeare repertoire (Hamlet, Macbeth, Romeo
and Juliet, Othello, Lear, Richard III, Julius
Caesar, the comedies less often). The popular
sentimental melodramas (_East Lynne, Camille, The
Lady of Lyons, Lady Audley's Secret, The
Octoroon_). The blood-and-thunder spectacles (_The
Black Crook_, the long-running musical extravaganza,
toured the West repeatedly). The new work (Ibsen
arrived late in the period and was controversial).

### The opera

Genuine opera (sung-through European opera) reached
the larger western cities. The Tabor Grand Opera
House in Denver (opened 1881) booked opera tours
from New York. The San Francisco opera houses had
genuine opera from the 1850s onward, the city
having had a substantial Italian and German
musical population from the Gold Rush. The smaller
mining towns booked the touring opera companies
when they could.

The "opera house" in a smaller town was often a
multipurpose theater; the term carried prestige
and was used to attract bookings. The Tombstone
Bird Cage Theatre, the Leadville Tabor Opera
House, the Central City Opera House (Colorado, the
oldest of the surviving frontier opera houses,
opened 1878) were genuine theaters that booked
the same companies that played the East Coast.

### The minstrel show

The minstrel show was the dominant American popular
theatrical form from the 1840s through the 1880s.
A minstrel troupe (a dozen white men in blackface
plus a handful of musicians) toured the country
performing a defined three-part program: the
opening (the troupe seated in a semicircle with
the interlocutor at the center and the endmen,
Tambo and Bones, on the wings, with songs,
witticisms, and stump speeches), the olio (a
variety bill), and the afterpiece (a short comic
sketch).

The minstrel show was viciously racist by any
modern standard. It was also enormously popular
and was the principal form through which Black
musical material entered white American popular
culture (in distorted and demeaning form). The
period audience laughed at the racial caricature
without reflection.

The minstrel show toured the West aggressively
through the 1860s, 1870s, and 1880s. The smaller
"specialty" companies (the Christy Minstrels, the
Bryant Minstrels, the Callender's Original
Georgia Minstrels — which by the 1870s was a Black
troupe in blackface, an additional layer of
period racial complexity) reached the smaller
mining towns.

### The Tom show

A traveling theatrical company that toured a
single show, _Uncle Tom's Cabin_, often as an
elaborate spectacle with bloodhounds and ice
floes and a dozen Eliza-with-baby crossings. The
Tom shows were a recognized subcategory of the
touring theater and toured continuously from the
1850s through the 1920s. By the 1880s a half-dozen
Tom companies might be on the road simultaneously
in the West.

### The Chautauqua and the lyceum

The lyceum lecture circuit (older, established in
the 1830s) and the Chautauqua (newer, established
1874 in upstate New York and spreading west by the
1880s) brought the lecture-and-edification model to
the West. The traveling lecturer (the temperance
lecturer, the political orator, the science
demonstrator with his magic lantern, the elocutionist
with her recitations) appeared at the local opera
house or church for an evening's edifying
entertainment.

The Chautauqua's full circuit (the multi-day tent
program with lectures, music, drama, and uplift)
peaked in the 1900s and 1910s. In the 1880s it was
still establishing itself and was as much an
eastern phenomenon as a western one.

## 8. The circus

The traveling circus reached the West by rail in
the 1870s and by wagon before. P. T. Barnum's
Greatest Show on Earth (1871, by 1881 in
combination with the Bailey show as Barnum &
Bailey's) and the smaller competitors (Forepaugh,
Sells Brothers, Hagenbeck, Robinson) toured the
West annually.

### The circus day

The arrival was the morning's event. The wagons
unloaded from the railroad cars; the parade
formed and went down the main street with the
band wagon, the calliope, the elephants, the
caged tigers, the clowns, the costumed riders;
the tents went up on the lot at the edge of town;
the matinee at two and the evening performance
at eight; the takedown by midnight; the train out
before dawn for the next town.

The performance was the standard three-ring (after
1872, when Barnum and Coup pioneered the form;
before that, single-ring): horseback acts,
acrobats, clowns, animal acts (lions and tigers,
elephants, performing dogs), the high-wire act,
the trapeze, the strongman, the freak show in the
side tent.

### The freak show

The side tent's "human curiosities" (the bearded
woman, the giant, the dwarf, the conjoined twins,
the tattooed man, the wild man, the fat lady, the
albino) were a defined category of frontier
entertainment. The customers paid an extra dime
to enter the side tent and saw the performers on
small platforms with their banners and their
patter.

The treatment of the human freak by the period
was exploitative by any modern measure and was
also, for the performer, often the only available
livelihood. Some performers maintained their
dignity within the format and earned substantial
wages. Most were paid little and lived in the
side tent's specific small society on the road.

## 9. Wild West shows

The Wild West show was a specific late-period
phenomenon. Buffalo Bill Cody opened his "Wild
West, Rocky Mountain and Prairie Exhibition" in
1883 in Omaha. By the late 1880s it was an
international touring spectacle.

### The form

An outdoor arena performance combining cowboy
contest acts (rough-riding, roping, shooting),
historical reenactments (the attack on the Deadwood
stage, the attack on the settler's cabin, Custer's
last stand), Native performers (genuine Lakota and
other Plains performers under contract), the
sharpshooting acts (Annie Oakley and her
contemporaries), the cavalry drill team. Two and
a half hours of action.

### The Native performers

The Wild West shows employed Native performers under
contract through the 1880s and 1890s. The
performers were genuine Lakota, Cheyenne, Arapaho,
Pawnee, and others, often warriors or chiefs of
recent reservation memory. They earned wages,
they traveled, they saw the East and Europe, they
returned home with cash and stories. The
arrangement was ambiguous: it provided income and
a measure of dignity unavailable on the
reservation, and it depended on the public
performance of the warrior identity that the
federal policy was simultaneously trying to
eradicate.

Sitting Bull toured with Buffalo Bill briefly in
1885 and disliked it but the income mattered.
Other figures spent years on the show circuit.

### The relation to the genuine West

The Wild West show emerged at the moment the
historical West it depicted was ending. The
1880s audience watched the buffalo hunt staged in
an arena while the actual buffalo were almost gone
and the actual hunting had already ended. The
conventions of the western that the twentieth-
century movies inherited (the cowboy as romantic
hero, the Native as warrior antagonist, the
sharpshooter, the stagecoach holdup) were
codified by the Wild West show before the dime
novel had quite finished codifying them.

For the working western writer this matters: the
Wild West show is the moment when the West
becomes its own performance. A scene set at a
Wild West show is a scene about the gap between
what was and what is now being sold as what was.

## 10. The rodeo

The rodeo as a defined competitive event begins in
the 1880s. Before that, informal cowboy contests
at the end of a roundup or at a town's Fourth of
July had existed since the Mexican vaquero
tradition arrived in the Anglo Southwest in the
1830s and 1840s.

### The early contests

The Pecos, Texas, claim of "first rodeo" (1883) and
the Prescott, Arizona, claim (1888, the first
formal "Cowboy Tournament" with prizes and a
program) mark the period when the informal contest
crystallized into a public event with admission
charged.

The events of the early rodeo:

- **Bronc riding.** The cowhand mounted a green
  unbroken horse and rode until the horse was
  thrown or the rider was. No saddle in the early
  contests; saddle bronc riding was added later.
- **Steer roping.** The mounted hand roped a
  running steer by the horns or by the front feet
  and threw it. The time was kept.
- **Steer wrestling (bulldogging).** Added later in
  the period (Bill Pickett, the Black cowboy from
  the 101 Ranch, popularized the technique by 1903).
  Not a 1870s or 1880s event in the standard form.
- **Calf roping.** As steer roping but for the
  smaller animal.
- **Wild horse race.** A team race to catch, saddle,
  and ride a wild horse across a finish line.
- **Ladies' relay race.** Mounted women relay
  racing. Added by the late 1880s as the rodeo
  developed its public character.

The bull riding event in its modern form is later
(twentieth century).

### The vaquero contest tradition

The Mexican vaquero contests of the California
ranchos and the Texas ranchos predated the Anglo
rodeo and contributed many of its events. The
charreada (the Mexican formal mounted contest)
is an older and more elaborate form that
continues in Mexico. The Anglo rodeo borrowed
from it and simplified it.

### The cowtown roundup celebration

By the late 1880s the cattle towns held annual
"Frontier Days" and "Cowboy Reunion" celebrations
combining the rodeo events with horse races,
parades, and a dance. Cheyenne (Wyoming) Frontier
Days began 1897 and is the standard reference; the
Pendleton (Oregon) Round-Up began 1910. The
1880s precursors were less formal.

## 11. The newspaper, the magazine, the dime novel

Print was entertainment as well as information.

### The frontier newspaper

Covered in `knowledge/documents-and-print.md`. The
weekly small-town paper was read aloud at the
family table or at the saloon by the man who could
read for the men who could not. The serialized
fiction in the newspaper (Dickens, the popular
novelists, the local color writers) was a
substantial portion of the household's reading.

### The magazine

The Atlantic Monthly, Harper's Magazine, Scribner's
Monthly, the Century, Frank Leslie's Illustrated
Newspaper, Harper's Weekly, the Police Gazette
(at the lower end). The frontier home subscribed
to one or two and read them through. The arrival
of the magazine was an event.

### The dime novel

Beadle's Dime Novels (founded 1860) and its
competitors (Munro's, Street & Smith's, Frank
Tousey's) sold cheap pulp fiction for ten cents.
The western was the dominant subject by the 1870s.
_Deadwood Dick, the Black Rider of the Black
Hills_ (Edward Wheeler, 1877 and after), _Buffalo
Bill, King of the Border Men_ (Ned Buntline, 1869,
the foundational novel of the Buffalo Bill myth),
_Calamity Jane, the Heroine of Whoop-Up_, the
Frank Reade boy-inventor stories, the Old
Sleuth detective stories.

The dime novel was the boy's reading and the
working-class adult's reading. It was disapproved
of by the educators and the ministers. It was
read anyway. The conventions of the western
(the gunfighter, the road agent, the cattle
rustler, the Indian fight, the climactic shootout)
were partly invented by the dime novelists in the
1870s and 1880s and were imposed retroactively on
the actual West when the Wild West show and then
the early movies needed material.

For the working western writer this is the layer
underneath the genre. The 1870s and 1880s reader
already knew the conventions. The dime novel was
already shaping what the actual frontier
participant thought he was doing while he was
doing it.

## 12. The vocabulary

The musical and entertainment world had its
specific words.

### Music

- **Fiddle** (the working term; _violin_ was the
  parlor and concert term for the same instrument).
- **Bow.** The fiddler's hair-on-stick. _Hair_ was
  the bow's working portion.
- **Rosin.** The block of pine resin the fiddler
  rubbed his bow with.
- **Tune.** The piece itself. _Air_ for the slower
  ones.
- **Reel, hornpipe, jig, breakdown, schottische,
  polka, waltz, two-step.** The dance forms.
- **Call** (verb and noun). What the caller did at
  the square dance. _Calling the figures._
- **Set.** The square of four couples at the
  square dance. _Form your sets._
- **Hoedown.** The country dance. Originally the
  fast breakdown tune; by extension the dance
  itself.
- **Frolic.** The country dance, particularly the
  informal at-home version.
- **Play-party.** The young people's gathering
  with singing games in the Methodist and Baptist
  households where dancing was forbidden.
- **The professor, the perfesser.** The saloon
  pianist.
- **Hurdy-gurdy.** The dance hall; the dance hall
  girl. (The instrument of the same name was the
  source of the term and was occasionally heard.)
- **Calliope.** The steam-organ on the circus
  wagon and the steamboat. Pronounced _kal-LIE-oh-pee_
  in the period American.
- **Air.** A tune, particularly a slow tune.
- **Set-to.** A musical exchange; also a fight.

### Theater

- **The boards.** The stage. _He's been on the
  boards since he was twelve._
- **The bill.** The advertised program. _What's on
  the bill?_
- **The opry house.** The rural pronunciation of
  opera house.
- **Drop, flat, flies, wings, apron.** The stage
  technical vocabulary.
- **Trouper.** A traveling performer. The
  derivative _trouper_ in the modern sense
  (someone who endures) is from this.
- **Barnstormer.** A performer who toured the
  small towns.
- **Stock company.** The resident company at a
  theater playing a rotating repertory. The
  alternative was the touring company playing a
  single show.
- **Star, leading man, leading lady, juvenile,
  ingenue, character, heavy.** The roles in a
  stock company.

### Circus

- **The lot.** The field where the tents were
  pitched.
- **The big top.** The main tent.
- **The side show.** The smaller tent with the
  human curiosities.
- **The midway.** The path between the tents with
  the food vendors and the games.
- **The ballyhoo.** The pitch the side show talker
  gave from his platform.
- **The mark.** The customer.
- **The grift.** The cheating games run by the
  short-con men attached to some circuses.
- **Roustabout.** The circus laborer who put up
  and took down the tents.
- **Razorback.** The roustabout who unloaded the
  wagons from the train. (Origin: _raise her back_
  — the call to lift the wagon's back from the
  flatcar.)

### Saloon and dance hall

- **Crib.** The single-occupancy prostitute's
  room, often a row of small wooden boxes behind
  the saloon or on a back street.
- **Parlor house.** The high-end brothel with a
  parlor, multiple rooms, and a madam.
- **Madam.** The woman who ran the parlor house
  or the dance hall.
- **Sporting woman, fancy woman, soiled dove,
  girl on the line, woman of the line.** The
  prostitute, by register.
- **Painted lady.** Disparaging.
- **Bouncer.** The man employed by the saloon or
  dance hall to handle drunk or violent customers.
- **Dive, deadfall, fancy house, sporting house.**
  The categories of saloon by reputation.

## 13. Sound, smell, light

What the reader hears, smells, and sees in a
frontier scene of music or entertainment.

### The country dance

Fiddle and banjo and the stamp of boots on the
puncheon floor. The caller's voice over the
music. The smell of the kerosene lamps and the
men's sweat and the women's lavender water and
the chewing tobacco. The cool night air through
the open door. The light: the kerosene lamps in
the corners and the lantern hung over the
caller. The dancers' faces flushed and shining.
The mothers' chairs along the wall and the babies
asleep on the bed in the back room.

### The saloon

The piano and the off-key voice of the man at
the bar and the click of the chips at the faro
table. The smell of stale beer and tobacco smoke
and unwashed men and the sawdust on the floor.
The light: the kerosene chandeliers and the
mirrored back-bar reflecting them. The pianist's
back to the room. The customer who has stopped
listening to the music and is concentrating on
his card.

### The medicine show

The proprietor's voice carrying down the street.
The banjo and the bones. The smell of the
lamplight and the crowd. The light: the
kerosene-and-reflector lamps on poles around the
platform. The bottle held up in the proprietor's
hand and the lamplight catching it.

### The opera house

The orchestra tuning. The murmur of the audience.
The smell of the gaslight (where there was gas
service) or the kerosene (where there was not)
and the perfume of the women in the boxes. The
light: the gas footlights or the kerosene
chimney lamps along the front of the stage. The
curtain. The first hush as the curtain rises.

### The circus

The calliope from a quarter mile off. The drum and
the horn of the band wagon. The roar of the
animals from the menagerie tent. The smell of
the elephants and the cooking grease and the
sawdust and the canvas. The light: the kerosene
flares around the tent's perimeter at night. The
crowd's faces upturned to the high wire.

## 14. Before / after — the writing

### Before (AI default, generic frontier music)

> The cowboy strummed his guitar by the campfire,
> singing a lonely song about the wide open plains.
> The other cowboys listened in silence, each lost
> in his own thoughts. Above them, the stars
> shone brilliantly in the vast Western sky. It
> was a moment of quiet reflection in the harsh
> life of the trail.

The passage has the wrong instrument (the trail
hand played the harmonica or sang unaccompanied;
the guitar was uncommon on the trail). It has the
wrong register (_lonely song, lost in his own
thoughts, vast Western sky, quiet reflection_).
It has nothing specific. It could be on any
frontier in any decade.

### After (the canon's register)

> Pruitt was on second watch and had been singing
> _Streets of Laredo_ for an hour when he ran out
> of verses he knew and started over. The herd was
> bedded along the dry wash and was not going to
> bolt. He sang the verses in the same low voice
> he had heard his uncle sing them in Lampasas in
> the year his mother died. The fiddler at the
> wagon had quit at supper and was asleep under
> the wagon. The other men were asleep where they
> had thrown their rolls. Pruitt rode the slow
> circle around the herd and sang the verse about
> the boy who had been shot in the breast and was
> dying.

The instrument is right (no instrument; he is
singing). The song is named. The function of the
singing (settling the herd) is implicit in the
fact that the herd is bedded. The fiddler at the
wagon places the music in the larger trail
economy. The personal dimension (the uncle in
Lampasas, the year his mother died) is handed
without emphasis. The passage ends on the boy in
the song dying, which is also the boy in the
saddle riding through his own night. No
editorial.

## 15. The cheat sheet

- The fiddle is the West's principal instrument.
  The fiddler is at every dance. Name his tunes.
- The harmonica is the cowhand's pocket
  instrument. The guitar is the Mexican
  household's instrument. The piano is the town
  instrument. The parlor organ is the prosperous
  household's instrument.
- The trail hand sings to settle the herd. He sings
  what he knows. Name the song.
- The country dance is the rural West's principal
  social institution. Render the puncheon floor,
  the kerosene lamp, the caller, the named tune,
  the tin cup of coffee at midnight.
- The saloon piano plays parlor songs and
  sentimental ballads, not ragtime. Ragtime is
  the next decade.
- The medicine show is rougher and lower than the
  cinematic version. The proprietor sells alcohol
  and opium under cover of a free show.
- The touring theater reaches the larger western
  towns. Edwin Booth has played in Denver and
  Leadville. The opera house is real and is
  booked by genuine companies.
- The minstrel show is racist by any modern
  standard and is enormously popular. The period
  audience laughs without reflection.
- The dime novel is already shaping what the
  frontier participant thinks he is doing while
  he is doing it.
- The Wild West show invents the frontier as
  performance at the moment the frontier is
  ending.
- The German singing society, the Czech band,
  the Welsh chapel choir, the Mexican corrido
  singer, the Chinese opera, the Black church
  choir all exist and shape the period's music.
  The West's musical culture is not Anglo by
  default.

The frontier evening was loud. The reader's
imagination supplies silence; the country
supplied sound. Render the sound, name the
instrument, name the song, and the evening
stands.
