# Documents and Print — The Paper World of the West

This file is the knowledge module for the documentary
and print culture of the 1870s West. It complements
`knowledge/towns-economy-law.md` (the newspaper, the
courthouse), `knowledge/outlaw-craft.md` (the wanted
poster), and `references/speech-patterns.md` (the
specific registers of legal, journalistic, and
epistolary prose). For dramatic use of documents
within scenes, see `references/story-structure.md`
(the document as plot device).

The popular West is largely an oral world: men talk,
men shoot, men ride, men drink. The historical West
was a paper world. Newspapers reached every town.
Wanted posters were nailed to every post office and
saloon door. Letters traveled constantly between
families, business associates, and government
offices. Legal documents — bills of sale, deeds,
wills, court records — were generated by every
significant transaction. Telegrams flew between
cities. Diaries and journals were kept by an
enormous fraction of the population.

A western that uses documents intelligently can carry
historical weight without exposition. The wanted
poster on the wall, the letter the widow burns, the
bill of sale the cattle thief carries with the
brand altered, the telegram the sheriff receives
late — these are scenes' anchors. This file is the
reference for getting them right.

---

## 1. The newspaper

By the 1870s every western town of any size had a
newspaper. A small town might have a weekly with a
two-man staff (publisher and printer); a larger town
might have a daily with a substantial staff and
imported wire-service news.

### The form

A typical 1870s western weekly: four pages, six
columns each, broadsheet size (about 22 by 28
inches). Pages 1 and 4 were often "ready-print" or
"patent inside" — sheets printed in eastern
cities with general-interest content (national news,
serial fiction, household tips, advertisements for
Eastern manufacturers) and shipped to small western
papers, which then printed pages 2 and 3 (local
news, local advertisements, editorials) themselves.

The local pages contained:

- **Local news.** Brief items: arrivals and
  departures, business openings, crimes, accidents,
  social events, marriages, deaths.
- **Editorials.** The publisher's opinions on
  national and local matters. Western editors were
  often vehement and partisan.
- **Letters to the editor.** From local citizens.
- **Advertisements.** Local merchants, lawyers,
  doctors, hotels, professionals. The sheriff's
  legal notices.
- **Court news.** District court calendars, jury
  lists, indictments.
- **Stage and railroad schedules.**
- **Stock and produce prices.**

### The voice

The 1870s newspaper voice was distinctive. Florid,
formal, often comically ornate by modern standards.
The local item:

> _Mr. Hiram Whittaker, our esteemed merchant on
> Main Street, has lately returned from a journey to
> St. Louis, whither he went on business connected
> with the spring goods trade. He brings with him a
> handsome new line of dry goods which we are
> reliably informed will be on display at his
> establishment by the first of June._

The death notice:

> _It is our painful duty to record the demise of
> Mrs. Eliza Patten, beloved wife of the late
> William Patten and a respected resident of this
> community for the past seven years. The deceased
> was forty-three years of age and is survived by
> two children. Funeral services will be held at
> the Methodist Church on Sunday at two o'clock,
> the Reverend Heaston officiating. Friends of the
> family are respectfully invited to attend._

The editorial:

> _The deplorable conditions on the road south of
> town have been the subject of correspondence in
> these columns for upwards of three months, and the
> County Commissioners continue in their inexplicable
> negligence. We are reliably informed that the
> recent freshet has rendered the Plum Creek
> crossing impassable for any vehicle larger than a
> light buggy. The freight wagons are detouring six
> miles to the eastward over country which can only
> be described as execrable._

A scene that includes a newspaper on a kitchen table
or a barbershop counter can render any of these
voices. A character reading aloud from the paper
can carry the period's voice without the writer
having to explain it.

### Famous editors

The frontier editor was a specific character type.
Some were notable: Mark Twain at the Virginia City
_Territorial Enterprise_ (1862-66), Bret Harte at
various California papers, Joseph Pulitzer at the
St. Louis _Westliche Post_ (German-language but
shaping his career), Eugene Field at the Denver
_Tribune_, William Allen White (slightly later, at
the Emporia _Gazette_).

The frontier editor's autobiography is a strong
source. Several survive: Twain's _Roughing It_
contains his Virginia City period; Mary Hallock
Foote's letters to her Eastern friends; the
collected letters of various western journalists.

### The country newspaper office

A scene set in the office can render the period's
mechanics: the hand-set type cases, the flatbed press,
the editor at his desk wearing an ink-stained apron,
the printer's devil (apprentice boy) cleaning type
or running errands, the local merchant arriving to
place an advertisement, the angry citizen arriving
to complain about an editorial.

The smell of the office: ink, kerosene (for cleaning
the press), tobacco smoke, the sweet metal smell of
type metal, the paper itself. The sound: the press
when it was running (a rhythmic thump), the editor's
typewriter (after the late 1870s; before that, all
copy was handwritten).

## 2. The wanted poster

The wanted poster was the West's principal means of
distributing the description of a fugitive. By the
1870s, posters were a routine product of sheriffs'
offices and major railroads (the Union Pacific
issued many notable posters for train robbers).

### The form

A typical 1870s wanted poster:

- **Headline.** _$5,000 REWARD_ (in large block
  letters) or _WANTED_.
- **The crime.** _For the murder of_ or _For the
  robbery of the_.
- **The name.** _John Smith, alias "Slim" Smith,
  alias_ —
- **The description.** Physical details: height,
  weight, hair, eyes, complexion, distinguishing
  marks (scars, tattoos, missing fingers, peculiar
  gait), age. _Thirty-two years of age. Five feet
  ten inches tall. Light complexion. Sandy hair.
  Blue eyes. A scar above the left eyebrow. Carries
  himself with a slight stoop. Speaks with a
  southern accent._
- **The photograph.** When available (rare in 1870
  for fugitives, common by the 1890s). A line
  drawing was sometimes used.
- **The reward terms.** _$5,000 reward will be paid
  by the undersigned for the arrest and conviction
  of the said party._ Or _Dead or alive_ (less
  common in formal posters than in fiction; usually
  _arrest and conviction_, with the implicit
  understanding that dead delivery was acceptable).
- **The contact.** The sheriff's office, the U.S.
  marshal's office, or the agency offering the
  reward (Wells Fargo, Pinkerton, Union Pacific).

### The mechanics

The poster was printed in quantity (a few hundred to
a few thousand depending on the case) and distributed
by mail to sheriffs, marshals, post offices, hotels,
saloons, and railroad agents across the affected
region. Many posters survived; collections are held
by libraries and historical societies.

### Use in scenes

The wanted poster on the wall is a standard western
visual. Its dramatic uses:

- **The fugitive sees his own face.** The classic
  scene: the outlaw walks into a saloon and his
  poster is on the wall. The reaction is the scene.
- **The bounty hunter studies the poster.** The
  scene establishes who he is hunting and what he
  knows.
- **The bystander recognizes the description.** The
  rancher's wife realizes the man at her supper
  table matches the description she read in the
  paper this morning.
- **The crooked sheriff covers the poster.** The
  sheriff who is in league with the outlaws
  removes the poster from the office wall.

### Wanted-poster prose

Render the poster as the period rendered it: the
formal, slightly stilted, descriptive prose of an
official document. Quote the relevant passage; do
not paraphrase. The reader's encounter with the
poster's voice is part of the scene's effect.

## 3. Letters and correspondence

The letter was the period's primary long-distance
communication. The mail was relatively reliable
(weekly to most established western towns by the
1870s; daily to railroad-served towns) and
relatively cheap (three cents for a domestic letter
in the period).

### The form

A standard 1870s letter:

- **Dateline.** _Cottonwood Springs, Colorado
  Territory, May 14th, 1875_.
- **Salutation.** _My dear Mother,_ _Dear Brother
  William,_ _My dearest husband,_ _Sir._
- **The body.** Generally formal in punctuation and
  paragraph structure. The opening often inquired
  after the recipient's health and reported on the
  writer's. The substance followed.
- **Closing.** _Your loving daughter, Eliza._ _Your
  affectionate brother, James._ _Your obedient
  servant, J. M. Henderson._ _With kindest
  regards, I remain very truly yours, William
  Patten._

### The voice

Letters of the 1870s were often more formal than
modern letters but also could be intensely
personal. The educated correspondent followed
nineteenth-century rhetorical conventions; the
less-educated wrote less correctly but often with
power.

A homestead woman's letter to her sister in the
East:

> _Dear Sister,_
>
> _Your letter of March the second came two weeks
> ago and I have been trying to find a quiet hour
> to answer it. The spring has come at last and we
> are very busy with the planting. James is in the
> field from before light to dark and I have the
> garden in and the chickens setting and the wash
> behind me, so this is the first letter I have
> written this month._
>
> _Little Hattie has had the croup these three weeks
> and I have not slept a full night the whole time.
> She is better now and the worst is past, I trust._
>
> _The country has not improved. We have had no
> rain since March and the wheat is yellow already.
> If we do not have rain by Decoration Day there
> will be no crop and we will have to think hard
> about what to do come fall. I do not say this to
> trouble you. I know you cannot help and I know
> you have your own troubles._
>
> _I miss you all more than I can write down._
>
> _Your loving sister, Eliza_

The voice is the homestead woman's voice on the page
in her own register: formal, practical, restrained
about her suffering, intensely concrete about her
work. A letter quoted in a scene can render her
inner life in her own voice without the narrator
intervening.

### Letter conventions worth knowing

- **Addressing the envelope.** The recipient's name
  on three or four lines: name, town, county or
  state, country if international. _Mrs. Mary
  Henderson / Athens, Ohio / U.S.A._
- **Postage.** Three cents for a domestic letter
  (one ounce); two cents after 1883. International
  postage variable.
- **Stamps.** The standard three-cent stamp of the
  1870s was green (Washington portrait).
- **Letter paper.** Folded sheets of writing paper.
  Envelopes were a separate item, increasingly
  standard.
- **The hand.** Most adults wrote in a Spencerian or
  Palmer-method cursive hand. Handwriting was a
  taught skill and varied considerably in legibility.
- **The post office.** The post office was a
  community institution. The arrival of the mail
  was an event. People gathered to wait. The
  postmaster (a federal appointee, often the local
  store owner who held the post office in a corner
  of his store) was a community figure.

### Letters as scene devices

A letter in a scene can:

- **Establish character through voice.** Render the
  letter and the reader hears the writer.
- **Deliver information dramatically.** The letter
  arrives with news the character had not known.
- **Mark the passage of time.** Reading a letter from
  weeks ago against the present.
- **Create dramatic irony.** The reader knows
  something the character does not, because the
  reader has read a letter the character will not
  receive in time.
- **Be the chapter's hinge.** The letter that
  changes the protagonist's plan.

## 4. Telegrams

The telegraph reached the major western cities by
the 1860s and was extending to smaller towns through
the 1870s. By 1875 most established western towns
had telegraph service.

### The form

A telegram was a short message paid by the word.
Brevity was the form. A typical telegram:

> _ARRIVED CHEYENNE STOP CATTLE SOLD AT 22 STOP HOME
> THURSDAY STOP HENDERSON_

Words were minimized. _STOP_ stood for the period
(at extra cost the punctuation could be sent). The
form encouraged a clipped, telegraphic prose that
became its own register.

### Use in scenes

The telegram in a scene can:

- **Arrive with urgent news.** The sheriff receives
  the telegram about the train robbery in
  Cheyenne. The chase begins.
- **Arrive too late.** The telegram about the wife's
  illness arrives the day after the funeral.
- **Carry coded language.** Business or military
  telegrams sometimes used code books. A telegram
  in code is mysterious until decoded.
- **Be intercepted.** Telegraph lines were not
  secure. The villain's confederate at the
  telegraph office might read or alter messages.

### The telegraph operator

A specific character type. Often a young man or woman
trained in Morse code, working at a small office in a
railroad station or general store. The operator was
the town's link to the wider world. He (or she —
women were a substantial fraction of telegraph
operators) heard the world's news first.

A scene at the telegraph office can render the
period: the operator at the key, the clicking, the
form pad, the customer waiting, the message
written out and handed across.

## 5. Legal documents

The 1870s West generated substantial legal paperwork.
Every land claim, every cattle sale, every will,
every contract, every court case left a paper trail.
The county courthouse held the records.

### Bills of sale

A handwritten document recording the transfer of
property — typically livestock. Standard form:

> _Know all men by these presents that I, John
> Henderson, of Cottonwood County, in the Territory
> of Colorado, in consideration of the sum of one
> hundred and sixty dollars to me in hand paid by
> James Whittaker of the same county, do hereby
> grant, sell, and convey to the said James
> Whittaker the following described livestock,
> to-wit: Forty head of mixed beef cattle, branded
> with the J-bar-H brand on the left hip, and
> further described as fourteen steers, sixteen
> cows with calves at side, and ten heifers, all
> being the issue and increase of my J-bar-H herd._
>
> _Witness my hand and seal this fourteenth day of
> May, in the year of our Lord one thousand eight
> hundred and seventy-five._
>
> _John Henderson._

The bill of sale was the cattle thief's forgery
target. A altered bill of sale (the original
seller's name forged, the brand description
altered) could launder stolen cattle.

### Deeds

A deed transferred real property. Filed at the
county courthouse. The deed described the property
("the southwest quarter of Section 14, Township 6
North, Range 8 West of the 6th Principal Meridian"),
recorded the consideration paid, and was signed by
the grantor before a notary.

The reading of a deed is normally not dramatic, but
a scene where the deed is in dispute (the deed has
been forged, the deed records something different
from what the parties remembered, the deed cannot
be located) can be central.

### Wills

Wills were drafted by lawyers (or copied from a form
book by the testator), signed before witnesses,
and filed for probate after the testator's death.
The 1870s will is often a substantial document
listing specific bequests:

> _To my beloved wife Eliza, all my household
> furniture, kitchen utensils, and personal
> effects, and the use during her natural life of
> the homestead and surrounding one hundred and
> sixty acres._
>
> _To my eldest son William, my saddle horse named
> Big John, my saddle, my Winchester rifle, and the
> sum of two hundred dollars._
>
> _To my second son Henry, my work team, my
> wagon, my plow, and the sum of two hundred
> dollars._
>
> _To my daughters Mary and Elizabeth, the sum of
> one hundred dollars each, to be paid at their
> majority or marriage._
>
> _All the residue of my estate I bequeath to my
> beloved wife Eliza, to be hers absolutely._

A will quoted in a scene establishes the family's
material world (the saddle horse, the Winchester,
the work team) and the testator's relationships
(the named bequests, the residual estate).

### Court records

Indictments, court orders, judgments, transcripts.
The 1870s western courthouse was a busy paper
factory. Court records survive in many county
archives and are the period's most reliable source
for understanding what cases actually came before
the law.

## 6. Diaries and journals

A substantial fraction of the literate 1870s
population kept some form of diary or journal.
Pioneer women's diaries, soldier's journals,
ranchman's account books with personal entries,
preacher's records of sermons preached and souls
converted — these constitute the period's largest
single archive of personal voice.

### The form

The 1870s diary was typically a leather-bound or
cloth-bound book with dated pages. Some printed
diaries had the dates pre-printed at the top of
each page, a few lines of space per day. Others
were blank books in which the diarist wrote as
he or she pleased.

### The voice

Diary voice varies. The minimal diary recorded only
weather, work, and visits (_May 14. Plowed the south
field. Wind from the west. Tom Henderson stopped
by._). The fuller diary recorded conversations,
reflections, complaints, prayers, hopes. The
homestead woman's diary often recorded her inner
life in ways her letters did not.

Reading: the published diaries of pioneer women
(many available; Lillian Schlissel's _Women's
Diaries of the Westward Journey_ is a standard
collection); the diaries of soldiers and officers'
wives; the journals of preachers (Peter
Cartwright's autobiography is a memoir built from
his journal); James Henry Cook's _Fifty Years on
the Old Frontier_ (memoir but heavily journal-
based).

### Use in scenes

A scene that quotes a character's diary entry can:

- **Reveal interiority.** The voice the character
  uses with herself.
- **Establish a date or place.** The dated entry
  anchors the timeline.
- **Set up dramatic irony.** The diary records
  what the character believes; the action then
  shows what is.
- **Be discovered after death.** The dead
  character's diary read by the survivor.

## 7. Photographs

By the 1870s photography was widespread in the West.
The cabinet card and the stereograph were standard
formats. Itinerant photographers traveled the country
making portraits, and established studios operated in
every town of size. Famous landscape photographers
(William Henry Jackson, Timothy O'Sullivan, A. J.
Russell) documented the West's geography.

### The form

- **Carte de visite.** A small (about 2.5 by 4 inch)
  photograph mounted on a card. Used as a calling
  card; collected by family and friends.
- **Cabinet card.** A larger (about 4.5 by 6.5 inch)
  photograph mounted on a stiff card. Standard
  portrait format from the late 1860s through the
  1890s.
- **Tintype.** A photograph on a thin iron plate.
  Cheap, durable, common at fairs and street
  photographers.
- **Stereograph.** A pair of nearly identical
  photographs mounted side by side on a card. Viewed
  through a stereoscope (a household viewer) for a
  3-D effect. Massively popular for landscapes.

### Scenes with photographs

A photograph in a scene can:

- **Carry memory.** The dead son's photograph on the
  mantel.
- **Identify.** The sheriff has a photograph of the
  fugitive; the deputy compares the photograph to
  the man at the bar.
- **Establish past.** The faded photograph from
  before the war.
- **Be made.** The scene of the family sitting for
  the photographer is itself a period event — the
  long exposure, the held still poses, the
  formality.

## 8. Voice — period prose register

Beyond the basic 1870s formality already discussed,
specific document types had specific registers.

### Legal register

Latinate, repetitive, formulaic. _Know all men by
these presents. To-wit. The party of the first part.
Hereby grant, sell, and convey. Witness my hand and
seal._

### Newspaper register

Florid, formal, periphrastic. _It is our painful
duty to record. We are reliably informed. Repaired
to. Wended his way. The aforesaid. The lately
deceased._

### Telegraph register

Telegraphic. Stripped to nouns and verbs. _Arrived.
Goods sold. Returning Thursday._

### Personal letter register

Formal salutations, then variable. The writer's
education and situation determine register beyond
the openings and closings.

### Diary register

Often minimal — the labor and weather. The fuller
diaries swing into reflection in their own
characteristic voice.

### Sermon register

Scripturally saturated, rhythmic, building. See
`knowledge/religion-and-faith.md` for the preacher's
voice and `references/speech-patterns.md`.

### Government / military register

Formal, bureaucratic, hierarchical. _I have the honor
to report. In compliance with instructions. Most
respectfully submitted._ See `knowledge/army-life.md`.

### Cattle-trade register

Brand-and-tally. _Forty head, three-up, mixed
yearlings and twos, branded J-bar-H on left hip,
swallow-fork in left ear._ The vocabulary is
specific to the trade.

## 9. Worked passage — the document scene

> Lewes opened the desk drawer and took out the
> bundle of papers and laid them on the table. He
> untied the string and went through them.
>
> The first was the bill of sale. It read out fine
> in Lewes's hand on the cleanest paper in the
> bundle. _Forty head of mixed beef cattle branded
> with the J-bar-H brand on the left hip._ The date
> was the fourteenth of May. Lewes had not been
> within sixty miles of Henderson's place on the
> fourteenth of May.
>
> The second was the original. He had pinched it
> from the desk at Henderson's place the night he
> took the cattle. It read out in Henderson's hand,
> _Forty head of mixed beef cattle._ The brand
> description was the same. The date was the
> fourteenth of May. The signature was John
> Henderson.
>
> The forged paper had James Whittaker as the
> buyer. The original had been left blank. Lewes had
> filled the buyer line with his confederate's name
> and signed Henderson's name at the bottom.
>
> He held the two papers up to the lamp. The forgery
> was passable in good light. In bad light, in a
> trail-driver's saddlebag after a week of rain,
> nobody would look at it twice.
>
> He folded the forgery and put it in his vest. He
> took the original to the cookstove and lifted the
> lid and watched it burn.

The scene's specifics — the desk drawer, the
bundle, the two bills of sale, the lamp, the
cookstove — render the document fraud
procedurally. The reader does not need a paragraph
explaining cattle-theft documentation. The verbs
carry it.

## 10. The cheat-sheet

- **Pick the document type for the scene.**
  Newspaper, wanted poster, letter, telegram, bill
  of sale, deed, will, diary, photograph.
- **Render the period voice.** Quote the document in
  its own register; do not paraphrase.
- **Use the document as scene anchor or hinge.** The
  document arrives, is found, is forged, is read
  aloud, is burned.
- **Know the mechanics.** Postage, telegraph rates,
  notaries, witnesses, courthouse filing.
- **Know the people.** Editor, postmaster,
  telegraph operator, county clerk, photographer.
  Each has a specific role and a specific voice.
- **The forged document is a recurring western plot
  device.** Use it well.
- **The diary is the period's underused interior
  voice.** Use it.

## 11. The rule the others serve

The western that uses documents intelligently has
the period's voice on its pages without the writer
having to manufacture it. The newspaper editorial,
the wanted poster, the letter from the East, the
telegram arriving late, the bill of sale that does
not match — each carries the period's specific
prose into the scene.

The reader who hears the wanted poster's voice and
the homestead woman's letter and the deathbed will
in their own registers is reading a novel that has
done its homework. The novel that paraphrases all
these into a single modern voice has wasted the
period's gift. The voices are there in the
archives, waiting.
