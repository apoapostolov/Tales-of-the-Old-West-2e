# Cowboy Life — The Working Knowledge

The cowhand's life is the spine of the western. Get the daily
work right and a scene grounds itself. Get it wrong and the
reader who knows horses puts the book down.

This file covers the working drover and the ranch hand from
roughly 1866 (the start of the Texas-to-Kansas drives) to about
1890 (when barbed wire and the railroads ended the open range).

---

## 1. Who he was

- Average age: 24. Many were teenagers; few were over 35.
- Often a southerner, often a Confederate veteran or the son of
  one. After 1875, plenty of Black hands and Mexican vaqueros.
- Most were unmarried. The work and the pay did not support a
  family.
- He was illiterate or barely so as often as not.
- He owned his saddle, his bridle, his bedroll, his pistol or
  rifle (sometimes), his clothes, his hat, and his rope. He did
  not usually own his horse — the outfit provided the remuda.
- He worked an average of seven to ten years before he was used
  up: broken back, broken leg, kicked head, bad lungs, bad
  knees, alcoholism, dead.

---

## 2. Pay and economics

| Position | Monthly pay (1870s, 1880s) |
| --- | --- |
| Cook (cookie, coosie, biscuit-shooter) | $30–$50 (more than a hand) |
| Trail boss | $100–$125 |
| Top hand / segundo | $35–$45 |
| Cowhand | $25–$40 |
| Wrangler / horse jingler | $20–$30 (often a teenage boy) |

Pay was held until the end of the drive and paid out in Abilene,
Dodge, Ogallala, or whichever railhead they made. A drover with
$100 in his pocket in Dodge in 1878 was a wealthy man for ten
days. Most of it went to:

- A hotel room and a bath (50 cents to $1)
- A new shirt, hat, boots ($5–$25 total)
- Whiskey
- The line of cribs and parlor houses (50 cents to $5)
- Faro, monte, poker (any amount)
- The trip home or the next outfit

A drover who saved his pay was unusual. The ones who did, became
foremen and small ranchers. The ones who did not, kept signing on
for another season until they could no longer ride.

## 3. The cattle drive — the timeline

The classic Texas drive ran from south Texas to a Kansas railhead:
roughly 1,000 miles. Mid-March to mid-September, more or less.

### Spring (March–April): the gather

The herd was gathered from the open range, ear-marked and
branded if not already. A drive of 2,500 head (a typical size)
required about ten to twelve hands plus a trail boss, a cook,
and a horse wrangler. The remuda — the saddle herd — ran 60 to
100 horses, six to eight per hand.

### The first weeks (April): breaking the herd

The first week of any drive was the worst. The cattle were not
yet trail-broken. Stampedes were common. Hands slept in their
boots. After two weeks the herd had a leader (an experienced
steer called the *lead steer*; sometimes the same animal led
drive after drive — Charles Goodnight's famous Old Blue led
eight). After three weeks, the herd could be moved with two
night riders instead of four.

### The trail (May–August)

A drive moved 10–15 miles a day in good country. Less in rough
country, less in wet weather, less when grass was poor. The
day:

- **3:30 a.m.** — Cook up. Coffee on.
- **4:00–4:30 a.m.** — Hands roll out. Coffee, biscuit, beans.
- **5:00 a.m.** — Wrangler brings in the remuda. Each hand
  ropes his morning horse from the rope corral.
- **5:30 a.m.** — Saddle, mount, take position. The herd starts.
- **All morning** — Riding the herd. Two point men in front,
  swing riders along the sides, flank riders behind them, two
  drag riders eating dust at the back. Drag was the worst job.
- **11:00 a.m.–noon** — Noon halt. The herd watered if water
  was available. The hands switched horses. Cookie served beans
  and bacon and biscuit out of the chuck wagon.
- **1:00 p.m.** — Move out again.
- **5:00–6:00 p.m.** — Bed-ground. The cook and the wrangler
  had ridden ahead and made camp. Supper.
- **After supper** — Two-hour night-watch shifts in turns.
  Every man rode at least one shift, usually two on a hard
  drive. Sleep in the bedroll on the ground, hat over the eyes
  if the moon was full.

### River crossings

The major rivers — the Red, the Canadian, the Cimarron, the
Arkansas, the Republican, the Platte — were the worst points of
any drive. A herd would not cross a swift river willingly. The
lead steer was driven in by riders on either side; the rest
followed. A drowning was common. So was the loss of a hundred
head if the herd milled in the current.

### Stampedes

Caused by lightning, by a dropped pan, by a coyote, by nothing.
A stampede in the dark was the great terror of the drive. The
herd ran for miles. Hands rode into the leading edge and tried
to turn the lead, working it into a tighter and tighter circle
until the herd milled and stopped. A man unhorsed in the middle
of a stampede was usually a dead man.

### The railhead (August–September)

Abilene 1867–71. Dodge City 1875–85. Ogallala on the Union
Pacific. Cheyenne. The herds were sold to buyers who shipped
east. The hands were paid off. Some signed on with northern
ranches and stayed. Most rode south for another season.

## 4. The chuck wagon

Designed by Charles Goodnight in 1866 from a converted Studebaker
army wagon. The chuck box at the back, with a hinged lid that
folded down into a worktable. Drawers for coffee, sugar, salt,
flour, baking soda, lard, beans, dried fruit. A water barrel
strapped to the side. A coonie (a stretched cowhide hung
underneath) for firewood collected on the trail.

The cook (cookie, coosie, biscuit-shooter, dough-puncher,
sourdough) was the second highest-paid man in the outfit and
ran the camp. His authority was absolute on his own ground:

- His chuck wagon was off-limits unless he invited you.
- The fire was his fire. You got coffee when he poured it.
- You did not ride past the wagon at speed (it raised dust into
  the food).
- You did not tie your horse to the wagon.
- The first plate went to the trail boss, not because of rank
  but to test the food.
- A bad cook ruined a drive. A good cook was guarded jealously.

### What they ate

- **Beans.** Pintos, boiled all day in a Dutch oven with bacon
  fat and salt pork.
- **Sourdough biscuits.** Made fresh twice a day from a starter
  the cook kept in a crock under the wagon. The starter was the
  most precious thing in camp.
- **Bacon and salt pork.**
- **Fresh beef** — but only from a stray or a crippled animal,
  rarely from the trail herd, which was money on the hoof.
- **Coffee.** Boiled in a big enamel pot, no filter, grounds
  in the cup. Black, no sugar usually, no milk ever after the
  second day out.
- **Dried apples or peaches**, stewed.
- **Molasses** in a tin.
- **Hardtack** when the flour ran low.
- **Antelope, deer, prairie chicken** when shot.
- **Wild greens** if the cook knew them.

No green vegetables on a long drive. Scurvy was uncommon
(citrus and fruit at the start helped) but vitamin deficiency
was real. Constipation was the universal complaint.

### What they drank

- Coffee, all day, every day.
- Water. Often bad. Often boiled into coffee for that reason.
- Whiskey at the end. Almost never on the drive — a drinking
  hand was a fired hand.

## 5. Gear of the working hand

(See `knowledge/material-culture.md` for full detail. Brief
here.)

- **Hat.** Stetson Boss of the Plains (1865) or a slouch hat.
  Felt. Wide brim against sun and rain. Used for fanning a
  fire, watering a horse from a creek, signaling, and
  occasionally fighting a fire on the prairie.
- **Bandanna.** Red or blue cotton. A filter for dust on drag,
  a wash cloth, a tourniquet, a sling, a hot-pad for a Dutch
  oven, a head-cover under the hat.
- **Shirt.** Wool or cotton, button-front collarless, with a
  collar buttoned on for town.
- **Vest.** Pockets for tobacco, matches, a knife, a watch.
- **Pants.** Wool, often denim by the 1870s (Levi's introduced
  the riveted denim trouser in 1873). Worn over the boot tops.
- **Chaparreras (chaps).** Leather over the pants for working
  brush country (shotgun chaps full leg) or for show (batwing,
  later).
- **Boots.** High-heeled (to keep the foot from sliding through
  the stirrup), pointed-toe (to find the stirrup blind), tall-
  shafted (to protect the calf). Bad for walking. Designed for
  the saddle.
- **Spurs.** Low-rowel for working stock, longer-rowel for show
  or for green horses.
- **Slicker.** Yellow oilcloth coat rolled behind the cantle.
- **Bedroll.** A canvas tarp wrapped around two wool blankets
  and a pillow that was actually a sack of clothes. Tied behind
  the cantle or carried in the chuck wagon.
- **Saddle.** Personal property. The cowhand's largest asset.
  $40–$60 for a working saddle in the 1870s. He'd sell his
  pistol before he sold his saddle.
- **Rope.** 30 to 40 feet of grass rope (manila), later
  rawhide. Coiled and tied to the saddle. Used constantly.
- **Pistol.** A Colt revolver or similar. Many drovers carried
  none on the trail (the boss often forbade it after the first
  drunken accidental shooting). Carried in town.
- **Rifle.** Carried in a saddle scabbard if at all. A
  Winchester '73 or '76 in .44-40 was common.
- **Knife.** A jackknife in the pocket, a sheath knife on the
  belt for serious work.

## 6. The horse

A working cowhand had six to eight horses in the remuda
assigned to him. He rode each one a few times a week.

- **The morning horse.** The fresh, cold-backed one. Often
  bucked at first light.
- **The cutting horse.** The trained one for working cattle out
  of the herd. Worth twice what a green horse was worth.
- **The night horse.** Sure-footed, calm, picketed near the
  bedroll for emergencies. The hand caught it without a saddle
  if the herd ran.
- **The river horse.** A strong swimmer kept for crossings.
- **The drag horses.** Two or three less-favored mounts used
  for the worst position.

Horses were broken hard and used hard. Many were ruined by age
seven. A cowhand who could break a horse without ruining it was
worth keeping. A horse with kindness in it was a treasure.

The relationship between hand and horse was working, not
romantic. The hand fed the horse before he ate. He pulled the
saddle and brushed the back. He checked the feet. He doctored
cuts. He talked to it as he worked. He did not name most of
them — the remuda mounts were known by description (the bay,
the gray, the snip, the stocking, the line-back) or by simple
names (Buck, Star, Pete).

## 7. The bunkhouse and the off-season

Between drives, hands worked the home ranch:

- Repairing fence (after barbed wire arrived in the late 1870s).
- Riding bog (pulling cattle out of mud holes).
- Branding calves in spring round-up.
- Cutting and stacking hay (ranchers who had hayfields).
- Breaking horses.
- Riding line — a solitary ride along a stretch of range, often
  weeks alone in a line shack with two months of bacon and
  flour.
- Hunting wolves or coyotes for the bounty.

The bunkhouse:

- A log or board building, eight to twenty bunks, a stove, a
  table, sometimes a deck of cards and a coal-oil lamp.
- Cold in winter, hot in summer.
- One wash basin outside, refilled from the well.
- Privy out back.
- The blanket-lice, the bedbugs, the rats in the rafters.
- The smell of unwashed wool, sweat, leather, tobacco, coal-oil,
  and the boot-grease the hands rubbed into their boots at night.
- Books were rare. The bible, a almanac, a copy of the *Police
  Gazette,* a dime novel passed from hand to hand.

Saturday night was bath night when the weather allowed. A wash
in the creek or a tub in the bunkhouse. A change of shirts. A
clean bandanna. The hands rode to town if there was a town
within reach.

## 8. Hierarchy

The outfit was a small kingdom on the trail.

- **Trail boss.** Absolute authority. Often a part-owner. Made
  every decision: route, speed, water, rest, who got fired.
- **Segundo / top hand.** The boss's deputy. Took over if the
  boss was absent.
- **Cook.** Authority over camp.
- **Hands.** Equal among themselves. Senior hands had moral
  weight. Disputes between hands were settled by the boss or
  by the men involved without the boss's interference.
- **Wrangler / horse jingler.** Often a boy of fourteen to
  seventeen, learning the trade. Slept apart with the remuda.
  The lowest of the camp.

A new hand was tested. He was given the worst horse, the worst
position, the worst chore. If he complained, he was finished. If
he held up, he was a hand.

## 9. The smell, the sound, the feel

For prose. The texture you put on the page.

### Sound

- The bawl of cattle at first light, before they have fed.
- The creak of saddle leather in the cold.
- The chuckwagon iron clanging against itself as the wagon
  jolted.
- The night-rider's slow song to the herd — *Streets of Laredo,*
  *Bury Me Not on the Lone Prairie,* *Dogie Lullaby* — sung
  slow and low to keep the herd quiet. Real practice. The
  singing told the herd a man was there.
- The wolf-howl half a mile off and the answering howl.
- The wind on the high plains, never stopping.
- The crack of a fire in the cook's pit at dawn.
- The thunk of a cinch buckle in the dark.
- The hiss of a hot iron on the calf's hide at branding.

### Smell

- Cow manure, fresh and dried. The constant background.
- Wood smoke from the chuck fire, mostly mesquite or dry
  cottonwood.
- Coffee on a cold morning.
- Bacon grease.
- Saddle leather, sweet and old.
- Horse sweat.
- The man's own sweat, in the wool of his shirt, after a week.
- Tobacco smoke — chewing plug, cigarette papers, the rare
  cigar.
- Coal-oil lamps.
- Whiskey on a man's breath the morning after town.
- A dead horse three days in the sun, downwind.

### Feel

- The cold of the saddle in the morning before the body warms it.
- The heat of the saddle in the afternoon.
- Saddle sores on the inside of the thigh after the first three
  days.
- Sun-blister on the lip and the back of the neck and the
  knuckles of the rein hand.
- Wet boot leather in the rain that does not dry until two days
  after.
- The shake in the hand after a day on the rope.
- Sleeping in clothes for weeks, the cloth going stiff with the
  sweat and the grease.
- The blister on the palm where the rope ran.
- Grit in the eye, in the mouth, in the food, in the bed.

## 10. The myth vs the work

The 20th-century screen western removed almost everything in this
file. The cowboy of the screen was clean, monogamous,
hat-handed, slow to anger, fast to draw, alone on a horse against
a sunset. The actual cowhand was filthy, single, profane, often
cruel to stock and to himself, in a crew of ten or twelve, on a
drive of weeks of monotony broken by hours of terror, paid
poorly, used up by thirty.

Write the actual cowhand. The myth is for a different shelf.
