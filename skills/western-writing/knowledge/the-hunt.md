# The Hunt — Hunting as Profession on the Frontier

This file is the knowledge module for professional
hunting in the 1870s and 1880s American West. It
complements `knowledge/work-and-trades.md` (which
covers hunting in passing as one of several
trades), `knowledge/frontier-survival.md` (which
covers hunting as a survival skill for the
homesteader and the traveler), `knowledge/material-culture.md`
(the firearms specifications), `knowledge/peoples-and-conflict.md`
(the political consequence of the buffalo
slaughter), and the harder companion
`references/dark-frontier.md` §12 (the buffalo
slaughter and the Red River horse killing as
institutional cruelty).

The popular West has rendered hunting as the
sportsman's pursuit and as a noble engagement with
the wilderness. The reality of professional hunting
in the period was different. The buffalo hide
hunter was an industrial worker shooting from a
prepared stand for a piecework wage. The market
hunter killed deer, antelope, and elk by the
hundreds for restaurants and railroad camps and
shipped the carcasses by rail. The wolfer poisoned
carcasses with strychnine for the bounty money and
was held in low regard even on the frontier. The
professional guide for Eastern and European
sporting parties was a service worker in the
nascent western tourism industry. The military
hunter was a contract employee who supplied the
fort's mess.

Each of these is a specific occupation with its
own gear, vocabulary, economics, social standing,
and consequences. The writer who centers any of
them must render the work and not the romance.

---

## 1. The buffalo hide hunter

The largest and most consequential of the
professional hunting occupations. Between roughly
1871 and 1883 the southern and northern Plains
herds of the American buffalo (more accurately
American bison) were destroyed by commercial
hunters working for the hide trade. The estimated
kill is fifteen million animals in a decade. The
plains were emptied as policy: the federal
government did not make the policy in writing but
made it in practice by refusing to enforce the
existing treaty protections and by treating the
slaughter of the buffalo as the means by which the
Plains nations would be brought to the
reservation.

For the political and human consequences, see
`knowledge/peoples-and-conflict.md` and
`references/dark-frontier.md` §12. This file
describes the work itself.

### The economic basis

The eastern leather industry, principally in
Pennsylvania and New York, developed in the late
1860s a tanning process suitable for buffalo hide.
The hide became commercially valuable as
industrial belting (drive belts for factory
machinery), as carriage and buggy upholstery, and
as the heavy lap robes used in winter travel by
sleigh and wagon.

The price of a hide at the railhead in 1872 was
roughly $3.50 for a prime bull, $2.50 for a cow,
$1.50 for a calf. By the late 1870s the price had
dropped as the supply increased and then risen
again as the supply collapsed. By 1883 the
remaining herds were too scattered for commercial
hunting and the trade ended.

A working hunter who took fifty animals on a good
day grossed perhaps $150. After the costs (the
skinners' wages, the wagon, the supplies, the
ammunition, the freight to railhead, the
percentage to the outfit's owner if the hunter
was working for one) the hunter cleared perhaps
half. A good hunter in a good season cleared
$2,000 to $5,000. A poor hunter starved or quit
or was killed.

### The outfit

The standard buffalo hunting outfit was the
hunter, two or three skinners, a cook, and a
teamster. They operated from one or two heavy
wagons that carried the supplies, the ammunition,
the camp gear, and the hides as they were taken.

The outfit moved between the herds. A camp was
made within a few miles of a herd; the hunter
went out on horseback or on foot at dawn; the
skinners followed with the wagons; the carcasses
were skinned where they fell; the hides were
dragged to the wagon, loaded, and brought back to
camp; the camp was a stinking field of stretched
hides and offal until the wagons were filled and
the outfit moved on.

A successful season might run from spring to fall
with the outfit moving across hundreds of miles of
range. The hunter might take two or three thousand
animals in a season. The skinners might process
ten or fifteen a day each.

### The stand

The hide hunter's technique was the stand. He
approached a herd from downwind, established a
prone position behind a rise or a clump of grass
within range (two hundred to four hundred yards),
and shot the dominant animal first — typically a
sentinel cow that the rest of the herd watched
for warning. With the sentinel down and the rest
of the herd milling without leadership, the
hunter shot animal after animal in succession,
working the rifle methodically, picking the
animals at the edges of the herd so the others
did not see them fall.

A good stand might yield twenty to fifty animals
before the herd finally broke and ran. A great
stand (the legendary Tom Nixon stand of 120
animals in forty minutes in 1873; the J. Wright
Mooar accounts of similar numbers) was the
career-defining event.

The technique required the right rifle, the right
position, the right wind, and the discipline to
work calmly through the kill. The herd's failure to
flee was the precondition. The buffalo of the
hunted herd had no instinct for the rifle's
distant report; the herd associated danger with
the visible threat (the wolf, the rider, the
running predator) and not with the muzzle flash
two hundred yards off.

### The rifle

The standard buffalo rifle of the trade was the
Sharps single-shot breech-loading rifle in heavy
calibers: the .50-90 Sharps, the .50-70 Sharps,
the .45-120, the .44-90. The Sharps weighed
twelve to fifteen pounds and fired a 400- to
550-grain lead bullet over a charge of 70 to 120
grains of black powder. The action was the
falling-block breech invented by Christian Sharps
in the 1850s and perfected through the 1860s and
1870s.

The Remington Rolling Block rifle was the Sharps's
principal competitor in the trade.

The Sharps was a target-grade rifle of remarkable
accuracy. A practiced hunter could put a bullet
in a buffalo's lungs at four hundred yards. The
recoil was severe; the hunter wore a pad on his
shoulder and developed a callus there. The rifle
was hot after a stand of fifty rounds and had to
be rested or cooled with a wet rag.

The cartridges were factory-loaded by the major
makers (Winchester, UMC, Sharps's own ammunition
plant) or hand-loaded by the hunter or by his
loader in camp. A working outfit went through
hundreds of rounds a day at peak.

The rifle was the hunter's principal capital. A
new Sharps Sporting Rifle in heavy caliber cost
$80 to $125 in 1875, more than two months' wages
for a cowhand. A used rifle could be had for
$40 to $60. The hunter cared for it
fastidiously; a fouled or damaged rifle ended the
season.

### The skinning

The skinner's work was harder than the hunter's
and was lower-paid and lower-status. The skinner
worked with a heavy butcher knife and a steel for
sharpening. He approached the carcass, made the
opening cuts (down the belly from throat to anus,
down each leg from hoof to belly), and then
worked the hide free with knife and hand and,
where the country allowed, a horse.

The horse method: a rope tied to the hide at the
neck and the other end to the saddle. The rider
walked the horse forward; the hide peeled off
the body in one piece. The rider returned, the
skinner cut the hide free at the tail, the hide
was rolled and dragged to the wagon.

A skinner did ten to fifteen animals a day in
good country, fewer in difficult ground. The
skinning was filthy work. The skinner's clothes,
hands, and hair were caked with blood and grease
and hair within an hour of starting. The smell
followed him for the season. He was paid twenty-
five cents per hide in the 1873 trade, rising to
fifty cents as good skinners became scarce. He
made the same as a hand on a cattle drive in a
better season.

### The flesh

The carcass was abandoned. The buffalo was killed
for the hide alone in the standard trade; the
meat was left on the prairie. A small portion of
the hump and the tongue might be cut for the
camp's own use, and the tongues were sometimes
salted and shipped as a delicacy to eastern
markets at fifteen to twenty-five cents apiece.
Almost everything else rotted.

The smell of a hunted herd within a week of the
hunters' departure was enormous. The dead
animals attracted wolves, coyotes, ravens, and
within a few weeks the bone collectors who came
behind the hunters with wagons to gather the
skeletons for shipment east as fertilizer
(carbon black for sugar refining and bone meal
for fields). The bone trade was a secondary
economy that lasted into the 1890s; the railhead
piles of buffalo bones in Dodge City and the
Texas panhandle towns are documented in
photographs of the period.

### The camp

The hunter's camp was austere. A wagon, a tent
or a brush shelter, a fire, the skinners' bedrolls
on the ground. The food was salt pork, beans,
biscuit, coffee, and what fresh meat the camp
took from the day's kill. The cook was often a
Mexican hand or a Black freedman, hired for the
season at twenty to thirty dollars a month.

The work day ran from dawn to dark with the
skinners pressing to keep up with the hunter. A
hunter who shot fifty animals before noon left
his outfit fifty animals to skin before dark. A
hunter who shot a hundred outran his skinners
and lost hides to the predators or to spoilage.
The good outfits kept the hunter's pace
calibrated to the skinners' capacity.

### The hazards

The buffalo herd was not the principal hazard.
The hazards were:

- **The Comanche, the Kiowa, the Cheyenne, the
  Lakota.** The Plains nations regarded the hide
  hunters as the existential enemy and attacked
  outfits when they could. The Adobe Walls fight
  (Texas panhandle, June 1874) saw a force of
  Comanche, Kiowa, and Cheyenne under Quanah
  Parker attack a hunters' settlement and was
  beaten off only by the Sharps rifles' long-range
  fire. Smaller engagements happened repeatedly
  through the 1870s. A hunting outfit caught in
  the open by a war party was usually killed.
- **The weather.** The Plains blizzard could
  catch an outfit on open prairie with no
  shelter. The summer thunderstorm with
  hail killed horses and ruined hides.
- **The accident.** The wagon overturning, the
  rifle bursting, the skinner's knife slipping
  in the wet, the horse falling on the rider.
  The frontier accident rate was high.
- **The disease.** The unsanitary camp at the
  end of a long season produced typhoid,
  dysentery, and the standard frontier infections.
- **The fight in town.** The hunter at the end
  of the season with cash in his pocket coming
  into Dodge City or Hays or Mobeetie was a
  drunk armed man among other drunk armed men.
  The mortality from interpersonal violence in
  the buffalo towns was substantial.

### Who they were

The hide hunters were predominantly Anglo, mostly
Southern and Western, mostly young men in their
twenties and early thirties. A substantial
minority were Civil War veterans of both armies
who had drifted west after the war and found in
the hide trade a use for their rifle skills. A
smaller number were Mexican, Black, and mixed-
race. A handful of Native men worked the hide
trade themselves, complicating any neat racial
account.

The major figures: J. Wright Mooar (the
documented hunter and memoirist who killed an
estimated 22,000 buffalo over his career, who
shot the white buffalo in 1876, and who lived to
1940 to write his memoir). His brother John W.
Mooar. Frank Mayer (the hunter and memoirist
whose 1957 oral history is one of the principal
period sources). Tom Nixon, Billy Tilghman (later
the lawman), Billy Dixon (who fired the famous
1538-yard Adobe Walls shot), Dirty Face Jones,
Wyatt Earp (briefly, before his lawman career),
and many others.

### The end

The southern herd was effectively destroyed by 1878. The northern herd lasted until the early
1880s when the railroads' expansion into Montana
and the Dakotas opened the last range to the
hunters. By 1883 the commercial hunt was over.
By 1900 the wild buffalo population in the lower
forty-eight states was under a thousand animals.
The species was saved by private conservation
efforts (Charles Goodnight's herd in Texas, the
Wichita Mountains preserve, Yellowstone) at the
last possible moment.

The hunters who survived their careers retired
or moved to other work. Many became cowhands,
some became lawmen (Tilghman, Earp), some
became merchants, some drank up their stake and
died in the saloons of the towns they had made
rich.

### How to render

The hide hunter is the frontier industrial worker.
He is not a sportsman. The work is monotonous,
filthy, and extraordinarily destructive. The
character must be rendered in the work: the
rifle on the shooting sticks, the powder smoke
hanging on the still morning air, the herd
milling and not running, the methodical pace, the
cartridge cases at the hunter's elbow, the call
to the skinners as the hunter rises.

The character may know what he is doing is the
end of the buffalo. He may not. The Mooar
brothers did not believe to the end of their
lives that they had done wrong; the buffalo, they
maintained, had been there to be taken. Other
hunters in their later memoirs expressed something
closer to remorse. Both reactions are
historically present and are available to the
writer.

The character should not deliver a speech about
what he is doing. He does the work.

The Native witness to the slaughter is the other
character. The Comanche or Kiowa hunter who rides
to the edge of a hunted herd's killing ground a
week after the outfit has moved on, who sees the
field of carcasses, who knows what it means for
his people, is one of the period's central
figures. His response is rage, grief, and a
specific resolution that produces (in the
historical case) the events of the Red River War.

## 2. The market hunter

Below the buffalo trade in scale and visibility,
the market hunter killed game animals for the
restaurant and the railroad camp. He worked deer,
antelope, elk, mountain sheep, prairie chicken,
quail, ducks, geese, sandhill cranes, the
remaining buffalo on a smaller scale, and any
other species that could be sold.

### The economics

A deer carcass in the 1870s sold at the railhead
for $1.50 to $3.00 depending on the cut and the
city of destination. An antelope sold for $1.00
to $2.00. An elk hindquarter sold for $3.00 to
$5.00. Prairie chicken and quail sold by the
dozen for $1.00 to $2.00. The hunter who took
ten deer a week grossed $15 to $30 net of the
freight and the bullets.

The principal markets were the urban
restaurants of the East (New York, Boston,
Philadelphia, Chicago) and the construction
camps of the western railroads (Union Pacific,
Central Pacific, Northern Pacific, Atchison
Topeka & Santa Fe). The railroad camp market in
particular was concentrated and immediate: a
camp of two hundred working men needed several
hundred pounds of fresh meat a week, and the
contractor would buy whatever the market hunter
brought in.

### The technique

The market hunter was a craftsman of approach
and shot. He worked the country he knew (his
range was typically a fifty- to two-hundred-mile
radius around his home base), knew the watering
places and the bedding grounds, knew the season
patterns of the animals. He hunted on foot or
horseback, often with one or two pack mules to
carry the carcasses out.

The shot was the careful single shot at the
unalerted animal at one hundred to two hundred
yards. Multiple shots scattered the herd and
wasted ammunition. The market hunter cared about
his ammunition cost and about the carcass's
condition; a heavily shot animal lost commercial
value.

The rifle was usually a smaller-caliber rifle than
the buffalo hunter's: the .44-77 Sharps, the .45-70
Springfield (the army's standard, sold cheap as
surplus and widely used by civilian hunters), the
Winchester 1873 in .44-40 (popular for the
shorter-range shooting and for its repeating
capacity), the Henry rifle in .44 rimfire (older,
less common by the late 1870s). The shotgun
(the breech-loading 10-gauge or 12-gauge) for
ducks, geese, and prairie chicken.

### The processing

The market hunter field-dressed his game where it
fell, gutting the animal and bleeding it out. He
packed the carcass on a mule or, for the
near-base hunting, dragged it on a travois or in
a wagon to his cabin. There he hung the carcass
in a meat house (a slatted wooden shed where the
carcasses could cool with airflow), butchered it
into commercial cuts, packed the cuts in barrels
of ice or salt, and shipped them by wagon and
rail to the buyer.

In winter the cold did the refrigeration. In
summer the hunter worked at a smaller scale or
sold to nearer markets that did not require
preservation.

The good market hunter had a permanent or
semi-permanent base camp: a cabin, a meat
house, a corral, a small horse herd, sometimes a
wife or hired man to handle the camp while he
hunted. The career market hunter was often a
small businessman in this sense: a sole
proprietor whose product was meat.

### Who they were

A more various population than the buffalo
hunters. Many were homesteaders supplementing
their farm income. Others were full-time
hunters whose main living was the trade. A
substantial number were former mountain men
whose fur trade had collapsed in the 1840s and
who had moved to game-meat hunting. Some were
Native men adapting traditional hunting skills to
the cash economy. Some were European
immigrants — Germans, Scandinavians — with
hunting traditions from the old country.

### The decline

The market hunting of game animals followed the
buffalo into commercial collapse but more slowly.
The deer and antelope herds of the open West were
substantially reduced by 1900. The first
state-level game laws came in the 1870s and
1880s and restricted commercial hunting to
defined seasons; the federal Lacey Act of 1900
banned interstate trafficking in illegally taken
game. The market hunting trade ended by the
early 1900s with the species' near-extinction
and the new regulatory framework.

For the 1870s and 1880s writer, the market hunter
is at his peak and is a recognized profession.
He is not yet villainized; he is the practical
provider of fresh meat to markets that need it.
The conservation conscience that would label him
a poacher is twenty years away.

### How to render

The market hunter is a craftsman. His work is
quieter than the buffalo hunter's, more skilled,
less industrial. He knows the country at a level
the homesteader does not. He knows where the buck
will be at first light in the third week of
October when the wind is from the north and the
oaks are in mast. He knows how long the carcass
will keep at what temperature in what season. He
knows the buyers in town and what they will
pay.

Render him at his work in the country, alone,
with the pack mule and the rifle and the
careful approach. Render the hunter's silence and
the country's silence and the moment of the
shot. The reader does not need a speech. The
reader needs the work.

## 3. The wolfer

The wolfer killed wolves for the bounty and for
the pelts. He held the lowest social standing of
any of the western hunters. The reason was the
method.

### The economics

State and territorial governments paid bounties
on wolf scalps from the 1860s through the 1890s.
The bounty varied by jurisdiction and year:
typically one to three dollars per wolf, more in
some places, less in others. Coyote scalps paid
fifty cents to a dollar. The Stockmen's
Association in some districts added a private
bounty on top of the public one.

The pelt itself sold at a fur house for two to
five dollars depending on the season and the
condition. A working wolfer in a good year
could clear a thousand to two thousand dollars
between the bounty and the pelts.

### The method

The standard wolfer's technique was strychnine
poisoning. The wolfer killed a buffalo, an
antelope, or a horse, opened the carcass,
laced the meat with strychnine sulfate
crystals, and left the carcass in the open as
bait. Wolves that fed on the carcass died within
hours. The wolfer returned the next day or the
day after, found the dead wolves around the
bait, scalped them for the bounty, skinned them
for the pelts, and moved on to set the next bait.

A single laced carcass could kill a dozen wolves
in a night. A wolfer working a string of baits
across a hundred-mile circuit might collect
fifty to a hundred wolf scalps a week in good
country.

### The collateral damage

Strychnine bait did not discriminate. It killed
coyotes, foxes, badgers, eagles, vultures,
ravens, magpies, dogs, and any other carnivore
or scavenger that fed on the carcass. The dead
predators in turn poisoned secondary scavengers
that fed on them. A single laced carcass could
sterilize a square mile of country of wildlife
for weeks.

The wolfer's bait poisoned the dogs of any
camp, settlement, or Native band that came near
it. The Native nations of the northern Plains
came to view the wolfer as a particular enemy
because the wolfer's strychnine killed their
dogs and, occasionally, their children who
played with what they found. The Cypress Hills
Massacre (June 1873, the southern Saskatchewan
killing of an Assiniboine band by a party of
American wolfers and traders) was the most
famous of the wolfer-Native confrontations.

### Who they were

The wolfer was almost always a solo operator or
a partnership of two or three men. He worked
remote country for months at a time. He was
viewed by other frontier men as a man who had
chosen the wrong side of the wage scale and who
could not associate with respectable hands. The
cattlemen valued his service (the wolves killed
calves) but did not invite him to the
headquarters table. The cowhands sneered at
him. The market hunters considered him beneath
them. The Native nations hated him.

His clothes carried the smell of the bait and
the carcasses. His hands were stained dark with
the work. He stayed away from settled places
because settled places did not want him. He
spent the winter in his line shack and came in
to a railhead in spring with his bundle of
scalps and his pelts.

### The end

The wolf was substantially exterminated from the
Plains by 1900. The wolfer's profession ended
with the wolf. The few wolves that survived in
the western mountains were poisoned out by
federal and state predator-control programs in
the early twentieth century. The wolf was
declared a protected species in most western
states only in the 1970s and 1980s; the
restoration of the wolf to Yellowstone in 1995
is the recent counter-movement.

For the 1870s and 1880s writer, the wolfer is
present and is one of the period's recognized
low occupations. He is the character the other
characters do not want at their fire.

### How to render

The wolfer is a hard character to write
sympathetically because the work itself is so
ugly. The honest approach is to render him as
he was: a solitary man doing solitary destructive
work for a small wage in a country that allowed
it. He may know what he is doing and may not
care. He may know and care and continue
because the alternative is starving. He may be
ignorant of the consequences and may be
indifferent to them when explained.

Render the bait, the dead wolves the next
morning, the wolfer's hands, the smell of the
carcass at four days, the scalps in the wolfer's
saddle bag, the silence of the country a month
after the wolfer has worked it. The reader does
not need editorial. The fact carries the
weight.

## 4. The professional sporting guide

By the late 1860s and through the 1870s and
1880s, the western frontier became a destination
for the American Eastern and the British and
European sporting tourist. The wealthy hunter
came west to hunt buffalo, elk, deer, antelope,
mountain sheep, and bear, and required a guide
to organize the expedition.

### The clientele

- **The American Eastern sportsman.** The
  merchant or banker or attorney from
  Philadelphia or Boston or New York with the
  means and the leisure for a six-week hunt. He
  came by rail with his case of English shotguns
  and his .45-70 hunting rifle and his servant
  and his wife if she was sporting (the Eastern
  sporting wife was a defined type). He took
  the trip to write about for the local sporting
  paper, to bring back trophies, to escape his
  business.
- **The British and European nobleman.** The
  more famous version. The Earl of Dunraven
  made repeated trips to the Estes Park region
  of Colorado in the 1870s and eventually
  attempted to acquire the entire valley. Sir
  William Drummond Stewart had toured the
  Rockies in the 1830s and 1840s and set the
  template. Grand Duke Alexis of Russia made
  the famous 1872 buffalo hunt with George
  Custer and Buffalo Bill Cody. The European
  sporting party came with a retinue and a
  budget.
- **The American officer's hunt.** Not strictly
  professional guiding but related. Senior
  officers stationed at frontier posts often
  organized hunting trips with civilian scouts
  and Native guides. The officer's wife or
  visiting Eastern relative was often included.

### The guide

The professional guide was often a former
mountain man, a former scout, a former hide
hunter, a homesteader supplementing his income,
or a Native man working the cash economy. He
provided the local knowledge, the route, the
camp, the cook, the horses, the wagon (where
the country took a wagon), the hunting strategy,
and the protection from hostile encounters.

Buffalo Bill Cody made his early reputation as
a guide before his showman career. He guided
Eastern and European parties through the late
1860s and early 1870s out of Fort McPherson and
later from his ranch on the North Platte. His
rates were among the highest in the trade: $50
a day for himself plus the costs of the camp
and the horses. The Grand Duke Alexis hunt of
1872 was the high-water mark of the trade.

Texas Jack Omohundro, Wild Bill Hickok briefly,
California Joe Milner, and dozens of less
famous figures worked the trade through the
1870s.

### The hunt

The Eastern sportsman's hunt was usually staged
as a more elaborate version of the cattleman's
hunt: a party of six to twelve riders, a wagon
or two with the camp, a cook, several
wranglers, a tent or two, the rifles, the
trophy preparation gear (the salt for the
hides, the boxes for the heads).

The hunting itself was the careful pursuit of
trophy animals, the long stalk, the shot at
two hundred yards, the photograph of the
hunter beside the kill. The party covered
twenty miles a day on horseback, hunted at
dawn and at dusk, ate the day's game at the
evening fire, slept in the tent.

The trophies were salted and packed and shipped
back east with the hunter. The mounted heads
in the libraries of the East Coast clubs and
country houses were the visible consequence.

### The economics

The Eastern hunting party paid the guide $300 to
$1,500 for a six-week trip depending on the
party's size and the guide's reputation. The
European nobleman paid more. The guide netted a
substantial portion of his year's income from
two or three good parties.

The guide also provided introductions and access
to specific country (the Wind River for elk, the
Rockies for bear, the Yellowstone country for
the rare experience of country not yet
overhunted). The country-knowledge was the
guide's principal asset.

### How to render

The guiding scene is the scene where the East
meets the West. The guide is a working man
serving a customer who is from a different
world. The customer often does not understand
the country or the work or the guide. The
guide is professional about the customer's
ignorance because he is paid to be.

The Eastern sportsman is sometimes ridiculous
and sometimes admirable. The literary tradition
(Theodore Roosevelt's western writings,
Owen Wister, the Eastern accounts in the
sporting magazines of the period) has rendered
him both ways. The honest approach is to render
him as he is: a man with money and equipment
and ambition, in a country he does not know,
served by a man who does. The interaction is
the scene.

## 5. The military and contract hunter

The frontier army post needed fresh meat. The
post commissary supplied the bulk staples
(salt pork, beans, hardtack, coffee) but the
fresh meat (beef, mutton, game) was contracted
locally. The contract hunter was a defined
position.

### The work

The post hired a hunter (or a hunter and a
small crew) on contract for a season or a year.
The hunter supplied the post mess with a
quota of fresh meat — typically three to five
animals a week for a small post, more for a
larger one. He hunted in the country around the
post, brought in the game, and was paid by the
pound or by the animal.

A contract hunter at a frontier post earned
$50 to $100 a month plus a supply of
ammunition. He sometimes had a small cabin near
the post, sometimes lived with his family in
the post sutler's settlement, sometimes had a
homestead nearby. He was not a soldier and was
not under military discipline; he was a
civilian employee.

### The figures

Many of the famous frontier figures held post
hunter contracts at points in their careers.
Buffalo Bill held one at Fort McPherson in the
late 1860s before his guiding career took off.
California Joe held one at various Western
posts. The standard frontier scout (the
civilian employed by the army to interpret
country and people for the cavalry columns)
often combined the scouting work with hunting.

### How to render

The contract hunter is a working man embedded in
the post's life without being of the post. He
knows the officers and the men but is not one
of them. He brings in the day's game, the cooks
butcher it, the men eat it. He sleeps in his
cabin or in his tent and rides out the next
morning.

The scenes available: the hunter coming into
the post at dusk with the wagon and the day's
kill; the cooks butchering on the trestles
behind the mess; the lieutenant coming over to
ask the hunter what the country looked like
that day; the hunter giving the lieutenant the
information without offering an opinion.

## 6. The trapper and the trader

The fur trade as the dominant western
occupation had ended by the early 1840s with
the collapse of the beaver hat market. A
residual fur trade in marten, fox, lynx,
otter, mink, beaver (for trim and not for
hats), bear, and badger persisted through the
1870s and 1880s, particularly in the Northern
Rockies, the Northwest, the Sierras, and the
Yellowstone country.

### The trapper

The fur trapper of the 1870s and 1880s was a
diminished figure compared to the mountain
man of the 1820s and 1830s but was not
extinct. He worked a trapline of a hundred or
more steel traps through a country he knew,
checked them every few days, skinned and
stretched the pelts at his cabin, and shipped
them to the fur house in spring.

The major fur houses (the Hudson's Bay Company
in Canada, the American Fur Company successors,
the smaller St. Louis and St. Paul houses)
bought through agents at frontier posts and
trading houses. The price for a marten in 1880
was $3 to $5; for a fox $2 to $4; for a
mountain lion $2 to $3 (more for the bounty
where one was offered); for a bear $5 to $20
depending on size and condition.

A working trapper in good country earned
several hundred to a thousand dollars a
season. He spent the winter in his cabin
working the line and the summer with his
family or in town.

### The trader

The frontier trading post (Bent's Fort in its
later phases, the Hudson's Bay posts in the
Northwest, the post sutlers, the Indian
agency traders, the small town merchants)
bought furs and hides from trappers and from
Native bands and shipped them to the eastern
markets. The trader extended credit to the
trapper or the band against the next season's
take, sold supplies (flour, ammunition, blankets,
tobacco, knives, beads, alcohol where it was
allowed and often where it was not), and took
his percentage.

The Native trade in particular was substantial
through the 1870s and into the early
reservation period. The agency trader in
particular held a position of considerable
power over the band's economic life and was
often a corrupt one.

### How to render

The trapper is a solitary figure. The trader is
a social one. Both are working men in a
specific economic system. The trapper's work
is physical and slow; the trader's is
commercial and continuous. Both are present in
the period and are available subjects.

## 7. The vocabulary of the trade

### Buffalo hunting

- **Stand.** The hunter's prone position and the
  episode of shooting from it.
- **The herd.** The group of animals being
  hunted.
- **The sentinel, the leader, the watcher.** The
  dominant cow the hunter shot first.
- **Shooting sticks.** The crossed wooden sticks
  the hunter used as a rest for the rifle.
- **The hide, the robe.** The skin. _Robe_ for
  the heavy winter coat used for sleighs and
  carriages.
- **Tongue, hump, marrow gut, fleece, boudins.**
  The cuts of buffalo meat the hunters ate (the
  tongues sometimes salted and shipped; the rest
  consumed in camp).
- **Stretching, fleshing, salting.** The
  preparation of the hide.
- **The bone trade, the bone wagon, the bone
  pile.** The secondary trade in skeletons for
  fertilizer.
- **The buffalo gun, the Big Fifty.** The
  Sharps rifle in heavy caliber.
- **Cartridge box, ammunition belt, primer
  pouch.** The hunter's gear.
- **Skinner, fleecer, fleshing knife.** The
  skinning vocabulary.
- **The runner.** The hunter who shot from
  horseback at a galloping herd, the older
  technique, mostly displaced by the stand.
- **The poison hunter.** Disparaging — a hunter
  who used poison rather than the rifle, rare
  in the buffalo trade.

### Market hunting and meat

- **Game, meat, venison.** _Venison_ for deer
  meat specifically, not for game generally as in
  modern usage. _Game_ was the live animal; _meat_
  was what came back to camp.
- **Field-dressing, gutting, bleeding out.** The
  immediate post-kill work.
- **Hanging, aging, curing.** The processing.
- **Quarter, side, saddle, hindquarter,
  forequarter.** The cuts.
- **Jerky, jerk meat.** Sun-dried thin strips of
  meat. (See `knowledge/food-culture.md`.)
- **Pemmican.** The pounded dried meat with fat
  and berries, a Native preparation adopted by
  the trappers and the freighters.

### Wolfing

- **Bait, set bait, lay a bait.** The poisoned
  carcass.
- **Strychnine, strych, the powder.** The
  poison.
- **Scalp, scalping, the scalp tally.** The
  bounty work.
- **Pelt, pelt house, fur shed.** The pelt
  trade.

### General

- **Skinning knife, butcher knife, boning knife,
  steel.** The blades.
- **Pack mule, pack horse, pack saddle, panniers.**
  The transport.
- **Cabin, line shack, base camp, hunting camp.**
  The hunter's accommodation.
- **Outfit.** The hunter's organized expedition
  with men, animals, equipment, and supplies.
- **Sign.** The tracks, droppings, browse marks,
  and other physical evidence of game presence.
  _Cutting sign, reading sign._
- **Stalk, approach, shot.** The sequence of the
  hunt.
- **Kill, take, drop.** The hunter's terms for the
  successful shot. _Took ten head, dropped a
  five-point bull, killed a sow with two cubs._

## 8. Sound, smell, light

### The buffalo hunter's stand

The crack of the Sharps and the long roll of the
echo across the rolling country. The smell of
the burning powder hanging on the still air. The
herd's confused bellowing as the leaders went
down. The crash of the heavy bodies hitting the
ground. The wind across the grass between
shots. The hunter working the breech and
loading and shouldering and firing again. The
smell of the powder mixing with the smell of
the blood as the kill grew. The skinners
arriving at noon and the smell of opened
animals rising from the field.

### The market hunter's morning

The cold dawn at the watering place. The buck
coming in to drink. The hunter at the edge of
the brush with the rifle on the rest. The
single shot. The buck dropping. The hunter
walking out across the meadow in the first
light. The smell of the wet grass and the
buck's last breath. The work with the knife
opening the carcass. The blood pooling on the
frosted ground.

### The wolfer's bait

The dead horse opened on the prairie three
days. The smell carrying for miles. The dead
wolves around it in the morning, contorted,
their lips drawn back from their teeth. The
ravens and the eagles dead at the edges. The
silence of the country. The wolfer with his
knife scalping the carcasses and stuffing the
scalps in his bag.

### The guide's evening camp

The tent in the mountain meadow. The hobbled
horses cropping in the dark. The cook at the
fire. The Eastern sportsman in his city boots
beside the fire with his glass of whiskey and
his cigar. The guide with his coffee at the
edge of the firelight. The talk of the next
day's hunt. The stars in the sky over the
mountains. The smell of the wood smoke and
the meat cooking.

## 9. Before / after — the writing

### Before (AI default, sportsman's romance)

> Jim raised his rifle and took aim at the
> magnificent buffalo grazing peacefully in the
> distance. He felt a moment of awe at the
> majestic creature, but the demands of his
> trade required action. With a steady hand he
> squeezed the trigger, and the great beast fell
> to the prairie grass. Another hide for the
> growing pile. Another day's work in the
> dangerous and lucrative trade of the buffalo
> hunter.

The passage fails. _Magnificent, peacefully,
moment of awe, majestic creature_ are
romance and editorial. _Steady hand, squeezed
the trigger_ is the cliché of cinematic
shooting. _Dangerous and lucrative trade_ is
summary, not scene. The buffalo is rendered as
the noble victim and the hunter as the
reluctant professional. Neither was the case in
the working trade.

### After (the canon's register)

> Mooar was on the rest at first light. The herd
> had bedded a quarter mile out and was up and
> grazing now and the wind was right. He picked
> the cow that was watching back at him on the
> rise and put the bullet through her chest.
> She went down and the herd milled and did not
> run. He worked the breech and loaded and
> shouldered and shot the next cow and the next.
> The powder smoke hung in the still air. The
> herd would not break while the leaders kept
> dropping where the others could not see them
> fall. He shot for an hour. When he stopped he
> counted forty-three down and the herd had
> finally moved off and Tom and the boys were
> coming up with the wagon.

The technique is right (the stand, the sentinel
shot first, the methodical work, the
forty-three count). The work is the subject.
The buffalo is a target, not a noble victim.
The hunter is competent and unsentimental. The
skinners are coming up with the wagon. There is
no editorial. The reader knows what is
happening and knows what it means.

## 10. The cheat sheet

- The buffalo hide hunter is the frontier
  industrial worker. Render him at the stand
  with the Sharps on the rest, the herd
  milling, the smoke hanging, the methodical
  work. Do not render him as a noble pioneer
  or a reluctant professional. Render him as
  the working man he was.
- The market hunter is a craftsman of the
  approach and the careful shot. He works
  alone or with a small crew. He knows his
  country. He ships meat to the railroad camp
  and the Eastern restaurant. He is not yet a
  poacher.
- The wolfer holds the lowest social standing
  of the hunters. He works strychnine bait. He
  kills indiscriminately. He smells of his
  work. The Native nations hate him. The other
  frontier men do not invite him to the fire.
- The professional sporting guide serves the
  Eastern and European hunting tourist. The
  scene is the East meeting the West. The
  guide is a working man, the customer is from
  another world, the country between them is
  the subject.
- The contract hunter at the army post is a
  civilian employee. He brings in the fresh
  meat. He knows the officers and is not one
  of them.
- The trapper of the residual fur trade is a
  diminished figure compared to the mountain
  man of the 1830s but is present. He works
  the trapline through the winter and ships
  the pelts in the spring.
- The political consequence of the buffalo
  slaughter is the destruction of the Plains
  nations' material base. The Native witness is
  the second character in any buffalo hunting
  scene that is being honestly written.
- The sound of the Sharps, the smell of the
  carcasses three days old, the silence of the
  country a month after the wolfer has worked
  it — these are the sensory anchors. Render
  them and the scene stands.

The frontier hunter was not a sportsman. He was
a worker. The work emptied a continent of its
animals in two generations. Render the work
and the country and the consequence, and the
hunter stands in his place.
