# Religion and Faith — The Spiritual World of the West

This file is the knowledge module for religious life
in the 1870s American West. It complements
`knowledge/towns-economy-law.md` (the church as a
town institution) and
`knowledge/native-material-culture.md` (Native
ceremonial life, treated separately because it
operates by different categories). For the preacher
or the believer as character, see also
`references/speech-patterns.md` (the registers of
sermon, prayer, hymn).

The popular West has been written largely as a
secular space: the cowboy and the gunman act in a
world without God. The historical West was nothing
of the kind. The 1870s West was a deeply religious
country. The circuit-riding preacher was as common a
figure as the deputy sheriff. The revival meeting
shaped towns. The Catholic mission was a power in
the Southwest. The Mormon settlements were a
sovereign religious polity in the Great Basin. The
Black church traveled west with Black migrants. The
Sunday morning of any western town was full.

A western that ignores religion misrepresents the
period. This file is the reference for getting it
right.

---

## 1. The major denominations and traditions

### Methodism

The dominant Protestant denomination of the western
expansion. The Methodist Episcopal Church (and after
1844 its split with the Methodist Episcopal Church,
South) sent more circuit riders, established more
churches, and shaped more western towns than any
other body.

Methodism's organizational genius was the circuit. A
preacher (not necessarily ordained — a "local
preacher" or "exhorter" might serve) was assigned a
circuit of preaching points, riding the circuit on
a regular schedule, holding services in homes,
schools, and meeting houses until a congregation
could build a church. The circuit rider was a
specific western character.

Methodist worship featured: extemporaneous prayer,
hymn singing (Charles Wesley's hymns dominant),
expository preaching, and the camp meeting (a
multi-day revival held in summer in a brush arbor or
clearing).

### Baptist

Strong throughout the South and migrating west with
Southern settlers. Baptist polity was congregational
(each church autonomous), which suited the frontier.
The Baptist preacher was often unpaid and worked at
another trade.

The Baptist revival was the central institution. A
visiting preacher held a week-long meeting; sinners
were brought forward to the mourner's bench;
conversions were public and emotional; baptism (by
full immersion) followed in the nearest creek or river.

By the 1870s the Southern Baptist Convention (founded 1845) was the dominant Baptist body in the western
South. Black Baptist churches were emerging in the
freedmen's communities.

### Presbyterian

Smaller numerically but socially established.
Presbyterian congregations were typically town
churches, with an educated minister and a more formal
service. The Presbyterian preacher's voice was
literate and reserved.

### Episcopal

The denomination of the Eastern establishment moving
west. Smaller numerically; concentrated in the
larger towns. The Episcopal church might be the only
church with a stained-glass window in town.

### Catholic

The dominant religious body of the Southwest, present
in California, New Mexico, Arizona, and Texas before
American annexation. The Catholic mission system
shaped the southwestern landscape: the chain of
California missions (largely secularized by the
1830s but the church bodies remained), the Pueblo
mission churches of New Mexico, the Texas missions.

In the 1870s the Catholic church was the church of:

- **Hispanic populations** throughout the Southwest
  and California.
- **Irish immigrants** in the army, the railroad
  camps, the mining camps.
- **German and Italian immigrants** in cities and
  some farming areas.
- **Native populations** that had been converted
  through the missions (overlapping with traditional
  practice in complex ways).

The Catholic priest was a specific figure: ordained,
educated (often in seminaries in Spain, Mexico,
France, or Ireland), holding sacramental authority,
operating under a bishop. The mission priest of the
Southwest had often spent decades in his parish and
spoke the local language and Spanish and the
ecclesiastical Latin.

### Mormon (Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints)

The Mormons settled the Great Basin in 1847 and by
the 1870s had built a network of communities from
southern Idaho to southern Nevada to northern Arizona.
Salt Lake City was the capital. Brigham Young died
in 1877, ending the founding generation's leadership.

The 1870s Mormon community was practicing polygamy
(plural marriage was officially endorsed until 1890).
The federal government's hostility to Mormon
practice produced repeated conflicts: the Edmunds
Act (1882), the Edmunds-Tucker Act (1887), and
eventually the Manifesto of 1890 ending official
polygamy.

The Mormon community was its own world: distinct
theology (additional scripture, restorationist
claims, temple ceremonies), distinct economic
organization (cooperative mercantile, tithing-funded
infrastructure), distinct political organization
(theocratic in practice through much of the period).
A Mormon character moves through the West differently
from any other character.

### Jewish

A small but real presence in the frontier West.
Jewish merchants in the larger towns (notably the
mining camps), Jewish ranchers in some areas, Jewish
peddlers traveling the country with goods. The
Bavarian-immigrant generation of Jewish merchants
included the Goldwaters of Arizona, the Spiegelbergs
of New Mexico, the Levi Strauss enterprise of San
Francisco.

Synagogue life was thin in the smaller towns. A
Jewish family might travel to a larger city for
holidays. Mixed marriage was common and led to
assimilation in many cases.

### Black church traditions

Black migrants west — Exodusters to Kansas in 1879,
freedmen to the Indian Territory, Black soldiers in
the Buffalo Soldier regiments, Black cowhands on
the trail — brought their churches with them. The
African Methodist Episcopal (AME) Church, the AME
Zion Church, and Black Baptist congregations were
established in the western Black communities.

Nicodemus, Kansas (founded 1877 by Exodusters), is
the canonical western Black settlement. Its
churches, schools, and community life are
documentable.

### Native traditions

Treated separately in
`knowledge/native-material-culture.md`. The 1870s
saw aggressive missionary efforts among Native
peoples, the suppression of traditional ceremonies
(the Sun Dance was banned in 1881), the rise of
syncretic movements (the Ghost Dance came in 1890,
just past our period), and the persistence of
traditional practice underground.

## 2. The circuit rider

The defining western Protestant figure. A
horseback preacher serving a circuit of preaching
points across hundreds of square miles.

### The work

The circuit rider's circuit might include twenty to
forty preaching points visited on a six-week or
quarterly rotation. He preached at each, performed
weddings and funerals, baptized children and
converts, organized class meetings (small groups for
mutual support and discipline), and moved on.

He rode in all weather. He slept in the homes of
parishioners. He ate what was offered. He earned
almost nothing — Methodist circuit riders in the
period earned about a hundred dollars a year. He was
celibate by economic necessity if not by choice; the
circuit could not support a family.

### The voice

The circuit rider's preaching voice was practiced and
distinctive: extemporaneous (he did not read a
written sermon), emotional, direct, scripturally
saturated, often with a chanted or rhythmic delivery
in the climactic passages.

A characteristic structure: opening text (a Bible
verse), exposition of the verse's meaning, application
to the congregation's daily life, narrative
illustrations, building to an emotional appeal for
conversion or commitment, closing with a hymn and
the call to the mourner's bench.

The voice on the page can be rendered in fragments —
a few minutes of preaching at the climax of a
service. The full sermon is too long for a scene; the
selected passage carries the weight.

### The figure

Some historical models: Peter Cartwright (the
Methodist itinerant of an earlier generation, but
his autobiography is the source); the Methodist
itinerants of Texas (William Stevenson, Henry
Stephenson); the Quaker preachers of the southern
plains; the Baptist farmer-preachers of Missouri and
Arkansas.

A circuit rider in a western novel is a way to bring
religion onto the page without making the whole
novel a religious document. He arrives, he preaches,
he moves on. The town is changed for a week or for a
year.

## 3. The revival meeting

The principal Protestant religious event of the rural
West. A multi-day meeting (typically four to seven
days) held in late summer or early fall, in a brush
arbor, a tent, or a meeting hall.

### The structure

A typical day at a revival:

- **Morning prayer service.** Smaller, for the
  faithful.
- **Afternoon preaching.** Open to all. A guest
  preacher (a famous evangelist if the meeting was
  large) preached a substantial sermon.
- **Evening preaching.** The main service. Larger
  attendance, more emotional. The altar call and the
  mourner's bench.

### The mourner's bench (anxious bench)

A bench at the front of the meeting where seekers
sat during the closing of the service. The
preacher and other believers prayed over them, urged
them, exhorted them. Conversions happened at the
mourner's bench — the sinner experienced the
moment of forgiveness, often emotionally (weeping,
shouting, falling).

### The conversion experience

The Protestant revival theology centered on a
specific moment of conversion: the sinner
acknowledged sin, accepted Christ, was forgiven, and
was changed. The experience was expected to be
felt — peace, joy, a weight lifted. The convert
testified publicly to the experience.

A character's conversion is a powerful scene to write
if it is rendered honestly. The temptation is to make
it transformative or ironic; both are easier than
the harder rendering, which is that the convert's
life is now both changed and not changed. The same
problems remain after conversion. The new
relationship to those problems is what has shifted.

### The camp meeting

The largest form of revival. A campsite (often
returned to year after year) where families brought
wagons, set up camp, and stayed for the duration.
Multiple preachers preached at multiple stands.
Hundreds or thousands gathered. Singing went on into
the night. Conversions and rededications were
counted in the hundreds.

The camp meeting was also a social event — the
year's gathering for scattered farm families. Young
people met. Marriages were arranged. Business was
done. Visiting was visited. The religious purpose
held the structure but was not its only function.

## 4. The Catholic mission and parish

In the Southwest, the Catholic parish or mission was
the religious center for most communities.

### The church

A New Mexican village church was typically:

- **Adobe construction.** Thick walls, small windows,
  a flat roof with the bell in a small bell-cote
  above the entrance.
- **A single nave.** Floor of beaten earth or
  flagstone. Pews introduced in the historic period;
  earlier, congregants stood or knelt.
- **The altar.** At the east end. A reredos (carved
  wooden screen) behind it, often painted in
  vivid colors with scenes of saints and the
  Passion.
- **The santos.** Wooden carved figures of the saints,
  often by local santeros (saint-makers). These are
  one of the great folk arts of the Southwest. The
  bulto (free-standing carved figure) and the
  retablo (painted wooden panel) are the major forms.
- **The campo santo (the burial ground).** Adjacent
  to the church, walled.

### The priest

The 1870s southwestern parish priest was often a
French or Spanish missionary, sometimes a local-born
priest. The Archbishop of Santa Fe in the period
was Jean-Baptiste Lamy (the model for the bishop in
Willa Cather's _Death Comes for the Archbishop_, who
served until 1885).

The priest performed mass (in Latin until Vatican II,
1965), heard confessions, performed the sacraments
of baptism, marriage, and last rites. He was
addressed as _Father_ in English or _Padre_ in
Spanish.

### The Penitentes

A lay confraternity of Hispanic Catholic men in
northern New Mexico and southern Colorado. The
brotherhood (Los Hermanos Penitentes, the Penitent
Brothers) maintained religious life in
priest-scarce villages, conducted Holy Week
observances (including processions, prayer, and
self-mortification), and provided mutual aid.

The Penitente practice — the morada (the meeting
house), the alabados (the ancient Spanish religious
hymns), the Holy Week rituals — was treated as
heretical by some incoming Anglo Catholic clergy
and went underground in some areas in the late
nineteenth century. By the 1870s the Penitentes were
a vital force in their villages.

A Penitente scene in a novel set in northern New
Mexico is grounded in real practice. Treat the
practice with respect; it is not horror movie
material.

### The mission Indian

The mission system through the colonial period had
brought Native populations into the Catholic church.
By the 1870s, populations in the Pueblo, the
California Mission, and other groups maintained both
Catholic practice and traditional ceremony, often in
parallel and in syncretic forms. The matachines
dance (a Mexican-Spanish folk drama performed in
Pueblo and Hispanic villages on saint's days) is one
example of syncretic practice.

## 5. The Mormon settlement

A Mormon village in the 1870s was a distinct
religious-economic community.

### Layout

A Mormon village was laid out on Joseph Smith's
"Plat of Zion": a grid of large blocks, each block
divided into four to ten lots, each lot supporting a
house, garden, and outbuildings. The streets were
wide enough to turn a wagon. The center of the
village had the meetinghouse and (in the larger
settlements) the tithing office and the cooperative
store.

### Ecclesiastical organization

A village was a "ward," presided over by a bishop
(an unpaid lay leader, not the same as a Catholic
bishop). The ward conducted Sunday meetings (a
sacrament service in the morning, a Sunday school for
children, an evening meeting). The ward also
organized welfare, mutual aid, and cooperative
labor.

A region of wards was a "stake," presided over by a
stake president and twelve high councilors.

### Polygamy

Plural marriage was practiced by perhaps twenty to
thirty percent of Mormon families through the 1870s
and 1880s. The men who took plural wives were often
ecclesiastical leaders or men of substance. The
practice produced its own household forms: a man
might maintain separate households for separate
wives, or wives might share a household, depending
on circumstances.

The federal anti-polygamy laws produced "the
underground" — Mormon men evading federal marshals,
some imprisoned, some hiding in remote settlements,
some serving prison time. By the late 1880s the
pressure forced the church's accommodation.

### The Mormon character in fiction

The Mormon character has been written badly more often
than any other religious character in western fiction.
The dime-novel sinister Mormon (the polygamous
patriarch with his harem and his Danites) is a
Victorian fantasy. The honest Mormon character is a
believer in a community with distinct theology, a
specific economic organization, and complex
relationships with surrounding non-Mormon populations.

A few sources: Wallace Stegner, _Mormon Country_ and
_The Gathering of Zion_ (sympathetic but Gentile —
i.e., non-Mormon — observation); Levi Peterson, _The
Backslider_ (Mormon-written novel); Vardis Fisher's
_Children of God_ (Mormon-raised author). The
journals and personal letters of Mormon settlers are
extensive and available.

## 6. The Black church on the frontier

A Black congregation in a western town in the 1870s
was the community's most important institution. The
church organized funerals, weddings, schooling
(especially before public education was available
to Black children), mutual aid, and political life.

### The setting

A small town's Black church might be a single-room
frame building, with a small congregation. The
preacher might be local or visiting. Services were
extended (longer than white services), with
substantial congregational participation in singing
and shouting, and with the call-and-response
preaching tradition.

### The music

The spiritual was the dominant Black religious music
of the period. The 1871 tour of the Fisk Jubilee
Singers brought the spirituals to white American
attention. By the 1870s the spiritual was sung in
Black churches everywhere, including the western
ones. _Swing Low, Sweet Chariot. Steal Away. Go Down
Moses. Wade in the Water._ The music carried
specific theological and historical content
(deliverance, exile, the river, the journey).

### The preacher

The Black preacher of the period might be ordained
in the AME or AME Zion church, ordained Baptist, or
serving without formal ordination. The preaching
voice — the rhythmic, building, call-and-response
voice — has been documented in many sources. James
Weldon Johnson's _God's Trombones_ (1927) preserves
the form.

A scene in a western Black church on a Sunday morning
is a scene the canon has rarely included. Worth
writing.

## 7. The cemetery

Every western town had its cemetery. The cemetery
tells the town's history.

### The marker

A wooden marker (a board, a cross) for the poor and
the early dead. A stone marker (limestone, sandstone,
later granite) for those who could afford it. The
marker recorded name, dates, and often a brief
inscription (a Bible verse, a sentiment).

### The arrangement

Cemeteries were typically arranged by church and by
family. The Catholic cemetery was separate from the
Protestant. Within a church's ground, family plots
were maintained.

The "boot hill" of legend (the unconsecrated burial
ground for those who died with their boots on — by
violence, by suicide, in disrepute) was a real
informal practice. The unmarked or briefly marked
grave was the common end for the man who died alone
on the trail or in the saloon.

### The funeral

A Protestant funeral involved: the preacher's
service at the home or church, the procession to the
cemetery, the service at the graveside, the
lowering of the coffin (often a plain wood box made
locally), the filling of the grave by the mourners
(or by the gravedigger if hired). Hymns sung. The
preacher's words. The wake or the meal at the home
afterward.

A Catholic funeral involved: the Mass for the dead
in the church, the procession to the campo santo
behind the cross and the priest, the graveside
prayers, the burial. The forty days of mourning.

A Mormon funeral involved: the meetinghouse service,
the testimonies of the bishop and family, the
graveside prayer, the dedication of the grave.

The funeral scene is the western's recurring close.
The body in the box. The words said. The dirt
filling. The walk away from the grave.

## 8. The voice — sermon and prayer

See `references/speech-patterns.md` for the broader
period speech reference. The religious voice has
specific features.

### The preacher's voice

Scripturally saturated. Quotations from the King
James Version are interwoven with the preacher's own
words. The preacher's vocabulary is Biblical
(_brethren, sojourner, the Day of the Lord,
sanctification, the lost sheep, the prodigal son_).

A sermon passage on the page should be a fragment —
a few sentences from the climactic moment, not the
whole sermon. The reader's imagination supplies the
context.

Example fragment:

> _Brethren, the Word of God is sharper than any
> two-edged sword. It cuts to the quick. There is no
> man here this evening who has not felt the cut of
> it. There is no woman here who has not heard the
> still small voice in the wilderness of her heart.
> The Lord is calling you. He is calling you tonight._

### The prayer voice

Extemporaneous prayer in the Protestant tradition is
addressed directly to God, in second person, with
specific requests, confessions, and thanksgivings. A
prayer at the table before a meal might be brief
(_Heavenly Father, we thank thee for this food and
ask thy blessing upon it. Amen._). A prayer at a
deathbed might be extended.

The Catholic prayer is liturgical: the Our Father,
the Hail Mary, the rosary's mysteries, the prayers
of the Mass. Often in Latin in the period (the
ordinary of the Mass, the Pater Noster, the Ave
Maria).

### The hymn

The western Protestant church sang. Common 1870s
hymns: _Amazing Grace, Rock of Ages, Just As I Am,
What a Friend We Have in Jesus, Nearer My God to
Thee, How Firm a Foundation, Shall We Gather at the
River._ A scene with hymn singing should name the
hymn (or quote a recognizable line) so the reader who
knows it hears it.

Black congregations sang the spirituals listed
earlier and many others. Catholic congregations sang
the Latin hymns of the church year (_Tantum Ergo,
Adoro Te Devote, the Marian antiphons by season_)
and the Spanish folk hymns (_alabados_) in
Hispanic communities.

## 9. A worked scene — the funeral

> The grave was a four-foot trench in the dry ground
> at the edge of the cemetery. Two men had dug it
> through the morning and the dirt stood in two piles
> beside it. The wind moved the piles slightly.
>
> Reverend Heaston stood at the head of the grave with
> his Bible open. The mourners stood around the
> grave: the widow Eliza Patten in her mother-in-law's
> black bonnet, her two children to either side, and a
> dozen people from the town behind them. Patten's
> partner Isaac Coberly stood with his hat in his
> hands.
>
> _The Lord is my shepherd,_ Heaston said. _I shall
> not want._
>
> The wind took some of the words. The widow's bonnet
> moved. The older child looked at the box and then
> looked at her mother and then looked at the box.
>
> _He maketh me to lie down in green pastures,_
> Heaston said. _He leadeth me beside the still
> waters._
>
> The town had no green pastures and no still waters.
> The coffin was pine and had been built by Coberly
> in the morning. Patten was forty-one years old. He
> had died of a kick from a mule.
>
> Heaston closed the Bible.
>
> _We commit our brother to the ground,_ he said. _Earth
> to earth. Ashes to ashes. Dust to dust. In the sure
> and certain hope of the resurrection unto eternal
> life._
>
> The two men who had dug the grave lowered the box
> into it on ropes. Coberly stepped forward and threw
> the first handful of dirt onto the lid. The widow
> stepped forward and threw the second.
>
> Then the two men with the shovels filled the grave
> while the others stood and watched. The sound of the
> dirt on the lid lasted as long as it lasted. When
> the trench was full one of the men packed it down
> with the back of the shovel.
>
> Heaston put on his hat and walked over to the widow
> and said _The supper at the church is ready when
> you are._

The scene's specifics — the four-foot trench, the
piles of dirt moving in the wind, the Twenty-third
Psalm against the dry country, the mule's kick that
killed Patten, the pine box made by the partner, the
widow's borrowed bonnet, the sound of the dirt on
the lid — render the funeral. The scripture is the
public language; the country is the contradicting
witness. Both are present in the prose.

## 10. Refusing the false moves

### The hypocrite preacher

The preacher who is secretly a fraud (a swindler, a
seducer, a coward in disguise) is the western's
oldest cheap religious move. Real frauds existed,
but the move is so overused it has lost its force.
A more interesting religious character is a
believer whose life is failing in spite of his faith
or because of it.

### The death-bed conversion

The dying outlaw who finds Jesus at the last moment.
A staple of Victorian sentimental fiction. Refuse it
unless you can render it as the difficult, ambiguous,
not-quite-believable moment it usually was.

### The deus ex machina prayer

The character prays and the prayer is answered by an
intervention that solves the plot problem. Refuse.
Render prayers that are not answered, or answered in
ways the character did not expect or want.

### The reduction of religion to social control

The view that religion was just the rich man's
mechanism for keeping the poor man docile. Some of
this happened. But the reduction misses what
religion actually was for the believer: a way of
making sense of suffering, a community, a discipline,
a relationship with what the believer experienced as
real. Render the experience, not just the function.

### The pious atheist narrator

The narrator who treats all religious characters as
deluded, all religious experience as merely
psychological, all religious institutions as merely
political. This is the modern educated voice
projected backward. In the 1870s most people, including
most narrators worth reading, took religious
experience seriously even when they did not share
it. Calibrate accordingly.

## 11. The cheat-sheet

- **Pick the tradition specifically.** Methodist
  circuit rider, Baptist revivalist, Presbyterian
  town minister, Catholic priest, Mormon bishop,
  Black AME preacher. Each operates by different
  procedures and speaks in a different voice.
- **The church is a building, an institution, and a
  community.** Render all three.
- **The preacher's voice is scripturally saturated.**
  Quote the King James Version.
- **The hymn is the music.** Name it.
- **The cemetery tells the town's history.**
- **The conversion experience is felt.** Render the
  feeling, not just the doctrine.
- **The Catholic Southwest is a different world.**
  The mission church, the priest, the santos, the
  Penitentes.
- **The Mormon settlement is a sovereign religious
  community.** Render its distinct organization.
- **The Black church carried west with Black
  migrants.** The spirituals, the call-and-response
  preaching, the AME and Baptist congregations.
- **Refuse the hypocrite preacher and the death-bed
  conversion as default moves.**

## 12. The rule the others serve

The 1870s West was a religious country. The character
who never thinks about God, never attends a service,
never quotes a Bible verse, never crosses himself, is
a modern character in a costume. The character of the
period thought about salvation, sin, and providence
in some specific tradition's vocabulary, even when
he was a backslider or a doubter or a cheerful
infidel.

Render the religious world the character moves in.
Render his relationship to it — believing, doubting,
fleeing, returning. The novel that gets religion
right gets the period right. The novel that ignores
religion is writing about a West that did not exist.
