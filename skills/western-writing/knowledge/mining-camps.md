# Mining Camps — The Hardrock World Underground and Above

This file is the knowledge module for hardrock mining
camps of the 1870s–1880s American West. It complements
`knowledge/work-and-trades.md` §2 (which covers the
mechanics of mining briefly) and `knowledge/food-culture.md`
(the boarding house table). For the camp as a social and
economic setting, cross-reference `knowledge/towns-economy-law.md`
and `knowledge/peoples-and-conflict.md` (the ethnic
composition of mine labor). For writing the underground
scene procedurally, load this file alongside
`knowledge/competence-procedures.md`.

The mine is one of the few western settings where the work
is invisible. The cattle drive happens under the sky. The
mine happens in the dark, in noise, in rock dust, in a
space the reader has never been. The writer who gets this
wrong puts too much light in and not enough weight.

---

## 1. What kind of mine

The 1870s West had two kinds of hard-rock mining and one
kind of soft:

**Hardrock gold and silver** is what the fiction almost
always means. An underground mine following a vein of ore
in consolidated rock — granite, quartzite, porphyry.
Nevada (Comstock), Colorado (Leadville, Cripple Creek),
Arizona (Tombstone, Globe), Idaho (Coeur d'Alene), Montana
(Butte) are the canonical districts.

**Placer gold** is worked at the surface — streams and
gravels. The prospector's world. Covered in
`knowledge/work-and-trades.md` §2a.

**Coal** is a different world — underground, shaft-based,
but in softer sedimentary rock, often in company towns
with a different ethnic and political character. Coal does
not appear in western fiction nearly as often as silver
and gold.

This file covers the hardrock mine.

## 2. The underground — what it actually looks like

### The shaft

The mine enters the mountain by a **shaft**: a vertical
opening, rectangular (six feet by eight feet in most
operations), lined with square-set timber from the top
down. The shaft drops level by level — the 500-foot level,
the 700-foot level, the 1000-foot level. Each level is a
horizontal floor in the mine. The men ride the cage — an
iron platform on a wire rope — up and down the shaft.
The hoist engineer at the surface controls the cage.

At depth: the air is hot. Below 500 feet in many Comstock
mines the temperature reached 120°F. The Comstock had to
pipe ice water underground. In most 1870s mines the deep
levels were simply hot, wet, and dark.

### Drifts and crosscuts

From each level, **drifts** run horizontally along the
vein. A drift is a tunnel with a roof and two walls and
a floor, square-set with timber. Seven feet high, six
feet wide in a well-run operation. The floor has a pair
of iron rails. The **mucking car** — a steel tub holding
half a ton of broken ore — runs on those rails. A man
pushes it to the ore chute at the shaft.

**Crosscuts** run perpendicular to the drift, cutting
across rock to reach a new vein or to connect workings.
They are driven blind — you do not know if the vein is
there until the drill and powder tell you.

### Stopes

A **stope** is the open working space left behind as ore
is removed. Where the drift follows the vein horizontally,
the stope opens vertically — the ore taken out above and
below the drift line. A stope might be twenty feet high
and sixty feet long. Square-set timbers hold it open.
Without the timbers the stope collapses.

### Raises and winzes

A **raise** is a vertical opening driven upward from a
lower level toward an upper one. A **winze** is driven
downward. They are connection shafts, ventilation paths,
and ore-pass chutes. A raise is driven by a man lying on
his back on a plank, drilling upward into the rock above
his face. The dust falls into his eyes.

### Square-set timbering

Deidesheimer's square-set system (1860, Comstock) was the
engineering solution that made deep hardrock mining viable.
The timbers are pre-cut to standard lengths and fit
together at right angles like a three-dimensional grid —
each set interlocking with its neighbors above, below, and
to the sides. A stope can be held open at any width. The
system travels west with every mine that opens after 1862.
The smell of wet pine and fresh-cut Douglas fir is the
smell of a working mine — the timberman's crew always
one level behind the miners, setting new sets as the ore
comes out.

## 3. The shift — a miner's eight hours

### First call

The day shift comes on at six in the morning. The men
change in the **change house** — a low building near the
shaft collar where they swap their street clothes for
their working clothes. The working clothes are wool in
winter (wool dries from the inside out), duck canvas in
summer. The boots are heavy leather, nailed with hobnails
for traction on wet timber. Each man carries his own
candle holder — a bent spike that can be jammed into a
timber crack — and a candle, or in better-equipped mines
by the late 1870s, a carbide lamp on his hat. The carbide
lamp makes a white acetylene flame and a soft hissing
sound and smells of a struck match.

### Going down

The men crowd the cage — six men, plus the shift boss who
rides with them to the working level. The cage drops fast
enough to lift the stomach. The shaft walls flicker past
the candle's range. At depth the air changes: warmer,
damper, carrying the smell of spent powder from last
night's shift and the underlying rock smell — iron, wet
clay, sometimes sulfur.

### The drill work

Most of the shift is drilling. The miners work in pairs:
one man holds the **drill steel** — a tapered iron rod —
against the rock face and turns it a quarter revolution
after each blow. The other man swings the **sledge** —
a four-pound hammer in the single-jack, an eight-pounder
in the double-jack. The rhythm is the mine's heartbeat:
strike, turn, strike, turn. The steel goes in an inch
at a time. A twelve-inch hole takes twenty minutes. The
noise in the drift — the ringing of the steel, the bass
thud of the sledge on the striker's end — carries through
the rock.

**Single-jack** work is a man alone: small hammer, lighter
steel, slower. Used in tight spaces, in raises where a
second man cannot stand.

**Double-jack** work requires trust. If the striker misses
the steel, the holder's hands take the blow. The men who
have worked together for years do not look at each other
during the strike. They listen for the rhythm.

By the 1870s, mechanical drills powered by compressed air
are appearing in the larger mines. The men call them
**widow makers** because the water injection that dampens
the dust often ran dry and the dry-drill dust went into
the lungs. A man on a widow maker in an unventilated stope
was breathing his future silicosis with every breath.

### Loading and firing

When the holes are drilled — a face receives eight to
twelve holes in a pattern the shift boss has marked — the
**powder man** loads them. Black powder until the early
1870s; dynamite after 1867, increasingly common in the
West through the 1870s. The powder man tamps the charge,
cuts the fuse, and begins at the holes farthest from the
exit. Each fuse is timed so the shots fire in sequence —
the cut holes in the center first, the relievers next,
the perimeter holes last. The sequence breaks the rock
efficiently. A bad sequence wastes powder or leaves a
wall standing.

The shift boss counts the men out of the drift. The
powder man lights the fuses. He counts:

> One fuse burning. Two fuses burning. Out. Run.

The rule is never to run before all fuses are lit. The
rule is never to walk. Between the two is a quarter of
the dead men in any mine.

The shots go off in sequence: a series of flat concussions
felt in the chest before they are heard, the rock breaking
in waves. Dust and smoke fill the drift. The men wait
outside the entry on the level until the smoke clears —
twenty minutes at minimum, longer in poor ventilation.
Then the muckers go in.

### Mucking

The **muckers** are the bottom of the mine's hierarchy
and the hardest-worked men in it. After a blast, the
face is a pile of broken rock — **muck** — extending
back ten or fifteen feet. The mucker loads the muck into
the car by hand with a square-faced iron shovel. The
car holds half a ton. He pushes it to the ore chute and
tips it. He goes back and does it again. For eight hours.

The mucker eats his lunch underground, sitting on a rock
with the candle between his knees. He does not usually
speak to the men around him. He has not enough to say.

### End of shift

The whistle at the shaft. The men ride the cage up.
Daylight again. The change house. The walk to the boarding
house or the saloon. The miner's day is either underground
or sleeping off underground. There is not much middle.

## 4. The workers — a mine's hierarchy

A working mine of any size has a distinct chain:

- **Superintendent.** The manager of the whole operation.
  Often an engineer (the Comstock and later mines were
  managed by graduates of the Columbia School of Mines
  or the Freiberg Mining Academy in Saxony). The
  superintendent answers to the owners or the board.

- **Shift boss.** The foreman underground. Assigns men to
  faces, decides which drifts to advance and which to hold,
  knows the mine's geology better than the superintendent.
  A good shift boss is irreplaceable. He is usually the
  highest-paid hourly worker in the mine.

- **Miner** (first-class miner, or just "miner"). The
  skilled man who drills, fires, and reads rock. Paid
  $3–$4 a day in the 1870s. He is not a laborer; he is a
  tradesman. He knows the difference between good ground
  and bad, can read a vein's dip and strike, knows when
  the timbers are talking.

- **Powder man.** Sometimes a specialized role, sometimes
  the senior miner on a face. The man who handles the
  dynamite is the man the crew does not argue with.

- **Timberman.** The man who sets the square-set. As
  skilled as the miner in his way — he reads the rock's
  pressure and knows where to put the timber so it does
  not move. The miner and the timberman work in sequence:
  the miner opens the ground, the timberman holds it open.

- **Mucker.** The unskilled laborer at the face. Often
  a recent immigrant: Cornish, Irish, Italian, Croatian,
  Mexican. The job that kills the back before the lungs
  go. A mucker who lasts three years becomes a miner.
  Most do not last three years.

- **Hoist man.** The engineer who operates the cage.
  Never seen underground by anyone who matters. He
  controls the lives of every man in the mine at the
  moment of the shift change and at every emergency.
  A good hoist man knows from the feel of the rope
  whether the cage is loaded or empty, whether it is
  stopping on a timber or a man.

- **Blacksmith.** The mine's own smith, who resharpens
  the drill steels. He works at the surface, at a
  coal-fired forge, sharpening steel all shift. One
  blacksmith can keep four or five drilling teams
  supplied. A mine that runs out of sharp steels stops.

## 5. Above ground — the camp

### The shaft collar and headframe

The surface above a mine is a complex of industrial
buildings. The **headframe** — also called the gallows
frame — stands over the shaft, a wooden A-frame structure
carrying the sheave wheel that the hoist rope runs over.
It is the mine's emblem. A camp with a headframe in
operation is a camp with money.

Beside the headframe: the **hoist house**, containing
the steam engine and hoist drum. The steam engine burns
wood or coal. It runs all day. The sound of the camp
above ground is the sound of the steam engine.

### The ore dump and mill

The ore from the cage goes to the **ore dump** — a pile
of broken rock beside the shaft. From the dump it is
sorted by grade. High-grade ore goes to the **stamp mill**:
a set of iron stamps driven by a cam, dropping onto the
ore in iron mortars, crushing it to sand. The mercury
amalgam process extracts the gold or silver from the
sand. The stamp mill noise is the other sound of a
mining camp: a constant, rhythmic, low-frequency thunder
that carries two miles. A man who has worked near a stamp
mill for a year is hard of hearing.

### The change house

The change house has two rooms: one for the men going down
(their street clothes hang on pegs), one for the men
coming up (wet, rock-dusted, sometimes bloody). The
change house smells of wet wool, tallow candle smoke,
and spent carbide.

### Camp layout

A mining camp is not planned. It grows around the shaft
collar. The boarding house closest to the shaft. The
company office beside the hoist house. The assay office
(a small room with a furnace and a scale) adjacent to the
ore dump. The superintendent's house on the highest
ground in reach, overlooking the works. The saloon
opposite the boarding house.

The camp's streets — when it has streets — are unpaved,
churned to mud by the ore wagons, white with rock dust
when dry. The dust is everywhere: on the grass that
grows between buildings, on the horses in the stable,
on the laundry that women hang in the camp's edges.

## 6. The boarding house

The miner's lodging. Not a hotel — a boarding house runs
on contract. The miner pays a fixed weekly rate (in the
1870s, $5–$7 a week for room and board) which the
company sometimes deducts directly from wages. The
boarding house keeper is often a widow or a woman whose
husband works in the mine.

A boarding house at a large camp has thirty to sixty men.
Two sittings at the long table — day shift at six in the
evening, night shift at six in the morning. The food is
plain: beef stew or salt pork, beans, bread, coffee.
The Cornish miners brought their own tradition: the
**pasty** — a half-moon of pastry filled with beef,
potato, and turnip, crimped so a miner can hold it
by the crust with his rock-dusted hands and not
contaminate the food.

See `knowledge/food-culture.md` for the boarding house
table in detail.

The room: six to a room, sometimes more in a boom camp.
Double bunks. A single lamp. The man who works nights
sleeps through the stamp mill's thunder because there is
nothing else to do.

## 7. The company store

In some mining operations — particularly coal, but in
hardrock mines in remote locations — the company owned
the store. The miners bought their goods with scrip
redeemable only at the company store. This system
(called the **truck system** in British usage) kept
the workforce in debt. A man who owed the company store
$40 did not leave the camp.

In the 1870s hardrock districts the company store was
more common in isolated camps; in Leadville or Butte,
where independent merchants competed, the miners had
options. The company store is not a universal of the
hardrock west, but it is a realistic feature of the
more remote operations.

## 8. The Cornish question

The Cornish miners — men from Cornwall, England's own
tin and copper mining country — were the dominant
skilled labor in the American hardrock mines of the
1860s–1880s. They came because Cornwall's own mines
were failing. They went everywhere silver and gold
was found. They ran the shift bosses. They trained the
muckers in drilling.

The Cornish were called **Cousin Jacks** — the standard
explanation is that a Cornishman asked to recommend
someone for a job always had a cousin named Jack.

A Cornish miner speaks with a rounded southwestern
English accent (not the Irish accent often assumed),
uses specific Cornish mining vocabulary (a **kibble**
for a bucket, a **skip** for the ore car, **pare**
for the crew assigned to a face), and has opinions
about the right way to timber that he will share with
anyone who stands still long enough.

Other nationalities at the mines:

- **Irish** — muckers primarily, some skilled men.
- **Mexican and Mexican-American** — common in
  Arizona and New Mexico mines. Often did the most
  dangerous surface work.
- **Scandinavian** — Norwegian and Swedish immigrants
  in the northern districts (Montana, Idaho, Colorado).
- **Chinese** — largely excluded from underground work
  by union pressure; some worked in the mills and on
  surface operations.

## 9. Labor and capital — the union and the Pinkertons

### The Miners' Unions

By the mid-1870s the miners in the major districts were
organized. The **Miners' Union** (formalized as the
Western Federation of Miners in 1893, but functioning
informally in the major camps from the early 1870s)
fought for the eight-hour day, the $3–$4 daily wage,
and safety conditions. The union controlled who worked.
A non-union man (a **scab**, a **blackleg** in Cornish
usage) could not get work at a union camp.

The union's power was simple: without skilled miners,
the mine did not produce. The mine owners knew this and
resented it with the particular resentment of men who
owned everything except the knowledge in other men's
hands.

### The Pinkertons

The Pinkerton National Detective Agency, founded 1850,
provided industrial espionage and private strikebreaking
on contract. At a mine facing a strike or a suspected
union organizing drive, the superintendent wrote to
Chicago. The Pinkerton sent a man undercover — hired
as a mucker, worked at the face, reported to the
superintendent what the men said in the dark.

The Pinkertons' undercover men wore the same clothes
and ate the same food and listened in the boarding
house at night. They were good at the work. They were
also, by the logic of their work, required to be
trusted by men they were preparing to betray.

The mining wars of the 1890s (Coeur d'Alene, 1892;
Cripple Creek, 1894) were the climax of this conflict.
For the 1870s fiction writer the tension is already in
place: every large camp has a union, every mine owner
has considered a Pinkerton, and the miner who is too
curious about his neighbors' politics is noticed.

## 10. The hazards

A mine in the 1870s killed men reliably. The methods:

**Cave-in.** A timber that failed, a stope that had been
mined too wide, a section of rock that moved without
warning. The cave might bury a single man or collapse a
level. Most bodies were recovered. Some were not. A
section of the Comstock known as the Dead Zone was
sealed off in 1869 after a cave trapped eleven men. The
bodies stayed.

**Fire.** The mine's worst enemy in a timbered operation.
A candle knocked from a timber crack, a carbide lamp
dropped into wood shavings, a fuse left burning. Once
the square-set timber caught, the fire spread through
the mine's ventilation like a chimney. The men below
the fire were in a chimney. The Yellow Jacket fire at
the Comstock in 1869 killed thirty-seven men. No fire
doors were standard in 1870s mines. In fiction, the mine
fire is the catastrophe from which there is no waking up.

**Flooding.** Every mine at depth is wet. The shallower
levels are drained by pumps. The pumps stop when the
steam engine stops. A flooded lower level means pumping
for weeks before it can be worked again. A man who falls
into a flooded sump does not come up. The sump is black
water in the dark at the bottom of everything.

**Gas.** Carbon monoxide and hydrogen sulfide accumulate
in dead-end drifts and raises. A canary carried by the
shift boss to a worked-out face was not a metaphor when
the practice began — it was safety equipment. The canary
died first. The shift boss then had thirty seconds to
turn around.

**Silicosis.** The slow killer. Rock dust into the
lungs, deposited over years, forming scar tissue that
replaced lung. The dry-drill widow maker was its agent.
A miner who had been underground twenty years often
could not climb the stairs from the boarding house
dining room to his bunk without stopping to breathe.
He did not usually say what he had.

## 11. Writing the mine — specific words and sense

### Vocabulary

- **Drift**: horizontal tunnel
- **Crosscut**: tunnel perpendicular to drift, cutting
  across to reach a vein
- **Stope**: open working space above/below the drift
- **Raise**: vertical opening driven upward
- **Winze**: vertical opening driven downward
- **Sump**: the lowest point in the mine, where water
  collects
- **Collar**: the surface opening of the shaft
- **Square-set**: the interlocking timber frame
- **Muck**: broken ore and waste rock after a blast
- **Mucking car** or **car**: the steel ore tub on rails
- **Skip**: another term for the ore car
- **Cage**: the elevator in the shaft
- **Kibble**: a bucket used to lift rock in shaft-sinking
  before a cage is installed
- **Single-jack**: one man, small hammer, alone at the face
- **Double-jack**: two men, large sledge, one holds the steel
- **Widow maker**: a mechanical drill (compressed-air drill)
- **Fuse**: the burning cord that fires the charge
- **Hangfire**: a fuse that does not fire on schedule;
  the most dangerous thing underground
- **Blackleg / scab**: a non-union worker
- **Cousin Jack**: a Cornish miner

### Sounds

Underground is not silent. It is:

- the ring of steel on steel when a double-jack pair is
  drilling — a sustained metallic note that echoes down
  the drift and back
- the crack and rumble of a blast behind the sealed drift
  door — felt in the chest before it reaches the ears
- the drip of water on rock, irregular, constant
- the creak of the square-set timber under pressure — the
  mine is always moving, always settling, always talking
  in the language of wood under load
- after a big blast, the absolute silence before the dust
  arrives — a second of nothing, then the roar

### Smells

- spent black powder or dynamite: acrid, sulfurous, long-
  lasting, clings to wool
- carbide lamp: sharp, like a struck match
- wet timber: pine sap and rot at once
- the miner's sweat: salt and iron
- hot rock: dry, mineral, faintly metallic at depth

### Light

A candle gives three to four feet of usable light. A
carbide lamp gives eight to ten feet. Beyond that is
the dark. The dark is total. Darker than any dark above
ground because there is no sky for the eyes to reach
toward. The man who drops his candle underground goes
still and does not move until he finds it.

The worked face catches the candle's light in the facets
of fresh-broken rock — quartz crystals, flecks of native
silver, the dull yellow of iron pyrite that fools no one
who has been underground a week. The ore the company wants
is often undistinguished: gray, heavy, sulfidic, nothing
that looks like wealth.

## 12. Before / after — writing the mine scene

### The tourist description (wrong)

> The mine was a dark and dangerous place. The miners
> worked hard in difficult conditions, drilling and
> blasting their way through the rock in the eternal
> search for silver. It was hot and loud and dangerous.
> The men who worked there were brave.

This is wrong for all the reasons in `references/anti-ai-
patterns.md`. It names no specific. It uses no procedural
verb. It editorializes (brave). It tells the reader what
to think.

### The competence register (right)

> Dolan set his candle in the crack above the face and
> picked up the steel. Viereck was already behind him
> with the eight-pound hammer and did not speak. Dolan
> put the point in the mark he had made last shift, a
> quarter turn past level, and held. Viereck struck. The
> ring of it went down the drift and came back wrong —
> too high, the rock hollow somewhere behind. Dolan
> turned the steel and Viereck struck again and this time
> the sound was right.
>
> The shift boss came by at the half-hour. He looked at
> the mark on the face and did not say anything. He moved
> on down the drift to the next pair.

The passage uses specific nouns (drift, face, steel,
eight-pound hammer). The verbs are transitive and
precise. The character knows what "too high" and "too
hollow" mean in the rock's sound; the reader does not
need to know — the character knowing is enough. No one
is brave. No one is admired. The work proceeds.

## 13. The rule the others serve

The mine is work. It is skilled work that kills the
worker's body over years, that can kill him in an instant,
that takes place in total dark at depth in conditions
no human body was designed for. The man who goes down
the shaft at six in the morning and comes up at two in
the afternoon has done something the reader has never
done and cannot imagine. The writer's job is to put the
reader underground in a body that knows the work.

Do not explain the work. Do not admire it from outside.
Set the candle in the crack. Pick up the steel. Hold.
