# Material Culture — Iron, Leather, Cloth

This file is the gear reference. Every object below should be
written with weight, smell, sound, cost, failure mode, and use.
A western that names a Colt without knowing it is single-action
loses the reader who knows.

---

## 1. Firearms

### a. Revolvers

**Colt 1851 Navy.** .36 caliber percussion (cap-and-ball).
Six-shot. Octagonal barrel, brass trigger guard. Carried by
Confederate cavalry, by Wild Bill Hickok (his lifelong choice),
by many veterans into the 1880s. Light, accurate, slow to
reload. Reload took 30–60 seconds in good light.

**Colt 1860 Army.** .44 caliber percussion. Six-shot. Round
barrel, larger frame than the Navy. The standard Union cavalry
sidearm. Common in the West through the 1870s carried over
from the war. Many were converted to cartridge.

**Remington 1858 New Model Army.** .44 caliber percussion.
Six-shot. Solid top-strap frame (stronger than the Colt), used
by Union cavalry and many veterans. The cylinder could be
swapped — a man with a spare loaded cylinder could reload in
under 30 seconds.

**Colt Single Action Army (Peacemaker, Frontier Six-Shooter).**
Introduced 1873. .45 Colt and later .44-40. Six-shot
cartridge revolver. The defining sidearm of the West from 1873
forward. Single-action: hammer cocked manually for each shot.
Loaded one round at a time through a loading gate. Empty cases
ejected one at a time through the same gate with a rod. About
$17 retail in 1873. About 2 lb 3 oz unloaded with a 7.5"
barrel. Barrel lengths: 4.75" (the _Quickdraw_), 5.5" (the
_Civilian_), 7.5" (the _Cavalry_).

**Smith & Wesson Model 3 (Schofield, Russian).** .44 American or
.44 Russian or .45 Schofield. Top-break design — barrel hinged
forward, ejecting all six empty cases at once. Faster to reload
than the Colt. Used by the U.S. Army (Schofield variant). Carried
by Jesse James, John Wesley Hardin (briefly), Frank James, Wyatt
Earp's brothers.

**Derringer.** A small, large-bore, one- or two-shot pocket
pistol. Remington Double Derringer in .41 rimfire was the
common model. Carried by gamblers, women, men in suits. Lethal
at the table; useless past ten feet.

### b. Rifles

**Sharps Model 1874.** .45-70, .50-70, .50-90 etc. Single-shot
breech-loading falling block. The buffalo rifle. Accurate at
500 yards in the hands of a hunter, lethal at 1,000. Heavy —
10 to 12 pounds. Used to exterminate the southern buffalo herd
1872–1874 and the northern herd 1876–1883.

**Henry Rifle (Model 1860).** .44 rimfire. Lever-action, 16-round
tube magazine under the barrel. _That damned Yankee rifle you
load on Sunday and shoot all week._ Carried by some Union
cavalry, by Confederate raiders who captured them, by westerners
through the 1870s.

**Winchester Model 1866 (Yellow Boy).** Brass-frame
lever-action, .44 rimfire. The Henry refined.

**Winchester Model 1873.** Iron then steel frame. Lever-action.
Magazine 12 to 15 rounds. Initially .44-40 — a cartridge
also chambered in the Colt SAA, so a man could carry one
ammunition for both rifle and pistol. Later .38-40 and
.32-20.

**Winchester Model 1876.** Bigger frame for bigger cartridges
(.45-75, .50-95). The buffalo-hunter's lever gun.

**Spencer Repeating Rifle / Carbine.** Used in the Civil War
and after. Tube magazine in the buttstock, seven .56-50 rimfire
rounds. Carried by Union cavalry, then by westerners. Out of
production after 1869 but still in use through the 1880s.

**Springfield Model 1873 (Trapdoor).** .45-70. Single-shot
breech-loading. Standard U.S. Army infantry and cavalry rifle
through the 1880s. Carried by Custer's troopers at the Little
Bighorn.

**Hawken rifle.** .50-cal percussion plains rifle. Pre-Civil-War
mountain man's gun. Still in use by older trappers and hunters
into the 1870s.

### c. Shotguns

**Coach gun.** Double-barreled 10- or 12-gauge with short barrels
(typically 18–20"). Carried by Wells Fargo guards on the box of
a stagecoach. Lethal at twenty feet. _Riding shotgun_ comes
from this.

**Long-barrel double 12-gauge.** Hunting and farm gun. Brass
shells, paper shells later, loaded with shot or with buckshot
("OO buck") for serious work.

### d. The realities of period firearms

- **Reloading is slow.** A Single Action Army takes about 30
  seconds to fully reload by a competent man. A man in a
  fight reloads behind cover or not at all. Most fights used
  one cylinder.
- **Range is short.** A pistol fight at 30 feet is a long
  pistol fight. Hits past 50 feet are unusual. The TV-western
  thirty-yard pistol shootout did not happen.
- **Black powder smoke is thick.** A man fires three rounds in
  a saloon and the saloon is fogged. He cannot see his
  opponent or his own front sight after the second shot.
- **Misfires are common.** Cap-and-ball especially — a damp
  cap, a fouled nipple, a cracked nipple. A pistol that has
  ridden in a holster through a rainstorm may not fire when
  needed.
- **Accidental discharges are common.** A Single Action Army
  loaded with six rounds will fire if dropped (the firing pin
  rests on the primer). Knowledgeable men carried five and
  rested the hammer on an empty chamber. A loaded pistol left
  in a saddlebag could go off when the horse fell.
- **The gun fight is rare.** Most western towns had one or two
  killings a year. Dodge City in its peak year had five. The
  Wild West of constant shootouts is myth.
- **The body of a man shot does not fly back.** Hollywood
  rule. A man shot folds, sits, drops, kneels, sometimes
  stands a moment.

### e. The fast draw

Mostly myth. The classic stand-and-draw duel happened. It was
not common. Most killings were:

- An ambush.
- A shooting in the back.
- A close-quarters fight starting with words and a grab for the
  pistol.
- A drunken accident.
- A killing of a man already disarmed.

Bat Masterson said most shootings were over before either man's
gun cleared the holster fully — the man who started shooting
first usually won. Wyatt Earp said the same. The myth of the
choreographed quick-draw came after 1900.

## 2. Edged weapons

- **Bowie knife.** Heavy, single-edged, 9–12" blade with a
  clip point. Designed for fighting and for general work.
  Worn on the belt by many westerners.
- **Sheath knife.** Smaller working knife, 5–8" blade. Skinning,
  butchering, eating, whittling.
- **Jackknife.** Folding knife in the pocket. Tobacco-cutting,
  whittling, cleaning fingernails, opening a can.
- **Hatchet / belt axe.** Standard camp tool.
- **Cavalry saber.** Carried by some mounted units through the
  Civil War, ceremonial after that. The Plains tribes used the
  saber as a trade item. Largely absent from the cattle frontier.

## 3. Saddles and tack

### a. Saddle anatomy

- **Tree.** The wooden frame inside, covered in rawhide.
- **Cantle.** The raised back.
- **Horn.** The pommel projection. Used for dallying the rope.
- **Skirts.** The leather that lies against the horse.
- **Stirrups.** Wood with leather covers (tapaderos in the
  Spanish-influenced Southwest), or open iron later.
- **Cinch (cincha).** Mohair or hair girth pulled tight under
  the horse's belly.
- **Latigo.** The leather strap that runs through the cinch
  and is tied off to the saddle ring.
- **Conchos.** Decorative metal disks. Saddle strings tied
  through them held tools and gear.

### b. Saddle types

- **California / Spanish saddle.** Heavy, ornate, used in the
  cattle country of the Pacific coast.
- **Texas / Plains saddle.** Lighter, plainer, working tool.
  The standard cowboy saddle.
- **McClellan saddle.** U.S. Cavalry saddle. Skeletal frame,
  hard on the rider over many miles, easier on the horse than
  most civilian saddles.

### c. Bridles and bits

- **Hackamore.** Bitless bridle, used to start young horses.
- **Bosal.** A braided rawhide noseband.
- **Snaffle bit.** Mild bit, jointed mouthpiece. For working
  young horses or finishing them.
- **Curb bit / spade bit.** Stronger bits with a chin chain.
  Used on finished horses and required a light hand. A heavy
  hand with a spade bit ruined a horse.
- **Ring snaffle, half-breed, full-spade.** Variations.

### d. Other tack

- **Saddle blanket / Navajo blanket.** Wool, often woven by Diné
  weavers and traded throughout the Southwest. Folded in two or
  four under the saddle.
- **Hobbles.** Leather straps that limited a horse's stride;
  put on at night to keep the horse near camp.
- **Picket pin and rope.** For a night horse staked near the
  bedroll.
- **Quirt.** A short braided whip on the wrist.
- **Spurs.** With rowels, sometimes with jingle bobs that
  jingled as the man walked.

## 4. Clothing — by class and region

### a. Working cowhand

See `cowboy-life.md` for the details of dress.

### b. Townsman

- Sack suit (rough-woven wool). Black or dark gray.
- Vest with watch chain.
- White shirt with collar (separate, attached daily).
- Bowler hat or top hat for the proper sort.
- Brogan boots or low shoes.
- Pocket watch on a chain across the vest.

### c. Gambler

- Frock coat (long, fitted at the waist).
- Brocade or silk vest.
- Brocade tie or cravat.
- Diamond stickpin.
- Derringer in a vest pocket or under the lapel.
- Soft boots that did not creak.

### d. Lawman

- Coat or duster, depending on weather.
- Vest with badge pinned to it (sheriff, deputy, marshal).
- Pistol on the right hip in a slim holster.
- Sometimes a coach gun under the arm.

### e. Soldier

- Blue wool coat (Union surplus) or U.S. Cavalry blue with
  yellow piping (cavalry).
- Forage cap or campaign hat (the wide-brim of the Indian
  Wars).
- Brass-buttoned blouse.
- Sky-blue trousers with yellow stripe down the seam (cavalry).
- Boots, knee-length.
- Saber knot, cartridge belt, gauntlet gloves (mounted).

### f. Mexican working man

- White cotton shirt and trousers (peón class).
- Serape (a wool blanket-cloak).
- Sandals or boots.
- Sombrero (the wide brim of working sun-country, not the
  high-peaked tourist version).
- For the rancho owner or vaquero of standing: short jacket,
  flared trousers, embroidery, silver conchos on the saddle.

### g. Native dress

Variable by nation, season, and ceremony. By the 1870s much
Native dress incorporated Anglo trade goods (calico shirts,
wool blankets, brass buttons, glass beads) alongside or
replacing traditional buckskin. A Cheyenne man on a reservation
in 1880 might wear blue Army-surplus trousers, a calico shirt,
a beaded belt, moccasins, and a wool blanket over his shoulders.
Detail by nation requires research; do not generalize.

### h. Women's dress

- Working homestead woman: linsey-woolsey or calico dress,
  apron, sunbonnet outdoors, brogan shoes, stockings, hair
  pinned up.
- Town woman of the middle class: corseted dress, bustle (in
  fashion through the 1880s), petticoats, button-up shoes,
  hat with feathers.
- Working girl on the line: cheap dress imitating fashion,
  short of skirt by current standards (showing the ankle and
  the calf above the boot), corset, often no hat indoors.

## 5. Tools

### a. The cowhand's tools beyond the gun

- Branding iron. Long iron rod with the brand's shape on the
  end. Heated red in a brush fire at branding.
- Wire stretchers (after 1875).
- Fence pliers.
- Shovel and pickaxe (for water-hole digging).
- Axe (for fence-post and firewood).
- Block and tackle (for pulling a cow out of a bog).

### b. The trade tools

- Smith: anvil, hammer, tongs, bellows, files, vises, the
  forge.
- Carpenter: handsaw, ripsaw, hand plane, chisel set, brace
  and bit, drawknife, plumb bob.
- Wheelwright: spokeshave, dish-cone, tire-bender, hot tire
  forge.
- Saddler: harness needles, awl, hammer, knife, edge bevelers.
- Doctor: lancet, fleam (for bloodletting, still practiced),
  forceps, bone saw, scalpels, opium tincture, calomel,
  mercury, quinine.
- Surveyor: chain (Gunter's chain, 66 feet), compass,
  transit, level rod.

### c. The household tools (homestead)

- Cast-iron Dutch oven. Cooking, baking, stewing.
- Skillet.
- Coffee mill.
- Boiler / wash kettle.
- Treadle sewing machine (after the Singer broke into the
  market in the 1860s — a major status object on the
  homestead).
- Butter churn.
- Hand-cranked corn mill.
- Coal-oil lamps and lanterns.

## 6. The wagons

- **Conestoga / prairie schooner.** Large wagon for emigrant
  family. Pulled by oxen (slower, cheaper, hardier) or mules
  (faster, more expensive, more delicate). Six oxen for a
  fully-loaded wagon.
- **Studebaker farm wagon.** The standard work wagon. Iron
  tires, wood box, often converted into a chuckwagon or
  hay-rake or freight wagon.
- **Freighter.** Heavy wagon, often hauled in trains of three
  or four behind a single team. The Murphy Wagon and the
  Studebaker Big Wagon. Oxen team of 8–14.
- **Stagecoach.** Concord coach (built by Abbot-Downing of
  New Hampshire). Six-horse team. Carried six to nine
  passengers inside, more on top.
- **Buckboard.** Light spring wagon, two seats, used by
  ranchers and townspeople for short hauls.
- **Buggy / surrey.** Town and Sunday driving.

## 7. Money

- **Gold coins.** $20 double eagle, $10 eagle, $5 half-eagle,
  $2.50 quarter-eagle, $1 gold dollar.
- **Silver coins.** Dollar, half-dollar, quarter, dime,
  half-dime (later nickel).
- **Greenbacks.** Paper money issued during and after the war.
  Discounted in some places against gold.
- **National bank notes.** Issued by individual banks.
- **Mexican peso (silver dollar).** Widely used in the
  Southwest at par with the U.S. dollar through much of the
  period.
- **Two-bit, four-bit, six-bit.** _Bit_ = 12.5 cents (one
  eighth of a dollar). Two bits = a quarter. Four bits = a
  half-dollar.
- **Coppers.** Pennies. Used for trade with children, with
  the very poor, and in nickel-and-dime gambling.

A common cowhand carried his money in a leather poke (drawstring
pouch) inside his shirt, or rolled in his bedroll, or in the
chuckwagon's strongbox. He did not trust banks and often did not
trust pockets.

## 8. The smell, the sound, the wear

How these objects appear after use:

- **The Colt** smells of black powder smoke and gun oil. The
  bluing wears thin on the muzzle, the cylinder face, the
  edges of the frame, where leather and hand contact rub it
  silver. The grip checkering wears smooth.
- **The saddle** smells of sweet old leather, horse sweat,
  saddle soap. The fork and the cantle are worn pale. The
  seat is dished from the rider. The stirrup leathers are
  twisted.
- **The boot** is cracked across the instep, worn through the
  sole, salt-crusted around the heel. The shaft is bowed
  forward where the calf has shaped it.
- **The hat** is sweat-stained around the band, dented to its
  owner's preferred crease, gray with dust on the crown. A
  cowhand's hat fits no one else.
- **The bedroll** is stiff with grease, smells of wood smoke,
  has a brown ring on the canvas where the bottle of laudanum
  the man used to keep against his back has slowly leaked.
- **The chuckwagon** is gray-weathered, the iron tires worn
  thin, the box battered by tools and feet. The starter crock
  is sour-sweet on the lid. The coffee pot is black inside
  and out.
- **The bunkhouse** smells of unwashed wool, leather, sweat,
  tobacco, coal-oil, the boot-grease in a dish on the table,
  the sourdough starter bubbling in its jar.

These are the textures the prose puts on the page.
