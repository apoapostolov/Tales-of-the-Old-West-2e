# Towns, Economy, and Law

The frontier town is the second great setting of the western
after the open country. Get the geography of a town right — what
it has, who runs what, where the law sits and where it does
not — and a town scene grounds itself.

This file covers the cattle town, the mining camp, the railroad
town, the army-post town, and the small county seat. It covers
money, prices, businesses, lawmen, courts, and how violence
ended (legally and otherwise).

---

## 1. Town types

### a. The cattle town

- Abilene 1867–71. Ellsworth, Newton, Wichita, Dodge City,
  Caldwell — the Kansas trail-head towns followed each other
  as the railroad pushed west and as quarantine lines pushed
  the cattle trade off old ranges.
- Population: 500–2,000 permanent, swelling to 5,000+ in
  shipping season (June–October).
- Layout: Front Street (the main, facing the railroad) with
  the saloons and the gambling halls and the dance halls. A
  red-light district on the far side of the tracks (in Dodge
  the _south side_, across the _deadline_ — a literal line at
  the railroad track that armed men were not to cross to the
  respectable north side).
- Dies after the cattle trade moves on. Many became farm towns
  or vanished.

### b. The mining camp

- Virginia City NV (Comstock silver, 1859 onward), Leadville
  CO (silver and lead, 1877), Cripple Creek CO (gold, 1891),
  Tombstone AZ (silver, 1877), Deadwood SD (gold, 1875),
  Bodie CA (gold, 1859), Butte MT (copper, 1880s).
- Population: a few hundred to 25,000 (Leadville 1880).
- Layout: builds onto the slope above the mine. Saloons and
  cribs and gambling halls outnumber every other building. A
  red-light district. A single church if any. Often no school
  for years.
- Wages high (a hardrock miner in Leadville made $3–$4 a day —
  triple a cowhand). Prices high (everything packed in by mule
  before the railroad). Boom-and-bust on the price of the
  metal.

### c. The railroad town

- The terminus or the divisional point. Cheyenne, Laramie,
  North Platte, Ogallala. Many founded by the railroad itself
  and dependent on it.
- Layout: parallel to the tracks. The depot, the section
  house, the roundhouse, the company hotel.
- Population: railroad workers, freight handlers, drovers,
  the trade that follows.

### d. The army-post town

- Hog Ranches and sutler's stores grew up just outside Army
  posts (off the reservation, where the post commander could
  not enforce regulation). Whiskey and women for the soldiers.
  Often violent.
- The post itself: officer's quarters, enlisted barracks,
  parade ground, sutler's store, headquarters, magazine,
  guardhouse.

### e. The county seat

- The town that had the courthouse. Often the largest town in
  the county. Lawyers, judges, the land office, the bank, the
  newspaper, the courthouse square.
- Stable. The cattle towns and mining camps came and went;
  county seats endured.

### f. The Mexican town

- Older than any Anglo town in the Southwest. Plaza in the
  center, church on one side, government building on another.
  Adobe construction. Markets in the plaza. Bilingual through
  the Anglo period.
- Santa Fe, Las Vegas (NM), Tucson, Mesilla. Each absorbed an
  Anglo overlay after the Mexican-American War and the Gadsden
  Purchase.

## 2. The buildings, building by building

### a. Saloon

- The dominant business. A cattle town of 1,500 might have 20
  saloons. Dodge City in 1878 had 19 licensed saloons.
- A saloon: a long bar of milled wood, a brass rail at the
  foot, a mirror behind the bar, bottles on the back wall, a
  painting of a reclining nude (the _fancy painting_) above
  the bar in the better places. Tables along the walls and in
  the back. Faro layout, chuck-a-luck wheel, a poker table.
  Sawdust on the floor, swept and replaced daily. Spittoons
  at intervals.
- Drinks: beer (when ice and breweries permitted) at 5 cents
  the schooner. Whiskey at 25 cents the shot in the cattle
  towns, 10 cents in the rougher places. Champagne and
  imported wines in the parlor saloons of the rich towns
  (Tombstone's Crystal Palace, Denver's Windsor Hotel bar).
- The saloon was the meeting hall, the post office (mail held
  behind the bar), the unofficial courthouse, the male
  parlor. It was open all hours.

### b. Brothel / dance hall / parlor house / crib

A clear hierarchy.

- **The parlor house.** The high end. A two-story Victorian
  house with a parlor, a piano, a madame, six to twelve
  women. A gentleman caller bought drinks (champagne $5 a
  bottle), spent time in the parlor, and went upstairs. $5
  to $20 the visit. The women were paid relatively well and
  worked in conditions the rest of the trade did not have.
- **The dance hall.** Customers paid for a dance with a
  woman (10 cents to 25 cents the dance), and after the
  dance bought her a drink (the bar cut her in on the
  drink). Many dance-hall women were also prostitutes.
- **The saloon girl.** Worked the floor of a saloon,
  pushing drinks. Often a prostitute as well. Worked on
  commission from the bar.
- **The crib.** A single-room shack, often in a row of
  identical cribs along a back alley (Dodge's Tin Can
  Alley, Leadville's Stillborn Alley). The lowest rung. The
  woman might see twenty or thirty men a night at 50 cents
  to $1. Disease, addiction, violence, suicide were the
  endpoints. Most crib girls were dead or gone by 30.
- **The hog ranch.** A roadhouse-brothel outside an Army
  post. Whiskey, gambling, women. Famously violent.
- **The married-out.** A prostitute who married a customer
  or a saloon man and left the trade. Common path out for
  the lucky.

The reformer's account of the trade as uniformly degraded was a
distortion; the celebrant's account of _soiled doves with hearts
of gold_ was a distortion the other way. The real population
ranged from highly paid madames to children dying of syphilis in
back alleys. Write any specific woman by her position. Do not
generalize.

### c. Bank

- A wood-frame or brick building. A small lobby with a teller's
  window. A back office. A safe (or a vault, in the better
  places). The bank was small — a county-seat bank might have
  $20,000 in deposits. A robbery of $5,000 was a major event.
- Bank robberies were not common — the James-Younger gang's
  Northfield Minnesota raid in 1876 ended in disaster (the
  townspeople shot the gang to pieces) and showed the danger.
  More money was taken from trains and from stagecoaches.

### d. Mercantile / general store

- The center of trade for everything not gun, drink, or woman.
  Flour, sugar, salt, coffee, beans, salt pork, canned goods
  (Underwood deviled ham, Borden's condensed milk, canned
  peaches), bolts of cloth, ready-made clothes, boots, hats,
  hardware, harness, saddles, ammunition, tobacco, patent
  medicine, books, newspapers, school slates, hair tonic,
  lamp chimneys, kerosene.
- Often the post office.
- Often the unofficial bank — the merchant carried accounts on
  the books for the local ranchers (paid up at shipping time)
  and paid out cash from the till.
- Long counter. Glass cases for small valuable goods. Barrels
  of crackers, pickles. Hams hung from the ceiling. The smell
  of coffee, leather, kerosene, and tobacco.

### e. Hotel

- Three classes:
  - The boarding house (a room in a frame building, $0.25 to
    $1 the night, meals extra).
  - The hotel (a two-story frame building, $1–$3 the night
    with meals).
  - The grand hotel (the Driskill in Austin, the Brown Palace
    in Denver, the Crystal Palace in Tombstone — $3–$10 the
    night, electric lights, indoor plumbing in the better
    rooms after the 1880s).

### f. Stables / livery

- A boarding stable for the traveler's horse. $0.50 to $1 a
  day for hay and oats and a stall. The town's blacksmith
  often worked at or next to the livery.

### g. Blacksmith and farrier

- Open-front building with the forge, the anvil, the bellows.
  Shod horses, made and mended hardware, mended wagons.
  Charged $1 to shoe a horse all around. The smith was usually
  prosperous and respected.

### h. Newspaper

- A weekly broadsheet, often four pages. Editorials, local
  gossip, telegraph news from the East, advertisements,
  legal notices. The editor was a notable man and often
  shot at. The press was an iron Washington hand-press.

### i. Church

- One in any decent town. The Methodist circuit rider preceded
  permanent churches. Catholics where the population was
  Mexican or Irish. Episcopalians, Presbyterians, Baptists.
  In the rougher cattle towns the church and the pulpit were
  late arrivals.

### j. School

- One-room schoolhouse, eight grades together. The schoolmarm
  (often a single Eastern woman who came west on contract)
  was paid poorly and watched closely. The schoolhouse doubled
  as a church, a meeting hall, a polling place.

### k. Courthouse

- In the county seat. Brick or stone if the county was rich,
  frame if not. The courtroom on the upper floor, the offices
  below. The town square or plaza around it.

### l. Jail

- One-story brick or stone if the county was rich; a log or
  frame building if not. Two to six cells. Iron bunk and a
  slop bucket. Often no separate cell for women, who were
  held in the jailer's house under guard.

### m. Undertaker / coffin maker

- The town carpenter usually. A photograph of the deceased
  posed in his coffin was period practice.

### n. Other businesses

- Doctor (often above the drugstore).
- Lawyer (often above the bank).
- Drugstore (patent medicine, tobacco, soda fountain in the
  fancier towns).
- Telegraph office (in any town on the railroad).
- Express office (Wells Fargo, American Express).
- Railroad depot.
- Land office (in any settlement zone).
- Barber shop (haircut 25 cents, shave 10 cents, bath 25 cents).
- Restaurants, chop houses, oyster houses (yes — canned and
  later iced oysters reached the West, where they were a
  delicacy).
- Chinese laundry (in any town with a Chinese community).
- Tinker, harness maker, gunsmith, jeweler, bookseller.

## 4. Law on the frontier — who did what

### a. The federal layer

- **Deputy U.S. Marshal.** Federal officer with jurisdiction in
  federal cases (mail robbery, federal land disputes, crimes
  in federal territories, crimes in Indian Territory). The
  most respected lawmen on the frontier. Bass Reeves, Heck
  Thomas, Bill Tilghman, Chris Madsen (the _Three Guardsmen_
  of Indian Territory) were deputy U.S. marshals. Paid by
  fee — so much per arrest, mileage, return.
- **U.S. Marshal.** The administrative head, appointed by the
  President. Ran the deputies. Kept a courthouse office.
- **Federal court.** Judge Isaac Parker at Fort Smith Arkansas
  had jurisdiction over the Indian Territory through the
  1880s — the _Hanging Judge,_ who sent 79 men to the gallows.

### b. The territorial / state layer

- **Sheriff.** Elected county officer. Served warrants, ran
  the jail, collected county taxes, served civil process.
  Could appoint deputies. The most powerful local law in most
  Western counties.
- **Texas Rangers.** A state mounted force of approximately
  500 men in companies of 30–80. State-wide jurisdiction.
  Effective and brutal. Suppressed Comanche raiding, hunted
  outlaws, fought Mexican raiders along the Rio Grande, and
  played a role in extralegal violence against Tejanos.
- **Arizona Rangers, New Mexico Mounted Police** — similar
  state forces, later (1900s).

### c. The town / municipal layer

- **Town marshal.** Hired by the town council. Jurisdiction
  inside town limits. The town marshal in a cattle town like
  Dodge or Tombstone enforced the no-firearms-in-town ordinance
  (every cattle town had one, in some form), broke up fights,
  walked his rounds at night, collected fines.
- **Constable.** Lower municipal officer.
- **Night watchman.** Hired by businesses to walk a beat. Often
  by Wells Fargo to guard the bank or the express office.

### d. The private layer

- **Pinkerton agents.** A national detective agency hired by
  banks, railroads, mine owners. Strikebreakers (often
  brutally), bounty hunters, infiltrators. Hated in working-
  class country.
- **Stock detectives / range detectives.** Hired by the cattle-
  men's associations of Wyoming, Montana, Texas. Some were
  killers (Tom Horn). The Johnson County War (Wyoming, 1892)
  was fought between large ranchers' detectives and small
  ranchers / homesteaders.
- **Wells Fargo detectives.** Investigated stage and express
  robberies. James B. Hume was the most famous.
- **Private guards.** Companies, mines, ranches.

### e. The extralegal layer

- **Vigilance committees.** Self-appointed citizen groups that
  arrested, tried, and hanged suspected criminals when (or
  before) the legal authority acted. Famous: San Francisco
  Committee of Vigilance (1851 and 1856), the Montana
  Vigilantes (1864–65, who hanged Sheriff Henry Plummer's gang
  in Bannack and Virginia City), the Ku Klux Klan and the
  White Leagues in the Reconstruction South.
- **Lynch mobs.** Less organized than vigilance committees. A
  crowd, sometimes drunk, that took a prisoner from the jail
  and hanged him. Common. Jails in the West frequently lost
  their prisoners.
- **Posses.** A sheriff or marshal could deputize _the posse
  comitatus_ — any able-bodied man within his jurisdiction —
  to pursue a fugitive. A posse was the line between law and
  mob, and the line moved.

## 5. The court

- **Justice of the Peace.** Local elected magistrate. Heard
  small civil cases and minor criminal matters. Could marry
  people, swear out warrants, fine drunks. Did not need legal
  training in most jurisdictions.
- **Police court / Mayor's court.** Heard ordinance violations
  in town.
- **County court / district court.** Heard the major cases.
  Often a _circuit_ judge who rode a circuit of several
  counties, holding court in each in season.
- **Trial.** Twelve-man jury. The accused had counsel if he
  could pay. Court-appointed counsel for capital cases by
  the 1880s in most jurisdictions. Witness testimony, cross-
  examination, closing arguments. Verdict.
- **Sentence.** Hanging for murder in most jurisdictions
  (replaced by long prison terms in the 1890s in some states).
  Penitentiary for serious felonies (Yuma in Arizona, Canyon
  City in Colorado, Deer Lodge in Montana, Huntsville in
  Texas, Leavenworth federal). County jail for misdemeanors.
  Fines.

### How a real frontier trial sounded

- Quick. A murder trial often lasted one or two days.
- Crowded. The town turned out.
- Press present. The newspaper printed the testimony in full.
- The hanging — when it came — was public, well-attended (in
  some places by ticketed admission), and followed by a
  picnic. Public hangings were largely abolished by state law
  in the late 19th century but private ones (with selected
  witnesses inside the jail yard) continued.

## 6. The reality of frontier crime

Two corrections to popular myth:

### a. Most western towns were not violent

Dodge City in its peak year had five killings. Tombstone in 1881
had five (one was the OK Corral). A modern American city of the
same size has more. The bulk of arrests in any cattle town were
for drunkenness, disorderly conduct, and prostitution
(licensed and fined as a revenue source).

### b. The big violence was in the country, not the town

Range wars, Indian Wars, vigilante actions in the unsettled
country, the lynching of Mexicans and Black men in the Texas
borderlands and the Reconstruction South, the killing of
homesteaders by hired guns of the cattle barons — the West's
real body counts were here.

The _gunfighter walking the dusty street at high noon_ is a
single tradition out of many. Use it sparingly. Use the rest.

## 7. The economy big picture

The West was a colonial economy supplying raw materials to the
industrial East and to Europe.

- **Cattle.** Beef on the hoof shipped east. Hides shipped to
  Europe. Tallow for candles and soap.
- **Mining.** Gold, silver, copper, lead, zinc shipped east
  and to Europe.
- **Hides and furs.** Buffalo hides (1872–83) shipped east for
  industrial belting and for fashionable robes. Beaver had
  been the trade of the mountain men a generation earlier.
- **Lumber.** Sierra and Pacific Northwest timber.
- **Wheat.** California, Kansas, the Dakotas. Shipped east and
  to Europe.
- **Wool.** New Mexico, Wyoming, Montana sheep flocks.

The frontier was capital-poor. Eastern and European money
financed the cattle barons (Scottish syndicates owned vast
ranches in Wyoming and the Texas Panhandle). British and Eastern
money financed the railroads. Local currency was scarce. Credit
ran the small economy.

This reality is missed in the screen-cowboy western. The cattle
baron in the page version is not a man on a horse — he is a
syndicate, a New York or Edinburgh director who has never been
west of Pittsburgh. The drover who works for him is the bottom
of a long financial chain whose top sits in a London office.
Write this and the West gets larger and stranger.

## 8. What the town smells like, sounds like

- **Smell.** Horse manure in the street (constant; a town of
  2,000 had perhaps 1,000 horses). Wood smoke from cookstoves
  and forges. Bakery bread in the morning. Tobacco. Whiskey
  through the saloon doors. Coal smoke from the railroad.
  Outhouse stench in summer. Flowers in the spring along the
  fenced yards of the respectable houses.
- **Sound.** Wagon wheels on dirt or on plank sidewalks. Iron
  shoes on the same. Hammers in the smith's shop. Piano in the
  saloon at night, badly tuned. The locomotive's whistle three
  times a day. The court bell. The school bell. The church
  bell on Sunday. Drunks shouting in the late hours. A pistol
  shot now and then, often at the moon.
- **Sight.** Mud or dust in the streets, depending on weather.
  False-front frame buildings with the storefront one story
  taller than the building behind. Boardwalks with hitching
  rails out front. Tied horses. Loose dogs. Children. Few
  trees in most western towns (planted ones were a status
  symbol).

A town scene with two or three of these on the page reads true.
A town scene with none reads like a movie set, which is what too
many published westerns settle for.
