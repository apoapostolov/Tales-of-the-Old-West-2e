# Native Material Culture — The Daily Physical World

This file is the knowledge module for the material and
daily life of the major Native nations encountered in
1870s western fiction. It complements
`knowledge/peoples-and-conflict.md` (the political and
demographic overview) and is the deeper procedural and
material counterpart to that file. For writing a Native
character within an English-language prose register, see
also `references/pov-and-distance.md` section 3 on
first-person voice and the closing rule of this module.

The prior peoples-and-conflict module covered who the
Native nations were and what happened to them. This
file covers what the people of those nations actually
held in their hands, slept under, ate, rode, made, and
lived through on a daily basis. The material level is
where the writing has to happen if the prose is going
to carry weight.

This file is organized by region and nation. Each section
gives the physical world: shelter, food economy, the man's
material kit, the woman's material kit, the seasonal
round, and the verbs of the work. Each section closes
with a writer's note on what to refuse and what to read.

## 1. Southern Plains — Comanche

The dominant power of the southern plains through the
mid-1870s. A horse culture so completely transformed
by the horse that the pre-horse Comanche life is
distant memory by 1870. Mounted, mobile, raiding, and
wealthy in horses.

Suggested reading: Pekka Hämäläinen, _The Comanche
Empire_; S. C. Gwynne, _Empire of the Summer Moon_
(useful but Eurocentric in places).

### Shelter — the tipi

The Plains tipi is the dominant Comanche dwelling. Its
specifics:

- **Cover.** Twelve to twenty buffalo cow hides, scraped
  thin, sewn together by the women. A new cover took
  weeks of work and the cooperation of multiple
  families. The smoke-darkened upper portion was always
  trimmed and replaced first.
- **Poles.** Fifteen to twenty lodgepole pine poles,
  twenty to thirty feet long. The poles came from the
  mountains (the Sangre de Cristos for southern bands,
  the Wichita Mountains for some). Pole acquisition
  was a separate trip.
- **Footprint.** Twelve to twenty feet diameter at the
  base. Slightly oval, with the back wall sloped more
  steeply for wind resistance.
- **Door.** A small flap, low. The door faced east as a
  near-universal rule.
- **Smoke flaps.** Two flaps at the top, controlled by
  poles outside. The flaps were turned to keep the
  smoke drawing regardless of wind.
- **Interior.** A central fire pit, sleeping platforms
  around the wall, parfleche bags hung from the lodge
  poles, a backrest of willow rods. The men's place
  was at the back opposite the door (the place of
  honor); women and children to the sides.
- **Setup.** The women of the family raised the tipi.
  An experienced woman could raise a tipi in twenty
  minutes. The men did not raise tipis.

### Food economy

- **Buffalo.** The center of the southern Comanche food
  economy through the early 1870s. The hunt was
  organized, communal, on horseback. The kill was
  butchered on the ground. Choice cuts (tongue, hump,
  liver eaten raw) were eaten on the spot; the rest
  was loaded onto travois and brought back to camp for
  drying and pemmican preparation.
- **Pemmican.** Dried buffalo meat pounded with fat
  and dried berries, stored in parfleche bags. The
  staple winter food. Could be eaten dry, cooked into
  stew, or carried on a raid.
- **Gathered foods.** Mesquite beans, prickly pear
  fruit, plums, sand cherries, wild onions. The
  women's domain. The mesquite bean meal was a
  significant secondary staple in the southern range.
- **Trade and raid.** Beef cattle, corn from agricultural
  Pueblo and Wichita neighbors, dried fish from
  trading partners, sugar and coffee and metal goods
  from Comancheros (the New Mexican traders who
  worked the southern plains).

### The man's kit

- **The horse.** A man's wealth and status was his horse
  herd. A wealthy Comanche man owned fifty to three
  hundred horses. A poor man owned five or ten. A
  man's two best horses were his war horse (kept apart,
  often hobbled near his lodge) and his buffalo horse
  (trained for the hunt).
- **The bow.** A short bow, three to four feet, made of
  Osage orange (bois d'arc) where available, sinew-
  backed for strength. The Comanche bow was famous
  for its power at close range from horseback. A
  Comanche could shoot multiple arrows in the time a
  man could fire and reload a single-shot rifle.
- **Arrows.** Reed or dogwood shaft, three feathers,
  iron point (trade iron, often cut from barrel hoops
  or wagon tires). Each man marked his arrows so a
  kill could be claimed.
- **The lance.** Eight to ten feet, iron point, often
  decorated. Carried by the warrior class.
- **The shield.** Heavy buffalo bull-hide, painted with
  protective designs received in vision. The shield
  was a sacred object, not just a defense.
- **The knife.** Trade iron, in a beaded sheath at the
  belt. Used constantly — cutting, eating, butchering.
- **The firearm.** By the 1870s, many Comanches had
  rifles. Henry repeaters, Spencer carbines, the
  occasional Sharps. Ammunition was the limit.
- **Dress.** Breechclout and leggings of buckskin or
  trade cloth. Moccasins (hard-soled Plains
  moccasin). A buffalo robe in winter, fur side in.
  Hair worn long, often in two braids wrapped with
  fur or cloth. Face paint for war and ceremony.

### The woman's kit

- **The travois.** The transport device. Two long poles
  attached to a horse or dog, with a hide platform
  between, dragging behind. The Comanche woman managed
  the family's movement of camp.
- **The fleshing knife.** A scraping tool for hide
  preparation, made from buffalo bone with an iron
  blade lashed to it.
- **The hide stake.** A wooden stake driven into the
  ground; the hide was pegged out for fleshing and
  drying.
- **The awl.** Bone or iron, for sewing hides and
  beadwork.
- **The mortar and pestle.** Wooden, for pounding
  pemmican and mesquite beans.
- **The parfleche.** A rawhide envelope, painted with
  geometric designs, for storing dried meat, pemmican,
  and personal goods. The parfleche was the woman's
  craft and was made by women.
- **Beadwork.** Bead and quillwork on dress, moccasins,
  cradles, bags. The woman's artistic domain. By
  the 1870s, glass trade beads had largely replaced
  porcupine quills.
- **Dress.** A two-skin dress (two deer or antelope
  hides sewn at the shoulders and sides), beaded
  yoke, fringed sleeves. Moccasins, leggings to the
  knee. Hair worn long, often parted in the middle.

### The seasonal round (southern Comanche, 1870s)

- **Winter.** Camped in the Llano Estacado canyons
  (Palo Duro, Tule, Quitaque) for shelter from the
  wind. Horses on grass in the canyon bottoms. The
  long boredom of the cold months.
- **Spring.** Camp moved out onto the plains. Buffalo
  hunting resumed. Raiding parties south into Mexico.
- **Summer.** Major encampments on the plains for the
  Comanche moon (the August moon of raiding). The
  great social gatherings, courtship, trading.
- **Fall.** Major buffalo hunt for winter meat. Hides
  prepared for trade. Camp moved back toward winter
  ground.

By 1875 the buffalo were nearly gone. The Red River
War of 1874-75 broke the southern Comanche power. The
last free Comanches surrendered to the army at Fort
Sill in 1875.

### Writer's notes

- The Comanche woman was not a beast of burden. She
  was a skilled craftsperson, the camp's chief
  logistician, and in many cases a trader of consequence.
- The Comanche raid was not random violence. It was an
  economic activity with specific goals: horses,
  captives (for ransom or adoption), and goods.
- The captive question. Comanche bands held many
  captives, especially Mexican and Tejano children.
  Some were adopted into the band; some were ransomed;
  some lived their lives as Comanches. Quanah Parker's
  mother, Cynthia Ann Parker, was a famous case. The
  captive's experience was its own subject and is
  rarely written without distortion.

## 2. Northern Plains — Lakota

The dominant power of the northern plains in the
1870s. Agreed by treaty (Fort Laramie 1868) to a
reservation including the Black Hills; gold discovered
1874; war 1876-77; defeat and confinement by 1881.

Suggested reading: Joseph Marshall III, _The Day the
World Ended at Little Bighorn_; James Welch (Blackfeet/
Gros Ventre), _Killing Custer_; the WPA-era
interviews collected by Walter Campbell and others.

### Shelter — the tipi (Lakota variations)

Lakota tipis were similar to Comanche but generally
larger and more elaborately painted. The famous
Lakota painted tipis (rare; reserved for men of
distinction) recorded vision experiences and war
honors.

The camp circle. Lakota bands camped in a specific
circle (the _hocoka_) with the tribal council lodge in
the center. The opening of the circle faced east. The
arrangement of family lodges in the circle was
prescribed by tradition. A camp circle of a major
gathering (the 1876 gathering at the Little Bighorn,
for instance) might include thousands of lodges in
multiple concentric circles.

### Food economy

Same buffalo-centered economy as the Comanche, with
some variations. The _wasna_ (the Lakota term for
pemmican). Wild turnip (_timpsila_), gathered by
women in early summer with the digging stick. Wild
plums, June berries, chokecherries.

By 1876 the northern buffalo were declining but not
yet gone. The buffalo hunt continued through 1880 in
the northern range; by 1883 the herds were extinct
on the open plains.

### The warrior society

Lakota men belonged to warrior societies (_akicita_)
that policed the camp, organized hunts, and fought
together. The major societies included the Strong
Hearts, the Kit Foxes, the Crow Owners, and others.
A man's society membership was a defining identity.
The societies had their own regalia, songs, and
duties.

### The Sun Dance

The annual major ceremony, held in summer. Vows made
during the year were fulfilled. The dance lasted four
days. Some dancers underwent piercing — skewers
through the chest muscles, attached by rawhide thongs
to a central pole, danced against until the flesh
tore free. The piercing was a vow of suffering offered
to the Wakan Tanka.

The U.S. government banned the Sun Dance on
reservations in 1881. The dance went underground and
was practiced secretly through the long suppression
that ended only with the Indian Religious Freedom Act
of 1978.

### The winter count

The Lakota historical record. Each winter, a band's
designated keeper added one pictograph to a buffalo
hide, recording the year's most memorable event.
Winter counts were generational and were used as
calendars and as historical references. Several
notable winter counts (Lone Dog, American Horse,
Battiste Good) survive in museum collections.

### The man's kit

Same general inventory as the Comanche, with
variations:

- **Pipe.** The Lakota _chanunpa_, the sacred pipe.
  Catlinite (red pipestone) bowl, wooden stem, often
  decorated. The pipe was a sacred object central to
  prayer and treaty-making.
- **Eagle feathers.** The Lakota war bonnet was
  developed and elaborated in the late nineteenth
  century. By the 1870s the bonnet was an established
  honor; each feather marked a specific war honor
  (counting coup, taking a horse, killing an enemy).
- **The shirt.** The hair-fringed war shirt, worn by
  men of the Shirt Wearer society. The fringes were
  hair (often the wearer's own or that of dead
  enemies, depending on the source). A man with a
  hair-fringed shirt was a man of recognized
  distinction.

### Writer's notes

- The Lakota are not "the Sioux." Sioux is an Algonquian
  exonym applied by the French and is a complex
  collective term. The Lakota are the western, plains
  branch (Oglala, Hunkpapa, Mniconjou, Sicangu, Sihasapa,
  Itazipco, Oohenumpa). Use the specific band name when
  possible.
- The Black Hills (_Pahá Sápa_) are sacred. They are
  not a backdrop. A Lakota character does not look at
  the Black Hills the way a white prospector does.
- The 1876 gathering on the Little Bighorn was the
  largest Plains Indian camp ever assembled. It was
  not a war camp; it was a summer encampment. The
  battle came to it.

## 3. Southwest — Apache

The Apache is several distinct groups: the Chiricahua
(southeast Arizona, southwest New Mexico, northern
Sonora and Chihuahua), the Mescalero (south-central
New Mexico), the Jicarilla (northern New Mexico), the
Western Apache (central Arizona), the Lipan (Texas
and northern Mexico). They are not one nation.

Suggested reading: Eve Ball, _Indeh: An Apache
Odyssey_; Geronimo's autobiography (as told to S. M.
Barrett, 1906); Angie Debo, _Geronimo: The Man, His
Time, His Place_.

### Shelter — the wickiup

The Apache wickiup is a brush dome, not a tipi. Eight
to ten feet diameter, four to five feet high at the
center. Frame of bent willow or cottonwood saplings,
covered with brush, grass, or hides depending on
season and material availability.

The wickiup is built fast (a few hours) and abandoned
when the camp moves. It is a temporary dwelling for a
mobile people. The Apache did not haul a tipi; they
built where they stopped.

The _ranchería_ — the Apache camp — was a cluster of
wickiups, often hidden in a brushy canyon or on the
side of a mountain where it was difficult to
approach.

### Food economy

The Apache economy was raid-and-gather, not buffalo-
based. The southwest ecology supported less large
game.

- **Mescal (agave).** The staple gathered food. The
  women cut the agave heart, baked it for two or three
  days in a pit oven (_mescal pit_), and ate the
  baked heart as the year's most important food
  source. Mescal could be dried and stored.
- **Pinyon nuts.** Gathered in the fall in the
  mountain country. A major winter food.
- **Mesquite beans, juniper berries, sotol, yucca
  fruit.** Seasonal supplements.
- **Game.** Deer, antelope, rabbits, occasional elk.
  The Apache were excellent hunters with bow and rifle.
- **Cattle and horses.** Raided from Mexican and Anglo
  ranches. The Apache economy from the 1860s through
  the 1880s was substantially based on raiding for
  livestock, which was traded with sympathetic
  Mexican and Anglo intermediaries.

### The raid

The Apache raid was the celebrated military art. Small
parties (six to twenty men), no horses ridden into the
target country (horses left at a remote camp; the
party moved on foot through difficult country),
strike at dawn, take livestock, drive them back across
country no pursuit could follow.

The Apache could cover seventy miles a day on foot
through mountains. The army could not follow. The
campaign against Geronimo's small band (1885-86) tied
up five thousand soldiers because the army could not
catch the Apache in the country the Apache chose.

### The man's kit

- **The bow.** Shorter than the Plains bow. Mulberry or
  juniper, sinew-backed.
- **The arrow.** Cane shaft with hardwood foreshaft.
  The Apache poison arrow was real (deer-blood and
  rattlesnake-venom mixture used in some bands), though
  not as universally as legend claimed.
- **The knife.** Steel, in a sheath at the belt.
- **The rifle.** By the 1880s most Apache warriors had
  rifles, often Winchester repeating rifles taken or
  bought from Mexican sources.
- **The headband.** A cloth band around the head holding
  the long hair. The Apache wore no feather bonnets.
- **Moccasins.** Distinctive Apache moccasin: knee-high,
  with a turned-up toe (to push aside thorns and
  rattlesnakes), often with a hard sole.
- **Dress.** Buckskin shirt and breechclout, or by the
  1870s often a calico shirt and trade cloth pants.
  Practical for the country.

### The woman's kit

- **The basket.** Apache women were master basket
  weavers. The burden basket (carried with a tumpline
  across the forehead) and the storage baskets were
  central to the household economy. Apache basketry
  is among the finest in North America.
- **The cradleboard.** The Apache cradleboard
  distinguished the family. The infant spent the first
  year strapped into the cradleboard for most of the
  day.
- **Pottery.** Some Apache groups made pottery (notably
  the Western Apache). Most relied on baskets and
  trade pottery from Pueblo neighbors.
- **The mescal-baking knowledge.** The pit oven was a
  women's knowledge. The selection of the agave, the
  preparation, the timing of the bake.

### Writer's notes

- The Apache were not "savages on horseback." They were
  a sophisticated mountain people whose military art
  was the most successful resistance to U.S. military
  power in North American history.
- Geronimo (Goyahkla) was a complex figure — religious
  leader, war leader, sometimes a problem to his own
  people, the last man to surrender (September 1886).
- The Apache campaigns of the 1880s used Apache scouts
  (mostly Western Apache) against Chiricahua bands.
  The internal Apache politics is rarely written
  honestly.

## 4. Southwest — Diné (Navajo)

The largest Native nation in the United States. Sheep
herders, weavers, silversmiths, agriculturalists. Bound
to the country between the Four Sacred Mountains.

Subjected to the Long Walk (1864) and the four years
at the Bosque Redondo (Fort Sumner, 1864-1868). Treaty
of 1868 established the reservation. The 1870s were
the rebuilding decade.

Suggested reading: Ruth Underhill, _The Navajos_ (older
but solid); Jennifer Nez Denetdale, _Reclaiming Diné
History_; the WPA Navajo interviews.

### Shelter — the hogan

The hogan is the traditional Diné dwelling. Single
room, log construction, mud-plastered, with the door
facing east as a near-universal rule.

- **The forked-stick hogan.** The older form. Three or
  four forked posts set in the ground forming a
  conical frame, covered with bark and earth.
- **The cribbed-log hogan.** The dominant form by the
  1870s. Logs laid horizontally in a hexagonal or
  octagonal pattern, corbeled inward to form the roof,
  the whole covered with earth.
- **The interior.** A central fire, smoke hole above.
  Sheepskins on the floor for sitting and sleeping. A
  loom against one wall for the women's weaving. Tools
  hung from pegs.
- **The summer shelter.** A brush ramada (an open shade
  structure) used in hot weather. The hogan was for
  cold weather and ceremony.

### The sheep economy

The Diné received sheep (originally Spanish churro)
in the 1600s and built a pastoral economy around them.
By the 1870s the Diné flock was rebuilding from the
slaughter at Fort Sumner.

- **The flock.** A family kept fifty to several hundred
  sheep and a few goats. The flock was the family's
  wealth. A wealthy man's flock numbered in the
  thousands.
- **The herding.** Done by women and children primarily.
  The flock moved between summer and winter pastures.
  The dog was essential.
- **The wool.** Sheared in spring. The wool was the
  raw material for the women's weaving.
- **The mutton.** The principal protein source. Sheep
  also provided wool, hide, sinew. Almost the entire
  animal was used.

### The weaving

The Diné woman's defining craft. The vertical loom of
two upright posts and crossbars. The wool was carded,
spun, dyed (with native plant dyes — indigo from
trade, red from cochineal trade, yellow from native
plants), and woven into blankets and rugs.

By the 1870s the great Chief's Blanket period was
declining and the rug period (woven for the trader,
not for native use) was beginning. The 1870s blanket
is the high point of the art and is found in major
museum collections.

### The silver

The Diné silversmithing tradition began in the 1850s
(learned from Mexican silversmiths) and was well-
established by the 1870s. The early silverwork — heavy
rings, conchos for belts, bracelets, the squash blossom
necklace — was made for native wear. The trader-driven
silverwork comes later.

### The clan system

The Diné are matrilineal. A child belongs to the
mother's clan and "is born for" the father's clan.
There are about sixty clans. A Diné introduces
himself by clan: _I am Bitter Water Clan, born for
Big Water Clan._

Marriage between members of the same clan is forbidden.
The clan system organizes ceremony, mutual aid, and
identity.

### The man's kit

- **The horse.** The Diné man was a horseman, though
  the herd was not the central economic unit it was
  for the Comanche.
- **The bow and the rifle.** Same as other groups.
- **The silver.** A man's belt was often heavy with
  silver conchos. Bracelets and rings. The silver
  was both ornament and portable wealth.
- **Dress.** Velveteen shirt (introduced from Spanish
  and Mexican trade), wool trousers, moccasins.

### The woman's kit

- **The loom.** The defining tool.
- **The weaving comb.** A wooden comb for beating down
  the weft.
- **The spindle.** A simple drop spindle with a wooden
  whorl.
- **The cards.** Wool cards (trade goods) for preparing
  the wool.
- **The grinding stones (mano and metate).** For
  grinding corn — the Diné agricultural economy was
  smaller than the Pueblo's but corn was a significant
  food.
- **Dress.** The two-piece dress (a blouse and a long
  pleated skirt) became the standard women's dress in
  the late nineteenth century, modeled on the dress of
  army wives at Fort Sumner during captivity. The
  squash blossom necklace, silver belt, moccasins.

### Writer's notes

- The Long Walk and the Bosque Redondo are not optional
  context. Any 1870s Diné character lived through that
  catastrophe or was the child of those who did.
- Kit Carson is not a hero in any Diné account.
- The Diné did not fight the U.S. extensively after 1868. The 1870s Diné experience is rebuilding, not
  war.

## 5. Southwest — Pueblo

The Pueblo peoples of the Rio Grande and the western
mesas. Ancestral inhabitants of multi-story stone and
adobe villages. Agriculturalists. Speakers of multiple
unrelated language families (Tanoan, Keresan, Zuni,
Hopi). Each pueblo is a sovereign community.

The 1870s Pueblo experience is continuity. The pueblos
had survived Spanish, Mexican, and now American rule,
maintaining their ceremonial calendars, languages, and
land bases. The Pueblo Lands Act controversies came
later (1920s).

### The pueblo

The multi-story adobe or stone village. Rooms stacked
in terraces, accessed by ladders. The plaza at the
center for ceremony. The kiva (the underground or
semi-underground ceremonial chamber) reached by a
ladder through a roof opening.

- **Construction.** Adobe brick (puddled adobe in the
  earlier sites, formed brick by the historic period)
  on a stone foundation. Vigas (pine roof beams), with
  smaller latillas crossways, covered with brush and
  earth. The roof of one story was the floor of the
  next.
- **Defense.** Original construction had no ground-
  floor doors. Entry was by ladder to the second story
  and down. By the historic period this defensive
  feature had relaxed but the pueblo's overall form
  was defensive.
- **Plaza.** The ceremonial center. Major dances were
  held in the plaza on saint's days and at the
  appointed seasonal points.
- **Kiva.** The men's ceremonial chamber. Women did
  not enter most kivas. Ceremonial knowledge was
  organized in kiva societies.

### Agriculture

The Pueblo economy was corn, beans, squash, with
significant cotton (in some southern pueblos) and the
peach orchards introduced in the historic period
(notably at Hopi).

- **Corn.** The staple, ceremonially central. Six
  major colors (white, yellow, red, blue, speckled,
  black), each with ceremonial associations. Corn was
  ground by women on stone metates, multiple times,
  to produce flour of progressively finer texture.
- **Irrigation.** The Rio Grande pueblos used
  irrigation ditches (acequias) drawn from the river.
  The mesa pueblos (Hopi, Acoma) used dryland farming
  and floodwater farming on the bottoms of washes.
- **Beans, squash.** The companion crops. The
  three-sisters planting (corn, beans, squash in the
  same hill) was practiced.

### The ceremonial calendar

The Pueblo year is organized by ceremony. Each
pueblo has its own calendar. Some events (the Hopi
katsina cycle, the Zuni Shalako, the Rio Grande
saint's day dances) are well-documented. The internal
ceremonial life is extensive and largely closed to
outsiders.

The Pueblo dances visible to outsiders (the Corn
Dance at Santo Domingo, the Buffalo Dance at Tesuque,
the Eagle Dance at San Juan, the Deer Dance at
Taos) are public. The ceremonies behind them are not.

### The man's kit

- **The hoe.** The agricultural tool. Wooden, with a
  metal blade after trade contact.
- **Bow and arrow, lance.** For ceremony and for
  hunting.
- **The katsina mask** (in some pueblos). A
  ceremonial object of great power.
- **Dress.** Cotton manta (a single rectangular cloth)
  belted at the waist, with cotton or wool leggings.
  Moccasins. The Pueblo man's dress varied by pueblo.

### The woman's kit

- **The metate and mano.** The grinding stone, used
  daily for hours. Pueblo women's hands and arms
  were shaped by the work.
- **The pottery.** Pueblo pottery is some of the finest
  in North America. Coil-built (not wheel-thrown),
  hand-polished, painted with native pigments,
  pit-fired. Each pueblo had its own pottery
  tradition (Acoma's thin walls, Santa Clara's
  black, Zuni's geometric designs, San Ildefonso's
  later black-on-black).
- **The dress.** The Pueblo manta dress over one
  shoulder, with a belt. Moccasins and white wrapped
  leggings. Hair worn in a bun (married women) or a
  squash-blossom whorl (unmarried young women, Hopi).

### Writer's notes

- The Pueblo are not one people. Each pueblo is
  sovereign and has its own language, ceremonies, and
  history. Acoma is not Taos. Zuni is not Hopi.
- Ceremonial life is largely closed. A writer who
  invents kiva ceremonies has crossed a line. Write
  what is publicly known and stop there.
- The 1870s Pueblo were under U.S. sovereignty for the
  first time and had specific legal questions (status,
  citizenship, land) being worked out.

## 6. Plains Indian Sign Language

Across the plains nations, a sign language was used
for inter-tribal communication. Many tribes spoke
mutually unintelligible languages; sign language was
the lingua franca.

A working knowledge of sign was expected of plains
men. A trader, a scout, an army interpreter all
signed. Sign vocabulary covered hundreds of common
concepts: tribe names, animals, terrain, numbers,
trade goods, friendship, war, peace.

A scene that includes inter-tribal contact can show
sign being used. The signs are described, not the
words. The reader infers the conversation.

A few example signs (described, not pictured):

- **Friend:** Two fingers raised together at the
  chest.
- **White man:** A horizontal line across the forehead
  (the brim of a hat).
- **Question:** A flat hand raised, palm forward,
  rotated.
- **Buffalo:** Two index fingers held curved up at
  the temples (the horns).
- **River:** A wavy horizontal motion of the hand.

For a writer doing a sign-language scene, the
authority is William Tomkins's _Indian Sign Language_
(1929; widely available reprint), which preserves a
substantial corpus.

## 7. Writing the Native character — the rules

This module ends as the violence module ends: with
hard rules. The Native character in western fiction
has been more often written badly than any other
character type. The rules are not optional.

### Rule 1: specificity over abstraction

The character is Comanche, or Lakota Hunkpapa, or
Chiricahua Apache, or Hopi Bear Clan. Not _an Indian._
The specifics determine everything — what he eats,
what he wears, what he believes, how he travels, how
he speaks. _Indian_ is a colonial abstraction.

### Rule 2: the material, not the mystical

The strongest Native scenes are grounded in physical
specifics: the work, the food, the camp, the horse,
the seasonal round. Vague spiritual gestures are
white writers' shorthand for _I do not know what I
am writing about._ The material level is where the
prose has to live.

### Rule 3: the language problem

A Native character thinks and speaks in a language
that is not English. The writer working in English
has to make a choice. Two acceptable approaches:

- **Stylized English.** A simplified, slightly formal
  English that signals to the reader that another
  language is being heard. Avoid the worst pidgin
  cliches (_Heap big, ugh, kemo sabe_). Use a clean,
  short, declarative register.
- **Naturalized English.** Render the speech as a
  fluent English equivalent, the way a translator
  would. The reader trusts that the original was a
  fluent native-language utterance.

Either is correct. Pick one and hold to it. Do not
mix.

### Rule 4: the conceptual world

A Native character does not have the same conceptual
furniture as a white character. The categories of
relation, ownership, time, sacred, profane, kin, and
self are different. The careful writer reads the
ethnographies and tries to render the difference
without exoticizing it.

A Comanche man's relationship to his horse is not the
relationship of a cowhand to his mount. A Diné woman's
relationship to her loom is not the relationship of
a homestead wife to her quilt. The differences matter.

### Rule 5: refuse the four lies

- **The noble savage.** The Native character who is
  pure, wise, and tragic. A stereotype that flatters
  the white writer and erases the Native person.
- **The savage savage.** The Native character who is
  cruel, irrational, and animal. The dime-novel lie.
- **The vanishing Indian.** The Native character whose
  story is his own disappearance. He does not
  disappear; he is still here in 1875 and 1925 and 2025.
- **The pan-Indian.** The character whose nation is
  unspecified, whose customs are a mash-up of plains
  and southwest and woodland. A failure of research.

### Rule 6: read the source

For any nation, read at least one work by a member of
that nation or a recognized scholar of it. A short
list:

- Comanche: Hämäläinen, _The Comanche Empire._
- Lakota: Joseph Marshall III, _The Lakota Way._
- Apache: Geronimo's autobiography; Eve Ball, _Indeh._
- Diné: Ruth Underhill, _The Navajos_; Edward Sapir's
  ethnographies.
- Pueblo: Alfonso Ortiz (San Juan Pueblo), _The Tewa
  World._
- General: James Welch's novels (_Fools Crow,
  Winter in the Blood_).

### Rule 7: the captive question

Many western settings include the captive — the white
or Mexican person taken as a child, raised in the
band, returned (or not) in adulthood. The captive
experience is one of the West's recurring subjects.
The careful writer knows that the captive's
testimony is complicated: many returned captives
preferred the band to the colonial society. Cynthia
Ann Parker's case is the canonical example.

The captive scene can be the bridge between worlds,
but it must be written from the captive's actual
divided experience, not from sentimental repatriation
narratives.

---

## 8. The rule the others serve

A Native character on a page is a person of a specific
nation in a specific year facing a specific situation
that the writer must take seriously enough to research.
The flat figure on the ridge — the silhouette, the
threat, the symbol — is the western's oldest sin and
the one the canon has worked hardest to abandon.

The prose meets the Native character on his ground,
in his lodge, with his tools in his hands and his
language in his head. The writer who cannot do this
should write a different character. The writer who
will do this can write the western that has not yet
been written enough times.
