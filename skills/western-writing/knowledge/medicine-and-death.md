# Medicine and Death

The 19th century was a slaughterhouse compared to ours. A wound
that we treat in an hour killed in 1875. A child's fever killed
the child by morning. A woman who survived three deliveries was
fortunate; many did not survive one. Write this honestly. Death
was at every door and the people knew it.

This file gives the writer the medical reality and the textures
of dying as it actually looked.

---

## 1. The doctor

By the 1870s a frontier town might have one. He was usually:

- A graduate of a two-year proprietary medical college (no
  internship, no residency, no licensing in most states).
- A Civil War surgeon with battlefield experience and outdated
  training.
- A self-taught man who had read Gray's _Anatomy_ and
  practiced.
- An itinerant who came through with a wagon of patent
  medicine and pulled teeth.

His instruments fit in a black leather bag:

- Lancet (for bleeding — bloodletting was still common practice
  through the 1880s for fevers and _plethora_).
- Bone saw.
- Bullet probe (a long thin probe with a porcelain tip — when
  it touched lead, it left a gray mark).
- Forceps.
- Trephine (skull saw, for relieving pressure on the brain).
- Scalpels.
- Catgut and silk for sutures.
- Tincture of opium (laudanum).
- Calomel (mercurous chloride — given for nearly everything,
  poisonous, caused tooth loss, ulcers, salivation).
- Whiskey or chloroform for anesthesia.
- Quinine for malaria.
- Mercury (for syphilis — the famous _one night with Venus,
  a lifetime with Mercury_).

His fee in a working town was $1–$2 for a visit, $5 for setting
a bone, $10–$25 for a major operation, $5–$15 for delivering a
baby. A country doctor rode 30 miles to a homestead birth, often
for nothing or for a chicken and a cured ham.

He knew germ theory was being discussed in the East after the
late 1860s. He may or may not have washed his hands or his
instruments. Joseph Lister's antiseptic surgery (carbolic acid
spray) reached the U.S. through the 1870s; many frontier
doctors never adopted it.

## 2. Gunshot wounds — what actually happened

A pistol or rifle wound was a death sentence with terrible
frequency. Black-powder firearms drove fragments of cloth from
the man's clothing into the wound, fouling it. Antibiotics did
not exist. Sterile technique did not exist on the frontier.

### Wounds by location

**Head.** Almost always fatal. A man shot through the brain
was dead before he hit the ground. A man shot in the face who
survived the first hour usually survived (jaws and cheekbones
absorbed the ball). A graze along the temple sometimes left a
man unconscious for hours and concussed for weeks.

**Chest, lungs.** Frequently fatal. The lung collapsed. The
man drowned in his own blood over hours. Sucking chest wounds
were sealed with a wet cloth or a flap of leather to allow the
man to breathe. Many such men died in three to twelve hours.
Some survived if the bullet missed major vessels and the great
blood vessels, but tuberculosis and chronic infection were
common after.

**Heart, great vessels.** Death in seconds to minutes.

**Abdomen.** The worst. Almost certainly fatal. Perforated bowel
spilled feces into the abdominal cavity. Peritonitis set in
over 24 to 72 hours. The man died of sepsis in three to seven
days, in agony, with high fever, distended belly, vomiting,
and finally coma. Frontier doctors did not open the abdomen.
A gut-shot man was given laudanum and made comfortable.

**Limb.** Survivable if the bullet missed the major vessels and
bone. Often a compound fracture (bone broken and broken through
the skin). The treatment was usually amputation if the wound
was severe — done with a bone saw, with whiskey or chloroform
if available, with the patient held down by friends if not.
Mortality after amputation: 30–50% from infection, depending
on the surgeon's hygiene.

**Spine.** Paralysis below the wound. Most such men died of
infection from pressure sores within weeks.

### What it sounds and looks like

- A pistol ball at twenty feet does not knock a man down. He
  staggers, sits, sometimes stands a moment looking surprised.
- The blood is not bright at first; it darkens.
- A lung-shot man breathes with a wet rattle and a faint hiss
  through the wound.
- A gut-shot man pales gray-green within an hour. Fever sets in
  in twelve. By day three he is delirious.
- A man shot in the abdomen often complains of being cold. He
  asks for water, which the doctor refuses (a perforated
  bowel cannot tolerate it).
- The smell of a gut wound by day three is unmistakable.

### Treatment

- Probe for the ball with a bullet probe. Extract if reachable.
- Pack the wound with lint or with charcoal.
- If a limb wound, splint or amputate.
- Laudanum for pain.
- Whiskey for shock.
- Wait.

If the patient survived the first 48 hours and did not develop
fever, he had a chance. If he developed fever and a foul
discharge, he was probably going to die.

## 3. Infection — the real killer

More men died of infection in the 19th century than of the
original wounds. Sepsis, pyemia, gangrene, tetanus.

### Sepsis (blood poisoning)

- Onset 2–7 days after wound.
- Symptoms: high fever, racing pulse, chills, confusion, falling
  blood pressure.
- Mortality before antibiotics: 70–90%.
- Treatment in 1875: nothing effective. Cool cloths, whiskey,
  prayer.

### Gangrene

- Wet gangrene (bacterial): wound turns gray-green, swells,
  stinks like rotting meat. The smell is so foul attendants
  could not stay in the room. Amputation upstream of the
  rotting tissue was the only chance. Often it was already too
  late.
- Dry gangrene (frostbite, ergotism, vascular disease): tissue
  turns black and shriveled. Less acutely lethal but still
  required amputation.

### Tetanus (lockjaw)

- Onset 7–14 days after a puncture wound (a horse manure-fouled
  cut, a barbed wire scratch, a nail puncture).
- Symptoms: stiffness of the jaw progressing to total muscular
  rigidity, fever, convulsions.
- Mortality: 80–90%.
- Treatment in 1875: none. Vaccine developed 1924.

### Erysipelas (St. Anthony's fire)

- Streptococcal skin infection. Bright-red advancing rash, high
  fever. Sometimes survivable, often fatal in the very young
  or old.

## 4. Disease

### Cholera

- Bacterial gut infection from contaminated water (Vibrio
  cholerae).
- Massive watery diarrhea (_rice-water stool_), violent
  vomiting, dehydration, death by cardiovascular collapse in
  hours to days.
- Multiple cholera waves through the West: 1832–34, 1849–54
  (devastated the wagon trains and the gold rush), 1866–67.
  Whole emigrant trains died on the trail. Graves at every
  spring.
- Treatment: nothing effective until rehydration was understood.

### Typhoid

- Salmonella typhi. Slow-onset fever rising daily for a week.
  Rose spots on the abdomen. Bowel perforation in the third
  week. Mortality 10–20%.
- Endemic across the frontier. Bad water.

### Typhus

- Rickettsial. Spread by lice. Camp fever. Common in soldiers,
  prisoners, the very poor. High fever, rash, delirium. 10–60%
  mortality.

### Smallpox

- Variola virus. Highly contagious. Fever, then the
  characteristic pustular rash. Mortality 30%, scarring almost
  universal in survivors. Vaccination available since 1796 and
  practiced widely in the East but spotty on the frontier.
  Native populations who had not been exposed had mortality
  approaching 90% in some outbreaks (the 1837 Mandan epidemic
  killed approximately 90% of the tribe).

### Tuberculosis (consumption, the white plague)

- Mycobacterium tuberculosis. The leading cause of death in
  the United States in the 19th century. Slow-onset cough,
  weight loss, fever, night sweats, and the famous bloody
  cough. Many tubercular Easterners moved West for the dry
  climate (Doc Holliday among them). Many died there anyway.
- A tubercular character is period-correct and gives the writer
  the cough, the handkerchief, the slow wasting, the second-act
  collapse.

### Diphtheria

- Bacterial throat infection. Children especially. A gray
  membrane formed across the throat and suffocated the child.
  Mortality in the unimmunized 30–50%. The child died gasping.
  Antitoxin developed 1894.

### Dysentery

- _The bloody flux._ Endemic. Common in soldiers, drovers,
  emigrants. Often fatal in the very young or weak.

### Malaria (ague)

- Common in the Mississippi valley and the Southeast, less in
  the dry West. Treated with quinine when available.

### Syphilis

- Common in the West, given the demography. Three stages:
  primary (chancre), secondary (rash, fever, then years of
  latency), tertiary (gummas, neurosyphilis, paresis). Treated
  with mercury, salvarsan after 1910. Penicillin not until
  1940s. A common cause of madness, blindness, paralysis in
  late-period adults.

### Gonorrhea

- _The clap._ Endemic among soldiers and drovers. Treated with
  silver nitrate, with little success.

### Childhood diseases

- Measles, mumps, whooping cough, scarlet fever — common, often
  fatal. Many homesteads had a small graveyard with three or
  four children's stones.

## 5. Childbirth

A woman in 1875 had roughly a 1-in-100 chance of dying in any
given childbirth. She had eight to twelve childbirths over a
lifetime. Maternal mortality across a reproductive lifetime
approached 1 in 8.

- Most births were attended by a midwife or a neighbor woman, not
  a doctor.
- Puerperal fever (_childbed fever_ — streptococcal infection of
  the uterus, often spread by the unwashed hands of the
  attendant) was the leading killer. Onset 2–10 days after
  delivery, high fever, foul discharge, death within a week.
- Hemorrhage. Eclampsia. Obstructed labor. Ruptured uterus.
  Each killed.
- The infant: roughly 1 in 5 died in the first year, roughly
  1 in 3 by age five.
- A homestead family of ten pregnancies often raised five or
  six children to adulthood and buried four or five.

A scene of birth on the page should reflect this — not as horror
for its own sake, but as the weight families carried.

## 6. Medicines, period-correct

- **Laudanum** (tincture of opium in alcohol). The universal
  painkiller. Bottle in every kitchen. Addiction common.
- **Paregoric** (camphorated tincture of opium). Milder,
  given to children for colic and diarrhea. Many an
  infant died of an overdose.
- **Calomel** (mercury chloride). Given for almost
  everything. Caused mercury poisoning.
- **Quinine.** Malaria.
- **Salts** (Epsom). Laxative.
- **Castor oil.** Laxative.
- **Cod-liver oil.** Tonic.
- **Whiskey or brandy.** Anesthesia, painkiller, stimulant for
  shock, treatment for snakebite (useless), drink.
- **Patent medicines.** Hostetter's Bitters, Lydia Pinkham's
  Vegetable Compound (high alcohol content), Hamlin's Wizard
  Oil. The medicine show. Many were 30–60% alcohol or laced
  with opium or cocaine. They sold because they worked, in a
  fashion: the patient felt better.
- **Snake oil.** Originally Chinese laborer's medicine made from
  Chinese water snake (with anti-inflammatory effect); appro-
  priated and faked by white peddlers selling unrelated mineral
  oil. Hence the term.
- **Carbolic acid (phenol).** As an antiseptic, after the
  1870s, in advanced practice.

## 7. Surgery

A frontier doctor performed amputations, set fractures, sutured
wounds, lanced abscesses, pulled teeth, delivered babies, and
occasionally trephined the skull or removed a bullet from a
limb. He almost never opened the chest or abdomen — the
mortality was so near total it was not attempted.

Anesthesia:

- **Whiskey** straight (not a true anesthetic; reduced inhibition
  and dulled sensation). The patient screamed.
- **Chloroform** on a cloth held over the face, after 1847. Risk
  of overdose; the patient occasionally died on the table from
  the chloroform itself.
- **Ether** likewise.
- **Local anesthesia** with cocaine after 1884.

Many surgeries were done with the patient awake and held down.

## 8. The dying — what it actually looks like

For the writer. Real death is messier and stranger than the
page usually allows.

### Cholera death

- Onset within hours of the first watery stool.
- Patient ages decades in a day. Eyes sink. Skin gray-blue
  (the _cholera face_). Hands and feet ice-cold while the body
  is fevered. Voice fails to a whisper. The man asks for water
  and water cannot stay in him.
- Death in 12–48 hours. The body is shrunken, the eyes still
  open.

### Pneumonia death

- The _old man's friend._ High fever, productive cough with
  bloody or rust-colored sputum. The breath grows shallow.
  By the third or fourth day a wet rattle is audible across
  the room (the _death rattle_ — fluid in the airways the
  patient can no longer clear). The patient becomes confused,
  then unconscious. Death is quiet.

### Gut-shot death

- Day 1: The man complains of the wound, of cold, of thirst.
  The doctor refuses water. Laudanum is given.
- Day 2: Fever. The belly distends. Vomiting begins, dark and
  foul.
- Day 3: Delirium. The man calls for his mother, for his wife,
  for men dead twenty years. The smell from the wound is foul.
- Day 4–7: Coma. Death.

### Tuberculosis death

- Years of slow wasting. The patient is light as a child by
  the end. The cheek is flushed (the _hectic flush_) in the
  afternoon, pale by morning. The cough, the bloody handker-
  chief, the night sweats.
- Death often by sudden hemorrhage — the patient coughs and
  arterial blood fills the mouth, and he drowns in it in
  minutes. Or the patient slips quietly away in the morning
  after a long night.

### Hanging death

- The drop. The neck breaks if the drop is calculated correctly
  (4–6 feet for a man of average weight). Death in seconds.
- The slow strangle — if the drop is too short or the rope
  improperly placed. Fifteen to twenty minutes of strangulation,
  the man's face purple, his tongue protruded, his bowels and
  bladder voiding. Witnesses said it was the worst thing they
  ever saw.
- The vigilante hanging from a tree branch with a horse pulled
  out from under: usually the slow strangle.

### Frozen death

- Hypothermia. Confusion. Then warmth and a feeling of well-
  being (terminal burrowing — the man undresses, paradoxical
  warming). Then unconsciousness, then death. Found in the
  spring melt with his shirt unbuttoned, his hat off.

### Thirst death

- 24 hours: thirst. 36: weakness, headache. 48: confusion.
  60: the tongue swells, the lips crack and bleed, the man
  cannot swallow. 72: hallucination, fever, coma. 96: death.
  In hot sun the timeline shortens to two or three days.

### Old age death

- Pneumonia, often. Stroke. Heart failure, slow.
- A man of seventy in 1875 was an old man. A woman of sixty
  was an old woman. Life expectancy from birth was around 40
  due to infant mortality; a man who reached 20 might reach
  60 or 65.

## 9. Burial

- **Graveyards.** Town graveyards on a hill outside town,
  fenced if the town could afford it. Wooden markers usually,
  stone for the affluent. Many graves unmarked within twenty
  years of burial.
- **Trail graves.** A rock cairn on the site, sometimes a
  marker. Many graves dug across the wagon trails.
- **Boot Hill.** The graveyard for the violent dead — those who
  died with their boots on. Tombstone, Dodge City, Deadwood,
  many others had one.
- **Cremation.** Almost unknown among Anglos in this period.
  Practiced by some Native nations.
- **Sky burial / scaffold burial.** Lakota and other plains
  tribes. The body wrapped in a blanket and lashed to a
  platform on poles or in a tree, exposed to the elements and
  scavengers.
- **Coffins.** Pine box, six boards, made by the town
  carpenter for $5–$15. The undertaker did not exist as a
  profession in many small towns; the carpenter built the
  coffin and the family or the deceased's friends dug the grave.
- **Embalming.** Civil War practice spread; common by the 1880s
  for shipping a body home. Uncommon in working towns where
  the body went into the ground the next day.

## 10. Writing the dying

The reader does not need every detail. The right detail.

Bad:

> Tom died slowly and painfully over the course of three days
> from his terrible gunshot wound to the abdomen, and everyone
> mourned him deeply.

Better:

> The smell came on the second day. By the third the doctor
> would not go in the room. Tom asked for water and they gave
> him laudanum instead. He spoke to his mother once, in the
> night, and after that did not speak again.

The trick is to choose two or three concrete sensory details,
trust the reader to do the math, and stop. Write the smell, the
refused water, the one moment of speech. The rest is in the
reader's mind, and what is in the reader's mind hits harder than
what is on the page.
