# Frontier Survival — Land, Water, Weather, Distance

The country in a western is a character, sometimes the antagonist.
A writer who does not know what kills a man on the Llano Estacado
or how far a horse can travel in a day cannot write the West. This
file is the reference for staying alive (or failing to) in the
country between the Mississippi and the Sierras.

---

## 1. Water

Water is the first problem on the western half of the continent.
West of the 100th meridian (roughly a line through central Texas,
Oklahoma, Kansas, Nebraska, the Dakotas), rainfall drops below
20 inches a year. West of the 105th meridian it drops below 15.
Nothing grows without irrigation. Travel is structured around
water, not around terrain.

### Daily need

- A man on foot in summer heat needs a gallon a day to live, two
  to work. He will die in three days without water in 100°F sun,
  in five in cooler weather.
- A horse needs 8–12 gallons a day. A working horse in the heat
  needs more. A horse will not work hard the day after a dry
  night and will die in four to seven days without water.
- An ox needs 10–15 gallons a day.
- A wagon train of ten families and their stock needed a creek
  every night, period.

### Sources

- Springs (the prized stop on a trail; many trails followed
  spring lines).
- Creeks and rivers (often alkali in the high plains, often
  fouled by buffalo or by stock above).
- Rain catchment in tanks (the _tinajas_ of southern Arizona —
  natural rock pools holding rainwater for weeks).
- Hand-dug wells on the homestead (10–60 feet typical; some
  reached 200 feet on the high plains).
- Windmill wells (after 1860s; in widespread use 1880s onward).
- Snow, melted, in winter.
- Buffalo wallows after a rain (the wallow held water for days,
  brown and stinking but drinkable).

### Bad water

- Alkali (white-crust water on the high plains). Causes severe
  diarrhea; can kill stock and men.
- Stock-fouled (cattle or buffalo standing in a hole turned it
  to soup).
- Stagnant. Mosquito-bred. Carrying typhoid, cholera, dysentery.
- Mineral (sulfur springs, salt springs).

### Purification

- Boiling. The reliable method, when fuel and time allowed.
  Most camps boiled water into coffee to make it safe (and
  this is one reason coffee was constant).
- Settling in a barrel with a handful of cornmeal — the meal
  drew dirt to the bottom in a few hours.
- Charcoal filter — a barrel with charcoal and sand, pioneer
  filtration.
- A man on the move drank what he had and trusted to luck.

### Talking about water in prose

Distances on the western trails are described by water:

- _Sixty miles to the next water._
- _We made the spring by sundown._
- _Three days without sweet water._
- _The Pecos was up. We went on without crossing._

A scene that ignores water in dry country reads false to anyone
who has ridden it.

## 2. Weather

Western weather kills more often than men do. Each region has
its own killers. The reader who knows the country knows the
weather; do not get it wrong.

### a. The high plains (Texas Panhandle through the Dakotas)

- **Blue norther.** A cold front out of Canada that drops the
  temperature 50°F in two hours. Black-blue cloud bank on the
  northern horizon. Wind to 50 mph. Stock and unprepared men
  die. Stories of riders frozen to the saddle. Common
  October–April.
- **Chinook.** A warm dry wind off the Rockies in winter that
  raises the temperature 40°F in a day and melts snow. Welcome
  but treacherous — the melt re-freezes that night.
- **Hailstorm.** Summer. Hail the size of marbles to baseballs.
  Kills calves and drives a herd into stampede.
- **Tornado.** Spring and summer. The warning was the green
  cast in the western sky.
- **Lightning.** The leading cause of stampedes. A single bolt
  hitting a tree near the herd put 2,500 cattle on their feet
  in seconds.
- **Drought.** Multi-year droughts (notably 1886–87 on the
  northern plains) killed millions of cattle. The _Big Die-Up_
  ended the open-range era.
- **The Hard Winter of 1886–87.** Snow in November, blizzards
  through January, temperatures below −40°F across Montana and
  Wyoming. Cattle starved by the hundreds of thousands. Charles
  M. Russell drew it on a postcard: a single starving cow
  surrounded by wolves, captioned _Waiting for a Chinook._

### b. The Southwest desert (Arizona, New Mexico, west Texas)

- **Heat.** Summer afternoons over 110°F. A man without water
  dies in hours.
- **Cold nights.** Desert temperatures drop 50°F after sundown.
  A man without a blanket can freeze in July.
- **Dust storm (haboob).** A wall of brown dust sometimes
  thousands of feet high, advancing across the desert ahead of
  a thunderstorm. Visibility to zero.
- **Flash flood.** A summer thunderstorm 20 miles upstream can
  send a wall of water down a dry arroyo without warning. The
  arroyo a man slept in last night becomes the river that
  drowns him today.
- **Sandstorm.** Wind off the desert with sand at 40 mph.
  Eyes, mouth, lungs choked.

### c. The mountains (Rockies, Sierras, Cascades, Bitterroots)

- **Snow.** September through May at altitude. Snowed-in passes,
  snowed-in cabins.
- **Avalanche.** On steep slopes after fresh snow.
- **Lightning above timberline.** Strikes the ridge man.
- **Hypothermia.** A wet rider in a Rocky Mountain summer rain
  at 9,000 feet can be in serious trouble in two hours.
- **Altitude sickness.** A man from the lowlands climbing fast
  to 10,000 feet is sick the first night.

### d. The Pacific coast and Great Basin

- **Sierra winter.** Donner-Party country. The Sierras catch
  Pacific storms and dump 30 feet of snow per season at altitude.
- **Great Basin summer.** Hot, dry, alkali. Death Valley.
- **Coast fog.** Wet, cold, persistent. A man on horseback
  along the California coast in winter is wet for three months.

### e. The southern plains (Texas, Oklahoma)

- **Heat with humidity** in eastern Texas. Dehydrating, sapping.
- **Norther** as on the high plains.
- **Tornado** alley.
- **Drought** cycles.
- **Hurricane** along the coast (the 1875 and 1886 Indianola
  hurricanes destroyed the Texas port).

## 3. Fire

Fire is life and fire is death.

### Starting a fire

- **Matches.** Available everywhere by the 1850s. Lucifer
  matches in the 1830s, friction matches by the 1860s. A man
  carried matches in a tin or a watertight rubber bag. Wet
  matches were useless.
- **Flint and steel.** Older method, kept as backup. A
  flint-and-steel man with charcloth and good tinder could
  raise a flame in two minutes in dry weather, twenty in wet.
- **Burning glass** (a magnifying lens) in sun.
- **Pistol shot into a wad of tinder.** Mountain man's trick.
- **Bow drill and friction.** Native technique. Slow.

### Tinder, kindling, fuel

- Tinder: dry grass, cottonwood fluff, cedar bark shredded,
  pitch wood, the dry inside of a fallen log.
- Kindling: dry twigs, splints split from a dry stick.
- Fuel: whatever the country gave. Mesquite (best on the
  southern plains). Cottonwood (the river-bottom standby).
  Pine (the mountains). Sage (high desert; burns hot and
  fast). **Buffalo chips and cow chips** (dried dung, the
  prairie's universal fuel where wood was absent — the high
  plains burned chips for fifty years).

### Cooking fire vs warming fire

- Cooking fire: small, contained, in a pit if possible.
  Cooked over coals after the flame burned down.
- Warming fire: larger. The reflector built behind the fire to
  throw heat at the bedroll. The lone-rider's small fire was
  three sticks under a coffee can.

### Wildfire

- Prairie fires raced faster than a horse could run, fueled by
  dry grass. A grass fire could cover ten miles in an hour with
  a wind behind it.
- **Backfire** — the only defense. Burn the ground in front of
  the advancing fire, retreat onto the burned ground.
- A man caught in the open with no time to backfire shot a cow
  or his horse, slit it, and lay in the carcass while the fire
  passed over. (Documented practice. Survived.)

## 4. Navigation

There were maps. They were often wrong. The wagon trails were
known and marked by ruts and graves. Off the trails a man
navigated by sun, by stars, by landmarks, by dead reckoning.

### Sun and time

- A pocket watch, kept wound and reset against true noon (the
  sun at zenith). Solar noon was the practical reset.
- A man without a watch read the sun's height to estimate time.
- The shadow at noon pointed north (in the northern hemisphere).

### Stars

- The North Star (Polaris). Found by the pointer stars of the
  Big Dipper.
- A traveler at night moved by the stars.

### Landmarks

- A mountain peak, a butte, a notch on the horizon. A traveler
  carried a mental map of his country.
- The Native scouts knew the country in detail no Anglo
  surveyor matched. The Army hired Pawnee, Apache, Crow scouts
  for this reason.

### Dead reckoning

- Estimate of speed and direction over time gives position. A
  man riding 30 miles a day on a known compass heading knew
  approximately where he was on the third day even with no
  landmark.

### Trail signs

Plains tribes and mountain men marked trails:

- A bent grass clump with the bend pointing the direction.
- Stones piled in a small cairn.
- A blaze cut on a tree (in country that had trees).
- A skull on a stake at the head of a draw.

A man who could read sign could follow a trail two days old.
A man who could not could pass within ten yards of a man hiding
in the brush.

## 5. Distance and travel speed

The reader's modern sense of distance is wrong. Get this right
or lose verisimilitude.

| Mode                                           | Daily distance, good country                            |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Man on foot, packed                            | 15–20 miles                                             |
| Man on foot, lightly packed, hard              | 25–30 miles                                             |
| Saddle horse, walked, all day                  | 30–40 miles                                             |
| Saddle horse, alternating walk-trot            | 40–50 miles                                             |
| Saddle horse, hard ride, one day               | 60–80 miles (horse exhausted)                           |
| Pony Express rider, fresh horse every 10–15 mi | 200+ miles in 24 h                                      |
| Cattle drive (herd)                            | 10–15 miles                                             |
| Wagon train (ox)                               | 10–15 miles                                             |
| Wagon train (mule)                             | 15–20 miles                                             |
| Stagecoach, with team changes                  | 100–125 miles in 24 h                                   |
| Train                                          | 20–30 mph cruising; coast-to-coast in 7 days after 1869 |

### Horse-killing facts

- A horse run hard for a day is no good for a week.
- A horse run hard for two days is dead or ruined.
- A man riding for his life changed horses if he could. If he
  could not, he killed the horse he was on by the second day.
- Killing a man's horse in the desert was killing the man.

### Walking

Hands hated to walk. Boots were not made for it. A cowhand
afoot was a man in trouble, and the joke in the bunkhouse was
_a cowboy on the ground is just a man with the wrong hat._

### River crossings

A flooded river stopped travel. Wagons were caulked (the box
sealed with pitch and floated as a boat) or built up on logs
or simply waited. Many drowned.

## 6. Regional terrain — the country itself

A working knowledge of where things are. (This is a writer's
brief, not a geographer's map.)

### a. The Llano Estacado (Staked Plains)

- West Texas, eastern New Mexico. A flat, treeless, water-poor
  high plateau, escarpment on the east. Comanche country until
  Mackenzie's 1874 Palo Duro campaign broke them.
- Almost no surface water. The few playas (rain-pools) were
  the only stops.
- The Comanchero traders crossed it east-to-west to trade with
  the tribes.

### b. The Brazos and Pecos

- The Brazos River — central Texas, ran east, the heart of
  ranch country.
- The Pecos River — west Texas / eastern New Mexico, ran
  south, alkali, treacherous quicksand. _West of the Pecos_
  came to mean lawless country (Judge Roy Bean's _Law West of
  the Pecos_).

### c. The Sonoran Desert

- Southern Arizona, northern Mexico. Saguaro country. Cholla,
  ocotillo, mesquite. Apache country.
- Hot. Dry. Beautiful in the spring bloom.

### d. The Mojave

- Southern California, southern Nevada. Higher and colder than
  the Sonoran. Joshua trees. Death Valley.

### e. The Sierra Nevada

- California's eastern wall. The Donner Pass. Yosemite.
  Goldfields on the western slope (1849 onward).

### f. The Rockies

- Spine of the continent. Pikes Peak, Long's Peak, the Tetons,
  the Wind Rivers, the Bitterroots. Mining country: gold and
  silver in Colorado (Cripple Creek, Leadville), Montana
  (Helena, Butte), the Black Hills.

### g. The Great Basin

- Nevada and western Utah. Cold desert, high, salty.
  Mormon country (Salt Lake settled 1847).
  Comstock Lode (Virginia City) silver.

### h. The Black Hills

- Western South Dakota / eastern Wyoming. Lakota sacred
  ground. Custer's 1874 expedition found gold; the Gold Rush
  of 1875–76 brought Deadwood, Lead, the breaking of the
  treaty, the Great Sioux War, the Little Bighorn.

### i. The Northern Plains (Wyoming, Montana, Dakotas)

- High, cold, rolling grass. Lakota, Cheyenne, Crow,
  Blackfoot, Arapaho country. Cattle country after 1880, ruined
  by 1886–87.

### j. The Indian Territory

- Modern Oklahoma. Resettled tribes from the Trail of Tears
  (Cherokee, Choctaw, Creek, Chickasaw, Seminole) in the east.
  Wild tribes and the unassigned lands in the west. _No-Man's
  Land_ on the panhandle. Outlaws hid here. Federal court at
  Fort Smith (Judge Parker, the _Hanging Judge_) had
  jurisdiction.

### k. The Pacific Northwest

- Oregon, Washington, Idaho. Wetter, forested. Nez Perce
  country (Chief Joseph's 1877 retreat). Settlement after the
  Oregon Trail in the 1840s.

## 7. Survival skills the cowhand assumed

A working westerner could:

- Build a fire in wind and rain.
- Catch a horse in a corral and saddle it.
- Pack a horse or a mule for a multi-day trip.
- Find north by the sun or the stars.
- Read animal sign and human sign on the ground.
- Skin and butcher a deer or a beef.
- Cook over a fire (poorly, usually).
- Shoot a pistol and a rifle to working accuracy.
- Doctor a horse for cuts, sores, bad shoes, mild colic.
- Doctor himself for cuts, splinters, snakebite (badly), gunshot
  (very badly).
- Ride 50 miles in a day if he had to.
- Sleep on the ground in any weather short of a blizzard.

A character on the page who cannot do these things is either a
greenhorn (and the prose plays it for what it is) or a
townsman, an Easterner, a tenderfoot. Set this expectation
clearly and the country reads honest.

## 8. The country as antagonist

The land in the West does not care. It is the central fact and
the central terror of the literature. Cormac McCarthy's
_Blood Meridian,_ A. B. Guthrie's _The Big Sky,_ Walter Van
Tilburg Clark's _The Track of the Cat_ — the land in these books
is older than the men, larger than the men, and indifferent. The
men move across it and the land goes on. Write the land like
that and the prose will carry the West's actual weight: not
_adventure,_ but _trespass._ The men are passing through. The
country was here before and will be here after.
