# Prices and Anchors — The Concrete Numbers of the 1870s West

This file is a working anchor sheet. Drop the right number into
the right scene and the prose grounds itself; pick the wrong
one and the scene drifts. The numbers below are period-correct
to the 1870s American West (with annotations where prices
varied sharply by year, region, or boom).

This file complements `knowledge/towns-economy-law.md`
(institutional context), `knowledge/material-culture.md` (gear
specs), and `knowledge/1870s-availability.md` (whether the
thing existed at all). The price tables in those files remain
authoritative; this file expands them and adds the _use
cases_ — when to reach for which number.

---

## 1. How to use a number in prose

A price in prose is not a price tag. It is a fact that does
work in the scene. The work is one of three things:

1. **The pinch.** The character cannot afford it. The number
   measures the gap between desire and means.
2. **The flush.** The character can afford it easily. The
   number measures freedom — the man with $300 in his pocket
   has options the man with $3 does not.
3. **The pact.** A price is named in a deal. The number is
   the lever. _Two hundred dollars in gold or one hundred
   head of your own steers._

A number that does no work in the scene is decoration. Cut it.

**Rule.** Drop a price the way the period dropped a price —
plain, in the same line as the action.

> _Bowman laid two silver dollars on the bar._

Not:

> _Bowman placed a sum of two dollars in silver coinage,
> representing payment for his drink, on the polished wood
> of the bar's surface._

The first earns the period. The second commits four AI
felonies in a sentence (Latinate qualifier stack, _placed_
for _laid,_ _representing payment_ explanation, decorated
noun phrase).

## 2. Wages — the working West

A worker's wage is the most useful anchor a scene can carry.
It tells the reader what a man's day is worth, and against
that it lets the reader weigh every price he encounters.

### a. Cattle country

| Role                              | Pay                       | Notes                                                                                                                                           |
| --------------------------------- | ------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Cowhand (working)                 | $25-40/month + found      | _Found_ = food, lodging, sometimes a horse. Cash pay only. Drovers paid for the months of the drive (April-October typically), then turned out. |
| Top hand                          | $40-50/month + found      | The most experienced; took the hardest jobs.                                                                                                    |
| Trail boss                        | $100-125/month + found    | For the duration of a drive only.                                                                                                               |
| Cook                              | $40-60/month + found      | More than a hand. Skilled work.                                                                                                                 |
| Wrangler (horse-handler)          | $20-30/month + found      | Often a youth.                                                                                                                                  |
| Segundo (second to trail boss)    | $60-80/month + found      | Drive-period only.                                                                                                                              |
| Ranch foreman (resident)          | $60-100/month + found     | Year-round work.                                                                                                                                |
| Stock detective / range detective | $100-150/month + expenses | Hired by cattlemen's associations.                                                                                                              |

A drive ran four to six months. A cowhand finished a typical
drive with $100-200 in cash, and most spent it in two weeks
on whiskey, women, gambling, hotel, clothing, and a good
meal. The phrase _broke as a cowboy in November_ is period.

### b. Mining country

| Role                                 | Pay            | Notes                                                                                                                            |
| ------------------------------------ | -------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Hardrock miner (Comstock, Leadville) | $3.50-4/day    | Union scale where a union held; $3 elsewhere. The eight-hour day was a union demand from the 1860s; the ten-hour day was common. |
| Mucker (shoveling broken rock)       | $2.50-3/day    | Less skilled.                                                                                                                    |
| Powderman (handling explosives)      | $4-5/day       | Extra hazard pay.                                                                                                                |
| Pump man (keeping the mine dry)      | $3.50/day      |                                                                                                                                  |
| Hoist operator                       | $4/day         | Skilled.                                                                                                                         |
| Mill hand (ore reduction)            | $3/day         |                                                                                                                                  |
| Placer miner (own claim)             | variable       | Some weeks $0; some weeks the strike. The romance and the bankruptcy.                                                            |
| Mine superintendent                  | $200-500/month | Engineer's pay; college-educated.                                                                                                |
| Assayer                              | $100-200/month | Skilled chemical work.                                                                                                           |

Mining-camp prices ran 2-3x cattle-country prices because
everything came in by mule until the railroad reached the
camp.

## 4. Distance and time anchors

The other half of the anchor is _how far_ and _how long._ A
character can leave Lampasas at sunup and reach Austin by
nightfall (eighty miles) only on a fresh horse and a hard
ride that would lame the horse. A character on the trail
between Texas and Kansas does not arrive in three weeks; he
arrives in three months. The wrong distance breaks the scene
the way the wrong gear does.

## 6. The composite anchor — example sums

These are useful set pieces — the kinds of money totals a
1870s scene reaches for. Drop them and the period reads
itself.

### a. The cowhand's roll at end of drive

- $25/month for 4 months on the trail = $100.
- Less small stable-hand and outfitting deductions = $80-90
  cash in the hand at Abilene or Dodge.
- Gone in 10-14 days. The bunkhouse routine: rooming $1,
  bath 50¢, shave 15¢, new shirt $1, dinner $1, whiskey at
  the saloon $5, faro losses $20, dance hall $5, parlor
  house $15. Repeat.

### b. The trail boss's stake

- $125/month for 5 months = $625.
- A trail boss who has run drives for several years has
  enough to buy his own outfit and become a small cattleman.

### c. The mining strike

- A placer claim that yields one ounce of gold a day at $20
  the ounce = $600/month. The claim that yields _only_ one
  ounce a day is a good claim. A bonanza claim yields
  pounds of gold in a week. The Bonanza Kings of the
  Comstock made millions.

### d. The bank robbery

- The James-Younger gang took $700 in the Northfield raid
  before being shot to pieces. The largest train robberies
  of the period took $5,000-$15,000 in cash and gold from
  the express car safe. The largest stage robberies took
  $1,000-$5,000. The bank in a small town held perhaps
  $5,000-$20,000 in the vault.

### e. The widow's annual budget (small Western town, 1875)

- Income (taking in laundry, sewing): $30-40/month, $360-
  480/year.
- Rent on a small frame house: $5-10/month, $60-120/year.
- Food for one woman and one child: $15-20/month, $180-240.
- Clothing, fuel, medicine, school for the child: $10-15/
  month, $120-180.
- Net: she breaks even in a good year, slips into debt at
  the mercantile in a bad one.

### f. The cattleman's profit

- 2,500 head trailed from Texas to Kansas. Cost in Texas:
  $15-20/head delivered to the trailhead in south Texas =
  $40,000. Trail cost (cowhands, cook, horses, supplies,
  losses): $5,000-7,000. Sale at Abilene: $25-30/head =
  $65,000. Net profit: $15,000-20,000.
- A successful drive made the trail boss rich and the
  cowman richer. A drive that lost half the herd to
  stampede or river ruined both.

### g. The doctor's fees over a year (small Western town)

- 200 visits at $1-2 = $200-400.
- 50 house calls at $2-5 = $100-250.
- 10 deliveries at $5-15 = $50-150.
- 20 minor surgeries / bone settings at $5-10 = $100-200.
- 2 major surgeries at $25-100 = $50-200.
- Patent medicine sales: $100-300.
- Total gross: $600-1,500.
- Less unpaid debts (perhaps half): $300-750 net.

A frontier doctor without a wealthy practice barely got by.

### h. The schoolmarm's year

- $30/month for 6-8 months (depending on district) = $180- 240.
- Board with a local family: free (counted as part of the
  pay).
- Net cash: she returns to the East with $50-100 saved if
  prudent.

## 8. The wages-to-prices ratio — what a man's day buys

A useful sanity check for any 1870s scene. A cowhand earns
roughly $1 a day after the trail's deductions and his food
is provided. His daily wage buys him (at saloon prices):

- 20 schooners of beer.
- 4 shots of decent whiskey.
- 1 cheap meal at the hash house and 4 schooners.
- 4 boxes of Bull Durham.
- 2 cigars.
- 1 bath.
- A shave and a haircut and a beer.

A hardrock miner earns roughly $3.50 a day. After paying
$1.25-1.50 for board (he buys his own), he has $2 of
discretionary income — twice the cowhand's. The miner's
camp prices are higher, however; the actual margin runs
similar.

A schoolmarm earns roughly $1 a day after board. Her
discretionary spending is mostly clothing (a $5 dress takes
a full week of net wages) and savings.

A captain in the Army earns $5/day plus allowances. He can
afford a hotel suite, imported wine, and the better tailor.
He cannot afford a ranch.

A small-town doctor in a slow year earns $50/month — about
the same as a sergeant — but is in debt for his medical
education and his stock of patent medicine.

The largest gulf in the period's economy was between cash
labor (the cowhand at $1/day) and the entrepreneurial classes
(the cattleman, the saloon owner, the mine owner) who could
clear thousands a year if they survived the cycle. A working
cowhand who saved his pay for ten years could buy a small
herd and become the next decade's small cattleman. Most did
not save.

## 9. The single rule

Every number in your scene is doing work or it is decoration.
If it is decoration, cut it. If it is work, get it right.

The reader who knows the period will trust the prose that
gets the silver dollar right. The reader who does not know
the period will trust the prose anyway, because the numbers
fit each other — the wage and the meal and the bullet and
the bottle all sit at the same level. Coherence is the
period's voice as much as diction is.

Drop the right number. Move on. Do not explain it.

> _He laid two silver dollars on the bar and the bartender
> set the bottle down between them._

That is the period in eighteen words. The bottle is a $1-2
rotgut bottle. Two silver dollars buys it. Both men know it.
The reader reads the scene without being told.

That is the use of an anchor.
