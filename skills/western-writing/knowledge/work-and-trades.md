# Work and Trades

A western lives or dies on the credibility of the work shown.
Most of the day in any character's life is the work he does to
stay fed. This file is the working knowledge of the trades that
filled the West.

Cross-reference `cowboy-life.md` for ranching detail.

---

## 1. Ranching

### a. Open range (roughly 1865–1886)

The system that made the western. After the Civil War, Texas
was overrun with maverick longhorns — cattle that had multiplied
on the open range during the war when no one was working them.
By 1866 a steer worth $4 in Texas was worth $20–$40 at a Kansas
railhead. The trail drives followed.

The open range:

- No fences (until barbed wire, patented 1874 by J. F. Glidden,
  spreading after 1875).
- Cattle ran on unfenced public domain land in commingled herds
  identified only by brand.
- Annual roundup brought all the cattle of an area into a
  central area, sorted them by brand, branded the spring
  calves, cut out the steers for shipping.
- Each ranch's home pasture was the area around the headquarters
  it could keep its hands on.
- Disputes resolved by stock associations, by the courts when
  necessary, by violence when neither worked.

### b. The Great Die-Up

The northern open range expanded enormously through the early
1880s as British and Eastern syndicates poured capital into
Wyoming, Montana, the Dakotas. The range was over-stocked. The
summer of 1886 was hot and dry; the grass burned early. The
winter of 1886–87 was the worst on record: snow from November,
temperatures below −40°F, blizzards that lasted days. Cattle
starved by the hundreds of thousands. Some outfits lost 90% of
their herd. The open-range system never recovered. Wire and
hay-fed feedlots replaced it.

### c. After the wire

By the 1890s the working ranch had:

- Fenced pastures.
- Hayfields cut for winter feed.
- Windmills for water.
- Better breeding stock (Hereford and Angus replacing the
  Texas longhorn).
- A smaller crew working a smaller area.

The trail drive was over by 1890. Cattle moved by rail.

### d. Sheep

A separate trade and a feud. Cattlemen hated sheep — the sheep
ate grass to the root, fouled the water, smelled wrong to cattle.
Sheep grazed New Mexico, Wyoming, Montana, Utah. The Hispanic
_pastores_ of New Mexico had run sheep since the 17th century.
Anglo and Basque sheep men moved north and west.

Sheepherder's life: solitary. A man and a dog and a wagon
followed several thousand head across the high country for
months. Lonely and dangerous; the herder was sometimes dry-
gulched (ambushed) by cattlemen.

Range wars between cattlemen and sheepmen: Tonto Basin War
(Arizona, 1880s-90s), various flare-ups in Wyoming, Colorado,
Montana through the 1890s.

## 2. Mining

### a. Placer mining

Working surface gravels and stream beds for free gold. The 1849
California Gold Rush was placer. Tools:

- The pan (gold pan, the iron _flash pan_ round, 12-inch).
- The rocker (a cradle that washed gravel through a screen).
- The long tom (a longer trough).
- The sluice (a long flume with riffles to catch the gold).
- Hydraulic mining (large monitors that washed entire
  hillsides into a sluice — environmental devastation,
  effectively banned in California by 1884).

Placer mining was the _poor man's mining_ — capital low, the
chance of independent success. Most placer ground was worked
out within a few years of discovery. Then the operations
consolidated and the men became wage-laborers.

### b. Hardrock mining

Following gold or silver veins underground. Capital-intensive.

- A shaft sunk vertically, with timbers (Cornish square-set in
  the Comstock — Philipp Deidesheimer's invention, 1860 — a
  cubic timbering that allowed mining of any width of vein).
- Drifts driven horizontally along the vein.
- Stopes — the open spaces left behind as ore was taken.
- Hoists (steam-powered cages) that brought men and ore to the
  surface.
- Pumps (Cornish pumps, then steam pumps) to keep the workings
  drained.

The work:

- Hand drilling: a _single-jack_ (one man with a hammer and a
  steel) or _double-jack_ (two men, one swinging a sledge, one
  holding and turning the steel). Strikes per minute mattered.
  The double-jack contests at Fourth of July picnics
  remembered the trade.
- After the 1870s, machine drilling (_widow makers_ — drills
  that filled the air with rock dust and gave the miner
  silicosis in five years).
- Black powder and after 1867 dynamite (Nobel's invention).
  _Lighting the fuse and counting_ — many miners blown up by
  miscount, by short fuse, by a hangfire that exploded as he
  walked back to check it.

The hardrock miner was paid $3–$4 a day in the 1870s in the
American West (compared to $1 a day in Cornish mines or
Pennsylvania coal). High wages. High mortality. Tuberculosis,
silicosis, broken bones, gas asphyxiation, cave-ins, fires.

### c. The major mineral districts

- Sierra Nevada gold (1849–1880s).
- Comstock Lode silver, Nevada (1859–1890s).
- Black Hills gold (1875–present, but the boom was 1875–80).
- Cripple Creek gold, Colorado (1891–1920s).
- Leadville silver and lead, Colorado (1877–1893; the silver
  panic of 1893 wrecked it).
- Tombstone silver (1877–1886).
- Butte copper (1880s onward — became the largest copper-
  producing district in the world).
- Coeur d'Alene silver-lead, Idaho (1880s onward — site of
  the violent labor wars).
- Bisbee copper, Arizona (1880s onward).

### d. Coal

- Wyoming, Colorado, Utah, New Mexico. Largely owned by the
  railroads (which used the coal). Worked by immigrant labor:
  Irish, Welsh, Italian, Slovak, Greek, Mexican, Japanese.
- Brutal conditions. Major massacres of striking miners
  (Ludlow Colorado, 1914 — Colorado Fuel and Iron Co. and the
  state militia killed 21, mostly women and children).

## 3. The railroad

### a. Construction

The Transcontinental Railroad (Central Pacific from Sacramento
east, Union Pacific from Omaha west) met at Promontory Point
Utah in May 1869. The Central Pacific over the Sierras was built
mostly by Chinese labor (12,000–20,000 men); the Union Pacific
across the plains was built mostly by Irish labor (often
Civil War veterans), with Mormons grading parts in Utah.

The work:

- Surveying the line.
- Grading (cutting and filling to a level grade).
- Tunneling (the Central Pacific tunneled fifteen Sierra
  tunnels, by hand drill and black powder; the deaths of
  Chinese workers in avalanches were noted by the company in
  brief).
- Bridge building (timber trestles in most cases, replaced
  later with iron and stone).
- Track laying — a crew laid up to ten miles of track in a
  single record-setting day on the Central Pacific.
- The _Hell on Wheels_ towns followed the railhead — temporary
  tent cities of saloons, gambling halls, brothels, and
  thieves that moved every few weeks. Cheyenne, North Platte,
  Laramie all began this way.

### b. Operation

By the 1880s the western railroads (Union Pacific, Central
Pacific / Southern Pacific, Northern Pacific, Atchison Topeka
& Santa Fe, Denver & Rio Grande, Great Northern) were the
single largest employers in the West.

The work:

- Engineer (driver of the locomotive). Top of the trade. Paid
  $4–$6 a day. Skilled and respected.
- Fireman (shoveled coal into the firebox). $2–$3 a day. Hard,
  dirty, dangerous.
- Conductor (in charge of the train).
- Brakeman (set the brakes by hand on each car before air
  brakes — an extraordinarily dangerous job, with high rates
  of death and limb-loss from coupling cars).
- Section hand (maintained a section of track). Often Chinese
  or Mexican on the western lines.
- Telegrapher (in every depot, sending and receiving train
  orders).
- Station agent (sold tickets, handled freight, managed the
  depot).

Train robberies were a real and lucrative crime. The James-
Younger gang, the Reno Gang, the Wild Bunch (Butch Cassidy's),
Sam Bass — train work paid better than bank work. The express
car was the target (Wells Fargo or American Express held cash
and gold shipments in safes). The Pinkertons rode the trains as
guards.

## 4. Freighting

Before the railroads reached every town and after, much of the
West moved by wagon.

- **Mule trains** — a string of pack mules in single file
  carrying ore from a mine to a railhead, supplies in to a
  remote camp.
- **Wagon trains** — long heavy wagons, often coupled in pairs
  (the _trail wagon_ coupled behind the _lead wagon_, with one
  team pulling both), drawn by 8 to 14 oxen or mules. The
  Russell, Majors & Waddell company at peak operated 75,000
  oxen and 6,250 wagons, employed 5,000 men.
- **Freighter (mule skinner / bullwhacker).** The man who drove
  the team. _Mule skinner_ for mule teams (worked from a saddle
  on the near-wheel mule, with a single rein — the _jerk
  line_ — and voice commands to the lead pair). _Bullwhacker_
  for ox teams (walked on the left side of the team with a
  long whip). A profane trade. The bullwhacker's
  _blue streak_ of cursing was famous.
- **Stagecoach.** Concord coach, 6-horse team, drivers (the
  _whip_ or _jehu_ held the reins; the _messenger_ sat next to
  him with a coach gun). Through the 1870s and 1880s before the
  rail extension closed each route. Wells Fargo, Butterfield
  Overland Mail, Holladay's Overland.

The freighter / driver was a respected character. Charles Russell
painted them. Mark Twain rode the Overland west and wrote
_Roughing It_ about it.

## 5. Smithing

The blacksmith / farrier was indispensable in any town and at
any major ranch.

- Shoeing horses ($1 the round). The smith heated, shaped, and
  nailed iron shoes to the horse's hoof, trimming the hoof
  with knife and rasp. A bad shoeing crippled a horse.
- Wagon work. Setting tires (heating an iron tire and shrinking
  it on the wooden wheel rim — the _sound_ of the cold water
  hitting the hot tire).
- Tool work. Making and mending shovels, picks, axes, knives,
  branding irons.
- Gun work in some cases (a smith with a file and a vise could
  fix many things on a Colt or a Winchester; specialized
  gunsmiths worked the larger towns).

The smithy: forge, anvil (often a 100–300 lb anvil), bellows
(hand-pumped, later water- or steam-driven), water-quench tank,
hammers, tongs, vises, files, hardies, swages. The smell of
coke smoke, hot iron, the sweet of the quench. The sound of
the hammer on the anvil — a _ringing_ sound that carried for
half a mile in still air. The smith pumped the bellows with
his foot or had a boy pump for him.

## 6. Tracking

A specialized skill, half art and half craft.

- Reading hoofprints (gait, weight, speed, direction, age of
  the track).
- Reading scuffed leaves, broken twigs, flattened grass.
- Reading droppings (fresh, dried, the contents).
- Reading distance (how far ahead the prey is, by how the
  track has weathered).

The Native scout was the master of this — Apache scouts ran the
Geronimo campaigns for the Army. Anglo and Mexican trackers
existed (the _brush popper_ of the Texas brush country knew his
stock by hoofprint). A man tracking a fugitive who lost the
track at a rocky stretch _cast for sign_ — circled, looking for
where the fugitive came back onto soft ground.

A character on the page who claims to be a tracker should be
shown doing the work in concrete detail or the claim falls flat.

## 7. Scouting

For the Army or for a wagon train. The job:

- Find water and grass.
- Find the enemy or the route.
- Carry messages.
- Interpret the country.

Scouts were often Native (Pawnee, Crow, Apache for the U.S.
Army) or mixed-blood (the Bent brothers, the Bridger family).
A small minority were Anglo (Buffalo Bill Cody scouted for
the Army before he became a showman; he killed Yellow Hair at
the Battle of Warbonnet Creek 1876, scalped him on the spot,
and made it the centerpiece of his stage show).

## 8. Hunting

### a. Buffalo hunting

A massive industry, brief and devastating, that destroyed the
Plains buffalo herds and with them the Plains tribes' economic
base.

- Southern herd: 1872–1875. Approximately 4 million animals
  killed.
- Northern herd: 1876–1883. Approximately 1.5 million killed.
- By 1884 the wild herds were gone. A few hundred animals
  survived in Yellowstone and in private herds.

The work:

- A _hunter_ with a Sharps .50 calibre big-bore rifle, lying
  prone with shooting sticks, shooting bulls at the edge of
  a herd from 200–400 yards. The hunter aimed for the lungs
  (a heart shot dropped the animal but the hunter wanted a
  _stand_ — to kill animal after animal at a single position
  before the herd moved off, which required a slow lung death
  that did not stampede the others). A skilled hunter
  killed 50–100 animals from one position in an afternoon.
- Two or three _skinners_ worked behind the hunter, taking
  the hides off the dead animals. A skinner was paid $50 a
  month and a share of the hides.
- The hides were pegged out, scraped, dried, baled, and freighted
  to a railhead. They sold for $1–$3 each, falling as the
  market saturated.
- The carcasses were left on the ground. The plains stank of
  rot for three years.

Famous hunters: Frank Mayer, Wright Mooar, Pat Garrett (later
the killer of Billy the Kid), Buffalo Bill Cody (in the buffalo
trade before scouting).

The Army actively encouraged the slaughter as a way to break
the Plains tribes. General Sheridan's quoted recommendation
that Texas should give the buffalo hunters medals because they
_had done more in the last two years to settle the vexed Indian
question than the entire regular army has done in the last thirty
years._

### b. Market hunting

Deer, elk, antelope, prairie chicken, ducks, geese for meat to
the urban markets and to the railroad construction camps.
Reduced the western game populations sharply. Largely ended by
state game laws after 1900.

### c. Wolfing

Killing wolves for the hide bounty (cattlemen hated wolves;
states paid $3–$10 a wolf scalp). The wolfer used poisoned
bait — strychnine in a piece of meat. Killed wolves, killed
coyotes, killed dogs, killed Native dogs — the wolfer was hated
by the tribes whose camp dogs died from his baits.

## 9. Farming and homesteading

### a. The Homestead Act of 1862

160 acres free to any settler who lived on and improved the land
for five years. Filed at the local Land Office. Paid the filing
fee ($10–$15). Built the _required improvements_ — at minimum a
dwelling and some cultivated ground. _Proved up_ after five
years. The patent (deed) was issued.

About 1.6 million homesteads were patented under the Act
through the 1930s. About 270 million acres total (10% of the
United States).

The land in the Great Plains:

- 160 acres was insufficient for dryland farming; many homesteads
  failed. The Kinkaid Act (1904) raised the Nebraska sandhills
  homestead to 640 acres. The Enlarged Homestead Act (1909)
  raised the western dryland homestead to 320 acres.
- The first year on a treeless plain was almost impossible. The
  first house was a sod house (_soddy_) — sod blocks cut from
  the ground and stacked. Cool in summer, warm in winter, leaky
  in rain, infested with snakes and bugs.
- Plowing the prairie sod was hard. The buffalo grass mat had
  to be cut by a sodbuster plow drawn by oxen. After the first
  breaking, the soil was rich.
- Drought, hail, grasshoppers (the Rocky Mountain Locust
  swarms of 1874 and 1875 destroyed crops across the Plains),
  prairie fire, blizzard.
- About half of all homesteads failed and were abandoned.

### b. Crops

- Wheat — the Plains' staple after the Russian-German Volga
  immigrants brought Turkey Red hard winter wheat to Kansas in 1874.
- Corn — eastern Plains.
- Oats, barley, rye — feed grains.
- Hay — wherever it grew. Critical for stock through the
  winter.
- Fruit — California, Oregon. Peaches, apples, citrus.
- Vegetables — the kitchen garden by every farmstead.

### c. The farm year

- Spring: plowing, planting.
- Summer: cultivating, haying.
- Fall: harvest. Wheat threshing was the great communal labor —
  threshing crews moved farm to farm, dozens of men, a team of
  women cooking for them, a steam thresher hauled in by a
  traction engine after the 1880s.
- Winter: care of stock, mending, butchering hogs, ice-cutting,
  cabin work.

### d. Homestead labor

Husband, wife, children. The wife's work:

- Cooking (three meals a day for the family and any hands, on
  a wood stove).
- Baking (bread, pies, biscuits).
- Preserving (canning, smoking, salting, drying).
- Laundering (with hand-pump well water heated on the stove,
  washboard, wringer, line).
- Sewing and mending.
- Gardening.
- Milking, butter, cheese.
- Childbirth and child rearing.
- Doctoring the family.
- Teaching the children to read.
- Helping in the field at planting and harvest.

A homestead wife's work day was longer than her husband's and
relentless. Mental illness and suicide rates were high.

## 10. Other trades

Brief notes — each could fill a page.

- **Telegraph operator.** A skilled job. Decoded Morse by ear.
  In a small-town depot the telegrapher was often the most
  literate person in town. Some women operators (Western
  Union began hiring women in the 1860s).
- **Newspaper editor.** Set type by hand, ran the Washington
  press, wrote the editorials. Often shot at; sometimes
  killed.
- **Surveyor.** Federal land surveyors laid out the township-
  and-range grid that organized the public domain. State and
  county surveyors did boundary work. The transit and the
  Gunter's chain.
- **Lawyer.** A man with two years' reading in another lawyer's
  office, examined by the local court, admitted to the bar.
  Rode a circuit between county courts. Did wills, deeds,
  divorce, criminal defense. The frontier lawyer was a maker
  of careers (Lincoln had been one a generation earlier, and
  hundreds followed him into local and territorial politics).
- **Doctor.** See `medicine-and-death.md`.
- **Schoolteacher.** Often a single woman in her early
  twenties on a one- or two-year contract. Boarded with a
  local family. Quit the trade on marriage (most jurisdictions
  did not employ married women as teachers).
- **Saloon-keeper.** Almost always a man. Often immigrant
  (Irish, German). Often the largest taxpayer in his town.
- **Madam.** The owner of a parlor house. Often retired
  prostitute. A businesswoman. Some — Mattie Silks of Denver,
  Diamond Lil of Leadville — were locally famous.
- **Banker.** A respectable merchant who took in deposits,
  loaned at 8–12% interest, lived on the spread. Often the
  banker also owned the largest store and the freight company.
- **Cook.** In a saloon, a hash house, a hotel, a cattle camp,
  a logging camp, a mining mess hall. Often Chinese in the
  Pacific West.
- **Laundry.** Often Chinese in the Pacific West, often Mexican
  or Black women elsewhere.
- **Domestic servant.** Often immigrant or Black or Native
  woman.
- **Soldier.** $13 a month for a private. Three-year enlistment.
  Hard discipline, monotonous garrison life punctuated by
  campaigns. Heavy drinking, frequent desertion (a quarter to
  a third of enlistments deserted before the term was up).
  See `peoples-and-conflict.md`.
- **Cowboy.** See `cowboy-life.md`.

## 11. The reader who has done the work

The risk is to write in generalities. _He worked the mine. He
broke a horse. He drove the herd north._ The fix is detail. Two
or three concrete actions, the right one for the trade, and the
reader believes you. _He set the steel and his partner swung.
The steel rang and the dust rose. They worked through the watch
without speaking and at the change he sat down on the muck and
poured a cup of cold coffee out of a tin and drank it._

That paragraph is not perfect, but it is set in a real mine in a
real shift. A general one is set in a movie. Choose.
