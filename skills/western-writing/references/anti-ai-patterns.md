# Anti-AI Patterns — The Tells

This is the longest list because it has to be. AI prose has
fingerprints. The reader does not always know the name of the
fingerprint, but they feel it: the prose reads as generated. Each
section below names a tell, gives the detection cue, and shows
the rewrite.

Run this file as the last pass before shipping a draft. If
anything below survives, the draft is not finished.

---

## A. Vocabulary kill list

These words and phrases mark a draft as generated within the first
paragraph. They are banned from manuscript prose and from any
fiction in the western register.

### A1. Therapy / wellness

_meaningful, impactful, journey, navigate (metaphorical), unpack,
process (as a verb of feeling), holistic, wellness, self-care,
trauma, triggered (modern sense), boundaries (modern sense),
toxic, healing journey, lived experience, hold space, sit with
(feelings), find closure, work through, communicate, validate,
honor (as a verb meaning respect feelings)._

### A2. Corporate

_leverage, optimize, scale, deliver, robust, seamless, key,
critical (outside surgery), strategic, alignment, deep dive,
circle back, take this offline, value-add, stakeholder,
ecosystem (metaphorical), space (metaphorical), landscape
(metaphorical)._

### A3. Blog

_it's worth noting, it bears mentioning, that said, that being
said, in this article, in this piece, dive into, explore,
discover, unlock, ultimate guide, must-know, you might be
wondering, here's the thing, the truth is, at the end of the
day, basically, in other words, this means that, the key
takeaway, fast forward, the bottom line._

### A4. AI signature phrases

_tapestry, rich tapestry, woven, intricate (when not literal),
delve, realm (when not literal), stark (overused), bustling,
serene (overused), nestled, quaint, quintessential, captivating,
breathtaking, awe-inspiring, profound (overused), remarkable
(overused), testament to, speaks to, lies at the heart of,
serves as a reminder, paints a picture, a sense of (anything),
the essence of, in essence._

### A5. Western-cliche AI

*rugged cowboy, lone gunslinger (gunslinger is fine; lone
gunslinger is cliche), grizzled veteran, weathered face, steely
gaze, dusty trail (only as flavor; never as the only descriptor),
the wild frontier, untamed wilderness, the vast expanse, the
endless plains, sun-baked, gun-slinging, six-shooter (period
term is six-shooter or pistol or revolver — all fine — but never
*trusty six-shooter*), trusty steed (banned), faithful companion
(banned).*

If a word from this list appears in your draft, find a concrete
substitute. If you cannot, the sentence is decoration and should
be cut.

## B. Structural tells

### B1. Em-dash overuse

**Detection:** more than one em-dash per paragraph, or em-dashes
in three consecutive sentences.

AI loves the em-dash because it can pad a sentence with apparent
nuance. Prose in the western canon uses em-dashes rarely. McCarthy
uses none. McMurtry uses few. Leonard uses few.

**Rewrite tactics:**

- Replace with a period and a hammer sentence.
- Replace with a comma if the clause is short.
- Cut the em-dashed clause entirely if it adds nothing.

**Before:**

> The horse — a tall bay with a white stocking — stood at the
> rail, breathing hard, its flanks streaked with sweat — the
> sign of a long ride.

**After:**

> The bay stood at the rail, breathing hard. Its flanks were
> streaked with sweat. He had been ridden hard for hours.

### B2. Rule of three

**Detection:** lists of three nouns or three adjectives. Especially
three escalating items.

The triadic list is rhythmic and AI knows it. Real prose uses
twos and fours and ones at least as often as threes.

**Before:**

> The plains were vast, empty, and unforgiving.

**After:**

> The plains were empty.

Or:

> The plains were vast and empty. Nothing in them forgave a
> mistake.

### B3. Negative parallelism (split-sentence negative)

**Detection:** _It is not X. It is Y._ / _Not X — Y._ /
_Not just X, but Y._

This is the most reliable AI tell after the em-dash.

**Rewrite tactics:**

- Collapse to a single sentence: _It is not X but Y._
- Drop the negative half: just say _Y._
- Replace with a concrete demonstration of _Y._

**Before:**

> This is not a bank. It has no ledger.

**After:**

> This is no bank — no lock, no ledger, no one to answer to.

**Before:**

> She was not just brave. She was reckless.

**After:**

> Bravery would have been the small word for what she was.

### B4. Copula avoidance / elegant variation of _to be_

**Detection:** _serves as, stands as, represents, embodies,
constitutes, functions as._

AI avoids _is_ because it has learned that _is_ is weak. _Is_ is
not weak. _Serves as_ is weak.

**Before:**

> The river served as a natural boundary between the two
> ranches.

**After:**

> The river was the boundary between the two ranches.

### B5. Synonym cycling / elegant variation of nouns

**Detection:** the same referent named three different ways in
three sentences. _The cowboy… the rider… the man._ _The pistol…
the firearm… the weapon._

The repeated noun is invisible. The cycled synonyms call
attention to themselves.

**Before:**

> The cowboy drew his pistol. The rider had been waiting for
> this moment. The man squeezed the trigger.

**After:**

> Maddox drew. He had been waiting for this. He squeezed the
> trigger.

(Use the name. Then a pronoun. Then another pronoun. Stop varying.)

### B6. Hedging stacks

**Detection:** _perhaps, maybe, somewhat, rather, quite, fairly,
arguably, in some sense, in a way, to some extent._

Two hedges in one sentence is one too many. The western voice
asserts. If the narrator is uncertain, the uncertainty is shown
through what is not said, not through hedge words.

**Before:**

> He was perhaps somewhat reluctant to leave, given that the
> situation was rather complicated.

**After:**

> He did not want to leave. The reasons were his own.

### B7. Sycophantic qualifiers

**Detection:** _truly, really, very, deeply, profoundly,
genuinely, incredibly, remarkably._

These intensifiers signal that the writer does not trust the noun
or verb to carry the weight. Trust the noun.

**Before:**

> He was truly a remarkably skilled horseman.

**After:**

> He could ride.

### B8. Generic positive endings

**Detection:** the last sentence of a paragraph or chapter
summarizes, consoles, or ennobles.

> _And so the long ride had been worth it after all._
> _In the end, what mattered most was the bond they had forged._
> _Tomorrow would bring new challenges, but they would face
> them together._

These are all dead. End on a hard noun, a body, a piece of
weather, a refusal.

### B9. Filler transitions

**Detection:** _meanwhile, in the meantime, suddenly, all of a
sudden, before long, eventually, ultimately, in due time, as
fate would have it._

The white space, the next sentence, or a hard time-marker
(_by noon, two days later, that night_) replaces every one of
these.

### B10. The anaphoric stack

**Detection:** three sentences starting with the same word for
rhetorical effect. _He rode. He rode. He rode._

Used once a chapter by McCarthy, this becomes wallpaper at three
times a chapter. Limit to once per chapter, and only when the
content earns the repetition.

### B11. The deflated metaphor

**Detection:** _like a [generic noun]._ _His face was like
stone. Her eyes were like fire. The night was like a blanket._

A metaphor must do work. A blanket-night does no work. The
metaphor that works in the western canon is the metaphor whose
vehicle is also a working object in the world: _the sky was the
color of a hot iron_ (working tool), _his hands were like oak
roots_ (oak roots are real things in the country these characters
live in).

### B12. The summary sentence inside a description

**Detection:** a sentence that tells the reader what the
preceding sentences mean. _The room felt heavy with sorrow._
_The whole town felt the weight of what had happened._

If the description worked, the summary is redundant. If the
description failed, the summary cannot save it. Cut.

### B13. The rule-of-three sentence opener

**Detection:** _To be sure, … For one thing, … On one hand…_

Essay-class connectives. Banned in fiction. Rare in manuscript
text — and when used, they appear once a chapter, not once a page.

### B14. The explanatory dialogue tag

**Detection:** _he said, knowing what was coming. she replied,
trying to hide her fear._

The narrator is telling the reader what the dialogue means.
Trust the dialogue. Use a beat or no tag.

### B15. The ascending tricolon

**Detection:** three items where the third is grand, abstract,
or mythic. _He had lost his horse, his rifle, and his faith in
men._ _They came for the gold, the land, and the dream._

The grand third item kills the rhythm. Either keep all three
concrete, or drop the third.

### B16. The ironic-self-aware ending

**Detection:** the paragraph closes with a wink — _if such a
thing could be said in such a country_ / _whatever that meant._

Sincerity is the western voice's default. Self-conscious irony
is a 21st-century reflex. Cut the wink.

### B17. The "found himself" verb pattern

**Detection:** _he found himself standing at the door / thinking
of his father / wishing he had stayed._

The verb passivizes the character. Western prose acts.

**Before:**

> He found himself thinking of his father.

**After:**

> He thought of his father.

(Or, better, show him doing something that reveals he thought of
his father — picking up the old man's knife, looking at the empty
saddle.)

### B18. The "in a moment that would [verb]" pattern

**Detection:** _in a moment that would change his life forever /
in a gesture that spoke volumes / in a silence that said more
than words._

Pure AI editorializing. Cut the construction; show the moment
or the gesture or the silence and let it speak.

### B19. Adjective-adjective-noun

**Detection:** any noun phrase with two adjectives stacked.
_The cold, dark night. The harsh, unforgiving land. The grim,
stoic rider._

One adjective only — the more concrete one. Or zero.

**Before:** the harsh, unforgiving land.
**After:** the hardpan.

### B20. The mood-without-object atmospheric

**Detection:** any atmospheric sentence that contains no physical
noun. _Danger lurked. Tension hung in the air. A sense of unease
settled over the camp._

Replace with a physical fact that produces the mood.

**Before:** A sense of unease settled over the camp.
**After:** The dog had stopped eating and stood with his ears
forward, looking at the brush north of the fire.

## C. Rhetorical tells

### C1. Telling the reader how to feel

The narrator says _the scene was haunting / the silence was
unbearable / the moment was profound._ The reader will not feel
what they are told to feel. They will feel what the prose makes
them feel.

### C2. The moral takeaway

A paragraph or scene ends by stating its lesson. _And so they
learned that…_ _It was a hard reminder that…_

Western prose does not extract lessons. The lesson is the scene.

### C3. The Wikipedia paragraph

A paragraph that reads like an encyclopedia entry has wandered
in from research. _Cattle drives in the post-Civil-War period
typically involved…_ Either embed in action or cut.

### C4. The character-thinks-about-history

A character conveniently remembers a fact the reader needs to
know. _He thought back to how the railroads had…_

Real characters do not deliver exposition by reminiscence. If
the fact must arrive, find an action or a line of dialogue that
carries it.

### C5. The safety scaffold

A passage labels itself sensitive. _Note: the following scene
contains…_ _It is important to acknowledge that…_

The manuscript and the fiction handle hard content by writing
it well. They do not warn around it. (Trigger warnings on a
title page or in front matter are publication decisions, not
prose moves.)

### C6. The author cameo

The narrator steps in to comment, joke, or wink at the reader.
_And here, dear reader, we must pause to consider…_

Banned. The narrator is a window, not a presenter.

### C7. The "showed no emotion" character

A character is repeatedly described as showing no emotion,
betraying nothing, etc., as a stand-in for actual interiority.
This is laziness; it tells the reader the character is hard
without making them experience the hardness.

Replace with action: what the character did or did not do at
the moment a softer character would have flinched.

### C8. The "he didn't say what he was thinking" pattern

A line of dialogue followed by _but he didn't say what he was
really thinking._

Cut the after-clause. The unsaid thing is unsaid.

## D. Mechanical tells

### D1. Title-case headings inside fiction

Fiction does not need section breaks every two pages. If you must
break, use a centered glyph (\*\*\*) or a blank line. Avoid
title-case headings inside a chapter.

### D2. Bullet lists inside fiction

Bullets do not appear inside fiction. They are appropriate in
rules text, in reference modules, in this skill. They are not
appropriate inside a story scene.

### D3. Bold and italics overuse

Italics in fiction = thoughts (sparingly), foreign words on
first appearance, titles of books. Bold does not appear in
fiction at all. Manuscript text uses bold for game terms.

### D4. The Oxford comma flip

Pick one and hold it through the whole document. Default for
this project: Oxford comma on.

### D5. Curly vs straight quotes

Default: curly in finished prose, straight in source markdown.
Do not mix in one paragraph.

### D6. Inconsistent capitalization of game terms

If GRIT is a game term, GRIT stays capitalized in every
appearance, including dialogue ("My grit's about used up,
Captain") — wait. Inside dialogue, lowercase the term unless
the character is naming it as a game-named thing. The bible
chapter on game-term style governs (`corebook/` chapter on
attributes).

## E. The dead similes list

These are banned because they are dead. If you must compare,
build a fresh comparison from the world of the scene.

- _quiet as a grave / silent as the dead_
- _cold as ice / cold as steel_
- _hard as nails / hard as iron_
- _eyes like daggers / eyes like fire_
- _face like stone / face like a mask_
- _busy as a bee / quick as a fox_
- _brave as a lion_
- _like a bat out of hell_
- _like greased lightning_
- _like a deer in the headlights_ (anachronistic)
- _like a hot knife through butter_
- _time stood still / time slowed down_
- _the silence was deafening_
- _all hell broke loose_

If you cannot resist, the bar is: the comparison must use a
working object specific to the scene's world. _His hands were
like oak roots_ is alive. _His hands were like rocks_ is dead.

## F. The detection workflow

Run the draft against this list in three passes.

### Pass 1: vocabulary scan

Search the draft (literal text search) for each word and phrase
on the kill list (section A). Each hit gets cut or replaced.

### Pass 2: structural scan

Read the draft on the page. Mark in margin:

- Em-dash count per paragraph (target ≤ 1).
- Triadic lists per paragraph (target ≤ 1, preferably 0).
- Negative parallelisms (target 0).
- Paragraph endings — circle the last word of each. If it is a
  qualifier, an abstraction, or a hedge, the paragraph fails.
- _Suddenly_ count (target 0).

### Pass 3: read-aloud

Read the draft aloud at conversational pace. Mark every place
the voice stumbles, slides into lecture, or loses the body of
the scene. Rewrite each.

### Pass 4: paragraph-job audit

For every paragraph, name the job in one word in the margin:
_define, describe, distinguish, warn, demonstrate, bridge,
land._ Any paragraph that resists naming is decoration. Cut or
rewrite.

A draft that survives all four passes is ready for review.
