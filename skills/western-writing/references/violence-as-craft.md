# Violence as Craft

This file is the dedicated craft module for writing
violence in the western register. It complements
`knowledge/medicine-and-death.md` (the physical facts of
wounds and dying), `references/uncomfortable-truths.md`
(the moral context of violence in the period), and
`references/pov-and-distance.md` (which determines whose
shoulders the violence happens over).

Violence is the western's recurring weather. Most slack
violence in popular fiction fails because it is
choreographed instead of recorded, slowed instead of
rendered at speed, and morally framed instead of left
to the reader. This file is the corrective.

---

## 1. The four canonical forms of violence

The western register handles four distinct violent forms.
Each has its own grammar, structure, and moral weight.
The skilled writer knows which one is being written.

### a. The duel

Two armed men, an agreed or quasi-agreed encounter, an
audience of some kind, a public stake. The classic main-
street meeting. Rare in life. Common in fiction. Has its
own ritual structure: the approach, the words, the draw,
the shot, the aftermath.

The duel's special property is that both parties have
consented to the form, even if one is reluctant. The
moral weight is shared.

### b. The brawl

Hands, fists, bottles, chair legs, knives. No firearms,
or firearms produced as escalation. Often in a saloon, a
cookhouse, a corral. The brawl ends in blood but rarely
in death. Its function in a scene is usually relational —
the men involved are bound by it afterward, as friends
or enemies.

### c. The ambush

One side has consent and one does not. The ambushed
party did not know it was a fight until it was. This is
the most common form of frontier violence in fact and
in the canon — the road agent's halt, the Comanche
raid, the Apache strike, the assassin in the alley.

The ambush's moral weight is asymmetric. The reader's
sympathy goes to whichever side was surprised, unless
the writer works hard to redirect it.

### d. The massacre

Many on few, or many on many noncombatants. The cavalry
on the village. The vigilance committee on the immigrant
camp. The Comanche on the homestead. The buffalo
soldiers on the breaks. Massacre is not a larger ambush;
it is its own form. Its moral weight is total. The
reader is not asked to take a side; the reader is asked
to witness.

McCarthy writes massacre. Hall writes the duel and the
ambush. Hansen writes the documentary aftermath.
Lansdale writes the brawl that becomes the massacre.

## 2. The grammar of a gunfight

A gunfight has three parts and a hinge. The parts are:
the silence before, the speed of the event, the silence
after. The hinge is the trigger — the moment one party
moves and the other has to.

### The silence before

The pre-violence silence is where the tension lives. It
should be longer than the event itself. The bodies are
held. The country is rendered with unusual specificity
because both parties are noticing every detail. Time
slows in the silence; it does not slow in the event.

> They stood twenty paces apart in the dust of the street
> and the wind came up off the flat and lifted the sign
> over the saddler's and let it drop. A dog came out
> from under the porch of the hotel and lay down in the
> shadow. Hatch held his hands at his sides and did not
> speak.

### The hinge

The hinge is one sentence. Often a single physical fact.
A hand moves. A word ends. The trigger of the event is
explicit and small.

> Caldwell smiled and his hand went down.

### The event

The event is fast. Sentences are short. No choreography.
No slow motion. No interior thought from the shooter.
The reader sees what could be seen by an observer with
good eyes — and the observer cannot follow every detail.
That confusion is the truth of the moment.

> Two shots. Hatch's first and Caldwell's a half-second
> after. Caldwell fell sideways and his pistol went off
> into the boards.

### The silence after

The silence after is also long. The dust settles. The
horses startle. The audience does not know yet what to
do. The wounded breathes. The dead does not. This is
where the moral weight lands — in the silence, not in
the event.

> The dog had not moved from under the porch. The wind
> was still up and the sign was still working. Hatch
> looked down at his own hand and then he looked at
> Caldwell.

### Rule

The silence-before-event-silence ratio is roughly
3:1:5. If the event takes more pages than the silence
after, the prose is performing, not recording.

## 3. The grammar of a brawl

A brawl has different physics. Bodies. Friction. Pain
that does not kill. Three properties.

### Close-range vocabulary

Hands grab cloth. Heads hit posts. Boots find ribs. The
nouns are body parts and small objects. The verbs are
short and percussive: _grab, hit, kick, pull, throw,
break._

### Confusion of bodies

In a brawl, no participant can see the whole event. The
prose follows one body's perception. He is hit and he
does not see by what. He hits and he does not always
know what he hit. The chaos is the truth.

### The aftermath as wounds, not glory

The brawl ends in cuts, broken noses, swollen hands,
ribs that will hurt for a month. Nobody walks out
clean. The morning after is part of the scene. Two men
who fought last night sit at the same breakfast and one
of them cannot lift his arm to take the coffee.

### Brawl example

> The first punch took him under the ear and he went
> sideways into the bar and his cup went off the bar and
> broke. He came up swinging and hit the man in the
> shoulder and his hand bent back and he knew it was
> broken. The man hit him again in the mouth and the
> taste of his own blood was in his throat. They went
> down into the sawdust together and a boot caught him
> in the kidney from above and the room went red around
> the edges.

## 4. The grammar of an ambush

The ambush has a pre-event the reader sees and the
victim does not, or has no pre-event at all. Three
choices.

### Choice a: the reader sees, the victim does not

This is dramatic irony. The reader watches the trap
being set, the rider approaching, the moment that will
end the rider. The tension is in the gap between the
reader's knowledge and the rider's. The McMurtry move
when he wants to break the reader.

### Choice b: the reader knows nothing more than the

victim

The ambush comes out of nowhere. The chapter has been
quiet, the country empty, the work routine. Then the
shot or the rush. The reader's surprise matches the
victim's. Lansdale's move; sometimes McCarthy's.

### Choice c: the chapter shifts viewpoint at the moment

of the ambush

The chapter has been with the victim. The shot comes.
The next scene is from the ambushers' viewpoint and the
reader learns who they were and why. Hall's move.

### Rule

Pick one. Do not telegraph the ambush in choice b. Do
not surprise the reader in choice a. Do not shift
viewpoint inside the event in choice c.

### Ambush example (choice b)

> They came up the trail at noon and the country was
> empty and the only sound was the bay's hooves on the
> rock. The first shot went past his head and he heard
> the report afterward and he was already off the horse
> and on the ground. The bay went sideways and down with
> the second shot and lay kicking. He crawled into the
> brush behind the bay and lay still.

No telegraphing. The reader rides into the ambush with
the rider.

## 5. The grammar of a massacre

Massacre has its own structural problem: the writer must
render scale without becoming pornographic, must witness
without exploiting, and must close without offering
catharsis.

### Six rules

1. **No protagonist's-eye-view that becomes spectator
   sport.** The viewpoint character is in the event,
   not above it. He sees what is in front of him.
   What happens fifty yards off, he hears.

2. **Render the human, then the count.** One specific
   death first — a child the viewpoint character knew, a
   mother in a doorway. Then the count: the rest of
   them. The specific carries the rest.

3. **No moral framing in the prose.** The narrator
   does not lament. The narrator does not explain.
   The narrator records. The reader supplies the
   horror.

4. **The aftermath is the chapter.** The event itself is
   often shorter than the aftermath — the burying, the
   accounting, the survivors, the silence. McCarthy's
   massacres get one paragraph; the rides afterward get
   ten.

5. **Do not let any side off.** A massacre committed by
   the protagonist's side is rendered the same as one
   committed against him. The writer does not arrange
   the chapter to comfort the reader.

6. **The country closes over.** The final image is the
   country, indifferent. Buzzards. Wind. The grass
   coming back over the bones in two seasons. See
   `references/landscape-as-prose.md` section 4.

### Massacre opening (one paragraph)

> They came down on the village at first light and the
> first shot was the captain's and the first man down
> was a boy of about ten who had come out of a lodge to
> see what the noise was. The lodges were burning before
> the sun was clear of the ridge. By the time it was over
> the count was forty-three counted and the troopers had
> lost two men, both shot by women.

## 6. Author-specific approaches

### McCarthy

Violence as weather. The narrator does not slow. The
massacre and the duel are recorded in the same
prose register as a sunrise. The reader is given no
guidance on how to feel. The horror arrives through
specifics — the manner of a death, an object on the
body, the posture of the killed.

McCarthy will spend a paragraph on how a body fell and
not a sentence on what the killer felt. He trusts the
fall.

### Hall

Violence as moral reckoning. _Warlock_ stages its
duels with full procedural attention to the social
ritual. The shooting itself is brief. The pages
before and after are the chapter — who did what, who
was seen, what the town will make of it.

Hall is the model for the duel as social form.

### Lansdale

Violence as eruption from the comic surface. The
chapter has been funny, the dialogue brisk, the
characters bickering. Then the violence comes and
it is grotesque and fast and there is no transition.
The contrast is the craft. The grotesque is the
truth the comedy was hiding.

Lansdale is also the model for violence with absurdist
detail — a death rendered with one ridiculous specific
that the reader will not forget.

### Hansen

Violence as documentary. _The Assassination of Jesse
James_ is the model. The shooting is rendered with the
precision of a coroner's report. The morning of, the
moment, the position of the body, the witnesses. No
poetry. No comfort. The horror is in the documentary
flatness.

### Hard rule

Pick the author for the chapter. Do not mix registers
inside one violent scene.

## 7. The wounds that do not kill

Most period firearm wounds did not kill at the moment
of the shot. They killed later, of infection, of
hemorrhage, of shock, or they did not kill at all and
the man lived on with the bullet in him or with the
arm gone. See `knowledge/medicine-and-death.md` for the
medical facts.

The craft consequence: a man shot does not drop dead.
He falls or staggers or sits down. He looks at the
wound. He may not yet feel it. The pain comes later, or
it comes immediately and overwhelms. He may speak. He
may go silent. He bleeds. The bleeding is the truth of
the wound; the count of the breaths is the truth of
the dying.

### The shot man example

> The slug went into him under the collarbone and out
> the back of his shoulder and he sat down hard in the
> dust. He did not say anything for a while. He looked
> at the front of his shirt where the blood was coming
> through and he put his hand on it. The hand came
> away red and he looked at the hand. Then he tried to
> get up and could not.

No melodrama. Specifics. The body's behavior.

### The dying man example

> By midnight he was hot to the touch and the wound
> was closed up red and shining. He had not spoken since
> sundown. His breathing came in short rasps with a
> wait between them. Bowman sat by him and put a wet
> cloth on his forehead and the cloth went dry inside
> of an hour.

The death has not yet happened on the page. The reader
knows it is coming.

## 8. The author's position

Who is the violence happening to in the prose? The
question is not who gets shot. The question is whose
shoulders the camera sits over. The author's position
on the violence is determined by POV.

### Three positions

1. **With the killer.** The reader is in the shooter's
   eyes. He aims, he fires, he sees the man fall. This
   is the rare position. It risks glorification. It is
   used when the writer wants the reader to feel the
   killer's specific moral weight.

2. **With the killed.** The reader is in the victim's
   eyes. He is shot. He sees the world come down. This
   is the harder position to write because the prose
   must follow the perception of a body in shock.
   Used when the writer wants the violence to be the
   reader's wound.

3. **With the witness.** The reader is in a third
   character — a friend of one of the parties, a
   bystander, a child watching. The witness sees the
   event but does not feel it. Most western violence
   is written from the witness position because it is
   the position with the most moral leverage.

### Choosing

The writer chooses the position that maximizes the
chapter's argument. A duel chapter usually sits with
the witness. An ambush chapter often shifts from the
victim to the witness. A massacre chapter usually
follows one body in the chaos and lets the rest be
heard.

## 9. Hard rules

These are non-negotiable inside this skill.

1. **No slow motion.** The event happens at the speed
   it happens. Sentences are short. No frame-by-frame
   choreography.

2. **No hero invulnerability.** The protagonist is not
   safe by virtue of being the protagonist. He gets
   hit. He gets cut. The first time he is in a fight
   the reader believes he might die.

3. **No clean wounds.** The body is meat and the meat
   behaves like meat. See
   `knowledge/medicine-and-death.md`.

4. **No revenge catharsis.** A man killed in revenge is
   killed; the killer is not made whole. The chapter
   after the revenge is harder than the chapter before
   it.

5. **No moral framing in the prose.** The narrator
   records. The author has positioned the camera. The
   reader supplies the moral weight.

6. **No silenced victim.** Even an antagonist killed
   has a body, has a name, has a moment of his own
   dying. The cardboard outlaw who falls and is
   forgotten is the western's worst sin.

7. **No final-shot triumph.** The chapter does not end
   on the shot or the kill. It ends on the silence after,
   or the wound, or the country. See
   `references/story-structure.md` section 4.

## 10. Before / after rewrites

### Choreography → record

**Before (slow choreography):**

> Time seemed to slow as Maddox saw Caldwell's hand
> drop toward his pistol. With practiced ease, Maddox's
> own hand moved to his weapon, drawing it in one fluid
> motion. He brought the gun up, level with Caldwell's
> chest, and squeezed the trigger. The hammer fell. The
> shot rang out across the dusty street. Caldwell
> staggered, a look of shock on his face, before
> crumpling to the ground.

**After (the event at speed):**

> Caldwell smiled and his hand went down. Two shots, a
> half-second apart. Caldwell sat down in the dust and
> the pistol fell out of his hand and lay on the
> boards.

### Hero invulnerability → wounded protagonist

**Before:**

> Bowman dispatched the three outlaws with quick,
> efficient shots, and walked away from the scene
> unscathed.

**After:**

> The first one he shot in the face and the second one
> went under the porch and shot at him from there. The
> slug went through his hat brim. He killed the second
> one and the third one came out of the alley and he
> killed him too and the third one's last shot took him
> in the meat of the leg. He sat down on the boards and
> bled.

### Moral framing → record

**Before:**

> The brutal massacre at the Comanche village was a
> shameful chapter in the history of the frontier, a
> reminder of the senseless violence that defined this
> dark era.

**After:**

> They counted forty-three dead and most of them were
> women and old men. The captain rode his horse through
> the lodges and gave the order to fire what would burn.
> The smoke went up black against the morning sky and
> the column rode out at noon and did not look back.

### Clean death → physical truth

**Before:**

> The bullet struck him in the chest and he died
> instantly, falling back into Bowman's arms.

**After:**

> The slug took him high in the chest and he sat down
> hard and looked at Bowman as if he had been asked a
> question. He tried to speak and a bubble of blood
> came out of his mouth. Bowman caught him as he went
> sideways. He was a long time dying.

### Revenge catharsis → consequence

**Before:**

> When it was over, Maddox felt a sense of peace at
> last. Tom was avenged. Now he could go home.

**After:**

> When it was over he stood in the street with the
> pistol still in his hand and his hand was shaking. He
> put the pistol away and walked back to where the bay
> was tied. He rode out. He did not go home that night
> or the next.

## 11. The violence cheat-sheet

- **Pick the form.** Duel, brawl, ambush, massacre. Each
  has its own grammar.
- **Three-part gunfight.** Silence before (long), event
  (fast), silence after (long).
- **Pick the position.** With the killer, the killed, or
  the witness.
- **Pick the author.** McCarthy, Hall, Lansdale, Hansen.
  Do not mix.
- **No slow motion. No choreography. No hero
  invulnerability. No clean wounds. No moral framing in
  the prose.**
- **The aftermath is the scene.** The event is shorter
  than what comes after it.
- **End on weight.** Silence, wound, country. Never on
  the shot itself.

---

## 12. The rule the others serve

The reader is being asked to witness. The author's
job is to set the camera, to record what is in front
of it, and to step out of the room. The horror is the
reader's. The judgment is the reader's. The author has
selected what to put in the frame and trusts that the
selection is enough.

The frame is the craft. The shot is the test.
