# The Author Canon

These are the operational models. Read the entry before you draft
in that register. Each entry contains: the spine of the writer's
voice, the properties to copy, the failure modes to avoid, and a
short pastiche guideline so you can produce prose in the spirit
of the source without paraphrasing copyrighted text.

A note on quoting: do not paste extended passages from copyrighted
novels into the manuscript. The entries below describe the moves
in technical detail. Use them to write _original_ prose that
behaves the same way.

---

## Cormac McCarthy — the spine

Source novels: _Blood Meridian_ (1985), _All the Pretty Horses_
(1992), _The Crossing_ (1994), _Cities of the Plain_ (1998),
_No Country for Old Men_ (2005).

### Voice

McCarthy writes with the cadence of the King James Bible filtered
through Faulkner and stripped of interiority. Long compound
sentences chained by _and_. No quotation marks. Spanish dropped
into English without italics or translation. Apocalyptic
landscapes treated as the only real character.

### Properties to copy

1. **Polysyndetic chains.** Sentences join clauses with _and_
   instead of subordinating them. The effect is inevitability —
   one thing then another then another, the way time actually
   runs. Use sparingly. One per page in a long passage. Two
   per page is too many.

2. **Landscape as moral weight.** The desert, the plain, the
   river crossing, the mountain pass are described with the
   patience reserved for villains. They have agency. They
   outlast everyone. The land does not care, and that
   indifference is the story.

3. **Violence as weather.** When violence happens, the narrator
   records it the way a clerk records a barometer reading. No
   moral framing, no slowdown for emotional processing. The
   reader provides the horror.

4. **Sparse dialogue, no quotation marks.** Characters speak in
   short declarative sentences. They do not explain themselves.
   What they say is a third of what they mean. The silence
   between the words does the work.

5. **Biblical diction in the right place.** Words like _that,
   wherein, for, came upon, was given to_ appear at the seams
   of paragraphs. Modern Anglo-Saxon nouns carry the rest:
   _blood, dust, bone, iron, hide, salt, lamp, axle, rope._

6. **No sentimentality.** McCarthy never consoles the reader.
   Characters die without redemption arcs. Loyalty is tested
   and sometimes fails. The prose offers no comfort.

### Failure modes to avoid

- **Pastiche-of-pastiche.** Endless *and*s with no underlying
  rhythm produce parody. Polysyndeton works because each clause
  earns its place by adding a fact, not because the connector
  sounds biblical.
- **Mistaking opacity for depth.** McCarthy's hardest passages
  reward re-reading because the literal facts are precise. If
  your sentence is unclear about what physically happened, it
  is bad McCarthy, not difficult McCarthy.
- **Performative bleakness.** McCarthy is bleak because his
  characters live in a bleak world and act inside it. Bleakness
  imposed on a scene where nothing is at stake reads as adolescent.
- **Foreign language as decoration.** McCarthy uses Spanish
  because the borderland is bilingual. If your scene is in
  Wyoming, do not drop Spanish in for flavor.

### Pastiche guideline

A McCarthy paragraph in the spirit:

> They rode south through the brushland and the sun came up
> behind them and threw their shadows long across the chaparral
> and the horses blew and shook their heads against the flies.
> By midmorning they had crossed the dry bed of the Pecos and
> the boy looked back once at the gray country behind them and
> saw nothing of the country they had left and turned and rode
> on.

What that paragraph is doing: cardinal direction, time of day
by light, body of the horse, named river, single backward look
without sentiment, refusal to comment on what was lost.

### Use McCarthy when

- Opening a chapter on landscape.
- Writing a long ride or crossing.
- Rendering violence without authorial intervention.
- Closing on a death without elegy.

## Larry McMurtry — the humanizer

Source novels: _Lonesome Dove_ (1985), _Streets of Laredo_
(1993), _Dead Man's Walk_ (1995), _Comanche Moon_ (1997),
_Horseman, Pass By_ (1961).

### Voice

McMurtry writes the West warm. Free indirect discourse drifts
through the consciousness of working men and women. Humor as
survival mechanism. Character defined by habit. Quotation marks
intact, dialogue plain. The mundane carries the page; violence
arrives as interruption.

### Properties to copy

1. **Character through habit and quirk.** Two behaviors and the
   character exists. Gus McCrae talks; Woodrow Call works; Jake
   Spoon gambles. Build a cowhand by what he does at sundown,
   not what he looks like.

2. **Humor as survival.** Crude, warm, self-aware jokes between
   men who have ridden together long enough to have nothing left
   to prove. The joke is never for the reader's benefit. It
   defuses, it bonds, it fills time. Game examples and NPC
   dialogue can carry this quality.

3. **Mundane detail as authenticity.** Pages on coffee, biscuit,
   horse-shoeing, river crossing, weather. Violence is the
   exception, not the point. The drive is the story; the bandit
   raid is a paragraph.

4. **Death without heroism.** Characters die of snakebite,
   infection, drowning, falling, bad luck. The deaths are quiet
   and often pointless. Deaths in shootouts are usually one
   sentence and over.

5. **Free indirect drift.** The narrator slides into a
   character's idiom without quotation: _Call wasn't given to
   talking and the boys knew not to expect speech from him at
   sun-up._ That sentence is the narrator using Call's diction
   without becoming Call.

6. **Women as agents, not symbols.** Lorena, Clara, Maggie —
   each has a ledger, a debt, a child, a refusal. They are not
   prizes and not victims-in-waiting.

### Failure modes to avoid

- **Sentimental cowboy.** McMurtry loves his characters; he
  does not romanticize them. Gus is funny and selfish. Call is
  competent and emotionally crippled. Do not write a cowboy
  who is wise and gentle and good with horses and good with
  children. He does not exist.
- **Humor that exits the period.** Modern stand-up phrasing,
  pop-culture beats, and self-aware bits break the spell.
  Humor in McMurtry is always about the person across the
  fire, the weather, the work, the body.

### Pastiche guideline

> Pea Eye had been with Call eleven years and could read his
> silences the way other men read print. When Call stood up
> from the fire and looked north for a long minute Pea Eye
> knew there would be riding before sundown and went to saddle
> the bay without being told.

What that paragraph does: long-tenure relationship as fact,
silence as language, action without speech, no commentary on
the bond.

### Use McMurtry when

- Writing a working camp.
- Rendering a long-tenure friendship.
- Putting a woman on the page who is not a love interest.
- Defusing a tense scene with a crude joke.
- Killing a beloved character quietly.

## Charles Portis — first-person voice

Source novel: _True Grit_ (1968).

### Voice

Mattie Ross narrates _True Grit_ as a 19th-century literate
woman: dry, exact, pious, formal, often funny without meaning to
be. Period-authentic diction without dialect-spelling. Religious
and legal vocabulary woven through. No contractions in narration;
contractions in dialogue.

### Properties to copy

1. **Formal register.** _I do not know_ instead of _I don't know._
   _It was given to me that…_ instead of _I learned that…_
   _Some say…_ instead of _People say…_

2. **Embedded authority.** Mattie cites scripture, lawyers,
   prices, distances, names. She earns her narrative authority
   by knowing things and refusing to embellish.

3. **Comedy through earnestness.** Mattie is funniest when she
   is most serious. The reader laughs because she will not.

4. **Period vocabulary without showing off.** _Particulars,
   reckon, allow as how, directly_ (meaning _immediately_),
   _figure_ (as a verb meaning _to estimate_), _dear_ (meaning
   _expensive_), _fetch_ (meaning _bring_), _bound_ (meaning
   _certain to_).

### Failure modes to avoid

- **Costume diction.** Sprinkling _yonder, reckon, mighty_ into
  dialogue without controlling the rest of the register. Either
  commit to the period voice or stay neutral.
- **Misplaced contractions.** A literate 1870s narrator writes
  _do not_ and _cannot._ An illiterate cowhand says _don't_ and
  _cain't._ Mixing them in one mouth breaks the spell.

### Use Portis when

- Writing first-person memoir-style narration.
- Voicing a literate character (preacher, schoolteacher, judge,
  lawyer, journalist).
- Rendering a deposition, letter, or telegram.
- Writing a child or young adult narrator who is smarter than
  the adults around her.

## Elmore Leonard — the dialogue spine

Source novels and stories: _Hombre_ (1961), _Valdez Is Coming_
(1970), _Forty Lashes Less One_ (1972), _The Tonto Woman and
Other Western Stories._

### Voice

Leonard's westerns move through speech. The narrator is invisible.
Characters reveal themselves through what they say, what they
refuse to say, and the small physical actions between lines.
Dialogue tags collapse to _said_; everything else lives in the
beat between speeches.

### Properties to copy

1. **Said is invisible.** _He averred, he opined, he rejoined_
   are dead. _He said_ and _she said_ are wallpaper; the reader
   does not see them. Use _said_ or no tag.

2. **Beats instead of tags.** Action between lines does the work
   of attribution and emotion:

   > _I'm not going._
   >
   > Russell turned the cup in his hands.
   >
   > _They will come for you here._

   Russell's silence and the hand on the cup tell you who is
   refusing and who is warning. No _said worriedly_ needed.

3. **Withholding.** Characters do not say what they want. They
   say something nearby. The reader closes the gap.

4. **The third line lands.** Leonard scenes often work in beats
   of three: setup, deflection, hard return. The hard return
   ends the exchange.

5. **Period-correct slang only.** Leonard researches his diction.
   No anachronistic phrasing.

### Failure modes to avoid

- **Witty banter.** Leonard's characters are not Tarantino's.
  They speak short, plain, often blunt. Cleverness is rare and
  costs the speaker something.
- **Author commentary in the beats.** Beats are physical actions,
  not authorial mood. _He turned the cup in his hands_ is right.
  _He turned the cup in his hands, knowing what was coming_ is
  wrong.

### Use Leonard when

- Writing any conversation longer than four lines.
- Writing a confrontation in a room.
- Writing a deal, a threat, a refusal.
- Writing a deathbed exchange.

## Oakley Hall — moral complexity

Source novel: _Warlock_ (1958).

### Voice

Hall writes the town western. The miners, the marshal, the
gambler, the citizens' committee, the deputies, the mining
company foreman, the doctor, the prostitutes — every faction
has a reason. Nobody is purely right. The reader chooses whose
reason to trust and is punished for the choice.

### Properties to copy

1. **Multiple POVs without a hero.** A scene in Warlock might
   sit with the marshal in the morning, the miner at noon, the
   judge at supper. The town is the protagonist; the people are
   its organs.

2. **Documents as structure.** Letters, journal entries, town
   records, newspaper clippings appear as chapter epigraphs and
   chapter texture. The town has a memory; the memory contradicts
   itself.

3. **Violence with consequence.** A killing in Warlock changes
   the price of beef, the marriage of a deputy, the politics of
   the next election. The bullet does not stop in the body.

4. **Civic vocabulary.** _Citizens' committee, vigilance
   committee, civic order, the ordinance, the writ, the warrant,
   the deputation._ The town western runs on paper.

### Use Hall when

- Writing a town with factions.
- Writing the aftermath of a killing.
- Writing a strike, a lynching, an election, a vigilante action.
- Writing a marshal with a divided loyalty.

## Joe R. Lansdale — grindhouse tempo

Source novels: _The Magic Wagon_ (1986), _Paradise Sky_ (2015),
_Dead in the West_ (1986).

### Voice

Lansdale writes the East-Texas pulp western: fast, profane,
funny, brutal. Dialogue is loose and in dialect. The supernatural
sometimes leaks in. Heads come off. The narrator is often a wry
first-person voice.

### Properties to copy

1. **Speed.** Short paragraphs. Hard cuts. The reader does not
   slow down because the prose does not let them.

2. **Profanity as period vocabulary.** Soldiers, drovers,
   railroad men, and saloon women cussed. The cussing is in
   period idiom, not modern fuck-as-comma.

3. **Earned shock.** Lansdale uses extreme violence to mark a
   line crossed. After the line crosses, the world does not
   recover.

4. **Black cowboys, Mexican vaqueros, immigrant outcasts as
   center, not margin.** _Paradise Sky_ puts a Black cowboy at
   the center of the Deadwood myth and renders him whole.

### Failure modes to avoid

- **Pulp drift into camp.** Lansdale's pulp works because the
  pain is real. If the reader cannot feel a hit, the speed
  becomes cartoon.
- **Dialect-on-the-page that condescends.** Lansdale gets away
  with it because his ear is sharp and his affection for the
  speakers is plain. Inexperienced writers should render dialect
  through word choice and rhythm, not aggressive eye-spelling.

### Use Lansdale when

- Writing a grindhouse interlude.
- Writing a non-Anglo cowhand at the center of the action.
- Writing a profane saloon scene.
- Writing a chase, a brawl, or a desperate fight.

## Glendon Swarthout — elegiac realism

Source novels: _The Shootist_ (1975), _The Homesman_ (1988).

### Voice

Swarthout writes the late western — the dying gunfighter, the
broken homesteader's wife, the failure of the dream. Prose is
plain and mournful without being sentimental. Period detail
researched to the saddle nail.

### Properties to copy

1. **Late-West mood.** Trains in the distance, fences going up,
   the open range closing. The character feels the era ending
   without naming it.

2. **Bodily decline.** _The Shootist_ is about a man dying of
   prostate cancer. The body fails on the page in concrete
   detail without melodrama.

3. **Women's interior West.** _The Homesman_ puts insanity,
   miscarriage, and rape on the page from a woman's POV without
   making the women symbols.

### Use Swarthout when

- Writing a character who knows he is finished.
- Writing the closing of the frontier.
- Writing a homestead failing.
- Writing a sick or dying body over time.

## Ron Hansen — documentary poise

Source novels: _The Assassination of Jesse James by the Coward
Robert Ford_ (1983), _Desperadoes_ (1979), _Atticus_ (1996).

### Voice

Hansen writes the West with the patience of a historian and the
ear of a poet. Long sentences pile precise period detail.
Characters are rendered with sympathy and without absolution.
Heavy use of researched fact: prices, weather records, train
schedules.

### Properties to copy

1. **Documentary saturation.** Specific newspaper names, specific
   gun makes, specific weather on specific days. The reader
   trusts the author because the facts are dense.

2. **Sympathetic villainy.** Robert Ford is rendered as a man
   with a story, not a coward-shaped hole. Same for Jesse.
   Neither is excused.

3. **Long sinuous sentences with concrete spines.** Hansen's
   long sentences work because each clause adds a hard noun.

### Use Hansen when

- Writing a real or quasi-real historical figure.
- Writing a chapter that needs documentary weight.
- Writing the inner life of a man on the wrong side without
  excusing him.

## A.B. Guthrie Jr. — the high plains

Source novels: _The Big Sky_ (1947), _The Way West_ (1949).

### Voice

Guthrie writes the mountain man and the wagon train with patient
landscape and quiet psychological realism. The Rocky Mountain
fur trade, the Oregon Trail, the Blackfeet country.

### Use Guthrie when

- Writing pre-1860 frontier (mountain men, fur trade,
  pre-cattle-drive era).
- Writing a wagon train or long overland journey.
- Writing a character who knows tribal politics from inside.

---

## Frederick Manfred — Dakota and the Northern Plains

Source novels: _Lord Grizzly_ (1954), _Riders of Judgment_ (1957),
_Conquering Horse_ (1959).

### Voice

Manfred writes the upper Missouri, the Black Hills, and the
Sioux country with mythic weight and physical precision. _Lord
Grizzly_ is the Hugh Glass story rendered with the bear-mauling
in clinical detail and the crawl back to civilization in
hallucinatory present-tense.

### Use Manfred when

- Writing the northern plains.
- Writing a survival ordeal.
- Writing a Sioux, Cheyenne, or Arikara POV character.

## Walter Van Tilburg Clark — moral weight

Source novel: _The Ox-Bow Incident_ (1940).

### Voice

Clark writes the lynching western. Tight time, tight cast,
moral pressure without speeches. Every character implicated.
Use as a model for any scene where a town does something it
will have to live with.

### Use Clark when

- Writing a vigilante scene.
- Writing a posse going wrong.
- Writing a character who knows he should object and does not.

## Pete Dexter — late-frontier corruption

Source novel: _Deadwood_ (1986).

### Voice

Dexter writes Deadwood as filth and commerce. Wild Bill, Charlie
Utter, the Chinese quarter, the smallpox, the mud. Comic and
brutal in the same paragraph. The HBO series learned from him.

### Use Dexter when

- Writing a boomtown.
- Writing famous frontier figures off-stage from their legends.
- Writing disease in a town.

## Patrick deWitt — modernist western

Source novel: _The Sisters Brothers_ (2011).

### Voice

DeWitt writes the western as a deadpan picaresque with a
brother-narrator who is gentle and slow and observant. Use as a
model for first-person voice that is funny, melancholy, and
period without being mannered.

### Use deWitt when

- Writing a road western with two travelers.
- Writing a first-person narrator who is not the dangerous one.
- Writing a melancholy comic register.

## Supporting shelf (read for texture, not voice)

- **Dorothy M. Johnson** — short stories _A Man Called Horse,
  The Hanging Tree, The Man Who Shot Liberty Valance._ Compact,
  morally complex.
- **Andy Adams** — _The Log of a Cowboy_ (1903). Non-fiction
  trail-drive log; the most authentic source on cowhand routine.
- **J. Frank Dobie** — folklore and ranch history. Source for
  authentic Texas idiom.
- **Mari Sandoz** — _Old Jules_ (1935), _Crazy Horse_ (1942).
  Plains and Native lives rendered with research and partisan
  warmth.
- **Willa Cather** — _My Ántonia, O Pioneers!_ For homesteading
  and Plains immigrant life.
- **Stephen Crane** — _The Bride Comes to Yellow Sky, The Blue
  Hotel._ Compressed western shorts.
- **Ambrose Bierce** — Civil War shorts inform the residue of
  veteran trauma in any western set after 1865.

## Composite: when to mix voices

Most chapters in a manuscript or fiction draft will require
blending. The default mix:

- **McMurtry** as the base — character, work, mundane detail.
- **McCarthy** for the openings, the landscape passages, the
  violence.
- **Leonard** for any sustained dialogue.
- **Portis** for first-person narration or in-world documents
  (a posted notice, a letter, a deposition).
- **Hall** when the town has factions and the killing has
  consequences.

Avoid: pure McCarthy for an entire chapter (becomes oppressive),
pure Lansdale for an entire chapter (becomes cartoon), pure
Hansen for an entire chapter (becomes a thesis).

## What none of these writers do

Use this list as a reverse check on your own draft.

- They do not explain their characters' feelings in interior
  monologue using modern emotional vocabulary.
- They do not give the reader a moral takeaway.
- They do not summarize a scene at its end.
- They do not break for safety scaffolding around hard content.
- They do not vary their nouns to avoid repetition.
- They do not use the word _journey_ metaphorically.
- They do not write _the silence was deafening_ or _time stood
  still_ or any other dead simile.
- They do not write _suddenly._ The action is sudden because
  it arrives without warning, not because the narrator says so.

If your draft does any of these things, it is not in the canon.
