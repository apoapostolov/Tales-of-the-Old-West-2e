# Uncomfortable Truths

The West of 1865–1900 was a country built on dispossession,
violence, racial hierarchy, sexual exploitation, disease, and
the ordinary small cruelties of poverty. A western that softens
those things into adventure is a lie. A western that wallows in
them for shock is also a lie.

This file describes what was true, how the canon writes it, and
how to write it without flinching and without exploitation.

The two failure modes to avoid:

1. **Bowdlerization.** The 1950s movie western. Clean towns,
   clean killings, noble Indians at a distance, women who exist
   to be rescued, no Black faces in the cast. This was a fantasy
   then and is unwritable now.
2. **Grimdark wallow.** The 21st-century reflex of piling on
   atrocity for atrocity's sake. The reader stops feeling. The
   atrocity becomes the same as decoration.

The third way is the canon's way: write the wrong thing as a
fact in the world the characters move through. Let the reader
provide the horror. Do not flinch and do not perform.

## 1. Race

### a. The actual demographic West

The cowhand workforce on Texas drives in the 1870s and 1880s was
roughly one-third Black, Mexican, or both. The Black cowboy is
not a recent invention; he was a working majority in many
outfits. Bose Ikard rode for Charles Goodnight. Nat Love wrote
his own memoir. Bass Reeves rode as a Deputy U.S. Marshal in the
Indian Territory and was one of the most effective lawmen in
American history. The Buffalo Soldiers (the 9th and 10th
Cavalry, the 24th and 25th Infantry) garrisoned much of the
Southwest after Reconstruction.

Mexican vaqueros — *vaqueros* before the word *cowboy* existed
in English — taught Anglo-Texans every piece of cattle work the
trail drove on. The saddle, the lariat, the chaparreras, the
remuda, the corral itself — all Spanish words because all Mexican
inheritances.

The Chinese laborers who built the Central Pacific from
Sacramento to Promontory Summit numbered around twelve thousand
at peak. They worked the railroad cuts and the Sierra tunnels
under conditions that killed hundreds. After the railroad, they
ran laundries, restaurants, and gardens in nearly every western
town. The Chinese Exclusion Act passed in 1882.

The Native nations were — at the period of most western fiction —
in the late stages of being broken: the Lakota and Cheyenne after
the Black Hills War (1876–77), the Comanche and Kiowa onto the
reservation in 1875, the Apache pursued through the 1880s, the
Ghost Dance and Wounded Knee in 1890. To set a story in 1875
Wyoming and ignore the Lakota is to set a story in occupied
France in 1942 and ignore the Germans.

### b. Period language

White characters in the period used racist language as a
default. The N-word, *Mexican* used as a slur, *Chinaman,
chink, John* (for any Chinese man), *redskin, savage, blanket
Indian, breed* (for any mixed-race person), *greaser, dago,
mick* — all in common Anglo working-class speech.

How to handle this in fiction:

- A character racist by the standards of his own time and
  place is not a villain. He is a man.
- Period racist language belongs in the mouths of period
  characters when it is needed to render them honestly. Use it
  rarely and only when its absence would be a falsification.
- The N-word in particular: most contemporary publishers and
  most contemporary readers will object to it on the page even
  in period fiction. McCarthy uses it sparingly. McMurtry
  mostly avoids it by writing characters who would have used
  it without using it. The cowardly compromise is to never use
  it; the honest compromise is to use it once or twice when a
  character uses it and to make sure the prose registers what
  has happened. The exploitative use is to use it as common
  texture.
- Slurs against Mexicans and Chinese should follow the same
  rule but the threshold is lower: *greaser* and *Chinaman*
  appear in plenty of canon fiction.
- Native characters are referred to in period speech by tribal
  name when known (*Comanche, Apache, Lakota*) and by *Indian*
  generally. *Native American* is a 1970s term. Do not use it
  in dialogue or in the narrator's voice in period prose.
- The narrator does not use slurs the characters use. The
  narrator may use *Negro, colored* (the period-respectful
  Anglo terms), *Mexican, Tejano* (when accurate), *Chinese*
  (not *Chinaman*), and the tribal name when known.

### c. Writing non-Anglo characters

The canon offers strong models:

- **Black characters at the center.** Lansdale's *Paradise Sky.*
  Charles Portis's *True Grit* (Lucky Ned Pepper's gang
  includes a Black member; the Texas Ranger LaBoeuf is a
  southerner with the racial views of a southerner of 1873).
  Sidney Poitier's screen presence in *Buck and the Preacher*
  pointed to a literary mode that has been underwritten.
- **Mexican characters at the center.** McCarthy's Border
  Trilogy. Oakley Hall's *Warlock.*
- **Native characters at the center.** Mari Sandoz's *Crazy
  Horse* and *Cheyenne Autumn.* James Welch's *Fools Crow.*
  Frederick Manfred's *Conquering Horse.* Thomas Berger's
  *Little Big Man.*
- **Chinese characters at the center.** Underrepresented in the
  classic canon. Recent: Tom Lin's *The Thousand Crimes of Ming
  Tsu* (2021).

### d. Hard rules for non-Anglo characters

- The character has a name, a family, a trade, a debt, a
  refusal. Same as any character.
- The character is not a wisdom-figure for the white
  protagonist. The Indian who exists to teach the cowboy about
  the land is a stock part. The Mexican who exists to feed the
  cowboy beans and disappear is a stock part. The Chinese man
  who exists to do the laundry and have his queue pulled is a
  stock part.
- The character speaks his own language at home with his own
  people. (Italicize first appearance; do not italicize after.
  Or follow the McCarthy practice and never italicize.)
- The character's dialect, if rendered, is rendered with the
  same care as an Anglo dialect. *No tickee, no laundry* is a
  white invention. Real Cantonese-accented English of the
  period had its own patterns; if you cannot research them,
  use plain English with a few period-appropriate phrasings
  and let the reader hear the voice.
- The character's relationship to the dominant Anglo world is
  not the only thing about the character.

## 2. Native genocide

The Plains Indian Wars and the Apache Wars were genocidal in
intent and in outcome. The buffalo were exterminated as a
deliberate strategy to break Plains nations. The treaty system
was a fraud. The reservation system was a slow killing.

### a. What the period reader / character knew

- White characters in 1875 knew there was an "Indian problem."
  Most Anglos believed extermination or removal was the right
  policy. A minority (Indian agents, some Quakers, some
  cavalry officers, certain reformers) believed the policy was
  unjust.
- Most Anglos had no contact with reservation Indians. They had
  contact with raiders and with Indian scouts working for the
  Army.
- Native characters in 1875 knew they were losing or had lost.
  The Comanche had been broken at Palo Duro in 1874. The
  Lakota would be broken at Slim Buttes in 1876. The Apache
  fought a longer war but knew the end.

### b. What the canon does

- It does not turn away from massacres (Sand Creek 1864,
  Washita 1868, Marias 1870, Wounded Knee 1890). When a story
  touches one of these, it touches it directly.
- It does not turn the Indian into a noble symbol. Mari Sandoz
  writes Crazy Horse as a man with politics, factions, and
  marriages.
- It does not turn the cavalryman into a one-dimensional
  villain. Custer, in the better fiction (Hansen, Welch), is
  vain and complicated.
- It does not let the white character get off easy. The cowboy
  whose family was killed at the homestead by Comanche raiders
  is a real character. So is the Comanche whose village was
  burned at Palo Duro.

### c. How to write a scene of frontier conflict

- Name the nation. Not *the Indians.* The *Comanche* or the
  *Cheyenne* or the *Apache* or the *Modoc.*
- Place it. The Comanche of 1874 are not the Lakota of 1876
  are not the Apache of 1885.
- Render the violence on both sides as bodily fact, not as
  set-piece. A village burned is a village burned. A wagon
  party scalped is a wagon party scalped. The reader knows
  which is the larger crime by accumulation, not by the prose
  weighting it.
- Resist the captivity-narrative cliche. The white woman taken
  by Comanche and "rescued" is one of the oldest western
  stories. Cynthia Ann Parker's actual life — taken at nine,
  raised Comanche, married Peta Nocona, mother of Quanah,
  forcibly returned to Anglo society at thirty-four, starved
  herself to death — is the antidote to every novel-version of
  the cliche.

### d. Rule: the genocide is in the air

If your western is set in the Plains or the Southwest between
1865 and 1890, the breaking of the Native nations is in the air
of every scene whether the scene is about it or not. The
character who has never thought about it is a character whose
willful blindness is a fact about him. The town that is built
on land taken in 1868 is a town that knows.

## 3. Slavery's residue

The Civil War ended in 1865. Most westerns are set in its
shadow. The frontier was settled by veterans of both armies and
by freedmen and freedwomen.

- The veteran ex-Confederate who went west to escape
  Reconstruction is a real and common character (Jesse and
  Frank James, Coleman Younger, Quantrill's surviving raiders).
- The Black freedman who went west for opportunity (Bass Reeves,
  Nat Love, the Buffalo Soldiers) is also real and common, and
  largely missing from the screen western.
- The Black town (Nicodemus, Kansas; Boley, Oklahoma) was a
  real settlement pattern.
- The Black freedman in a white cattle outfit was treated by
  the working code with a complicated mix of acceptance (he
  was a hand) and continued racial subordination (he often
  cooked, he often slept apart, he was often paid less, the
  trail boss could and sometimes did beat him with impunity).
- The ex-Confederate riding next to the freedman on a drive is
  one of the underwritten scenes of American fiction.

The bushwhacker / jayhawker memory of the Kansas-Missouri border
war shaped a generation of western outlaws. Quantrill's raid on
Lawrence in 1863 — over 150 unarmed men and boys killed — was
the formative trauma of the James-Younger gang.

## 4. Women's lives

The actual conditions of women in the West, by class:

### a. Anglo wife on a homestead

Up at four. Built the fire, cooked breakfast for husband and
hands, milked the cow, fed the chickens, gardened, baked, washed
(once a week, all day), churned (twice a week), made and mended
clothes, schooled the children, nursed the sick, attended births
and deaths in the neighborhood, often did the books. Childbirth
killed roughly one in two hundred mothers per birth. A woman
might bear eight to twelve children and bury three or four.

### b. Anglo woman in town

Schoolteacher (often single), preacher's wife, doctor's wife,
boarding-house keeper, laundress, milliner, dressmaker, cook in
a hotel, telegraph clerk (later), postmistress (rarer but real).

### c. Black woman in the West

Most as cooks, laundresses, nursemaids, midwives, schoolteachers
in Black communities. Smaller numbers as ranchers and farmers.
The lifespan disadvantage was severe: poor nutrition, harder
work, less access to medicine, more childbirth deaths.

### d. Mexican / Tejana woman

The household economy of the rancho was hers. The kitchen, the
garden, the children, the sick, the religious life of the
household. In larger rancho operations she ran a substantial
enterprise.

### e. Native woman

Pre-reservation, her work was the lodge, the food, the hides,
the children, the move. The economic backbone of nomadic Plains
society. Post-reservation, she lost most of that work and most
of the social standing that came with it.

### f. The line — the women in the trade

A western town of any size had women selling sex. They were
arranged in a hierarchy:

- **Parlor-house women.** Top of the trade. A piano in the
  parlor, a madam, regular clientele, dresses, decent food.
  Lifespan still short — alcohol, opium, syphilis, occasional
  violence.
- **Saloon girls / hurdy-gurdy girls.** Worked the saloon
  floor. Sold drinks, danced, sometimes more. Drank with the
  customers (the drinks were usually cold tea for them).
  Lifespan shorter.
- **Crib girls.** Bottom of the trade. A single small room
  on a back street, a bed, a basin. Took twenty to fifty
  customers a night in a boomtown. Average lifespan in the
  trade about four years. Alcoholism, opium, beatings,
  syphilis, suicide. The suicide rate was extraordinary.
- **Soiled doves who married out.** Some women left the trade
  by marriage to a customer or a former customer. The marriage
  was sometimes a rescue and sometimes another captivity.

The canon writes these women as people. Lorena Wood in *Lonesome
Dove.* The women of *Deadwood* (Pete Dexter and the HBO series).
The captive women of *The Homesman.*

How to write them:

- Name the price.
- Name the work.
- Render the small dignities the woman insists on (the dress
  she keeps clean, the saved coin, the refused customer, the
  Bible on the bedside).
- Render the costs without flinching: the laudanum bottle, the
  bruises, the dead infant in the privy, the morning the
  madam finds her cold.

## 5. Disease

The frontier killed more people by germ than by gun. See
`knowledge/medicine-and-death.md` for the medical detail. The
relevant truth here:

- **Cholera** swept the Oregon and California trails in the
  1849–52 emigrations and killed thousands. Buried roadside.
- **Smallpox** ravaged Native populations who had not been
  exposed. Reduction of 50–90% of population in many tribes.
  Anglo populations were largely vaccinated by mid-century but
  outbreaks happened.
- **Tuberculosis (consumption)** was the leading killer of
  young adults. Many western towns existed for the climate
  cure.
- **Typhoid** killed in any town with bad water.
- **Diphtheria** killed children in waves.
- **Syphilis and gonorrhea** were widespread, especially among
  soldiers and the women of the line. Mercury treatment was
  brutal and ineffective.
- **Influenza** waves killed seasonally.
- **Childbirth fever** killed mothers.
- **Measles, mumps, scarlet fever, whooping cough** killed
  children.

The character who buries a child of diphtheria has a different
inner life than the character who has not. The town with a
graveyard of small markers is a town with a story.

## 6. Suicide

Common, especially among:

- Veterans of both armies (Civil War PTSD was unnamed but
  pervasive).
- Women on the line.
- Failed homesteaders.
- Broken alcoholics.
- Men ruined in the panic of 1873 or the panic of 1893.

The methods were close to hand: a pistol, a rope, laudanum, the
river. The canon writes suicide minimally and with respect. A
character's suicide is rendered in two or three sentences and
is not explained.

> They found Jensen in the barn at first light. He had used
> the carbine. The note on the workbench said he was sorry for
> the trouble.

That is the entire scene, in many westerns. The reader
constructs the rest.

## 7. The futility

A through-line of the literary western is the futility of the
work. Cattle drives ruined ranchers more often than they made
them. Mines played out. Homesteads failed. Towns died when the
railroad bypassed them. The Indian wars ended with the Indian
ways ended, but the white settlement they enabled ruined as many
of the settlers as it enriched.

The canon does not pretend the project worked. McMurtry's *Dead
Man's Walk* and *Comanche Moon* are about how the Texas Rangers
made the country safe at terrible cost — and at the end the
country is dust. McCarthy's *No Country for Old Men* sits in the
ashes of all of it.

A western that ends in unambiguous triumph is fighting the
canon. If your story ends well, end it with somebody dead, with
a debt unpaid, with a piece of the country lost.

## 8. The soldier coming home

The Civil War produced about 750,000 dead and an equivalent
multiple of veterans with what the period called *soldier's heart*
or *nostalgia* and what the 21st century calls PTSD. Many of these
men went west. Many of them never recovered.

How to write the veteran:

- He startles at sudden noise.
- He drinks more than he can afford.
- He does not speak of the war except by accident.
- He carries the rifle he carried in the war or has gotten rid
  of it on principle.
- He has scars he does not explain.
- He has a wound that aches in the cold.
- He does not sleep through the night.
- His friends are other veterans, often of the same regiment,
  often of the opposing army. (Veterans of opposing armies
  drank together more than veterans of opposing armies talk
  about.)

## 9. Cruelty to animals

The frontier was hard on horses, mules, oxen, dogs, and cattle.
A working horse on a long drive was used until it failed. A
mule was beaten to keep it pulling. A dog that bit was shot. A
cow with a broken leg was shot. A horse that broke a leg in a
hole was shot.

Render this as fact, not as color. When a character is
*deliberately* cruel — beating a horse for the joy of it,
torturing a captured dog — the prose marks the cruelty as a
fact about that character.

The character who is *gentle* with animals against the period
norm is a character marked by his gentleness. Charles Goodnight
was famous for a humane stockman's reputation in a brutal trade.
His fame was the proof of how rare the practice was.

## 10. The small wrongs

The big wrongs (genocide, slavery's residue, the killing of the
buffalo) get attention because they are large. The small wrongs
are equally a part of the texture:

- The drover who beats the cook.
- The trail boss who docks a Black hand's pay.
- The husband who beats the wife and is not stopped by the town.
- The father who works a son to disability.
- The town doctor who short-changes the Mexican family.
- The judge who hangs a fifteen-year-old for stealing a horse.
- The deputy who shoots a fleeing prisoner in the back.
- The neighbor who jumps the dead man's claim before the body
  is cold.

The western that names these things at the level of a cup of
coffee is the western that breathes. The reader does not need
to be told they are wrong. The prose treats them as the country's
weather.

## 11. How to write a hard scene

- **Decide what the scene is for.** A hanging, a rape, a
  killing, a beating, a burial of a child. If the scene exists
  to advance plot, render minimally. If the scene exists to
  mark a moral fact, render at the length the fact requires
  and no longer.
- **Render bodily.** The smell, the sound, the weight, the
  position of the body. Do not editorialize.
- **Withhold the worst at the worst moment.** The reader's
  imagination does the work. The page that describes the
  rapist's face in detail is a page that has overworked.
- **Do not give a moral.** The scene is the moral.
- **Do not soften the aftermath.** The bruise lasts. The
  funeral arrives. The marriage breaks. The town remembers.

A worked example. A homestead burned by raiders. The scene the
canon writes:

> They came on the place at noon. The barn was down and the
> cabin was a black square in the snow. The man was lying in
> the dooryard with his head against the well-stone and a
> bullet in his chest. The woman was inside. They did not let
> the boy go in.

That is the scene. There is no description of the woman's
condition. The reader knows. The line *they did not let the boy
go in* does the work of a paragraph of description without the
indignity of doing it.

## 12. The author's stance

The western canon writes hard things because the country
contained them. The author's stance is not "this was good" and
not "this was bad" — the stance is "this was so." Moral judgment
is left to the reader.

This is harder than either alternative. The reader trusts a
narrator who reports without flinching. The narrator who editorializes
the horror loses the reader's trust because the reader feels managed.
The narrator who hides the horror loses the reader because the
reader knows the country.

Write the country. Write what was so. Trust the reader.
