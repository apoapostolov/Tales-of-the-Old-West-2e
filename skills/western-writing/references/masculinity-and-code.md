# Masculinity and the Code

This file is about the interior life of hard men in a hard
country, and about how to put it on the page without modern
therapy talk and without cartoon swagger. The western canon's
men are not monoliths. They are competent, scared, loyal,
shamed, lustful, hungry, tired, and aware. They show none of it
the way a 21st-century man shows it. Writing them well requires
a different operating system.

This file is also about women in the same world, because the
masculinity of the West is shaped at every turn by the women
who tolerate, marry, leave, and bury it. _Lonesome Dove_ is a
book about men because it is also a book about Clara, Lorena,
Maggie, and Elmira.

---

## 1. The first principle: action is interiority

The western page does not get inside a character's head for long.
The reader knows what the character feels because of what the
character does, refuses to do, says, fails to say, eats, drinks,
or stares at.

| What the character feels | How a modern novel shows it                          | How the western shows it                                                                               |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Grief                    | Internal monologue, memory of the lost               | He saddled the dead man's horse and led it back to the corral and unsaddled it again.                  |
| Fear                     | "His heart pounded. He could feel the panic rising." | He noticed his hand on the cup was steady and was surprised.                                           |
| Love                     | Stated or thought                                    | He had ridden eight days out of his way to pass the house.                                             |
| Shame                    | Stated or thought                                    | He did not look at the boy when he handed back the rope.                                               |
| Rage                     | Stated or thought, or shown by violence              | He did not say anything. He went and got his rifle and sat down on the porch with it across his knees. |
| Loyalty                  | Stated or thought                                    | He stayed.                                                                                             |

If your draft has a sentence beginning _He felt…_ or _He
realized…_ — interrogate it. Most of those sentences should
become an action.

### Before / after

**Before:**

> Maddox felt a deep sadness as he looked at the empty corral.
> He realized he was going to miss his old friend.

**After:**

> Maddox stood at the corral a long time. He went back to the
> bunkhouse and got the bay's old halter and put it on a peg by
> the door.

## 2. The codes

The code is a set of unwritten rules that govern what a man can
do and still face other men. Different codes governed different
parts of the West. They overlap, they contradict, and a character
is often inside more than one at once.

### a. The cowboy code (working drovers and ranchers)

- A man rides for the brand. While drawing his pay he gives the
  outfit complete loyalty.
- He does not steal another man's horse. Horse theft is a
  hanging offense and the cowhand expects it.
- He does not shoot a man in the back. He does not draw on an
  unarmed man. (Ideal — frequently broken.)
- He pays his debts at the saloon and the mercantile.
- He does not complain about the food, the weather, or the
  work in front of the boss. He complains to his fellow hands
  in the bunkhouse.
- He looks after the horses before he looks after himself.
- He shares his last water and his last tobacco.
- He never asks another man's business unprompted, especially
  about where he is from or what name he was born with.

### b. The outlaw code (post-war men on the scout)

- You do not turn on the men you ride with.
- You do not give a man up to the law. The law is not your
  arbitrator.
- You take care of a wounded man unless he is dying, in which
  case you may be obliged to shoot him.
- You do not rob a working man. You rob the railroad, the bank,
  the express company. (Ideal. Plenty of outlaws robbed
  anybody.)
- You stand by the fence-cutter, the hider, the squatter — the
  small man — against the big concern.
- You never ask another man's name.

### c. The hill code (Appalachian / Ozark / Hill Country

emigrants)

- Family first, last, always. A wrong against your kin is a
  wrong against you.
- A debt of blood is collected.
- A guest under your roof is sacred until they leave the porch.
- Hospitality is offered by default and refused at one's peril.
- Outsiders are presumed dangerous.

### d. The soldier's code (Confederate veteran, Union veteran,

Indian Wars cavalry)

- You do not abandon a wounded man if there is any way to bring
  him out.
- You obey the chain of command in front of an enemy. You
  argue with it later or never.
- You do not speak of the war unless asked, and not always then.
- You drink with men who served in the same army. You may drink
  with men who served in the other army if enough time has
  passed.
- You do not bring up Andersonville, Sherman's march, the
  Lawrence raid, Sand Creek, or the Wita Tanka unless you were
  there, and even then probably not.

### e. The peddler / drummer / townsman code

- You do not call out a man who can shoot you.
- You bow to the buying customer.
- You keep your books straight.
- You keep your wife and daughters out of the saloon end of
  Main Street.

### f. The Native codes (vary by nation, generalized

cautiously)

- Different nations had radically different practices. A
  Comanche raider's honor system was not a Lakota's was not a
  Diné's was not a Hopi's was not a Cheyenne dog soldier's. Do
  not generalize a "Native code." See
  `knowledge/peoples-and-conflict.md`.

### Code conflict is the engine of western drama

A scene works because two codes meet. The cowboy who rides for
the brand and the outlaw he grew up with. The Confederate
veteran and the Buffalo Soldier sergeant. The hill man whose
brother was hanged by the law and the marshal who hanged him.
_Lonesome Dove_ runs on Call's hill code (a man's son is his
son even if he won't admit it) breaking against his soldier's
code (a man does not lie about his blood). McMurtry never names
either code. He just makes them collide.

## 3. Period interiority

The men of the western canon do not have access to the
vocabulary of psychology. They feel the same things modern
people feel and they call those things by 19th-century names,
or they do not name them at all.

### Translation table: modern emotion → period interiority

| Modern              | Period equivalent (verb / phrase)                                              |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Process my feelings | Sit with it. Chew on it. Sleep on it.                                          |
| Set boundaries      | Tell him plain. Lay down what I would and would not do.                        |
| Feel triggered      | Lose my temper. Get my back up. Have it come over me.                          |
| Trauma              | What the war done. What I seen. The thing at the river.                        |
| Anxiety             | Nerves. The fidgets. A bad turn. Worry.                                        |
| Depression          | The black dog. Low spirits. Melancholia (literate).                            |
| Suicidal ideation   | He had thought of ending it. He kept his pistol on the night-stand.            |
| Self-care           | Tend to oneself. Look after one's own. (Rare phrasing — the concept is alien.) |
| Closure             | Make peace. Settle accounts. Bury him proper.                                  |
| Communicate         | Speak plain. Have words. Talk it over.                                         |
| Healing             | Mending. Coming round. Pulling through. (Bodily, not emotional.)               |
| Validate            | Allow as how he had a point. Grant him that.                                   |
| Toxic               | Mean. Bad. No good. Sour. Poisoned.                                            |
| Empower             | (No equivalent. Concept is anachronistic.)                                     |
| Anxiety attack      | A spell. The horrors. (Specifically alcohol withdrawal but generalizable.)     |

### Before / after

**Before:**

> He needed to process the trauma of what had happened at the
> river crossing. He felt triggered every time he heard rushing
> water.

**After:**

> The river was still in him. He could not hear running water
> without seeing the boy go under, and he had not been able to
> hear it for three years.

## 4. The interior monologue, when it is allowed

Western prose does dip into a character's head. The McMurtry
free-indirect mode is the most useful tool. The voice of the
narration drifts toward the character's diction, then drifts back.

### Worked example

> Call was up before the cook. Call was always up before the
> cook. There was no use lying in the bedroll listening to a
> man snore when there was light enough to count the herd, and
> any man who couldn't get up at first gray didn't deserve a
> drover's pay.

The sentence about who deserves a drover's pay is Call's opinion
in the narrator's voice. The narrator never says _Call thought._
The drift does the work.

### Rules for the drift

- The character must have a recognizable diction. Otherwise the
  drift is invisible.
- Drift in for a sentence or two and drift out. Do not stay.
- Do not drift into modern language even if the character is
  modern-minded for his time. The character is in 1875.

## 5. Fear, on the page

A frightened man in the western canon does one of three things:

1. **Acts.** Goes for the rifle, mounts, rides, draws.
2. **Refuses to act.** Does not get up from the chair. Does not
   answer. Does not look up.
3. **Acts wrong.** Spills the coffee. Drops the rein. Misses the
   first shot. Talks too much.

The narrator does not say the man is afraid. The reader sees
which of the three is happening.

### Worked example

> Sundown poured the coffee with both hands on the pot. The
> coffee went into the cup and over the cup and across the
> table. He set the pot down and looked at the spill and did
> not move to wipe it.

The reader knows. No _he was afraid_ needed.

## 6. Shame, on the page

Shame is the most underwritten emotion in commercial fiction and
the most useful one in the western. A man caught in a wrong, or
a man who failed his code, carries shame as a physical fact: he
will not look at certain people, he will not be in certain rooms,
he will drink alone, he will leave town.

### Worked example

> When the boy was buried Call did not stand with the others at
> the grave. He stood at the edge of the cemetery with the bay,
> holding the bridle in both hands, and when the preacher was
> done he led the bay back to the wagon and rode out of the
> town and did not come back to it for eleven years.

Eleven years is shame. The narrator never names it.

## 7. Lust and intimacy

The western canon handles physical intimacy with restraint, not
prudery. McMurtry writes Lorena and Gus's connection in _Lonesome
Dove_ with directness — what happens, who paid, who said what
afterward — and without graphic spectacle. McCarthy in _Cities of
the Plain_ writes John Grady's love for the prostitute with
patience and attention to small physical details: how she
crossed the room, how she held a coin.

### Rules

- Sex on the page is rare and short. The before and the after
  carry the meaning.
- Brothel scenes are common; the work is named without
  euphemism (_she was working the line_, _she was on the line_,
  _she had a crib_) and the women are named, paid, given a
  story.
- Romance is rendered through small acts: the man rides far out
  of his way; the woman keeps a letter; the man fixes a porch
  post; the woman saves a meal.
- Modern explicit erotica is not the register. Romance-novel
  euphemism (_his throbbing manhood_) is also not the register.
  When in doubt, withhold and end the scene early.

### Worked example

> She had a small house at the end of the street with a green
> door. He had been there twice in two years and the second
> time she had asked him to stay for breakfast and he had. He
> rode for a week thinking about the breakfast.

## 8. Friendship between men

The defining western relationship is the long-tenure male
friendship that does not get articulated. Gus and Call, Charlie
and Wild Bill, Russell and Mendez. The friendship is shown by
years of evidence and by what one man will do for the other when
the time comes. It is almost never spoken aloud.

### Rules

- Compliments are rare and small. _He'll do._ _He's no quitter._
  _He'll ride._ These are high praise.
- Insults inside friendship are constant and warm.
- A man does not say _I love you_ to another man in 1875. He
  rides out to die for him.
- The death of a friend is rendered minimally. The aftermath is
  rendered at length.

### Worked example

> They had been together since the war and Pea Eye knew that
> when Call put his coffee down with the cup half full it meant
> the day's good news was finished. He set his own cup aside
> and stood up.

## 9. Grief

Grief in the western canon arrives late. The man who has lost
something does not collapse. He keeps doing the work. The grief
shows up months later in a wrong turn, an unprovoked fight, a
night of drinking, a refusal to leave a campfire.

### Worked example

> A year after the boy was gone Call took to walking out at
> sundown with the bay and not riding her, just walking her on
> a long lead, and the hands learned not to follow him on those
> walks.

## 10. Lived masculinity vs posed masculinity

The canon's men are not posers. The poser is a stock character
who appears for the canon's men to see through.

### Lived masculinity (write this)

- Competent at specific tasks. Knows how to throw a diamond
  hitch, dress a horse's hoof, tell a thunderhead from a
  cumulus, read a hoof print.
- Does not narrate his competence. Does the thing.
- Carries fear without showing it.
- Would rather be quiet than be admired.
- Tolerates discomfort as a default state. Heat, cold, hunger,
  wet boots, saddle sore, bad water — endured without comment.
- Speaks plainly when he speaks. Says less than he knows.
- Acts on his code. Pays his debts. Stays for the wounded.
- Drinks with control or with intent — rarely for show.
- Knows the difference between a fight he must take and a fight
  he can walk away from.

### Posed masculinity (write this only as a stock to deflate)

- Announces his competence. _I'm the fastest gun in the
  territory._
- Swaggers and brags in a saloon.
- Threatens before he hits.
- Picks a fight with a smaller man.
- Treats women as property to be displayed.
- Wears the gun rig wrong — too low, too tooled, too clean.
- Drinks loudly, laughs loudly, complains about the food.
- The first man down in a real fight.

The collision of the lived and the posed is a reliable scene
engine: the bunkhouse newcomer with the silver-inlaid Colt and
the working hand with the worn Model 1860 Army.

## 11. The shadow side: when masculinity in the canon goes wrong

The western canon does not romanticize the men it writes. It
shows the wreckage these men make:

- **Domestic violence.** Drovers and outlaws and sober ranchers
  beat wives and children. The canon (McMurtry, Swarthout,
  Hansen) puts this on the page without spectacle.
- **Alcoholism.** Drinking destroys plenty of the canon's
  characters. Doc Holliday, the broken father, the failed
  preacher.
- **Casual racism.** Most Anglo characters in 1875 used
  language and held views that are unspeakable in 2026. See
  `references/uncomfortable-truths.md`.
- **Casual misogyny.** Most men of the period did not consult
  their wives on major decisions. The characters who did were
  remarked on.
- **Suicide.** Common, especially among veterans and broken
  men. Rendered minimally and respected.
- **Cruelty to animals.** Rendered as it actually happened —
  horses broken brutally, dogs shot for cause, mules beaten —
  but never as casual color. When a character is cruel to an
  animal, the prose marks it.

The canon writes these things because they were the texture of
the world. It does not write them for shock. The reader feels
the wrongness because the prose treats it as a fact, not as a
spectacle.

## 12. Women in this world

A long file on masculinity is incomplete without naming what the
women were doing. See `knowledge/peoples-and-conflict.md` for
the demographic and sociological detail. Brief here:

- **Wives** ran the household, the garden, the dairy, the
  schooling of children, the sick-bed, the books, and (often)
  the actual ranching while the men were on the drive.
- **Widows** held property and ran businesses. Widow Smith was
  a real economic actor in every western town.
- **Schoolteachers** were almost always women, often unmarried,
  often the most literate person within a hundred miles.
- **Doctors' wives, preachers' wives, sheriffs' wives** ran
  parallel social institutions.
- **Working women** — laundresses, cooks, seamstresses,
  midwives, hotel keepers, milliners, dressmakers, telegraph
  operators (later).
- **Working girls** — prostitutes, dance-hall girls, hurdy-gurdy
  girls, parlor-house women. The women on the line had a
  lifespan of about thirty-six years on average. Most were
  there because the alternatives were starvation.
- **Native women** carried the entire economic structure of
  most Plains tribes — the lodge, the food preparation, the
  hide-tanning, the children, the move.
- **Mexican and Tejana women** ran the household economy of the
  ranchos and the small farms; many ran their own enterprises.

A western that writes only the men is a western that is missing
half the world. Even a story that follows only male POVs should
have women on the page who are doing things — the woman at the
hotel desk, the woman tending the bar, the woman buying the
flour at the mercantile, the woman who refuses to come out of
the house when the riders pass.

### How to write a woman in the western voice

The same rules apply. Action is interiority. No therapy talk.
Period diction. The woman is competent in her own work the way
the man is competent in his.

> She had been at the desk eleven years. She knew which men
> would pay in coin, which in chits, and which would leave by
> the back stairs before sunup, and she had a different price
> for each.

## 13. Boys and the making of men

Boys in the western canon are present and at work. A twelve-
year-old on a ranch broke colts. A fourteen-year-old on a drive
rode point. A sixteen-year-old in a town apprentice held a job.
The making of a man is the breaking of a boy: the first kill,
the first whore, the first time he held a man down for someone
else, the first time he didn't.

The canon (McCarthy in _All the Pretty Horses_; McMurtry in
_Lonesome Dove_; Portis in _True Grit_) handles the boy-into-
man arc with weight. Modern coming-of-age beats (the wise mentor
speech, the inner-child reconciliation) do not appear. The boy
is changed by what he does, not by what he is told.

---

## 14. The failure of the code

Every code fails somebody. The cowboy code fails the cowhand who
won't betray a brand whose owner is rustling. The hill code fails
the daughter who marries outside the family. The soldier's code
fails the veteran who cannot stop fighting after the war is over.

The most powerful western scenes are the ones where a man is
forced to choose between two codes and is broken by the choice.
This is the engine of _Warlock,_ of _The Searchers,_ of _Streets
of Laredo,_ of _No Country for Old Men._ If your scene has only
one code in play, it is probably not as deep as it could be.

---

## 15. Final caution: the difference between the canon's men

and a 21st-century fantasy of them

The canon's men are not the men of the 1950s screen western,
who were chivalrous, monogamous, plain-spoken, and right. The
canon's men are confused, cruel, loyal, drunk, broken,
occasionally heroic, mostly tired. The reader who finishes
_Lonesome Dove_ does not finish it admiring Call. The reader is
left with the cost of being Call.

Write that. Not the screen version.
