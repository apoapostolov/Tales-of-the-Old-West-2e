# Point of View and Narrative Distance

This file is a craft module for the single most important
strategic decision a western prose writer makes before the
first sentence: who is seeing, from how far, and with what
access to interior. It complements `references/voices.md`
(which authors model which choices), `references/prose-craft.md`
(sentence and paragraph mechanics), and
`references/masculinity-and-code.md` (what kind of interior
the period allows at all). For dialogue voice fingerprints
within a chosen POV, cross-reference `references/speech-patterns.md`.

POV is not a stylistic preference. It is a contract with the
reader about what the prose can know and what it cannot. Most
slack writing — AI and otherwise — fails because that contract
is never set or is broken inside the same paragraph. This file
fixes both problems.

---

## 1. The four POVs that matter for western prose

Other modes exist. These are the four that produced the canon.

### a. Close third

The camera sits behind one character's eyes for a scene or a
chapter. The prose can render what that character sees, hears,
smells, remembers, and thinks — but only those. If the
viewpoint character cannot see the rider on the next ridge,
the rider on the next ridge cannot enter the prose until the
viewpoint character turns his head.

This is the McMurtry mode. _Lonesome Dove_ rotates close-third
across half a dozen viewpoint characters chapter by chapter.
Inside any one chapter the discipline is strict: when we are
with Newt, we hear what Newt hears and we know what Newt
knows.

The strength: intimacy without the limits of first person.
The trap: drift. The narrator slips from _Newt watched the
horses come up out of the draw_ into _The horses were tired
from a hard day's drive,_ which Newt cannot know unless he
has been with the horses or sees the lather. Every time the
prose claims a fact the viewpoint character cannot have, the
contract is broken. The reader does not name the break, but
the reader feels it.

### b. Omniscient

The camera floats. It can sit in any character, or none. It
can pull back to the country, the weather, the century. It
can speak in its own voice — judgment, irony, lament — that
no character holds.

This is the McCarthy mode. _Blood Meridian_ uses an omniscient
narrator who sees the kid, the judge, the comanches, the
mountains, and the cosmology in the same sentence. The
narrator has opinions and a syntax no character could speak.
The omniscient pull-back to landscape — the long sentence
that lifts above the action and renders the country in
geological time — is the move only an omniscient narrator
can make.

The strength: scale. The trap: the narrator becomes the only
voice in the book. Characters become specimens. If the
omniscient narrator never tightens to a single character's
sweat and fear, the prose has weight but no pulse.

### c. First person, retrospective

The narrator is one of the characters, telling the story
after the fact, with the perspective of distance. The voice
is locked to that character's diction, education, and
prejudices. The narrator can see only what the character
saw, and can know later facts only as they were learned later.

This is the Portis mode. Mattie Ross in _True Grit_ tells the
story of her fourteen-year-old self from many decades on. The
prose registers both the girl who acted and the woman who
remembers. The voice is the engine. Lose the voice for a
sentence and the book loses its ground.

The strength: voice as world. The trap: the writer's vocabulary
leaking into the narrator's. If Mattie says _impactful_ the
book is over.

### d. First person, contemporaneous

The narrator is one of the characters telling the story as
it unfolds — letters, journal, deposition, the chapter as
the day's entry. The prose has no retrospective shaping. The
narrator does not yet know how it ends.

Less common in the canon but powerful for specific forms:
the homesteader's letter, the soldier's journal, the
deathbed deposition. Pulls the reader inside the limits of
the moment. The trap: contrivance. The narrator must have
plausible time and reason to write what is being written.

### Why these four and not second person, multiple first, or

unreliable third

Second person reads as exercise. The canon does not use it.
Multiple first (rotating I-narrators) is novel-shop trickery
that breaks the western register's plain contract. The
unreliable narrator works only when the unreliability is the
point — `True Grit` flirts with it, but Mattie's narration
is reliable; her judgment of others is not. If the writer
wants unreliability, build it into close third or first
retrospective and let the reader feel the gap. Do not
announce it.

## 2. Distance — the dial inside the chosen POV

Distance is the variable inside any POV. It is the gap
between the narrator and the viewpoint character's interior.
It is set sentence by sentence. The skilled writer moves
the dial; the slack writer leaves it stuck.

### Five marks on the dial

| Mark | Description                              | Example                                                                  |
| ---- | ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 1    | Far. Camera on country, character a dot. | _A rider came up out of the draw and crossed the river at the shallows._ |
| 2    | Mid. Character shown from outside.       | _Call sat his horse and watched the herd._                               |
| 3    | Near. Character's perception filtered.   | _The dust was in his teeth and the sun was a hammer on his neck._        |
| 4    | Inner. Character's thought rendered.     | _He thought about the bay and what the bay had cost._                    |
| 5    | Direct. Character's voice in the prose.  | _The bay had cost too much and the bay would die in this country._       |

A long scene moves up and down the dial. A single sustained
mark gets monotonous. A chapter that opens at 1, drops to
2, settles at 3 with one paragraph at 4 and a single
sentence at 5, then closes at 1, has the rhythm a reader
can ride for fifty pages.

### The McCarthy dial vs. the McMurtry dial

McCarthy works mostly at 1 and 2, with rare drops to 3. He
almost never hits 4 or 5. The reader is held at the camera's
distance. Interior is implied by behavior or refused
altogether. When McCarthy does drop to 4, it is a single
short sentence and the prose climbs back out immediately.

McMurtry works mostly at 3 with frequent drops to 4 and
occasional sentences at 5. He pulls back to 1 only between
scenes or chapters. The reader is mostly inside the
character's perception, sometimes inside the character's
thought.

Both modes are correct. They are not the same. Pick one for
the project.

## 3. The interior problem

The single hardest discipline in western prose is rendering
interior without slipping into modern therapy talk. The
period did not narrate feelings the way contemporary fiction
does. A man in 1875 did not _process his grief._ He buried
his brother and went back to work. The grief is in the
work — in the way he handles the shovel, the time he stands
at the grave before mounting, the way he does not eat that
night.

`references/masculinity-and-code.md` covers what kind of
interior the period allows. This module covers how to
render that interior at the four upper marks of the dial.

### Rule: action is interior

The default move at marks 3 and 4 is to show the character
doing something small that carries the feeling. He cleans
the rifle. He stands too long at the corral. He drinks
water he is not thirsty for. The reader supplies the
emotional name. The prose does not.

### Rule: thought in his own words

When the prose drops to mark 5, the interior must sound like
the character — his vocabulary, his rhythm, his prejudices.
Not the author's. If the cowhand thinks, the thought has
the cowhand's syntax. If the educated woman thinks, the
thought has her syntax.

**Bad (interior in the author's voice):**

> Bowman experienced a profound sense of loss as he contemplated
> his brother's death and what it meant for the future of the
> family ranch.

**Good (interior in the character's voice, mark 5):**

> The ranch was his now. He had not asked for it and he did not
> know yet whether he could hold it without Tom.

### Rule: name the body, not the feeling

When the prose moves to mark 3 or 4 and the character is in
strong feeling, the reliable move is to name the body's
response. Hands. Breath. The thing in the throat. The
cold in the gut. The reader knows what those mean. Naming
the feeling directly — _he felt sad, he felt afraid_ — is
the AI default and reads as cheap.

**Bad:**

> She was overcome with terror as she realized what was happening.

**Good:**

> Her mouth went dry. She put her hand on the stock of the
> rifle and she did not take it off.

## 4. POV drift — the most common failure

POV drift is when the prose, settled in one character's
close third, slips for a sentence or a clause into another
character's perception or into facts the viewpoint character
cannot have. It is the AI default and it is also the
default of competent writers who lose discipline.

### Drift type 1: claiming a fact the viewpoint character

cannot know

> Newt rode out at dawn. Behind him, in the bunkhouse, Pea Eye
> was already rolling his blankets and worrying about the river.

If we are in Newt, we cannot know what Pea Eye is doing or
worrying about unless Newt sees or hears it. The fix:
either drop the second sentence or move it to a separate
section break and shift to Pea Eye's POV.

### Drift type 2: vocabulary the character does not have

> The boy watched the rider come up. There was a melancholy
> beauty to the morning light on the hardpan.

If we are in a sixteen-year-old ranch boy with a fourth-grade
education, _melancholy beauty_ is the narrator's vocabulary,
not his. The fix: either tighten to mark 2 and let the
narrator speak (in omniscient) or rewrite at mark 3 in the
boy's perception: _The light was hard on the flats and the
rider came on slow._

### Drift type 3: telegraphing emotion the character is

not yet feeling

> He picked up the letter that would change his life.

The viewpoint character does not know yet that the letter
will change his life. He picks up a letter. The reader
finds out what it does later. Anything else is the narrator
sticking his head in.

### Drift type 4: the omniscient pull-back inside close third

> Bowman crossed the creek at the shallows. The creek had
> been crossed at that ford by Comanche, by Spaniards, by
> the buffalo before any of them, and would be crossed by
> men long after Bowman was bones in the ground.

This is a beautiful sentence. It is also a violation if the
chapter is in Bowman's close third. McCarthy can write it
because his contract is omniscient. McMurtry would not. If
you want this sentence, set the contract to omniscient at
the start of the section, or earn it with a transition.

## 5. The omniscient pull-back, used correctly

The omniscient pull-back to landscape or to time is the
most powerful move in the western register. It is also
the most overused-by-imitation. Five rules.

1. **Earn it by setting an omniscient contract.** If the
   chapter is omniscient, the pull-back is native. If it is
   close third, the pull-back is theft.

2. **Use it once or twice per scene, not throughout.** The
   weight comes from rarity. A page of pull-back is a page
   of nothing.

3. **Tie it to a physical specific.** The pull-back is not
   _The land was old._ It is _The river had cut its channel
   into limestone two million years before any horse drank
   from it and it would cut another foot of channel before
   the century was out._ Geological time, named.

4. **Do not editorialize.** The pull-back records. It does
   not lament. _And so the cruelty of the West continued
   its weary cycle_ is editorial pulp. The pull-back tells
   the reader what is, and trusts the reader to feel it.

5. **Land it on a specific.** The pull-back closes by
   returning to the scene's physical anchor. _The river had
   cut its channel into limestone two million years before
   any horse drank from it. Bowman watered the bay and rode
   on._ The reader rides back into the scene with the
   weight in his pocket.

## 6. The first-person voice trap

The strength of first person is also its risk: the voice is
the world. Three traps.

### Trap 1: the writer's vocabulary

The narrator must speak in his own diction, not the author's.
A frontier woman with a Bible-school education does not say
_juxtaposition._ A Confederate veteran with an eighth-grade
schooling does not say _trauma._ Every word the narrator uses
must be a word the narrator could use.

### Trap 2: hindsight unearned

The retrospective narrator knows how it ended. Used cheaply,
this becomes _Little did I know_ foreshadowing — the worst
sin in the form. Used well, the hindsight is folded in by
implication: _I did not understand then what Pa was doing.
I understand it now._ The reader feels the time gap; the
narrator does not exploit it.

### Trap 3: the narrator becomes the author's mouthpiece

If the narrator's opinions all happen to be the author's
twenty-first-century opinions, the form has failed. Mattie
Ross is racist by our standards and Presbyterian by hers,
and Portis writes her without flinching and without
endorsing. The narrator is a person of her time, not the
author in costume.

## 7. POV transitions — how to shift cleanly

A novel-length western will shift POV between sections,
chapters, or both. Three rules.

1. **One POV per scene.** If the scene needs both Call's and
   Augustus's interior, write two scenes or hold to one and
   render the other from outside.

2. **Mark the shift typographically.** A blank line, a
   chapter break, a section header. The reader needs the
   visual cue.

3. **Re-anchor immediately in the new POV.** The first
   sentence of the new section names the new viewpoint
   character or shows the world through his perception.
   The reader resets in one sentence, not three pages.

**Bad transition:**

> Newt rode out at dawn.
>
> The morning was cold. The wagon stood ready by the cookhouse
> and the cook was kneading biscuit. Pea Eye thought about the
> river.

The break is invisible and the reader thinks Newt is still
the viewpoint when suddenly Pea Eye's interior arrives. Fix:

> Newt rode out at dawn.
>
> ---
>
> Pea Eye stood at the cookhouse door and watched him go. The
> cook was kneading biscuit. Pea Eye thought about the river.

## 8. Before / after rewrites

### Drift fix

**Before (close third drift to omniscient editorializing):**

> Maddox sat his horse at the rise and watched the column of
> smoke. He did not know it yet, but his life was about to
> change forever. Down in the valley, in the burning house,
> his daughter was already dead.

**After (close third, held):**

> Maddox sat his horse at the rise and watched the column of
> smoke. He did not know whose place it was. He touched the bay
> with his heel and started down.

The reader will find out about the daughter when Maddox does.
The dread is in the not knowing.

### Distance fix

**Before (stuck at mark 3 throughout, no rhythm):**

> She felt the cold air on her face. She heard the horses in
> the corral. She tasted the coffee on her teeth. She watched
> the light come up over the ridge.

**After (moves the dial 2 → 3 → 4 → 1):**

> She stood at the door with the cup in both hands. The coffee
> was bitter and the cold was on her face and the horses were
> moving in the corral. She thought about the letter and she
> did not move. Beyond the ridge the sky had gone the color of
> tin.

### Interior fix

**Before (modern therapy interior):**

> He needed to process his anger about his brother's betrayal
> before he could move forward with his life.

**After (period interior, action carries the feeling):**

> He had not seen Tom in eleven months and he did not intend
> to see him again. He cleaned the rifle and put it back over
> the door.

### First-person voice fix

**Before (author voice, not narrator voice):**

> The juxtaposition of the morning's beauty with the violence
> we had committed the night before was profoundly disorienting.

**After (Mattie Ross register, narrator speaking in her own
diction):**

> It was a fine morning and the light came up clean over the
> Winding Stair, but I will say I was not in a frame of mind
> to admire it. We had killed two men in the night and one of
> them by my hand.

## 9. POV cheat-sheet

Pick one before drafting. Tape it above the desk.

- **Close third, McMurtry mode.** One viewpoint per scene.
  Camera sits behind the eyes. Mostly mark 3, drops to 4,
  rare 5. Pull-backs only between scenes. No fact the
  viewpoint character cannot have.
- **Omniscient, McCarthy mode.** Camera free. Mostly mark 1
  or 2. Long pull-backs to landscape and time, tied to
  physical specifics. Interior rare and short. Narrator has
  syntax characters cannot speak.
- **First person retrospective, Portis mode.** Voice is
  king. Narrator's diction holds every sentence. Hindsight
  by implication, not announcement. Period prejudices on
  the page. Author absent.
- **First person contemporaneous.** Letter, journal, or
  deposition form. Plausible writing time. No retrospective
  shaping. Each entry knows only what was known on the day.

---

## 10. The rule the others serve

A page of western prose has one viewpoint and the reader
trusts it. Trust is built by the prose never claiming what
the viewpoint cannot have, never speaking in a voice the
viewpoint cannot speak, and never naming a feeling when the
body or the work could carry it. Break the trust once and
the reader, who could not name what went wrong, will not
believe the next sentence either.

The contract is the craft. Set it. Hold it. The prose will
hold with it.
