# Prose Craft — The Mechanics

This module is the operating manual. `voices.md` tells you which
authors to listen to. This file tells you how to put a sentence
together so it stands up.

Every section ends with at least one before/after rewrite. The
_after_ is the manuscript standard. The _before_ is what an AI
or a careless writer produces by default.

---

## 1. Sentence shapes

A page of western prose alternates four sentence shapes. The mix
is the rhythm. A page of one shape is a page of mush.

### a. The hammer

Subject, verb, object. Five to nine words. Lands hard.

> The horse fell. The boy did not get up.

> Maddox shot him in the throat.

Use to open a scene, to end a paragraph, to mark a turn, to deliver
violence, to land an emotional fact.

### b. The chain

Two or three independent clauses joined by _and_. Records sequence.
McCarthy's bread and butter.

> He cut the rope and the body dropped and the dog stood up out of
> the dust and watched it fall.

Use for action sequences, for a long ride summarized, for a fire
catching, for the slow work of butchering. One per page is plenty.

### c. The clause stack

Long sentence with an embedded subordinate or two. Each clause
adds a hard fact.

> By noon they had crossed the dry bed of the Salinas, watered
> the horses at a seep no wider than a hat, and ridden up the
> shaded side of a rise from which they could see the smoke of
> Comanche fires forty miles north.

Use for landscape, for the sweep of a day's travel, for the
inside of a long thought.

### d. The fragment

Not a sentence. A phrase or a noun. Used for emphasis after a
chain or a stack.

> They rode all day. Forty miles, no water, two horses gone.
> Nothing.

Use sparingly. Two or three per chapter. Overuse turns the prose
into Cormac karaoke.

### Mix rule

In any paragraph of more than four sentences, vary the shape at
least twice. The reader should not be able to predict the next
sentence's length from the last.

### Before / after

**Before (AI default):**

> The cowboys traveled across the dusty plains for many hours,
> their horses growing tired beneath the relentless sun, and they
> began to feel the heavy weight of exhaustion as the day wore on.

**After:**

> They rode through noon and into the long part of the afternoon.
> The horses' shoulders were dark with sweat. By four o'clock the
> bay was lame.

Why: the after has three shapes (chain, hammer, hammer). Each
sentence carries a fact. No abstract weight, no relentless sun,
no growing exhaustion — just the bay going lame.

## 2. Paragraph jobs

Before writing a paragraph, name its job in one word. The named
job determines the shape.

| Job                           | Shape              | Ends on                         |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------ | ------------------------------- |
| **Define** (rules text)       | Stack + hammer     | The mechanical fact             |
| **Describe** (place, body)    | Stack + chain      | A material noun                 |
| **Distinguish**               | Hammer + hammer    | The operative difference        |
| **Warn** (rules or fiction)   | Hammer             | The consequence                 |
| **Demonstrate** (example)     | Chain or scene     | A line of dialogue or a fact    |
| **Bridge** (between sections) | One stack          | The next subject                |
| **Land** (close a section)    | Hammer or fragment | A noun or a verb of consequence |

If you cannot name the job, do not write the paragraph. If you
named it but cannot land it on the right kind of ending, you
named it wrong.

### Before / after

**Before (no job):**

> Cowboys faced many challenges on the trail. The weather could be
> harsh, with sudden storms and oppressive heat. Wild animals were
> a constant threat, and there was always the possibility of
> conflict with rustlers or hostile groups. It was a tough life
> that required a great deal of resilience.

**After (job: warn):**

> The trail killed men in three ways. Lightning across an open
> herd, infection from a small cut, the pistol of another rider
> with a grudge. Resilience was a word the survivors used about
> themselves later.

Why: the after has a named job (warn), a triadic spine of three
specific killers (lightning, infection, pistol), and a closing
sentence that ironizes the AI cliche it is replacing.

## 3. Dialogue

Western dialogue in the canon is short, plain, declarative, and
loaded with what the speaker will not say. The narrator stays out.

### Tag rules

- _Said_ and _asked._ That is the list. _Averred, opined,
  rejoined, declared, exclaimed, retorted_ are dead.
- _Said softly, said quietly, said angrily_ are weaker than a
  beat. Use a beat instead.
- A beat is a physical action between two lines of dialogue.

### Beats over tags

> _I'm not going._
>
> Russell turned the cup in his hands.
>
> _They will come for you here._

The beat (turning the cup) shows Russell's deliberation. No tag
needed. The reader knows who said the second line because Russell
is the only other man at the table.

### Withholding

Characters do not say what they want. They say something nearby.

> _You see Maddox in town?_
>
> _I seen him._
>
> _He say where he was riding?_
>
> _He didn't._

What the speaker wants to know: is Maddox coming for me. He does
not ask. The other man knows. The reader knows.

### Period register

- Drop _just_ (modern hedge). _I just wanted to ask you_ → _I
  was wanting to ask you_ or _I come to ask you._
- Drop _like_ as filler entirely.
- Use _reckon, allow, figure, suppose, expect, mean to_ as the
  verbs of intention. _I aim to leave at first light._ _I
  reckon he won't come back._ _I figure two days._
- _Ain't_ is correct in lower-register speech across all regions.
- _You-all_ (later _y'all_) is post-Civil-War South and Texas;
  not Wyoming, not Oregon.
- _Hell_ and _damn_ are universal. _Goddamn_ is common. _Son
  of a bitch_ is common. _Fuck_ exists in 19th-century English
  but is rare in print and uncommon in the western mouth — use
  it once a chapter, not once a page.
- _Reckon_ is southern and southern-diaspora. _Suppose_ and
  _expect_ travel further north.

### Dialect on the page

Render dialect through word choice and rhythm, not aggressive
eye-spelling. _I cain't_ is fine; a paragraph of _I cain't be
gittin' nowheres if'n the hoss won't move_ is condescension.

### Quotation marks

Default: use them. McCarthy does not, but he is McCarthy. If you
drop them, drop them everywhere in the piece — never mix.

If using quotation marks, the line of dialogue ends with a comma
inside the quotes when followed by _said_:

> "I aim to leave at first light," he said.

If the line is its own sentence and the next sentence is action:

> "I aim to leave at first light." He stood and walked to the
> window.

If using McCarthy-style no-quotes, set dialogue on its own line
or fold into the paragraph:

> Russell stood up.
>
> I'm done eating, he said.
>
> He took his hat from the chair.

### Before / after

**Before (AI default):**

> "I really don't think we should go that way," John said
> nervously, looking around at the dark forest. "It just doesn't
> feel safe to me."
>
> "Don't worry about it," Sam replied with a confident smile.
> "I've been through here many times before."

**After:**

> _Not that way._
>
> John nodded at the timber. The wind had stopped and the birds
> were not in it.
>
> _I been through here._
>
> _You been through here in daylight._

Why: tags gone, beats carry attribution, the disagreement is
not narrated, the period register holds (_been through, in
daylight_), and the silence of the birds does the work of the
_nervously_.

## 4. Exposition

Exposition is the place AI prose dies most reliably. The default
failure is the lecture: the narrator stops the action and tells
the reader a thing.

### Three modes that work

**a. Embedded in action.** The fact arrives because a character
does something with it.

> He poured a measure of black powder into the muzzle, set the
> patch and the ball, and rammed it home with the hickory rod.
> The rifle was a forty-cal Hawken his father had carried at
> Adobe Walls.

The reader learns: muzzleloader, .40 caliber, Hawken, family
provenance, Adobe Walls (1874). None of it was lectured.

**b. Embedded in dialogue (one line).** A fact arrives because
one character tells another, in a way the second character would
need to hear it.

> _That's a Sharps. Fifty-caliber. He'll take you at six hundred
> yards if the wind lays down._

**c. Direct narration, short.** When the fact must be stated, the
narrator states it in two sentences and gets out.

> The Spencer carbine took a tube magazine of seven rimfire
> cartridges. A man who knew what he was doing could empty it in
> half a minute.

### What does not work

- The author-paragraph: _In the West of the 1870s, the cattle
  trade had become…_ The reader skips it.
- The character-thinks-about-history: _Maddox remembered how the
  herd had grown over the years…_ Characters do not deliver
  exposition by remembering on cue.
- The Wikipedia voice: _Settlers from the East often faced
  significant challenges, including…_

### Before / after

**Before:**

> The cattle drive was a dangerous undertaking. Cowboys had to
> contend with stampedes, river crossings, hostile encounters,
> and the constant need to find water and grazing for their
> herds. The drive from Texas to Kansas could take months.

**After:**

> They had been on the trail eighty-one days. Two stampedes,
> one drowning at the South Canadian, three head lost to a
> shorthorn bull that ran off into the Nations and was not
> recovered. The water in the keg tasted like rust and the
> grass north of the Cimarron was already thin.

Why: the same information (duration, dangers) arrives as the
specific accounting of one drive. Embedded, not lectured.

## 5. Scene grammar

A scene is not a passage. A scene has a unit of action, a turn,
and a landing.

### The four-beat scene

1. **Ground.** Where, who, what is happening physically right
   now. Two or three sentences. End with something the reader
   can hear or smell.
2. **Pressure.** Something arrives, is said, is noticed, or
   refuses to happen. The pressure is what the scene is about.
3. **Turn.** A character does something — speaks, moves, draws,
   refuses. The turn is the small action that makes the rest
   inevitable.
4. **Land.** One sentence or one image that closes the scene.
   Often a body, a sound, a piece of weather, a line of
   dialogue, a refusal.

A scene that lacks any of these reads as drift.

### Worked four-beat scene

> **(Ground.)** The cook had the coffee on by the time the gray
> showed in the east. Pea Eye sat with his back against the
> wagon wheel and watched the herd, three hundred yards out,
> shift in the half-light.
>
> **(Pressure.)** A rider came in from the north at a walk. He
> did not call out and he did not raise a hand.
>
> **(Turn.)** Pea Eye set the cup down and stood.
>
> **(Land.)** _Get the captain_, he said to the cook, and walked
> out to meet the rider with his hand at his belt.

The whole scene is twelve lines. Every beat is named without the
narrator naming it.

## 6. Time and transition

The western page handles time in three modes.

### a. Real time

Beat-by-beat. Used for action, dialogue under pressure, the moment
of a death. The page-minute equals the story-minute.

### b. Summary

A paragraph compresses hours, days, weeks. Always carry a hard
fact: a count of days, a named place, a body of weather, a wound
that has worsened.

> They rode south for three days and saw no one. On the morning
> of the fourth day a column of dust stood up east of them and
> hung in the sky an hour and was gone.

### c. White space

A blank line is the cleanest transition the western page has.
Use it for jumps in time, in POV, in location. Do not write
_Meanwhile, twenty miles to the south…_ Use the white line.

## 7. Weather as character

Weather is never decoration. It does work.

- **It tells time** (sun angle, dew, frost line on a horse's
  flank).
- **It tells terrain** (the wind a man feels in the Llano
  Estacado is not the wind he feels in the San Juans).
- **It causes** (the storm that strips a man's bedroll, the cold
  that takes two fingers, the mud that cripples the bay).
- **It signals** (the smell of rain on the wind two hours before
  the rain).

A weather sentence with no consequence is decoration. Cut it.

### Before / after

**Before:**

> The sun was hot and the sky was a beautiful clear blue.

**After:**

> The sky was the color of a hot iron and the canteen water was
> blood-warm by ten o'clock.

Why: hot iron is a hard noun simile, blood-warm is a body fact,
ten o'clock anchors the day, and the canteen is a tool the
character will need.

## 8. Naming

Names carry period and place.

- **First names** common in the West, by region:
  - Anglo: Charles, James, William, John, Thomas, Henry,
    Frank, Joe, Sam, Ben, Tom, Will, Ned, Cole, Pearl, Mary,
    Sarah, Hannah, Lorena, Clara, Ada, Maggie.
  - Tejano / Mexican: Juan, Antonio, Esteban, Carlos, Pablo,
    Tomás, Maria, Esperanza, Luz, Concepción.
  - Black freedmen: classical Greek and Roman names were
    common (Caesar, Cato, Hector, Pompey) for those born
    enslaved; biblical names (Isaac, Jacob, Daniel, Hannah,
    Sarah) very common after emancipation.
  - German immigrant: Friedrich, Heinrich, Wilhelm, Karl,
    Margarethe, Anna.
  - Irish: Patrick, Sean (rare in print, _John_ on paper),
    Michael, Liam (rare), Mary, Bridget, Kathleen.
- **Last names** of the working West are usually Anglo-Scots
  monosyllables and bisyllables: Call, Glanton, Tate, Ford,
  Stark, Boone, Russell, Maddox, Hines, Grady, Morgan, Hayes.
  Avoid invented-fantasy double-barreled names (Ironwood,
  Blackthorne) — they read as RPG product.
- **Nicknames** carry biography: One-Eye, Big Hat, Sundown,
  Slim, Dutch, Tex (only outside Texas), Doc (anyone with
  schooling, not necessarily a doctor), Cap (former officer
  of any war).
- **Place names** in the actual West are mostly Spanish,
  Native, French (in the upper Missouri), or descriptive
  Anglo (Rock Springs, Dry Creek, Lone Pine, Eagle Nest).
  Avoid coined fantasy place names. Use a real one or build
  one from the same patterns.

## 9. Numbers, money, distance

Specifics build trust. Vague ranges break it.

- A cowhand's pay on a Texas trail drive in the 1870s: $25 to
  $40 a month, plus food. The trail boss earned $100 to $125.
- A Colt Peacemaker (Single Action Army) at retail in 1873:
  about $17.
- A Winchester Model 1873 rifle: about $50.
- A good cow pony: $15 to $30. A trained cutting horse: $50
  and up.
- A whiskey at a saloon: 5 to 25 cents depending on what was in
  it.
- A meal at a town restaurant: 25 to 75 cents.
- A hotel room: 50 cents to $2 a night.
- A herd of cattle Texas-to-Kansas covered roughly 10–15 miles
  a day in good country.
- A horse and rider could cover 25–40 miles in a day at a working
  pace; 60 in a hard day; 80 in an emergency that probably
  killed the horse.

Use real numbers when you can. See `knowledge/towns-economy-law.md`
for the full table.

## 10. The anti-AI pass for prose

After drafting, scan the page for these specific patterns. Each
one is treated in detail in `anti-ai-patterns.md`. The short
form here:

- Em-dash in three sentences in a row → cut at least one.
- _Not just X, but Y_ → rewrite.
- Three nouns in a row separated by commas (_the dust, the
  heat, the silence_) → keep only the strongest.
- _Suddenly_ → cut.
- Final sentence that summarizes the paragraph → cut.
- Adjective-adjective-noun (_the cold, dark night_) → strike
  one adjective.
- _It was a place where…_ → rewrite.
- _He couldn't help but…_ → rewrite.
- _In a world where…_ → cut entire sentence.
- _Little did he know…_ → cut entire paragraph.

## 11. Final read-aloud test

Read the draft aloud. The page passes if:

- You do not stumble on any sentence.
- No paragraph ends weak.
- No two paragraphs in a row have the same shape.
- The dialogue sounds like speech, not like writing.
- The exposition arrives by action or by one line.
- You can name the job of every paragraph.
- Nothing in the prose tells the reader how to feel.

If any of those fails, return to the draft. Do not ship the page.
