# Speech and Writing Patterns — Voice Fingerprints by Archetype

This file is the operational complement to
`knowledge/language-and-diction.md` (which gives the period
vocabulary) and `references/voices.md` (which gives the author
canon). Where those files tell you what words are allowed and
which authors to imitate, this file tells you how a specific
character on the page will _sound_: sentence shape, breath
length, what they refuse to say, what they say twice, what they
do with their hands while they speak, and how the narrator
should render it.

Cross-reference `references/prose-craft.md` for the dialogue
mechanics (no-quote rendering, beats, attribution discipline)
that apply across all archetypes.

---

## 1. Why archetypes, not characters

Every character is specific. But every specific character draws
on a recognizable speech _register_ — a way of using English
shaped by trade, region, education, age, and company. A reader
who hears the register correctly will fill in the specifics. A
reader who hears the register wrong (the cowhand who talks like
a Yale graduate, the judge who talks like a saddle tramp) will
not believe the rest of the page.

A speech fingerprint is six things at once:

1. **Sentence length and shape.** Short and declarative. Long
   and periodic. Compound and rolling. Fragment and pause.
2. **Vocabulary band.** Anglo-Saxon and concrete. Latinate and
   abstract. Trade-specific. Biblical. Profane.
3. **Idiom inventory.** The fixed phrases the speaker reaches
   for under load. The cowhand's _I reckon._ The judge's _be
   that as it may._ The veteran's _ain't no use._
4. **Refusals.** What the speaker will not say. The hard man
   does not name fear. The lady does not name money. The
   preacher does not curse. The Black freedman in 1875
   chooses his words around white men.
5. **Body and beat.** What the hands do, where the eyes go,
   what the speaker does with the silence before he answers.
6. **Tag and attribution.** What the narrator does with the
   line — bare, with a beat, with a brief tag, with no tag at
   all. See `references/prose-craft.md` for the rules.

A speech fingerprint is not an accent. Phonetic spelling
(_Howdy, podner, I reckon as how the bay hoss has done throwed
a shoe_) is a sin in modern western prose. The accent is in the
syntax, the diction, the pause, and the refusal. Trust the
reader.

## 2. The laconic ranch hand

The default voice of working cowboy fiction. McMurtry's Pea
Eye, McCarthy's John Grady Cole when the conversation does not
demand more, every old hand in every western that knows its
business.

**Sentence shape.** Short. Often a single clause. Sometimes a
fragment. Compound only when telling a story over coffee.

**Vocabulary.** Anglo-Saxon and trade-specific. _Bay, sorrel,
roan, paint, gelding, mare, colt. Rope, dally, honda, slack.
Cinch, latigo, tree, skirts._ Latinate words appear only
through the Bible (_deliverance, abomination_) or through the
army (_detachment, requisition_).

**Idiom inventory.** _I reckon. I expect. I figure. I aim to.
Looks like. Could be. Might could. He don't. We ain't. Suit
yourself. Cain't say. Don't appear so. That's how she lays.
Beats me. By God. The hell you say._

**Refusals.** Names no feeling. Will not say _I am afraid_ or
_I am sad_ or _I love you._ Will say _I'd hate to lose that
horse,_ and the reader will know.

**Body and beat.** Looks at the ground. Looks at the horse.
Builds a smoke. Spits. Touches the brim of his hat. Sets the
cup down before he answers a hard question. The pause before
the answer is its own line.

**Tag and attribution.** Almost no tags. _He said_ is enough,
once a paragraph. After that, action beats. The cowhand
attribution paragraph looks like this:

> What do you aim to do about him.
>
> Nothing.
>
> Bowman watched him a while. He drank off the coffee and set
> the cup on the rock and looked out across the country to the
> south where the dust was rising.
>
> Nothing at all, he said.

Notice: the question and the answer carry no tag. The beat
carries the second response. _Nothing_ and _Nothing at all_ do
the work that a paragraph of inner monologue would do in a
worse book.

**Worked dialogue.**

> Where's Tom.
>
> Gone.
>
> Gone where.
>
> South.
>
> When.
>
> Last night sometime. He took the bay and the pack mule and
> the .50.
>
> Did he say anything.
>
> No.
>
> Goddamn him, the old man said.
>
> Yes sir.

**Common AI failure.** The AI writes the laconic hand as
_terse_ but gives him modern psychological precision. _I am
worried about Tom_ is wrong. _That bay throws a shoe by Tucson
he'll be afoot_ is right. The hand expresses worry by naming
the consequence, not the feeling.

## 3. The orotund judge / the educated speaker

Portis's Judge Parker. McMurtry's Captain Call when speaking
formally. Hall's lawyers in _Warlock._ Any man with Latin in
his schooling, English law in his head, and an audience in
front of him.

**Sentence shape.** Long. Periodic. The main verb arrives late.
Subordinate clauses stack. Parallel structure (the _not only…
but also,_ the _neither… nor,_ the rolling triplet) is welcome
because the speaker is performing.

**Vocabulary.** Latinate and abstract. _Apprehend, contravene,
adjudicate, mitigate, predicate, malfeasance, infamous,
recreant, providential, expediency._ Mixed with biblical
phrasing (_the iniquity of, the wages of, in the fullness of
time, of a truth_). Sprinkles of legal Latin (_habeas corpus,
mens rea, prima facie, in camera_) without translation; the
audience will understand or pretend to.

**Idiom inventory.** _Be that as it may. I am of the opinion.
The court will note. It is my conviction. With all due respect.
I shall not detain you. Suffice it to say. In point of fact.
The plain truth is. By no means. Upon my honor._

**Refusals.** Will not use slang in chambers or at the bench.
Avoids contractions in formal address (_do not,_ not _don't_).
Will not raise voice. Will not interrupt unless to gavel.

**Body and beat.** Speaks slowly. Looks over reading glasses.
Drums the bench with the flat of a hand. Refills a glass of
water. Pulls out a pocket watch. The beat is performance.

**Worked dialogue.**

> Mr. Goudy, the judge said. I have been on this bench since
> the Grant administration and I have heard a great many
> arguments advanced for the proposition that a man who shot
> another man down in the street had nevertheless a defense.
> I have admitted some of them. Yours is not one of those.
> Sit down.
>
> Goudy sat down.
>
> The defendant will rise.

**Worked monologue (sentencing).**

> It is the judgment of this court that you, Mortimer Joseph
> Pruitt, having been duly tried by a jury of your peers and
> by them found guilty of the willful murder of Henry
> Cottrell on the evening of the twelfth of August in the
> year of our Lord eighteen hundred and seventy-five, shall
> be by the sheriff of this county taken hence to the place
> appointed and there hanged by the neck until you are dead.
> May God in his mercy receive you. The court stands
> adjourned.

Notice: the formal date (_on the evening of the twelfth of
August in the year of our Lord eighteen hundred and seventy-
five_) is period and right; a modern _on August 12, 1875_ is
not what a judge would have said in chambers. The closing
_May God in his mercy receive you_ is period boilerplate.

**Common AI failure.** The AI writes the educated speaker as
verbose but soft. The real period educated speech is verbose
_and hard._ _It is the judgment of this court that you shall
hang_ is colder than _I sentence you to death._

## 4. The talkative scout / mountain man

Gus McCrae. Portis's Rooster Cogburn. Guthrie's Boone Caudill
in _The Big Sky._ The man who has been alone too much and
makes up for it.

**Sentence shape.** Long, anecdotal, digressive. Begins one
story and slides into another. Comes back. Punctuates with
_now_ and _well sir_ and _you take._ Rises to a punchline or
falls to a moral.

**Vocabulary.** Working-man Anglo-Saxon with frontier
specifics. _Sign, spoor, Snake, Crow, Blackfeet, beaver, plew,
buffler (buffalo), greenhorn, pilgrim, Yankee, child (of
himself), this hoss._ The mountain-man third-person self-
reference (_this child, this hoss, this nigger_ in the older
white parlance) is period and used by Guthrie and Manfred and
Berger; handle with care.

**Idiom inventory.** _Now you take. I mind the time. I'll tell
you what. Here's what I done. By gum. By God. Beats anything I
ever seen. That child was took. The way it was. The way I
heard it. Don't you know._

**Refusals.** Will not be rushed. Will not give a straight
answer to a stupid question. Will not stop talking when he has
the floor.

**Body and beat.** Spits. Refills the cup. Cuts a chew. Slaps
the table. Leans back. Eyes the audience. The story is a
performance for company; he watches who is listening.

**Worked dialogue.**

> Now you take a Comanche, Gus said. He'll set on a ridge a
> day and a half and look at you and you won't see him
> neither. I knowed a man up on the Brazos got bit on the
> calf by a copperhead while he was on the porch playing
> the fiddle. He didn't pay it no mind, he was that drunk,
> and by the time he laid down to sleep that leg had swole
> up like a stovepipe. He died of it the next day. The
> point bein, you don't pay attention, the country will
> kill you, and so will a snake. I forget what we was
> talking about.
>
> Comanches, Call said.
>
> Comanches, Gus said. Yes.

**Common AI failure.** The AI cuts the digression. The whole
charm is the digression. The AI also misses that the talkative
scout _interrupts himself_ and that the listener supplies the
thread (_Comanches, Call said_).

## 5. The silent gunman

McCarthy's Chigurh in modern register and Glanton's company in
_Blood Meridian._ Hall's Tom Morgan. Leonard's Valdez. The man
who speaks little because speech is not how he answers
problems.

**Sentence shape.** Single sentence. Often four words. Often a
question that is not really a question.

**Vocabulary.** Plain, almost no idiom. No biblical. No legal.
The trade words of his work (_pistol, shell, hammer, sights_)
when he uses any.

**Idiom inventory.** _Yes. No. I don't know. We'll see.
Suit yourself. That'll do. Step out._

**Refusals.** Will not explain. Will not threaten. Will not
boast. Will not name the thing he is going to do before he does
it.

**Body and beat.** Stillness. The hand that does not move when
it should. The eyes that do not move when the room moves.
McCarthy's discipline: the silent man is a body the narrator
tracks with the same attention given to weather.

**Worked dialogue.**

> The man at the bar turned and looked at him.
>
> You looking for me, he said.
>
> Are you Tate.
>
> Who's asking.
>
> I am.
>
> The man set the glass down. He looked once at the door
> and once at the windows.
>
> All right, he said.

Notice: no adverbs. No _coldly, quietly, menacingly._ The
prose is colder than any adverb would make it. The cadence
does the work.

**Worked dialogue (variation, with the gunman as second
party).**

> What do you want, Bowman said.
>
> Bowman waited.
>
> The man stood in the door and looked at him a while
> longer.
>
> Your gun, the man said.
>
> No, said Bowman.
>
> All right.
>
> The man drew and Bowman drew and the room filled with
> smoke and Bowman was on the floor.

**Common AI failure.** The AI gives the silent gunman one
ominous monologue (_You think you can escape what you have
done?_). He cannot. He gets four words and a bullet. The
ominous monologue is the supervillain register and is fatal.

## 6. The drunk veteran

The figure on the porch outside the saloon. The man who served
under Sherman, under Forrest, under Sheridan; was at Gettysburg
or Shiloh or Antietam; came west with nothing and is finishing
on whiskey. McMurtry's Wilbarger. Hansen's Bob Ford in his
later pages. Berger's Jack Crabb when reflective.

**Sentence shape.** Variable. Lucid in stretches; tangled when
the drink takes him. The same sentence sometimes loops back on
itself.

**Vocabulary.** Working-man with military overlay (_reb, blue-
belly, picket, vidette, color, regiment, the Old Man,
canister, Minié ball, the bloody angle_). Reaches for a
biblical quote when feeling philosophical; gets it slightly
wrong.

**Idiom inventory.** _I tell you what. I was at. I seen. There
was a time. Many a man. Don't speak to me of. The hell with it.
A man my age. I knew a fellow._

**Refusals.** Will not name the worst things. The friend who
died will be _a fellow I knew._ The wife who left will be _a
woman I was acquainted with once._ The horror at the angle of
the wall at Gettysburg will be _bad business that day._

**Body and beat.** Holds the glass two-handed. Stares past
the listener. Blinks slowly. Sometimes weeps without
acknowledging it. The hand that shakes.

**Worked monologue.**

> I was at the angle, the old man said. The bloody angle,
> they call it now in the books. We didn't call it nothing
> at the time. We was just there.
>
> He drank.
>
> A boy from Ohio name of Pleasant Riggs took a ball through
> the throat and laid down beside me and looked at me and
> said _Mama_ and died. He wasn't seventeen. I don't know
> what become of his people. He had a tintype of a girl in
> his pocket but I never could find them after. I tried for
> a while. I quit trying.
>
> He drank again. He looked at the bottle.
>
> A man my age, he said. You got to forgive a man my age
> for talking. There ain't but the talk left.

**Common AI failure.** The AI gives the veteran modern PTSD
language (_I haven't been the same since, I have nightmares,
I struggle with_). He does not have those words. He has
_bad nights, the shakes, can't sleep on rainy weather, the
whiskey, the Lord won't let me forget._ He will not say
_trauma._ He will say _I seen things over there._

## 7. The educated woman

Mattie Ross in _True Grit_ (Portis's masterstroke). The
schoolmarm. The doctor's wife. The widow who runs the boarding
house. The Easterner who came West for love or land or the
work of the church.

**Sentence shape.** Full and complete. Periodic when she is in
her element. Subordinate clauses balanced. Comma discipline.
She was taught grammar and she has not forgotten.

**Vocabulary.** Latinate and decorous. The biblical and
literary references of a New England common-school education
or a finishing-school course. Avoids profanity entirely (or
uses _Mr. Cogburn_ the way another woman might use _that
goddamned man_). Calls people by surname and title.

**Idiom inventory.** _I will say. I do not pretend. I should
think. It is my belief. I have observed. There can be no
question. I will not be moved on the matter. As I have
indicated. As to that._

**Refusals.** Will not be patronized. Will not bargain on a
matter of principle. Will not weep in front of men. Will not
name money in mixed company without a polite circumlocution
(_the matter of expense_).

**Body and beat.** Sits straight. Hands folded. Eyes level. The
look that drops a man's voice. The deliberate adjustment of a
glove, a hat-pin, a Bible.

**Worked monologue (Portis-style).**

> I will tell you what I told the Sheriff. My father was
> shot down in Fort Smith by a man named Tom Chaney and the
> man took my father's saddle horse and his California gold
> pieces and rode off into the Choctaw Nation. I aim to
> have him back to Fort Smith and see him hanged on a
> Federal warrant. I have come to your office for the
> purpose of hiring a man to assist me in this work. I am
> told you are the meanest deputy marshal in this
> jurisdiction and that is what I require. The matter of
> compensation we will settle now or not at all.

**Worked dialogue (the educated woman in argument).**

> Mr. Goudy, I am not interested in your opinion as to
> whether a girl of fourteen can or cannot conduct
> business. I have conducted my father's business since
> the day he died. I will conduct it now. If you wish to
> assist me you may do so. If you do not, kindly say so
> and I will look elsewhere.

**Common AI failure.** The AI writes her _spunky._ She is not
spunky. She is competent and unyielding and her diction is
adult. _Spunky_ is the modern-condescending register applied
to a competent woman; period prose did not patronize Mattie
and modern western prose should not patronize her either.

## 8. The plain-spoken woman

The cowman's wife. The freighter's mother. The midwife. The
Tejana ranchwoman. The Black laundress. Less educated than the
Mattie type, more authority than her husband at home, fluent
in the practical world.

**Sentence shape.** Short. Direct. Functional.

**Vocabulary.** Working Anglo-Saxon with the household trade
words (_dough, sourdough, lye, lard, salt, biscuit, beans,
churn, cream, calico, gingham, gum boots, flatiron, washboard,
clothesline_). Childbed and animal-husbandry vocabulary that
the men avoid (_she's coming on, the cow won't take, it's a
breech, she's fevered_).

**Idiom inventory.** _You best. You ought. Mind you don't.
Come here. Sit down. Eat. Drink that. Hush. I told you. Don't
you sass me. Get on. Lord help. Lord knows. The good Lord._

**Refusals.** Will not be lied to in her own kitchen. Will
not put up with foolishness from a man who should know
better. Will not let the work go undone.

**Body and beat.** The hands always doing something — kneading,
peeling, mending, washing, churning, nursing. Speaks across
the work. The pause is when she stops to look up.

**Worked dialogue.**

> Where you been, she said.
>
> Town.
>
> She did not turn around. She kept the iron moving across
> the shirt.
>
> Drinking.
>
> Some.
>
> Did you bring the salt.
>
> He did not answer. She set the iron down on the stove
> plate.
>
> John.
>
> I forgot it, he said.
>
> She picked the iron back up. She did not look at him.
>
> Sit down and eat, she said. Tomorrow you go back.

**Common AI failure.** The AI writes her sentimental or
saintly. The plain-spoken woman is neither. She is the working
hand of the household. She loves her family by feeding them
and she disciplines them by not pretending the salt got bought.

## 9. The boy

The cowhand's son. The orphan on the trail drive. The boy in
the kitchen who runs errands. McMurtry's Newt Dobbs. Berger's
young Jack Crabb. McCarthy's John Grady Cole as a boy.

**Sentence shape.** Direct. Few subordinate clauses. The boy
hears more than he says.

**Vocabulary.** Pulled from the men around him; he will pick
up cowhand idiom faster than diction. Will say _I reckon_
because the men say it. Will not yet have the depth of profane
or biblical reference.

**Idiom inventory.** _Yessir. Nosir. I'll do it. I never
seen. I never. Can I. May I. I want to._

**Refusals.** Will not show fear in front of the men if he can
help it. Will not cry. Will not ask twice.

**Body and beat.** Watches everything. Stands when the men
sit. Holds his hat in his hand. The eyes go to the man he
admires before they go anywhere else.

**Worked dialogue.**

> Can I ride point tomorrow.
>
> No.
>
> Newt did not say anything.
>
> When you can rope, Call said.
>
> I can rope.
>
> Call did not look at him.
>
> When you can rope a calf out of the brush in the dark,
> Call said. When you can keep the iron straight on the
> hip. When you can sit a horse for sixteen hours and still
> see at the end of it. Then point.
>
> Newt looked at the ground.
>
> Yessir, he said.

**Common AI failure.** The AI writes the boy _plucky_ or
_precocious._ He is neither. He is a boy in a hard country who
wants to be a man and is paying attention.

## 10. The immigrant — German, Irish, Scandinavian, Chinese

The German farmer. The Irish soldier or section hand. The
Norwegian homesteader. The Chinese cook or laundryman. Each
imports the syntax of his first language and avoids what he
cannot pronounce.

**General rule.** _One marker per long speech._ Never write the
whole utterance in dialect. The reader needs one sign that the
speaker is not native and then the prose can be clean.

### a. The German

- Verb at the end of the subordinate clause.
- _Ja_ and _nein_ slip in. _Ach._
- Drops articles or uses the wrong one.
- _V_ for _w_ (_wery good_ becomes _vary good_) — write this
  rarely if at all.
- Uses the formal _you_ (_you, sir, must understand_) where an
  Anglo would say _you got to see._

> The land here is good, but the wind, my God, the wind.
> Three years I have been here and three years the wind. My
> wife says we should go back. I say to her, ja, and to what,
> the rent? Here we own the dirt. Even if the wind takes it.

### b. The Irish

- Inverts to-be (_it's that I'm thinking, is it angry you are
  with me_).
- _Sure_ as a sentence-opener.
- _And isn't it_ as a tag.
- _Himself_ as a definite reference (_himself in the corner is
  the trouble_).
- Saint and Mary references woven in.

> Sure and it's not the work I'm complaining of, but the pay.
> A man can break his back for the railroad and what's he got
> at the end of the month but a tin cup and a sore head, and
> isn't that the truth of it. Mary preserve us.

### c. The Scandinavian (Norwegian, Swedish)

- _Yes_ and _yah_ and _no._
- Inverts subject-verb in question (_come you with us tomorrow_).
- _Th_ hardens to _t_ or _d_ in heavy accents — write rarely.
- Long pauses. Few words. The Scandinavians of the period had
  a reputation among Anglos as silent and reliable.

> The cow is sick. Two days now. I think she does not live.
> I am sorry to ask. We have no other.

### d. The Chinese

- Pidgin English in the period was real and the period prose
  is full of it. Modern western prose mostly avoids the
  caricature _me likee_ register. Better practice: write the
  Chinese character speaking economical English with one
  marker (_can do, no can do_) or, in narration, render his
  speech as it sounded to an Anglo while making clear in the
  prose that the Chinese speaker is fluent in his own language
  and stripped down only because English is the second
  language.
- A literate Chinese clerk speaks better English than a
  mountain man.

> Mr. Bowman, your shirt is ready. The collar I have mended.
> The cuff also. It is one dollar twenty cents. I give credit
> until Saturday only.

**Common AI failure.** The AI writes ethnic dialect heavy and
the result is a 1930s minstrel page. Light hand. One marker.
Trust the reader.

## 11. The Black freedman / freedwoman

A separate register. The Black population of the West in the
1870s included Buffalo Soldiers, cowhands (perhaps a quarter of
working trail hands were Black or Mexican), settlers, town
laborers, women in domestic and trade work. Each had a public
voice (around white men) and a private voice (within the
community).

**Public voice (around white men).**

- Polite. _Yes sir._ _No sir._ _Mister._
- Carefully grammatical or carefully ungrammatical, depending
  on what the speaker has decided is safer in this town with
  these men.
- Will not contradict in public. Will request, will inquire,
  will defer. The deferral is not character — it is survival.

**Private voice (in the community, in church, with family).**

- Full range. Same humor, profanity, biblical cadence,
  argument, song as any other Anglo register, plus the
  speech traditions of Black America (the call-and-response of
  the church, the play of the dozens, the word from the
  pulpit, the songs of the work).
- The biblical register in particular was deep and operative,
  and Black church speech in the 1870s — particularly in
  African Methodist Episcopal congregations and in the
  community churches founded after Emancipation — was a major
  literary register.

**Worked dialogue (public, with a white sheriff).**

> Sheriff, I come about the boy.
>
> Which boy.
>
> My son, sir. Daniel. He was took up last night.
>
> Drunk and disorderly.
>
> Yes sir, I expect that's so. He is a young man and he
> done wrong. I have come to settle the fine if it can be
> settled. I have got the money here.

**Worked dialogue (private, the same man at home).**

> Daniel, I had to walk into that office and call that man
> sir, and you know what that cost me. You know what it
> cost your mother. Now you tell me what got into you.
>
> I got hot, the boy said.
>
> You got hot. There's a man in Tyler swung off a tree
> three months ago for getting hot. You hear me.
>
> Yes sir.
>
> Don't _sir_ me, boy. I'm your daddy. _Yes daddy._
>
> Yes daddy.

**Common AI failure.** The AI writes the public voice as the
whole voice and the result is a flattened character. The
private voice — the full literary range — is the place the
character lives.

A second AI failure is romanticizing the dignity. The dignity
is real and it costs the man real things. Show the cost.

## 12. The Mexican / Tejano / Californio

In Spanish at home and in the plaza. In English with Anglos,
to the degree of his English. A Tejano cattleman whose family
has run cattle on the Rio Grande for a hundred and fifty years
is not an immigrant; he is on his own land. His diction
reflects this.

**With other Spanish-speakers.** Spanish, often with regional
inflection (Sonoran, Norteño, Tejano, Californio). The narrator
in English prose can render this as clean English with
occasional Spanish words held untranslated, and the reader
will understand the language is Spanish.

**With Anglos.** Working English with Spanish syntax leaking
through. Often a slightly more formal English than the Anglo
he is speaking to (the speaker is being careful in the second
language and speaks the way his English teacher taught).

**Worked dialogue (Tejano rancher to Anglo cattleman).**

> Señor Bowman. The cattle you have brought across my
> grass have eaten what is mine. I will not say you stole
> it. I will say only that it is gone. We must arrive at a
> price.
>
> What price.
>
> I have considered. Two hundred dollars in gold or one
> hundred head of your own steers, I do not care which. I
> ask you also to cross by the lower ford in future. The
> upper is for my father's cattle.

Notice: _Señor_ is period and used by Anglos and Tejanos
alike. _I will not say… I will say only_ is the periodic
formal English of a careful second-language speaker. _I have
considered_ is more formal than an Anglo would phrase it.

**Common AI failure.** The AI writes the Tejano either as a
_peon_ (subordinate, broken English, Hollywood) or as a
_caballero_ (florid, stiff, museum-piece). The real range — a
working rancher, a craftsman, a soldier, a priest, a
politician, an outlaw, a child — is missed.

## 13. The preacher

The Methodist circuit rider. The Baptist tent revivalist.
The town minister. The Catholic priest serving a Mexican
parish. The AME bishop in a freedmen's town. Each speaks a
different register, but all share the King James as the
literary substrate.

**Sentence shape.** Long, parallel, building. The triplet (_the
Lord… the Lord… the Lord_) earns its place here because the
preacher is in fact preaching. The pause for breath at the end
of the period is a homiletic device.

**Vocabulary.** Biblical. The whole inventory of Reformation
English as inherited through the King James, the _Book of
Common Prayer,_ and the hymnal. Specific names from scripture
(_Esau, Jacob, Lazarus, Dives, Lot's wife, the prodigal_) used
without explanation; the audience knows.

**Idiom inventory.** _Brethren. Sisters. Beloved. Of a truth.
Verily. Unto. The wages of. Behold. Come unto me. Lay it down.
Cast it off. The Lord is mindful. The Lord is faithful. The
Lord requireth._

**Worked monologue (homiletic).**

> Brethren, the Lord did not bring us out of bondage, did
> not lead us through the wilderness, did not deliver us
> upon this land of milk and honey, that we should forget
> Him in the prosperity of our hand. He brought us through
> the Red Sea. He fed us on manna in the desert. He gave us
> the law on Sinai when we had nothing but the law. And
> shall we now, with full barns and full table, with barn
> and table that He gave us, shall we now turn from Him?
> The man who turns from the Lord in his prosperity is the
> same man who cried unto the Lord in his want. Brethren, I
> tell you, the Lord remembers.

**Worked dialogue (the preacher off the pulpit).**

> Reverend.
>
> Mr. Bowman.
>
> I come about the funeral.
>
> Sit down. Have you eaten.
>
> Yes sir.
>
> Tell me about your brother.

The preacher off the pulpit is mostly an ordinary man with a
slightly more formal vocabulary. He is not always at high
register. Reserve the homiletic for the homily.

**Common AI failure.** The AI gives every preacher the high
register all the time. He is exhausting on the page. The
preacher in the parlor is plain. The preacher in the pulpit is
operatic. The contrast is the character.

## 15. Writing patterns — beyond dialogue

The voice fingerprint applies to the page as well as to the
mouth. A first-person narrator carries his voice into the
narration. A close-third narrator can borrow the voice of the
focal character. A document — a letter, a journal entry, a
telegram, a court transcript, a newspaper article — has its
own form constraints.

### a. The first-person narrator

The single most powerful voice tool the western has. Portis's
Mattie is the model. Other first-person western narrators:
Berger's Jack Crabb in _Little Big Man,_ the boy in _The Shootist_
(Swarthout), the unnamed narrator of much of Hansen's
historical work.

**Rules.**

- The narrator's diction is the character's diction, period.
- The narrator's blind spots are the character's blind spots.
  Mattie does not understand grief and does not narrate grief;
  she narrates business.
- The narrator's distance from the events governs the prose.
  Mattie at fifty narrating the events of fourteen has a
  retrospective formality the events themselves did not have.
- The narrator's vocabulary is the character's vocabulary plus
  what the character has read since.

### b. The close-third narrator

McCarthy's mode. The narrator stays close to one character but
the prose is the narrator's, not the character's. The
character's voice surfaces in dialogue and in occasional free
indirect discourse (_the country lay open before him and there
was nowhere now to be but on it_).

**Rules.**

- The narrator does not enter the character's modern interior.
  No _he wondered why he felt this way._
- Free indirect discourse pulls in the character's idiom only
  when the prose is reaching for the character's perception
  (_the bay had thrown a shoe and they were sixty miles out_).
- The narrator describes the world the character sees, in the
  vocabulary appropriate to the character's competence (the
  cowhand sees _bay, sorrel, roan_; the Easterner sees _brown
  horse_).

### c. The omniscient narrator

McCarthy's other mode (_Blood Meridian_). The narrator is a
voice from outside time. Latinate, biblical, geological. Used
sparingly in genre prose — it is the high register and it
exhausts a chapter quickly.

### d. The document — letter

A 19th-century letter has form. The date upper right. The
salutation (_Dear Brother John, Dear Mr. Bowman, My dear
Mary_). The opening pleasantry (_Your letter of the third
inst. came to hand and was the cause of much rejoicing in
this house_). The body. The closing pleasantry (_With kind
regards to your wife and the children, and trusting this finds
you in good health_). The valediction (_Your affectionate
brother, Your obedient servant, Yr most obedient_). The
signature.

The letter's voice tracks the writer's education exactly. A
half-literate cowhand writing his mother sounds different from
a banker writing his Eastern partner.

**Worked example (literate cowhand to his mother).**

> _Lampasas, Texas, March 12, 1875._
>
> _Dear Mother,_
>
> _Your letter of last month finally found me here. I am
> sorry I did not answer sooner but we have been on a drive
> to Kansas and back. I am well. The bay horse you remember
> me writing of has been sold. I have a sorrel now and he
> is a good one. We had two men killed coming home, one by
> drowning at Red River and one by lightning on the Canadian.
> I thank God I am safe._
>
> _I will send you twenty dollars by Wells Fargo when this
> reaches Austin next week. I wish it was more. The pay is
> what it is._
>
> _Tell Sister to write me at Lampasas care of Wright &
> Harris. Tell her I think of her every day._
>
> _Your son,_
>
> _Tom_

### e. The document — journal

A 19th-century journal is private and terse. Date. Weather.
Distance. Event. Sometimes a sentence of feeling.

**Worked example.**

> _June 12. Hot. Made twelve miles. Bay went lame in the
> afternoon. Camped on the Brazos. Coffee, bacon, hardtack.
> Mosquitoes. R. shot a deer. Did not eat much._
>
> _June 13. Bay still lame. Laid up. Cleaned the rifle.
> Wrote to Mother but did not send._

### f. The document — telegram

Period telegrams cost by the word. The form was abrupt and
businesslike. _STOP_ in place of period was a 20th-century
movie convention; period telegrams used periods or no
punctuation.

> BOWMAN AUSTIN HOTEL DRISKILL. CATTLE ARRIVED ABILENE. PRICE
> EIGHTEEN. WIRE INSTRUCTIONS. MATTHEWS

### g. The document — newspaper

Period newspaper prose is florid by modern standards. Long
sentences, Latinate, fond of the editorial _we,_ willing to
moralize.

> _On Tuesday last, this community was shocked by the
> intelligence that Mr. Henry Cottrell, of this place, had
> been shot down in the public street by one Mortimer
> Pruitt, lately of San Saba County, the said Pruitt
> immediately mounting his horse and making good his escape
> in the direction of the Red River. Mr. Cottrell, who
> leaves a wife and two small children, was a man of
> excellent reputation and the killing has cast a pall over
> the town. We trust the law will pursue Pruitt to the
> uttermost._

### h. The document — court testimony

Question-and-answer form. The clerk's record is dry and
formal. The witnesses speak in their own register, which the
clerk transcribes accurately.

> _Q. State your name and place of residence._
>
> _A. James W. Bowman, Lampasas County, Texas._
>
> _Q. Were you in the saloon known as the Bull's Head on the
> evening of the twelfth of August last?_
>
> _A. I was._
>
> _Q. State what you saw._
>
> _A. I seen Pruitt come in. He had been drinking, that was
> plain. He went up to Cottrell at the bar and called him a
> name. Cottrell turned around. Pruitt drew his pistol and
> shot Cottrell in the chest. Cottrell fell. Pruitt walked
> out._

The witness speaks his register (_I seen, that was plain_).
The clerk's questions are formal. The contrast is the texture
of the record.

## 16. Beats, tags, and the page geometry of dialogue

A reminder, expanded. The dialogue mechanics in
`references/prose-craft.md` apply to every archetype above. The
short version:

- One _said_ per page is plenty once the speakers are
  established.
- A beat is better than a tag. _He set the cup down. I never
  liked him, he said._ The beat does the work.
- An adverb on _said_ (_he said quietly, he said angrily_) is
  almost always a sign that the dialogue is not doing its job.
  Rewrite the dialogue to carry the tone.
- _Said_ is invisible. _Replied, retorted, exclaimed, declared,
  averred, opined, intoned_ are not. They draw attention to
  the prose and away from the line.
- McCarthy famously omits quotation marks. Many of the modern
  literary western voices follow him. The corebook prose for
  Tales of the Old West uses standard quotation marks; the
  fiction openers may follow McCarthy. Be consistent within a
  passage.
- A line of dialogue and the action that contains it belong in
  the same paragraph. The convention of _speaker change = new
  paragraph_ is not a rule about who speaks; it is a rule
  about who acts. If the speaker is the actor, the action
  goes with the line.

## 17. The diction of silence

Western fiction is partly written in what is not said. The
beats above are silence. So are the long sentences of landscape
that interrupt a hard scene. So is the white space between
paragraphs.

A character who is not speaking is communicating. Track:

- What he does not answer.
- What he does instead of answering.
- What he stops doing when the question is asked.
- How long the pause runs before the answer arrives.

McMurtry's Call refuses to answer Newt's question for a
hundred and fifty pages and then dies without answering it.
McCarthy's John Grady Cole sits for paragraphs and the prose
follows the wind across the country and we read his refusal
through the wind.

The narrator's job: render the silence, do not explain it.

---

## 18. The single rule for all of this

Listen to the character. Do not write _for_ him. Set the page
quiet, hold the year and the rank and the trade and the
company in mind, and write the line he would say. Then read
it aloud. If it does not sound like a person of his year and
his rank speaking in his own mouth, rewrite it.

The dialogue is the fastest place an AI breaks the spell. It
is also the fastest place a careful writer earns the spell
back.
