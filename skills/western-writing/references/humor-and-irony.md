# Humor and Irony — The Western's Other Half

This file fills a production defect: the prior skill modules
treated the western register as if it were uniformly grim.
McMurtry is funny on every other page. Portis's Mattie is
ironically comic in her deadly seriousness. Leonard's
dialogue turns on wit. Lansdale's dark comedy defines his
Texas work. The tall tale is a genuine western literary
form. The laconic deadpan is a voice mode.

This module is the craft of period humor. It complements
`references/voices.md` (which authors do which kinds of
funny), `references/speech-patterns.md` (the voice
fingerprints that carry comic delivery), and
`references/prose-craft.md` (sentence shapes for the
deadpan).

Humor is not relief from the western register. Humor is
the western register. Drop it and the prose loses half its
truth.

---

## 1. Why the prior modules omitted this

The prior modules emphasized weight, restraint, and
refusal of consolation. Those are correct rules. They
were misread as a ban on humor. They are not. The same
McMurtry who refuses to soften death also gives Augustus
McCrae the line _I tell you, Newt, the only good time to
get up is when you are obliged to._ The same McCarthy
who will not relieve a massacre will also write a black-
comic riff on the conversion of a Tennessee preacher by
a bear. The Western canon is funny when it is funny and
hard when it is hard, and the same prose carries both.

The mistake to avoid: comic relief. The modern habit of
writing a tense scene and following it with a wisecrack
to release the reader is not the western mode. The
western mode is comic and tense at the same time, in
the same line.

## 2. The five comic registers

Western humor falls into five overlapping registers. The
skilled writer knows which one is being used and does not
mix them sloppily.

### a. The laconic deadpan

The straight face. The understatement. The line delivered
with no signal that it is funny, and so funnier for the
absence of the signal. This is the McMurtry-Leonard core
register.

> _Cold this morning,_ Newt said.
>
> _Mostly always is, this time of year,_ Pea Eye said.
>
> _Reckon it will warm up?_
>
> _It generally does, by August._

The exchange is funny because nobody is trying to be
funny. The boy is making conversation; the older hand is
ending it. The reader smiles; the characters do not.

### b. The tall tale

The escalating fiction told straight. Period vernacular
form. A man tells a story about something that happened
to him or to a man he knew, and each beat is more
implausible than the last, and the teller never breaks.
The audience pretends to believe and the teller
pretends not to notice that they are pretending.

This is a structured form: setup (a thing happened),
escalation (it was bigger than you might think),
escalation (and the next thing was bigger than that),
escalation (and then this), straight-faced delivery
(I would not lie to you about a thing like this).

### c. The ironic narrator

The retrospective narrator who sees what the past self
did not, and reports the past self's actions with deadpan
precision. Mattie Ross is the model. The fourteen-year-
old marches into a horse trader's office and demands her
money back; the adult narrator records the demand
without commentary, and the gap between the girl's
ferocious certainty and the adult's quiet recording is
where the comedy lives.

### d. The competence-gap comedy

The dude among cowhands. The Easterner trying to eat
trail food. The educated woman among the cowhands. The
new boy on his first drive. The comedy is not
condescension toward the outsider; it is the friction
between two competence systems, both real.

McMurtry runs this constantly. Leonard runs it. Portis
runs it (Mattie is the outsider in the man's world of
manhunting). The comedy is the friction; the writer's
job is to be fair to both sides.

### e. The dark or grotesque

Lansdale's register. The violence that is also funny
because it is so absurd, the death that arrives with
one ridiculous detail. McCarthy occasionally does this
(the dancing bear). The grotesque comic is the most
dangerous register because it can become cruel; the
discipline is to make sure the laugh is at the
absurdity of the situation, not at the suffering of a
character the reader is supposed to care about.

## 3. The laconic deadpan — how to write it

The deadpan is the western's default comic register. Six
properties.

### a. No signal

The line is delivered without any prose flag that it is
funny. No _he said with a smile,_ no _he joked,_ no
ellipsis suggesting a beat. The line stands. The reader
catches it or does not.

### b. Understatement

The line claims less than it means. _Cold this morning_
when it is twelve below zero. _A long ways_ when it is
two hundred miles. _Some_ when it is a thousand head.
_He was a fair shot_ when the man was a killer.

### c. Topic-drift

The character answers a question by answering a different
one, or extends a conversation past its useful point.

> _You think it will rain?_
>
> _It rained on me once at Adobe Walls in '74._

The drift is the joke. The character has been asked a
weather question and has answered with a memory.

### d. The unanswered straight line

Sometimes the joke is one line and the other character
does not respond. The silence after is the second beat.

> _I do not believe I have eaten a worse biscuit in my
> life,_ Augustus said.
>
> The cook went on rolling out the next batch.

### e. The exchange that ends on the smaller line

A short exchange ends not on the punchline but on the
straight line that takes the wind out of the punchline.

> _I shot a man at Tascosa once,_ Joe said.
>
> _What for?_
>
> _He had a hat I wanted._
>
> _Did you get the hat?_
>
> _He bled on it some._
>
> _So you did not get the hat._
>
> _Not the hat itself, no._

The last line lands. The earlier lines were setup.

### f. The vernacular precision

The deadpan depends on period diction. _Reckon, allow,
suppose, suit yourself, that may be, well now._ The
vocabulary of slow speech. See
`references/speech-patterns.md` for the full
fingerprint.

## 4. The tall tale — structure

The tall tale is the formal comic mode of the period.
Practiced by drovers, trappers, prospectors, and
preachers. Written by Twain (Roughing It), referenced
by McMurtry, used by Lansdale. The structure is
canonical.

### Five beats

1. **The credentialing setup.** _I knew a man once over
   on the Pecos._ The teller establishes that he was
   there or knew the man. The credentialing claims
   reliability.

2. **The plain opening event.** _He was riding back from
   the trading post with a sack of meal._ The opening is
   modest. The reader is not yet on alert.

3. **The first escalation.** _When he topped the rise he
   come on a bull buffalo had broke his leg in a hole._
   The unusual is introduced but not yet impossible.

4. **The further escalations.** Each beat goes one
   notch past plausible. The teller's voice does not
   change. _So he gets down and the buffalo lifts him in
   his teeth and carries him three mile to a cottonwood
   and sets him in the fork of it._ Each detail is
   delivered as if it were obvious.

5. **The deadpan close.** _Anyway. That was the same
   spring his wife left him._ The teller closes by
   walking away from the impossibility, often with a
   line that suggests the impossibility was not even
   the point of the story.

### Worked tall tale

> _I knew a man up on the Cimarron in '76,_ Augustus
> said. \_Name of Hatchett. He had a horse name of
> Pilgrim that could count to seven.
>
> \_One time we were short of beef and Hatchett rode
> Pilgrim out into the breaks to look for a stray. Found
> him a steer about a mile off and the steer took
> off into a draw. Pilgrim run him down and Hatchett
> roped him and they got him back to the wagon by dark.
>
> \_What no one had noticed was a bear was working the
> draw same time. Pilgrim must have noticed because he
> stayed clear of it. Anyway when they got back to the
> wagon Pilgrim wouldn't drink at the trough. Hatchett
> looked at him and Pilgrim was holding up four hooves.
>
> _He could count to seven, like I said. Hatchett took
> the rifle and went back to the draw and there was the
> bear. He was so impressed by Pilgrim he wrote a poem
> about it later but his wife threw the poem in the
> stove. She never did like Pilgrim either._

The tall tale ends not on the bear or the poem but on
the wife not liking the horse. The story has been
about the wife the whole time. The teller has hidden
the joke inside the story.

## 5. The ironic narrator — Mattie Ross mode

The first-person retrospective narrator is the great
comic engine of the western. Three properties.

### a. The narrator records the past self without

defense

The grown Mattie does not defend her younger self. She
records what the girl said, what the girl thought, what
the girl did. The defense is not the narrator's job. The
reader sees the girl's certainty and the narrator's
quiet later perspective and the gap is the laugh.

### b. The narrator's diction is period-formal

Mattie's diction is the diction of an educated
nineteenth-century Presbyterian woman. _I shall, I have
no doubt, I will not have it._ The formal register
applied to absurd subjects (haggling over a horse,
demanding the death of a coward) is the joke.

### c. The narrator's prejudices are recorded, not

shared

The narrator has period prejudices the author does not
share. The narrator records them straight. The reader
gets to watch them and judge them. This is the harder
craft: the narrator is not the author and the author
does not editorialize.

### Worked ironic narrator

> Mr. Stonehill was a thief. I did not call him so to
> his face but I let it be understood by my expression
> when I named the price he had asked. He gave me a
> long look and put his hands behind him and addressed
> me as if I were a customer of substance.
>
> _I do not in the usual way of business deal with
> children,_ he said.
>
> _I am sure you do not,_ I said. \_Children are too
> sensible to deal with you. I am here on my father's
> account and I will not be cheated whether I am
> fourteen or forty. The horses go for thirty dollars
> apiece and not a cent above, and the saddle for
> seven, and that is my last word.
>
> He took it for his last word as well, and we shook
> hands, and I returned to my room at the boarding
> house to write my mother a full report.

The girl is fourteen and is winning. The narrator is
the woman she became and is recording.

## 6. The competence-gap comedy

The dude among cowhands. The greenhorn at the cookfire.
The Eastern lawyer at the trail. The educated woman in
the line camp. Five rules for writing this without
condescension.

### a. The outsider's competence is real

The Eastern dude has competences the cowhands do not.
He reads law. He keeps accounts. He knows the markets.
The comedy is the friction of two competence systems,
not the failure of one.

### b. The cowhand's competence is shown, not stated

The reader sees the cowhand do the work; the reader
sees the dude trying to do the work. The prose does not
say _the cowhand was good at it._ The prose shows him
saddling the horse in twelve seconds.

### c. The dude is given his moment

If the comedy is one-sided the comedy fails. The dude
gets one scene where his competence is the one that
matters — he reads the contract, he names the disease,
he handles the law. The cowhands stand back and watch.

### d. The line that lands

The competence-gap scene lands on a line, usually from
one of the cowhands, that is funny without being
mean. The cowhand respects the dude enough to make
the joke; the dude is in on the joke or not, but the
joke is not at his expense alone.

### e. The dude does not get fixed

The comedy does not end with the dude becoming a
cowhand. He stays a dude. He has been on the trail
and he has seen the country, but he is going back to
Boston. The friction does not resolve.

### Worked competence-gap

> Mr. Pickering had eaten one biscuit and then he had
> stopped, and the biscuit was on his tin plate in two
> halves where he had cracked it. He looked at the
> halves for a long time. Then he looked at the cook,
> who was bent over the dutch oven setting in the next
> batch.
>
> _What is in the biscuit, Mr. Frye?_ Mr. Pickering
> said.
>
> The cook did not look up. _Flour,_ he said.
>
> _And what else?_
>
> \_Lard. Salt. Soda. Some water.
>
> Mr. Pickering took up the half biscuit and looked
> through it as a man might look through a curiosity.
>
> _And how long, Mr. Frye, would you say it has been
> in the oven?_
>
> _Long enough,_ the cook said. \_I generally take them
> out when they are done.
>
> Newt was eating his fourth biscuit and watching this
> exchange. He set the biscuit down and went and
> brought the coffee pot and refilled Mr. Pickering's
> cup. _You will find the coffee easier on the teeth,_
> he said.

The cook is the cowhand competence; Pickering is the
Eastern competence; Newt is the boy who has the manners
to bridge them. The line lands on the boy. Nobody is
the butt.

## 7. The dark or grotesque comic

The Lansdale register. Most dangerous to write because
the line between the absurdly funny and the cruel is
thin. Three rules.

### a. The grotesque target is not a sympathetic

character

The man who dies stupidly should be a man the reader
was not asked to mourn. If the dying man was anybody's
friend, the grotesque close is cruelty. The grotesque
death is reserved for the antagonist or for the random
casualty whose death does not have moral weight.

### b. The absurd detail is one detail

Not three. The reader gets one ridiculous specific.
The man dies and his hat goes one way and the rest is
recorded plain. _He fell sideways and his hat went
into the punch bowl._ The hat is enough. The prose
does not push.

### c. The chapter does not stop on the grotesque

The grotesque is a beat in the chapter, not the
chapter's close. After the absurd detail, the prose
moves on. The chapter ends on something else — the
silence, the country, the survivor's face.

### Worked grotesque

> Caldwell drew first and he was about half slow with
> it and Hatch shot him in the throat. Caldwell sat
> down hard and put both hands on his neck and his
> hat fell off backwards into the trough behind him,
> where it floated for a while and then sank brim
> down and full of water.
>
> Hatch put the pistol away and went and sat on the
> bench in front of the saddler's. The dog had still
> not moved from under the porch.

The hat in the trough is the absurd detail. The chapter
ends on the dog, not on the hat.

## 8. Period humor forms — context

The period had its own organized comic forms. The
writer who knows them can use them as scene devices.

### The newspaper squib

A short comic item in the local paper, typically a
satirical squib about a politician, a rival town, or a
local character. The squib's voice is mock-formal and
heavy on archaic vocabulary. _We are reliably informed
that the Hon. J. P. Calloway, esteemed mayor of Dry
Bone, was last evening seen entering Mrs. Harrison's
boarding house wearing only one of his customary
boots, the other having been lost, our correspondent
reports, in a wager._ See
`knowledge/documents-and-print.md`.

### The stump speech

A comic political address, often performed at a town
meeting or barbecue. Period vocabulary, mock-rhetorical
flourishes, escalating absurdity. The character
delivering it is usually the village's known orator
who is laughed with rather than at. Mark Twain's
lecture career is the fully professionalized version.

### The practical joke

The trail-camp prank or the bunkhouse joke. The
greenhorn given the snipe-hunt. The new hand sent for
a left-handed monkey wrench. The boy told to hold a
candle in a snipe sack. These have a moral weight
inside the cowhand world: the prank is initiation.
The boy who takes it well is welcomed; the boy who
takes it badly is not.

The writer who writes a prank scene must remember: the
boy is being tested and the test has weight. The scene
is funny but it is also serious.

### The bunkhouse story

The story told around the fire. The cowboy's repertoire.
Stories of dead men, fights survived, women lost, horses
that could count. The bunkhouse story is the tall tale's
home form. The audience knows what is coming and they
let it come because the telling is the pleasure.

## 9. What is funny vs. what is cruel

A skill-level distinction.

The period had cruel humor and the canon does not
endorse it. Mocking the Chinese cook for his English.
The slur about the Mexican vaquero. The casual
sneer at the freedman or the Indian. These exist in
period sources and they are not the humor this skill
teaches.

The line: comedy that depends on a character's
membership in a class the period oppressed is cruelty,
not comedy. Comedy that depends on the friction of
work, of competence, of misunderstanding between people
who recognize each other as people is humor.

The cowhand laughing at the Eastern dude is humor: the
dude is a person, the friction is competence, the dude
will be fine. The cowhand laughing at the Chinese cook's
English is cruelty: the cook is being denied
personhood for the sake of the laugh.

The writer can show the cruelty as a fact of the period
without endorsing it. A character who is cruel can be
shown to be cruel, and the prose's silence is the
judgment. See `references/uncomfortable-truths.md`.

## 10. Before / after rewrites

### AI-default joke → deadpan

**Before (telegraphed):**

> _It's mighty cold out here this morning, isn't it?_
> Newt said with a chuckle, his breath forming clouds
> in the frosty air.
>
> Pea Eye laughed heartily. _Cold ain't the word for
> it, son! I reckon it's cold enough to freeze the
> tail off a brass monkey!_

**After (deadpan):**

> _Cold this morning,_ Newt said.
>
> _Mostly always is, this time of year._
>
> _Reckon it will warm up?_
>
> _It generally does, by August._

### Comic relief → comic and tense at once

**Before (relief after tension):**

> The standoff was over and Augustus had killed both
> men. He holstered his pistol and turned to Call. _Well,_
> he said with a wink, _that was a bit livelier than I
> expected._

**After (comic in the tension itself):**

> Augustus had killed both men and the second one had
> bled on his boots. He stood looking down at the
> boots.
>
> _These were new,_ he said.

### Cruel comedy → fair comedy

**Before:**

> The Chinese cook brought the food, mumbling
> something incomprehensible. _What's that, John?_
> the cowhand laughed. _Speak American!_

**After:**

> Wong brought the platter and set it on the table
> without speaking. He had been told once what the
> men called him and he had stopped speaking to them
> at meals. The food came hot and on time. He went
> back to the kitchen. The men ate.

The first is cruelty for the laugh. The second
records the situation and the cook's quiet refusal
without comment.

### Tall tale flat → tall tale escalating

**Before:**

> _I once knew a horse that was very smart. It could
> do tricks. Anyway, my wife didn't like it._

**After:**

> See section 4 above for the full Pilgrim story.

### Modern wisecrack → period deadpan

**Before:**

> _Well, that escalated quickly,_ Augustus said.

**After:**

> _That was more than I had counted on,_ Augustus said.

The first is a 2010s line in 1875 dress. The second
is period.

## 11. The humor cheat-sheet

- **Humor is not relief. Humor is the register.** Comic
  and serious in the same line.
- **Five registers:** laconic deadpan, tall tale, ironic
  narrator, competence-gap, dark/grotesque. Pick.
- **No signal. No flag. No _he chuckled._** The line
  stands alone.
- **Understatement, topic-drift, the unanswered straight
  line.** The deadpan tools.
- **Tall tale has five beats:** credential, plain
  opening, escalation, escalation, deadpan close.
- **Mattie Ross mode:** narrator records past self
  without defense, period-formal diction, prejudices on
  the page.
- **Competence-gap is friction, not failure.** Both
  competences are real. The dude does not get fixed.
- **Grotesque on antagonists, one absurd detail, do not
  close the chapter on it.**
- **Cruelty is not comedy.** No laugh that depends on
  denying a character's personhood.
- **Period diction in every comic line.** No 2010s
  wisecracks in 1875 mouths.

## 12. The rule the others serve

The west was harder than the modern reader can quite
believe and the people who lived in it laughed every
day. The prose that refuses the laughter refuses the
truth. The prose that uses laughter as relief from the
truth also refuses the truth. The right western prose
holds the comic and the hard in one line, and the
reader laughs and is uneasy at the same time, which is
how the people in the country actually felt when they
told the story around the fire.

Augustus McCrae could joke about a dead man. He did so
because the dead man was the kind of man you could
joke about. The reader who did not know the man laughs
and is reminded that there are men you cannot joke
about, and the next chapter is one of those.
