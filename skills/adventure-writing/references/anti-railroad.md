# Anti-Railroad — Patterns That Turn an Adventure Into a Rail

A railroad is an adventure that moves the players from
beginning to end on a fixed track regardless of what they do.
Railroads collapse the consequence ledger, render dilemmas
hollow, and waste the cast.

Most railroads are not designed deliberately. They emerge from
patterns the writer didn't notice. This file is the kill list:
the phrases, structures, and design moves that quietly install
a rail under the chapter.

Run this pass on every draft before publishing.

## The kill list

Search your draft for these phrases and patterns. Each entry
names the symptom, the diagnosis, and the rewrite.

### "The players must…"

> _"The players must accept the contract from Captain
> Rowe."_

**Diagnosis:** The chapter requires the players to do
something. They have no choice.

**Rewrite:** Replace with a hook the players have a reason
to want, and a fallback if they refuse. _"If they refuse the
contract, the killing in Section 3 happens to someone they
already know — Mercer's botched theft frames a friend."_ The
chapter still triggers; the players still have agency.

### "The GM should ensure…"

> _"The GM should ensure the players reach Calder Ranch
> before nightfall."_

**Diagnosis:** The chapter is asking the GM to cheat on its
behalf. The structure cannot survive natural play.

**Rewrite:** Build a structural reason. The road forks; both
forks lead to a plausible Arc II opener. Or: if they don't
reach Calder Ranch, the hay-yard fight comes to them at a
different ranch with a different host.

### "If the players succeed at the roll, X. If they fail, the

adventure cannot continue."

**Diagnosis:** Single-point-of-failure clue gating.

**Rewrite:** See `references/clue-discipline.md`. Every fact
must have three carriers. Every roll must fail forward.

### "Unfortunately, the players cannot…"

> _"Unfortunately, the players cannot kill Vane in Arc II
> without breaking the chapter."_

**Diagnosis:** The chapter has a fragile NPC.

**Rewrite:** If the players can plausibly kill Vane in Arc
II, plan for it. Either Pike absorbs Vane's role; or another
NPC steps in; or the chapter accepts the early death and
shifts. Never write "unfortunately."

### "When the players reach X, the following happens…"

**Diagnosis:** Scripted event. Players have no agency in the
event's occurrence.

**Rewrite:** Make the event _contingent_ on something the
players did or didn't do. The raid happens because the
remuda is poorly guarded — and the players' watch
discipline determines whether they are surprised or
prepared.

### "Read the following text aloud to the players…"

This is fine in itself; the kill version is when the
read-aloud contains _plot_, not tone.

> _"Read aloud: 'You enter the camp and see Captain Rowe.
> He explains that the survey is in danger and asks for
> your help.'"_

**Diagnosis:** Read-aloud as plot delivery. The players
cannot avoid this scene; it is being installed by recital.

**Rewrite:** Read-aloud is sensory only. Plot delivery
happens in the GM's voice, in dialogue, and in character
behavior. See `references/read-aloud.md`.

### "The villain escapes."

**Diagnosis:** Pre-decided outcome. Whatever the players do,
the villain escapes for the next chapter.

**Rewrite:** Plan for the villain's death. The next chapter
inherits the _consequences_ of the death (orphaned network,
power vacuum, vengeance from underlings) — not the villain
themselves. If the villain is too valuable to lose, that is
a failure of cast design.

### "This NPC is essential to the plot."

**Diagnosis:** Single-point-of-failure NPC.

**Rewrite:** No NPC is essential to the plot. Every NPC has
a robustness clause (see `references/npcs.md`). If the
chapter genuinely cannot survive an NPC's death, redistribute
their function across two or three NPCs.

### "The party will surely choose to…"

**Diagnosis:** The writer has predicted player behavior. The
chapter has invested in the prediction.

**Rewrite:** Write three branches: what they will surely
choose, what they might surprisingly choose, and what they
might do that you didn't expect. The third branch is just a
short note: "if they do X, here is the new shape."

### "After this combat, the party should be on track to…"

**Diagnosis:** The chapter has a "track" the players are
"on." It is a railroad.

**Rewrite:** After this combat, name the _consequences_
(track shifts, NPC stances, resource costs) and the
_available options_. The party chooses what to do with the
new state.

## Structural rails

Phrases are the easy catches. The harder catches are
structural.

### The bottleneck scene

A scene that funnels every branch back into a single fixed
event. Common form: "regardless of what the party did in the
previous scene, they end up at the boarding house at midnight
when the assassin strikes."

**Diagnosis:** Branches are illusory. The chapter resets at
the bottleneck.

**Rewrite:** Either the assassin attacks in different places
based on the party's location, or the assassination has
multiple plausible targets and the party's prior choices
determine which one is hit, or the assassination doesn't
happen if the party prevented it.

### The gated stairwell

A series of scenes that can only be unlocked by completing
the previous scene "correctly." If the party flubs Scene 4,
Scene 5 doesn't trigger.

**Diagnosis:** Linear gating.

**Rewrite:** Each scene fires on a _trigger condition_, not
on previous-scene completion. Triggers can be:

- Time passing
- Players entering a location
- A specific faction acting
- A track crossing a threshold

If the party flubs Scene 4, Scene 5 still triggers — but
with worse conditions.

### The wrong-information rail

The chapter assumes players will believe a particular NPC
and act on that NPC's information. If they doubt or
investigate, the chapter has no Plan B.

**Diagnosis:** The chapter assumes player credulity.

**Rewrite:** Plan for the party that doesn't trust the
informant. What investigation paths are available? Which
NPCs can the party verify the information against? The
chapter survives skepticism.

### The pre-killed darling

The chapter introduces an NPC and kills them in the next
scene. The players never had a chance to interact. The
death is supposed to be emotionally weighty, but isn't.

**Diagnosis:** Importance-by-fiat.

**Rewrite:** Either give the players time with the NPC
before the death, or make the death about something other
than the NPC. Bell's death in the Yellowstone Line works
because the _aftermath_ drives the chapter — the laundress's
hard words, the missing satchel, Bell's sister waiting on
cash. The players didn't know Bell, but the residue makes
them care.

### The mandatory revelation

The chapter requires the players to discover a specific
secret (Vane is a railroad agent) at a specific moment for
the chapter to function.

**Diagnosis:** Scripted information delivery.

**Rewrite:** The secret is available through multiple
carriers (see `references/clue-discipline.md`). The chapter
functions whether the players discover it in Arc I, Arc II,
or never — though the consequences differ.

### The sacrificed dilemma

The chapter sets up a dilemma, then writes only one branch.

> _"The players must decide whether to surrender Keene to
> Rowe or hide him. If they surrender him, [long branch].
> If they hide him, see Section 14."_

Section 14 is one short paragraph. The "hide him" branch is
under-developed.

**Diagnosis:** Asymmetric branching. The chapter has a
preferred outcome.

**Rewrite:** Equal investment in each branch. If the chapter
cannot afford to write both branches in full, the dilemma
is too big — break it into smaller dilemmas with smaller
branching.

## Genre rails

Some rails are inherited from genre conventions. Watch for
them.

### The hero rail

The chapter assumes the players are heroes. NPCs treat them
as heroes by default. Antagonists target them as heroes.

**Diagnosis:** The chapter has installed a moral position the
players didn't choose.

**Rewrite:** NPCs treat the players based on the consequence
ledger and the players' visible actions. Antagonists target
them based on threat assessment. "Hero" is a track, not a
default.

### The chosen-one rail

The chapter assumes the players have a unique role only
they can fulfill.

**Diagnosis:** The chapter cannot survive the players
declining the role.

**Rewrite:** The party is one possible solution. There are
others. If the players walk away, the chapter happens
without them — and they live with the consequences.

### The save-the-world rail

The chapter assumes the players will care about the global
stakes.

**Diagnosis:** The chapter has no local stakes.

**Rewrite:** Add stakes the players already care about.
Their named NPCs. Their territory. Their money. Their
reputation. Local stakes are what move parties; global
stakes are window dressing.

### The redemption rail

A villain's redemption is scripted. The chapter writes the
moment the villain turns. The players witness it.

**Diagnosis:** Player choice is bypassed for the villain's
arc.

**Rewrite:** The villain's redemption is _available_, not
scripted. Players can earn it (offering an out, demonstrating
a third path) or refuse it (forcing the villain into death
or escape). The villain's arc is contingent on the party's
arc.

## Theme as rail

The most insidious rail is the chapter's theme spoken aloud.

### Symptom

> _"In this adventure, the party will discover that the
> price of progress is paid by the powerless."_

The chapter announces what it means. Once the meaning is
announced, the players cannot discover it. They can only
agree or disagree.

### Diagnosis

The chapter has installed an interpretive frame the players
didn't earn. Whatever they choose, the chapter pre-judges.

### Rewrite

Cut every sentence that names the theme. Themes emerge from
events. Let the burned ranch, the dead laundress, the
forged route notes, the Crow warning ignored, do the work.

If a reader cannot identify your chapter's theme without
your help, the events are not doing their job. Strengthen
the events. Do not state the theme.

The Yellowstone Line never says "the railroad will win" or
"the West is being stolen from those who live here." Those
themes are _enacted_ — by Pike's billing, by Vane's deals,
by Hannah's brand register, by the survey stakes that bring
tax men. The reader and the players reach the theme without
being told it.

## The anti-railroad pass

Run this on every draft before publishing.

1. **Search for "must," "should," "ensure," "essential,"
   "the party will," "the GM should."** Every hit is a
   railroad symptom. Rewrite or justify.
2. **For every scene, ask: can the players refuse this
   scene?** If not, the scene is a station, not a beat.
3. **For every NPC death, ask: does the chapter survive?**
   If a single death breaks the chapter, redistribute the
   NPC's function.
4. **For every clue, ask: how many carriers?** Three
   minimum. (See `references/clue-discipline.md`.)
5. **For every dilemma, ask: are both branches written
   with equal weight?** If not, the chapter has a preferred
   outcome.
6. **For every read-aloud, ask: is this tone or plot?** If
   plot, move it to the GM voice.
7. **For every scene, ask: what changes if the players do
   nothing?** If nothing changes, the chapter is on rails
   that drag them along.
8. **For every theme, ask: did the chapter say it aloud?**
   If yes, cut.
9. **For every faction, ask: do they act on their own
   interests, or only as the chapter requires?** If the
   latter, the faction is a railroad device.
10. **Read the chapter as if the players burned every
    paper, killed every named NPC, and refused every offer
    in Arc I.** Does the chapter survive? If not, identify
    the rail and remove it.

## What survives the pass

A chapter that passes is recognizable. It has:

- Multiple NPCs carrying every fact.
- Multiple branches at every choice.
- Multiple end-states keyed to tracks.
- No NPC the chapter cannot lose.
- No scene the players cannot refuse.
- No theme spoken aloud.
- Read-aloud that is sensory, not informational.
- Faction behavior driven by faction interest, not chapter need.

That chapter is robust. It will survive the table.
