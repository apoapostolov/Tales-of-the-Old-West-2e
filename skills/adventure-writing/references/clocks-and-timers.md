# Clocks and Timers

An adventure without urgency is a series of options
with no cost of time. Players can investigate
indefinitely, rest when they want, and approach each
problem at their preferred pace. This is comfortable
and produces flat adventures.

Clocks impose the cost of time. When time passes,
the world changes. Some changes are bad for the
party. Some are bad for everyone. None are neutral.

## What a clock is

A clock is a named countdown attached to a faction,
threat, resource, or situation. It has a number of
segments — typically four, six, or eight. Each
time the clock advances, the situation changes.

When the clock fills, a specific consequence fires.
The consequence is not the end of the adventure.
It is a new situation that creates new pressure.

Clocks are structural tools, not punishment
mechanics. They do not punish players for playing
badly. They change the world in response to time
passing — which is what the world does.

## Three clock types

### (1) The villain timeline

The antagonist is doing something that proceeds
step by step regardless of the party's actions.
Each segment represents a completed step.

Design format:

```
CLOCK: Holt surveys the northern route
Segments: 4

Segment 1 fires: Holt's surveying crew arrives
at the Reyes ranch.
Segment 2 fires: The Reyes family is served notice
to vacate within 30 days.
Segment 3 fires: Holt files the route survey with
the territorial office. The claim becomes legal.
Segment 4 fires: The eviction party arrives at the
Reyes ranch.
```

The villain timeline makes the antagonist feel real:
they are acting independently of the party's moves.
Even if the party never engages with the clock
directly, it advances in the background and changes
the world the party is moving through.

### (2) The resource clock

An NPC, location, or opportunity is deteriorating.
Every advance represents further deterioration.

Resource clocks include: a wounded NPC's health,
a town's water supply in a drought, a ship's
structural integrity in a storm, a witness's nerve
before they recant. The resource is specific and
named.

Design format:

```
CLOCK: Isabel Reyes's resolve
Segments: 3

Segment 1 fires: Isabel stops speaking to outsiders.
She is afraid.
Segment 2 fires: Isabel prepares to leave the ranch.
She has given up.
Segment 3 fires: Isabel is gone. Whatever she knew
dies with her departure.
```

A fully-advanced resource clock closes a path or
raises a cost. It does not end the adventure.

### (3) The discovery clock

The party's actions are generating evidence that the
antagonist's agents are collecting. When the clock
fills, the antagonist becomes aware of the party's
investigation and responds.

Discovery clocks reward stealth and punish repeated
visibility. They make NPC awareness feel like a
real variable rather than a static fact.

Design format:

```
CLOCK: Vane tracks the party's movements
Segments: 4

Triggers:
- Party is seen at the land office (+1)
- Party speaks to any member of the Reyes family
  in the open (+1)
- Party confronts Aldermoor directly (+1)
- Party recovers the deed copy (+2)

Segment 4 fires: Vane reports to Holt. The
antagonist now knows who the party is, who they've
spoken to, and that the deed is in play. Holt
activates the response tree.
```

Discovery clocks can have variable-cost triggers
(some actions advance the clock by one; some by two).

## Visible versus hidden clocks

**Visible clocks**: the party knows the countdown
is running. "The wagon train leaves in three days."
"The witness goes before the magistrate in 48 hours."
Players can make deliberate choices about how to
spend their time.

Visible clocks create intentional urgency. Players
feel the weight of every rest, every side-trip.

**Hidden clocks**: the party does not know the clock
exists until it fires or until they discover evidence
of it. The antagonist's survey is proceeding. The
witness is losing nerve. Players discover the clock
by finding its evidence.

Hidden clocks create discovered urgency. The moment
of discovery — "you realize you've been on a clock
you didn't see" — is a pressure escalation.

**Mixed use**: most good adventures use both. The
party knows about the visible clocks and is trying
to stay ahead of them. They don't know about the
hidden clocks until the evidence accumulates. The
combination produces pacing rhythm: known pressure
driving deliberate action, discovered pressure
interrupting plans.

## Clock triggers

When does a clock segment advance? Never randomly.
Always on specific, named triggers.

Trigger types:

- **Time-based**: "every full night of rest advances
  this clock by one."
- **Action-based**: "each time the party reveals
  evidence to an outside party, this clock advances."
- **Event-based**: "when the party reaches Act II
  without resolving the survey, this clock advances
  to segment 2."
- **Failure-based**: "each time the party fails a
  social roll with an Aldermoor faction NPC, this
  clock advances."

State the trigger explicitly in the clock entry.
A clock whose trigger is ambiguous will advance
inconsistently and lose its structural function.

## How many clocks

One to three active clocks per adventure.

More than three: the GM cannot track them consistently.
The clocks interfere with each other and become
overhead rather than tension.

Fewer than one: the adventure has no structural
urgency. The party acts without consequence of time.

For a one-shot: one clock, visible or mixed.
For a campaign chapter: one to three, mixed visibility.
For a horror adventure: start with one visible
(resource deterioration) and one hidden (discovery or
villain timeline). Add a third only if the adventure
spans multiple sessions.

## Clock cash-out

When a clock fills, a specific consequence fires.
The consequence must be:

1. **Named**: the exact thing that happens, not
   "the situation gets worse." "The eviction party
   arrives at the Reyes ranch" is named.
2. **Non-terminal**: the adventure continues after
   the consequence. The world is changed; the party
   still has options. Exception: some resource clocks
   can be terminal for the resource (the witness
   leaves; that path is closed). The _adventure_
   still continues.
3. **GM-actionable**: the GM can describe the
   consequence immediately when it fires without
   additional preparation.

## Clocks and consequence tracks

Clocks advance situations. Tracks record relationships.
They operate at different registers and complement
each other.

A villain timeline clock can fire a consequence track
shift: "When Holt's clock reaches segment 3, Army
Respect of the Holt faction drops by 1." The clock
governs the timing; the track governs the faction's
position in the world.

See `references/factions-and-tracks.md` for track
mechanics. Cross-reference clocks and tracks when
an antagonist's timeline affects faction relationships.

## The open clock

Some clocks never fill because the party moves fast.
An unfilled clock at the adventure's end is not
wasted design — it is a sandbox exit.

The antagonist's partially-completed plan continues
into the next chapter. The resource that was
deteriorating is still deteriorating. The next adventure
inherits the clock's current position.

See `references/endings-and-sandbox.md` for how
open clocks become forward threads.

## Anti-patterns

**The hidden clock that punishes without explanation**:
a clock fires and something bad happens, but the
players have no way to understand why or what they
could have done differently. This is a GM fiat
consequence dressed up as a clock. Fix: ensure the
clock had visible evidence before it fired.

**The clock that advances regardless of player agency**:
every trigger is time-based only. Nothing the party
does slows or stops the clock. This is a countdown
to a scripted outcome. Fix: ensure at least one
trigger is action-based and that the party can take
actions that slow the clock.

**The single clock as the only pacing structure**:
one countdown, one consequence, no texture. Fix:
add at least one resource or relationship element
that changes in response to the party's moves,
even if it isn't a formal clock.

**The invisible clock the GM forgot**: the clock
was designed, given triggers, and never advanced
because the GM lost track. Fix: list all active
clocks on the GM's session sheet. Check triggers
at the end of each scene.
