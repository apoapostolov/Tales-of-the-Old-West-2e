# Levers and Fallbacks — How to Win More Than One Way

`clue-discipline.md` teaches that no fact lives in one mouth. This file teaches the parallel discipline for **outcomes**: no adventure's resolution should live in one route. The party that refuses the obvious path, fails the obvious path, or never finds it must still be able to reach a reckoning — a different reckoning, but a real one.

This is the engine of the sandbox (`sandbox-structure.md`) and the backstop of the chapter. A chapter with one winning route is a puzzle with a fight at the end. A chapter with a lever menu is a story the party owns.

## The lever, defined

A **lever** is a route to the adventure's reckoning that does not depend on any other route. Each lever is:

- **Sufficient.** Holding this one lever, the party can reach an ending.
- **Held.** It belongs to an NPC, a place, or a faction who must be moved, paid, fought, or persuaded to release it.
- **Costed.** The lever's cost is distinct from every other lever's cost. If two levers cost the same thing, the choice between them is decorative.
- **Combinable but not required to combine.** Levers stack, but no stack is mandatory. The party can bring one, two, or none.

The contrast: a **critical path** is the single route an adventure's resolution depends on. A critical path with branches off it is still a critical path. A lever menu is many sufficient routes, none mandatory.

## The lever menu — worked example

The _Quiet Title_ adventure runs nine levers against one situation (breaking or bypassing a corrupt land-grant company's court case). The party can win through any one or any combination:

| Lever | Held by | What it breaks | What it costs |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| The surveyor's field book (true line, witnessed) | The dead surveyor's hiding place, the guide's knowledge | The Company's boundary claim outright | The most danger; everyone will kill for it |
| The false surveyor's affidavit | The broken surveyor, drunk, terrified | The Company's boundary claim nearly as well | A way out for the witness; coercion makes it worthless |
| The original grant language (minerals reserved) | The grantee's granddaughter, lonely and angry | The Company's _gold_ claim, not the boundary | A promise to press her family's claim too |
| The cemetery register (possession since the founding) | The parish priest | Slow in court, unanswerable in the plaza | Trust with the priest; the register's safety |
| The bought witness (the guide who saw the murder) | The guide himself, terrified | The murder, which unravels everything | The guide's life; a court that may not swear a mixed-blood |
| The Company's own mail | Intercepted in transit | The Company hangs itself on its postage | The logistics of interception; exposure |
| The railroad's money | The railroad agent, patient | Buys a resolution without a court at all | The party's independence; a continental debt |
| The community's debt | The store's ledger | Title by debt, slow and ugly | Cash, years, and Eufemia's arithmetic |
| A range war | The country itself | Makes the grant too expensive for the owners to keep | Blood, federal attention, moral weight |

Nine routes. No two cost the same. The party needs two or three to reach the strongest reckoning, but can reach _a_ reckoning with one, and can reach the reckoning with _none_ by playing the country's collapse differently. That is a lever menu.

## Designing the lever menu

Build the menu before drafting the scenes. The scenes are where the levers live; if you draft scenes first, you will accidentally write a critical path.

1. **Name the situation's center of gravity.** What is the one fact, document, alliance, or act that, if it landed in the right hands, would resolve the situation? That is your loudest lever — the one the adventure's title seems to promise. (In _Quiet Title_: the field book.)
2. **Ask: if the party refuses the loudest lever, what else resolves this?** Write down every answer you can think of. Each is a candidate lever. Aim for five to nine.
3. **For each candidate, assign a holder, a cost, and a distinct consequence.** If two candidates have the same holder and same cost, merge or cut one.
4. **Cut any lever that is just a harder version of another.** "The field book, but translated" is not a separate lever. "The grant language, which breaks a different claim entirely" is.
5. **Write at least one lever that does not touch the central conflict at all and still wins.** The railroad's money wins by _sidestepping_ the court case. The range war wins by making the case moot. A lever that changes the game rather than winning the game is the sign of a rich menu.

## The cost distinctness rule

The most common lever-menu failure is **cost collapse**: three levers that all cost "a fight with the antagonist's men." If every road costs the same thing, the party's choice of road is aesthetic, not strategic. Force each lever to cost something different:

- One costs **danger** (the field book — everyone will kill for it).
- One costs **trust** (the priest's register — burn the register's safety and lose the priest forever).
- One costs **promise** (the grant language — bind the party to a third claimant's cause).
- One costs **cash** (the debt — money and years).
- One costs **independence** (the railroad's money — a debt across a continent).
- One costs **moral weight** (the range war — blood and federal attention).

When the party chooses between levers, it is choosing between costs. That is the choice that makes the adventure feel owned.

## Fallbacks — when the party misses a lever

A lever is a route _to_ the reckoning. A **fallback** is a route _from_ a missed lever back into the adventure. They are not the same thing. The lever menu gives the party many winning routes; fallbacks ensure that missing one route does not strand the party.

`clue-discipline.md` covers the three-clue rule for _information_. Fallbacks are the same principle for _action_: if the party refuses or misses one route, the next route is still reachable.

### Fallback design

For each lever, ask: **if the party never touches this lever, does the adventure still work?** The answer must be yes. If the answer is no, the lever has become a critical path in disguise, and you must either add another lever that covers the same ground or make the reckoning readable without this lever's contribution.

The _Quiet Title_ design: the field book is the loudest lever, but if the party refuses it (burns it, never climbs the cañón, sells it to the railroad immediately), the adventure still reaches a reckoning through the affidavit, the grant language, the cemetery register, or the simple fact that the community's possession is older than the grant. The field book makes the strongest case in court. Its absence does not end the adventure.

### The fallback is not a do-over

A fallback is not the adventure handing the party the missed lever through a different door. It is the adventure having _another sufficient route_ that does not require the missed one. The party that burned the field book does not find a second field book. It finds the affidavit, which is a different document that does similar work, held by a different person, at a different cost.

## The "truth survives the holder" table

The single most useful fallback artifact is a **truth-survives-the-holder table**: for each load-bearing NPC, name where their truth goes if they die, run, or are bought off before the party reaches them. This is the action-layer equivalent of the three-clue rule.

_Quiet Title_ example:

| If the holder dies or is lost... | The truth moves to... |
| --- | --- |
| The guide (the murder witness) | The murder victim's last words; the surveyor's cached letters; the Apache scout's memory of his cousin on the ridge |
| The false surveyor | His partial notes in his room; his draft affidavit; his letters to the Company in the Company's safe |
| The Company superintendent | The Ring lawyer in the county seat; a Company director wired from London; the railroad agent, who was never as separate as he claimed |
| The judge | The hearing collapses and the suit moves to a higher court, or to the newspaper and the court of public opinion |
| The grant language copy | The original in the county archives; a transcript filed with the parish; the granddaughter's own notes |

No single NPC's death ends the adventure. Each death tightens the knot elsewhere. Remove the wrong person and the knot tightens around the party.

Write this table for every adventure, chapter or sandbox. It is the fastest way to find the single-point failures hiding in your design.

## Levers in the chapter, not only the sandbox

The lever menu is the sandbox's defining engine, but it belongs in chapters too. A chapter with a three-act arc and a single winning route is fragile. A chapter with a three-act arc and a lever menu is robust: the party that misses the designed climax can still reach a reckoning by a road the author built for it.

The minimum lever menu for a chapter is **three**: the designed route, one alternative that costs differently, and one that sidesteps the central conflict. Below three, you are back in critical-path territory.

## The lever audit

1. **Name your loudest lever.** Can the adventure reach a reckoning if the party refuses it? If no, add two independent levers.
2. **Do any two levers share a cost?** If yes, merge or differentiate.
3. **Is there at least one lever that sidesteps the central conflict?** If no, the menu is tactical, not strategic. Add the sidesteper.
4. **Does every load-bearing NPC have a truth-survives line?** If no, you have hidden single-point failures.
5. **Can the party reach a reckoning with _no_ levers?** It should be a bleak reckoning — the country's verdict on a party that engaged with nothing — but it should exist. A party that does nothing still gets an ending.

If all five land, the lever menu is whole.
