# NPCs — Casting, Voicing, and Stance

NPCs are the surface of the adventure. Players touch the world
through them. A good cast carries a chapter even when the plot
falters; a weak cast collapses even strong material into mush.

This file defines the NPC template, the voicing register, and
the stance principle that separates living NPCs from quest
dispensers.

## The non-negotiable: every NPC has a stance toward the PCs

Most published adventures answer "what does this NPC want?"
That is half the work. The full question is:

> **What does this NPC want, and what do they think the PCs
> are?**

Stance is what the NPC reads the PCs as: tools, threats, marks,
witnesses, peers, prey, allies, instruments. The stance shapes
every line of dialogue and every transactional choice.

Two NPCs can want the same thing and behave completely
differently because their stances differ. A captain who sees
the PCs as "irregular tools" speaks to them in clipped orders.
A railroad agent who sees the PCs as "purchased assets" speaks
to them in soft confidences. The want is similar — both want
the survey delivered. The behavior diverges entirely.

Write the stance line for every named NPC. It is the line you
will return to most often at the table.

## The cast template

Every named NPC entry in the chapter uses this fixed structure.
One paragraph, four to seven sentences. Always in this order.

1. **Name, demographic, body** — one sentence. Race or peoplehood,
   apparent age, build, one defining physical feature.
2. **Defining quality** — one short clause. Three to five words
   naming what the character _is_. Not what they do.
3. **Mannerism** — one or two sentences. Two or three observable
   tics. Things the players will see at the table. Touches,
   gestures, gaze patterns, postures.
4. **Speech register** — one sentence. How they talk. Length of
   sentences. What they avoid. What they over-explain.
5. **Stance toward PCs** — one or two sentences. What they read
   the PCs as. Begins with "He sees players as" or equivalent.

### Worked example

> `Captain Nathaniel Rowe` is a white man in his early forties,
> Army to the bone, narrow through the waist, and hard in the
> saddle. He is order held up by temper. He keeps gloves clean
> in dirt and looks past men before they finish speaking. He
> talks in clipped sentences. He sees players as irregular
> tools: necessary for a mean country, but not people he trusts
> before they hold their nerve.

Sentence 1: name, demographic, body, defining feature.
Sentence 2: defining quality.
Sentence 3: mannerism.
Sentence 4: speech.
Sentence 5: stance.

This template scales. Use it for the captain, the clerk, the
laundress who curses over a corpse. The five-sentence structure
forces compression and prevents the GM-novel problem.

## The defining-quality discipline

Each NPC's defining quality must be unique within the chapter.
Two NPCs cannot both be "order held up by temper." One NPC is
"order held up by temper." Another is "smooth, never hurries."
A third is "steady, built from old leather."

If two of your NPCs have similar defining qualities, one is
redundant — collapse them or differentiate.

A useful exercise: write the defining qualities of every NPC in
your cast on one page. If you cannot tell them apart at a
glance, your cast is undifferentiated.

## Mannerism: observable, not interior

Mannerisms are what the players see. Not what the NPC feels.

> Bad: _"He is nervous about the missing papers."_
> Good: _"He touches his coat pocket twice while speaking and
> trims sentences toward safety."_

> Bad: _"She is tired of dealing with the Army."_
> Good: _"She watches men like she watches weather."_

The Insight check (or your system's equivalent) reads
mannerisms. Internal states are off-limits to the GM voice.

## Speech register: the fingerprint

Each NPC has a distinct way of using language. The fingerprint
is built from three traits:

1. **Sentence length.** Clipped, plain, drawling, flowery,
   technical.
2. **What they avoid.** A subject, a name, a register, a tense.
3. **What they over-explain.** A subject, a credential, a
   distance, a price.

Examples:

- _Rowe_: clipped sentences, avoids personal questions,
  over-explains regulations.
- _Vane_: lowered voice, avoids naming his backers,
  over-explains the inevitability of his preferred outcome.
- _Hannah Calder_: flat plain sentences, avoids requests for
  help, over-explains the cost of nails and damp flour.

Drop one signature line of dialogue per major NPC into the
chapter, in italics, set as a quote. One line. Not a speech.
The line is the GM's tuning fork.

> _"You put stakes in the ground,"_ Hannah says, _"and the
> rest follows behind."_

## Stance verbs

When you write the stance line, use verbs that name a
relational reading. Avoid bland "thinks the players are."

Useful verbs:

- _sees_ ("sees players as irregular tools")
- _measures_ ("measures players against the men he buried")
- _judges_ ("judges players by whether they hold their nerve")
- _treats_ ("treats players as paying customers until they
  miss a payment")
- _reads_ ("reads players as marks the moment they show
  hesitation")
- _classifies_ ("classifies players as instruments, witnesses,
  or competitors")

The verb does work. _"Sees"_ is observational; _"reads"_ is
calculating; _"measures"_ is moralized; _"treats"_ is
transactional.

## Stance evolution

Stances change during play. Write at least one named NPC
whose stance is designed to _evolve_ in response to PC choice.

> _"Shows-the-Ford sees players as unmarked men who might yet
> justify the trouble of knowing them."_

That stance is provisional. The chapter teaches the GM what
shifts it: a kept word, a shared danger, a refused bribe.

For each track in the consequence ledger, name one NPC whose
stance toward the PCs explicitly shifts as the track moves.

## How many NPCs

A chapter sustainably holds:

- 8–12 named NPCs (full template)
- 3–6 named-but-minor NPCs (one-line introductions)
- unlimited unnamed extras (laundress, mule skinners,
  troopers, clerks, drovers, faceless raiders)

More than 12 named NPCs and the GM cannot voice them all.
Players blur them. The cast loses force.

## Robustness clauses

Every named NPC needs a robustness clause: what happens to
the chapter if this NPC dies in their first scene.

The clause goes in the "If the players freeze or break the
plan" section (see `references/architecture.md`), not in the
NPC entry itself.

For each major NPC, name:

- which clue or function they carry
- which secondary NPC absorbs that function on their death
- what the chapter loses (texture only, never plot)

Example:

> **If Keene dies early:** Move his copy to Mercer, Bell's
> satchel, or Reed's spare case. The copied-paper rot
> survives Keene.

The chapter must reach its reckoning even if every named NPC
dies. If one death breaks the chapter, you have a single-
point-of-failure NPC and you have not done your work.

## NPC types and ratios

A balanced cast contains roughly:

- **2–3 authorities** (legitimate power)
- **2–3 antagonists** (illegitimate power, opposed agendas)
- **1–2 outsiders** (representatives of factions outside the
  central conflict — Native, foreign, religious)
- **1–2 grounders** (witnesses, victims, locals — the moral
  anchor characters who do not want anything from the PCs)
- **1–2 wild cards** (NPCs whose loyalties are unclear, or
  whose interests cut across the factions)

The grounders are essential. Without them, every NPC is
transactional and the chapter feels like a marketplace. Hannah
Calder, the laundress over the body, the settler with the
pathetic claim marker — these are the NPCs who give the
chapter its weight.

## What NPCs are not

- **Not stat blocks.** Stat blocks live in a separate file or
  appendix. Reference them by name; do not put numbers in the
  cast entry.
- **Not quest dispensers.** Every NPC has standing interests
  before the PCs arrive. They will continue having interests
  after the PCs leave.
- **Not theme statements.** No NPC speaks the theme of the
  chapter aloud. They speak about money, horses, weather, or
  paper. The theme is what they push against, not what they
  say. (See `references/anti-railroad.md` on theme-as-rail.)

## The dialogue test

Take any two of your NPCs and write a one-line exchange
between them. If you can't tell which line belongs to which
NPC without a name tag, your fingerprints are blurry.

> _Rowe: "Mr. Vane, you carry no rank in this column."_
> _Vane: "I carry something better, Captain. I carry the
> men whose money you spend."_

The lines are interchangeable in topic but unmistakable in
voice. That is the standard.
