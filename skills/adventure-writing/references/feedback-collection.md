# Feedback Collection

Feedback is not a grade. It is a dataset. The author's
job during feedback collection is to listen, record, and
resist every defensive impulse. The adventure is not
being evaluated — it is being measured.

## Timing

Collect feedback 12-24 hours after the session. Not
immediately after.

Immediately after a session, players are tired,
emotionally flooded, and either riding an adrenaline
high or processing disappointment. Their reactions
are real but not yet sorted. The author is in the same
state. Immediate debriefs produce visceral responses,
not structural critique.

After a night's sleep, the session organizes in memory.
Players can distinguish between "that didn't work" and
"I didn't enjoy that, but I understand why it was there."
Authors can hear criticism without reflexive defence.

Wait the night. Then debrief.

## The debrief structure

A short structured conversation or written form with
seven questions. Use the written form if:

- Players are geographically dispersed.
- You want more considered, less performance-driven
  responses.
- The session had difficult content and players may
  want to process privately.

Use the verbal conversation if:

- The group is comfortable and the session ended well.
- You want to follow up on specific answers.
- The group has a strong culture of honest discussion.

The written form is `templates/feedback-form.md`.

## The seven questions

### Five player questions

1. **Which moment in the session would you most want
   to play again?**
   _What this finds_: the sections that worked. The
   answer names the emotional high. Note it. Build
   more of it.

2. **Which moment do you wish had gone differently?**
   _What this finds_: the sections that failed or
   frustrated. Do not prompt them to identify causes —
   only the moment. You will identify the cause.

3. **Was there anything you couldn't figure out, or
   something you wanted more information about?**
   _What this finds_: clue failures, opaque NPCs,
   unclear stakes. This is the most direct question for
   structural gaps.

4. **What did you want to do that the adventure
   didn't support?**
   _What this finds_: agency gaps. If multiple players
   name the same action, it is a missing branch.

5. **What would you tell another player before running
   this adventure?**
   _What this finds_: the player's summary of the
   experience. Often surfaces what landed most and
   what didn't without the author having to ask
   directly.

### Two GM questions

1. **Was the text sufficient to run from without any
   input from the author? If not, which sections
   needed clarification?**
   _What this finds_: text failures. Any section the
   GM couldn't run from is a failure of description
   or instruction, not a failure of the adventure's
   concept.

2. **Were there moments where you improvised because
   the chapter didn't cover the situation?**
   _What this finds_: gaps in the adventure's branch
   coverage. The GM's improvisations are the scenarios
   the author missed.

## How to listen

Take notes. Do not speak until a player has finished.
Do not say "yes, but" or "that's because" or explain
what the adventure was trying to do.

When a player says "I couldn't figure out Vane's
motivation," the correct response is: _[write: "Vane's
motivation — unclear to players"]_ and then: "Say more
about that?"

The explanation of what the adventure intended is not
relevant to the debrief. The debrief is not a defense
hearing. It is a measurement.

The single exception: if a player's comment is based
on a factual misunderstanding (they missed a clue
that was clearly in the text), note that the clue
was present — but also note that it wasn't received.
A clue that exists but isn't received is still a
structural failure.

## The feedback triage

After collecting all feedback, sort every item into
one of three buckets before making any decisions.

**Bucket A — Structural failures**: Things in this
adventure that need to be fixed before it is published
or run again. Named clue carriers, NPC entries,
unclear transitions, missing context. Act on these
immediately. Do not file them in `learnings/` — apply
the fix to the adventure text.

**Bucket B — Author learnings**: Patterns observed in
this session that have implications for future adventures.
An NPC who didn't land despite having the five-sentence
template — what made them land flat? A scene that ran
long because the players engaged in a direction the
text didn't support — what would have made the text
more responsive? These go to `learnings/`. See
`learnings/README.md`.

**Bucket C — Preference mismatches**: Players wanted
a different kind of adventure. The group preferred
action and this was a slow investigation. One player
wanted more horror. These are real signals about this
group's preferences, but they are not structural
failures of the adventure. File them under "player
preference notes" — they matter for choosing what to
write next, not for fixing this adventure.

## Red flags: when feedback signals a structural failure

These patterns in feedback always indicate structural
failures, not preference mismatches:

- **Multiple players flag the same moment**: If two or
  more players identify the same scene as confusing or
  broken, it is structural. One player's reaction can
  be idiosyncratic; two is a pattern.

- **"Hard to find" clues named by more than one player**:
  Add another carrier before publishing. The three-clue
  rule failed.

- **"Flat" or "confusing" NPC named by more than one
  player**: Revise the NPC's five-sentence template.
  The motivational chain is unclear or the behavior
  entry isn't specific enough.

- **"We didn't know where to go next" from the GM**:
  The scene's exits or orientation beat are missing or
  unclear. Fix the text.

- **GM made the same improvisation twice**: The
  adventure is missing a branch. The GM improvised
  the same scenario twice because the text forced them
  to. It should be in the text.

## What to do with author learnings (bucket B)

For each bucket-B item:

1. Write the learning in the format defined in
   `learnings/README.md`.
2. File it in the appropriate `learnings/` file.
3. Note which reference file the learning might
   eventually update.

Don't update the reference files immediately from a
single session's data. Wait for the learning to be
confirmed by a second session (same or different
adventure). See `learnings/README.md` for graduation
protocol.

## Protecting the author

Negative feedback is data. It is not a verdict on the
author's worth, ability, or future career. An adventure
that generates rich structural critique was doing
something ambitious enough to fail in interesting ways.

If feedback stings, set aside your notes for 24 hours
before triaging. Triage requires clear-headedness.

The goal of feedback collection is not to find out if
the adventure was good. The goal is to find out what
to fix and what to learn. Both of those goals are
served by honest, specific feedback — even when that
feedback is uncomfortable.

The fastest path to a better adventure is a thorough
debrief of a flawed one.

## Anonymous feedback channels

Some players will not give honest critical feedback in a verbal
debrief or in a form with their name on it, especially when the
author is also their friend, GM, or community member.

For adventures being broadly published or run with strangers,
offer an anonymous return path:

- A form (Google Form, Tally, simple webform) with no name field.
- Link the form in the adventure's GM notes for blind playtesters.
- Make submission optional. Anonymous channels work because
  participation is voluntary.

Anonymous feedback is lower-bandwidth than a debrief but catches
specific failure modes the debrief misses: the player who didn't
like the adventure but will not say so to the author's face, the
GM who found the text confusing but does not want to seem
incompetent in front of the table.

For home-table playtests with established friends, anonymous
channels are usually unnecessary. For convention runs, blind
playtests, and any release intended for strangers, build the
channel.
