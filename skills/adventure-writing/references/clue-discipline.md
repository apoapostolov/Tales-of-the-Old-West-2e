# Clue Discipline — Distributed Truth

The single most common failure mode in published adventures is
the load-bearing clue: a fact that lives in one mouth, one
note, or one search check. When that single point fails, the
chapter halts.

This file teaches the discipline that prevents the failure: no
fact lives in fewer than three carriers, and no carrier
requires a successful roll to surrender the fact under sufficient
player pressure.

## The three-clue rule

> **For every conclusion the players need to reach, there must
> be at least three independent clues that point to it.**

Originally formulated for investigative games (the GUMSHOE
tradition, Justin Alexander's writing on the subject), the
three-clue rule generalizes to all adventure design. A fact
that matters to the chapter's branching must have three
independent pathways to discovery.

Independent means:

- Different NPCs (so killing one does not bury the fact)
- Different locations (so skipping one place does not bury it)
- Different skills/approaches (so a party of one type can find it)

A fact backed by three NPCs in the same building is not three
clues. It is one clue with redundant witnesses. If the
building burns, the fact dies.

## Carriers

A clue can be carried by:

- **A person** (an NPC who knows it, willingly or not)
- **An object** (a letter, a ledger, a weapon, a brand)
- **A place** (a grave, a path, a damaged structure)
- **A behavior** (an NPC's nervous tick, a faction's movement)
- **A consequence** (a missing person, a price change, a
  withdrawn troop)

The strongest distribution uses at least three different
carrier _types_ per fact. A fact carried by three NPCs is
fragile (kill them all, lose the fact). A fact carried by
one NPC, one document, and one observable consequence is
robust.

## Worked example

Take the fact: **"Mercer is copying survey notes for Vane."**

A bad distribution:

- Keene tells the players if they roll Persuasion.

That is one carrier, one skill, one fail-state. If the roll
fails or Keene dies, the chapter has lost a load-bearing fact.

A good distribution:

- **Person:** Keene confesses under pressure (multiple roll
  paths: Persuasion, Intimidation, Insight).
- **Person:** Mercer himself is observed checking paper
  custody twice in camp (Insight or just attention).
- **Object:** Bell's body has a survey pencil shaving in his
  hand (visible without rolls; survey men do not normally
  reach the picket line).
- **Object:** A spare ink bottle in Mercer's kit shows
  fingerprint marks consistent with rapid copying (close
  search).
- **Behavior:** Vane visits Mercer's tent at odd hours
  (anyone watching the camp at night).
- **Consequence:** Reed's working notes show pages
  re-sequenced; whoever copied them put the originals back
  out of order (Reed himself notices, eventually).

Six carriers, four types, multiple skill approaches. The
chapter will not lose this fact unless every carrier is
destroyed simultaneously.

## The carrier-redundancy table

For every important fact in your chapter, fill out this table.
Keep it in your prep notes; do not put it in the published
chapter (the chapter expresses redundancy through the cast and
side events).

| Fact                  | Carrier 1 (person) | Carrier 2 (object)    | Carrier 3 (place/behavior) | Carrier 4 (consequence)    |
| --------------------- | ------------------ | --------------------- | -------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Mercer copies notes   | Keene              | survey pencil at body | Vane's tent visits         | Reed's pages out of order  |
| Pike pads ledgers     | Hannah Calder      | spoiled flour barrel  | Pike's late-night counting | town merchants' grievances |
| Crow warned the route | Shows-the-Ford     | a marked map          | LaRoche's repeated remarks | sudden Lakota knowledge    |

Three rows, three carriers each. That is the minimum density
for a chapter's central facts.

## Failure-resilient roll design

The three-clue rule is necessary but not sufficient. You also
need to design rolls that fail forward, not fail closed.

A failed roll must produce _information about the failure_,
not silence.

> Bad: _"On a failed Insight roll, you learn nothing."_
> Good: _"On a failed Insight roll, you sense Mercer is
> hiding something but misread what. Pick a wrong suspect."_

Failed-forward rolls keep the chapter moving. They also
generate the small mistakes that make replays of the chapter
feel different.

The four standard fail-forward modes:

1. **Wrong target.** The information is real but points to
   the wrong NPC.
2. **Delayed.** The information surfaces but later, when it
   is more costly to act on.
3. **Costly.** The information surfaces but the asking
   damaged the relationship.
4. **Partial.** Half the truth surfaces, with the wrong
   half.

Use all four modes across your chapter. Variety prevents the
"every failure is the same" feel.

## What never gates on a roll

Some information must be _roll-free_. The chapter cannot
function without it. Make these obvious to any player who
shows up.

- **The factions' existence.** Players must know who is
  fighting before they can choose sides. Faction existence
  belongs in the rumor table and the cold open.
- **The central pressure.** The "why this is happening"
  must be visible. The Northern Pacific wants the route. No
  roll required.
- **NPC stances.** A captain who treats the players as
  irregular tools must be obviously curt and dismissive in
  his first scene. No Insight check needed to know he is
  hard to please.
- **The rumored crimes.** What people in town _think_ is
  going on. Rumors are free. Confirmation costs.

Roll for _confirmation_, _detail_, and _secrets_. Not for
the existence of the situation.

## Dead-mouth reroute

When an NPC carrying a clue dies before delivering it, the GM
needs an immediate reroute. The chapter text must provide it.

Standard reroutes:

- **Body-search reroute.** The dead NPC's pockets contain a
  written or material version of what they would have said.
- **Witness reroute.** A second NPC, named or unnamed,
  observed enough of what the dead NPC knew to repeat the
  essentials.
- **Aftermath reroute.** The consequence of the dead NPC's
  knowledge becomes visible: the unmade payment, the
  unfollowed instruction, the unmet rendezvous.

Write at least one reroute for every named NPC who carries a
clue. Bury it in the "If the players freeze or break the
plan" section.

## Don't reveal too early either

Distributed truth is not the same as obvious truth. The
chapter still depends on the _order_ of revelation: the
players must follow a thread.

Useful pacing rule: in any single scene, deliver no more
than one major fact through clue, and no more than two
minor facts. If a scene reveals three or more, you are
front-loading.

The Yellowstone Line gives the players six independent
threads (Bell's killing, Keene's notes, Mercer's divisions,
Vane's deal-making, Pike's freight theft, Shows-the-Ford's
warning) but distributes them across nine scenes. No scene
hands them more than one major thread.

## The clue-discipline pass

When auditing a draft chapter, run this checklist:

1. **List the central facts.** Every conclusion the players
   must reach.
2. **For each fact, count the carriers.** Three minimum.
3. **For each carrier, name the failure mode.** What if this
   carrier is destroyed, missed, or unread?
4. **For each failure mode, name the reroute.** Where does
   the fact resurface?
5. **For each scene, count the major facts revealed.** No
   more than one.
6. **For each rolled gate, name the fail-forward outcome.**
   No silent failures.

If any line of this checklist is empty, the chapter is
fragile. Fill the gap before publishing.

## Why clue discipline is the most-skipped step

Designers skip it because it feels redundant. "Why write the
clue three times?"

Because every table is different. Because players miss things.
Because dice fail. Because someone gets a stomachache and
misses the session where the key NPC speaks. The redundancy
is not for the perfect playthrough; it is for the messy
ones, which are all of them.

A chapter with disciplined clue distribution is the chapter
that survives a TPK, a cancelled session, a player who
arrived late and missed the briefing. Those chapters are the
ones that get talked about ten years later.
