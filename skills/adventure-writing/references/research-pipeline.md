# The Research Pipeline

Research is not optional. An adventure without research
is a collection of aesthetics with no substance. Players
feel the difference. An adventure with research feels like
a real world — one that existed before the party arrived
and will persist after they leave.

This file covers the research process: what to find, in
what order, and when to stop. For how research appears in
the finished text, see `references/historical-grounding.md`.

## The six research questions

Answer these before drafting a single scene. They apply to
every setting — historical, fantasy, science fiction, or
invented. The questions are structural, not decorative.

1. **What is the political or power situation?** Who holds
   authority? Who is challenging it? Who has authority on
   paper but not in practice? What is the mechanism of
   control (military, economic, religious, cultural)?

2. **What are people fighting over economically?** Land,
   water, labor, trade routes, resources, licenses,
   monopolies. Money shapes behavior faster and more
   reliably than ideology in most adventure situations.

3. **What technology and communication reality exists?**
   How fast does news travel? What weapons exist and who
   can afford them? What can be done in hours vs. days
   vs. weeks? Communication lag is a story generator.

4. **Who is being displaced or exploited?** Every setting
   has a population whose labor, land, or body is being
   consumed by the power structure. Name them. They will
   have the most urgent stake in any adventure that
   touches the power structure.

5. **What does daily work look like for the people in
   this adventure?** Not the party — the NPCs. What does
   a rancher do at dawn? A telegraph operator at noon?
   A marshal on a quiet day? Work routines create
   authentic behavior.

6. **What happened here in the year or two before the
   adventure opens?** A drought, a mine collapse, a
   murder, a failed election, an arrival, a departure.
   The adventure is the next event in a sequence. Know
   the sequence.

## When to stop researching

When you can answer all six questions in two sentences
each, you have enough research to draft. More research
after this point delays the draft without improving the
adventure.

The test: sit down and write two sentences per question
from memory, without looking at your notes. If you can
do it, you are ready. If you can't answer one of them,
research that question and that question only.

## Source hierarchy

Use sources in this order, from highest to lowest
specificity:

1. **Primary sources**: period documents, diaries,
   newspapers, government records, letters. These give
   specific details — prices, names, places, phrases —
   that no secondary source can match. For historical
   adventures, primary sources are the highest-value
   research investment.

2. **Secondary historical works**: histories, academic
   studies, regional chronicles. These contextualise the
   primary data and point toward further primary sources.

3. **Setting bibles** (for published-world adventures):
   the canonical sourcebooks for the game's world. Treat
   these as primary sources within the fiction.

4. **Invented** (for wholly invented settings): when no
   prior source exists, you invent. But invention must
   still pass the six questions. An invented world has a
   power situation, an economic struggle, a technology
   ceiling, displaced people, daily work, and a recent
   history — even if you made them up.

Do not mix tiers without flagging it. A historical
adventure that invents a fictional murder is mixing tier
4 into tier 1. That is legitimate fiction, not historical
inaccuracy, but the author should know which facts are
grounded and which are invented.

## The research register

A working document, one page maximum, completed before
drafting. It answers the six questions, lists the three
to five most useful sources consulted, and notes any
factual gaps that require further research or deliberate
invention.

Keep the research register. It is not published but it
is used:

- During drafting, to keep invented details consistent.
- During the editing pass, to verify historical notes.
- During the anti-railroad pass, to check that the
  world's logic is coherent.

### Research register format

```
RESEARCH REGISTER
Adventure: [title]
Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]

1. Power situation (2 sentences):

2. Economic conflict (2 sentences):

3. Technology and communication (2 sentences):

4. Displaced or exploited population (2 sentences):

5. Daily work for key NPCs (2 sentences):

6. Recent history (2 sentences):

KEY SOURCES:
- [Source 1: title, author or archive, year, and what it
  was used for]
- [Source 2]
- [Source 3]

INVENTED FACTS (deliberate departures from the record):
- [Invented element 1 and why it was invented]
- [Invented element 2]

FACTUAL GAPS (questions still open):
- [Gap 1: what is unknown and whether it matters]
```

## Worldbuilding verification for non-historical settings

Fantasy and science fiction settings must pass the same
six questions. The power situation is the empire, the
guild, the ruling council. The economic conflict is the
spice trade, the mana wells, the FTL fuel monopoly. The
displaced population is the conquered culture, the
indentured colonists, the enslaved or converted.

An invented world that cannot answer the six questions
is not a world — it is a backdrop. Backdrops produce
shallow adventures.

If the setting bible does not answer a question, invent
an answer consistent with the established facts and file
it in the research register under "Invented Facts."

## Research anti-patterns

**Paralysis**: researching instead of drafting. Symptoms:
you have fifty browser tabs open, a notes document of
fifty pages, and you have not drafted a scene. Fix: run
the two-sentence test (see above). If you pass, close the
tabs and draft.

**Lecturing**: importing research directly into the
adventure text as exposition. A three-paragraph history
of the Rio Grande land grant system is not adventure
content — it is a museum placard. Fix: cut all exposition
to a single sentence of grounding context per scene.
Everything else goes into the GM's historical notes.

**Period purity**: refusing to draft a scene because you
haven't verified a specific detail (the exact fare for a
stagecoach in 1873, the precise weight of a cattle
contract). Fix: invent a specific and plausible detail,
flag it as "to verify," and continue drafting. Accuracy
passes happen after the structural draft is complete.

**Scope drift via research**: the research reveals an
adjacent story that seems more interesting than the one
you planned. Fix: file the new story as a sandbox exit
or a future adventure hook. Do not change the adventure
scope mid-research.

## For lean settings: minimum viable worldbuilding

When the setting bible is thin (a new system with little
published lore, an original setting, or a historical
period with sparse secondary sources), use this minimum
viable worldbuilding process:

1. Answer the six questions using the best available
   sources.
2. For each factual gap, make a single specific invention
   and record it.
3. Establish three to five proper nouns: place names,
   faction names, NPC surnames. Proper nouns make an
   invented world feel real faster than paragraphs of
   history.
4. Establish one economic fact (a price, a wage, a
   trade relationship) that the adventure will reference.
5. Establish one piece of technology or communication
   that will create pressure or opportunity in the
   adventure.

That's the minimum. It is enough. Draft.

## A note on AI-assisted research

Large language models can compress hours of background reading
into minutes. They can also confidently invent facts that sound
true. Use them with discipline.

**Acceptable use**:

- Generating a list of candidate primary sources to verify
  independently.
- Summarising a known body of period material when you already
  have a sense of the shape of the truth.
- Brainstorming proper nouns or period vocabulary that you then
  verify against actual sources.
- Cross-checking your draft for anachronisms after a human
  research pass.

**Not acceptable**:

- Citing facts in the adventure text that came only from a model.
  If the fact is not traceable to a primary or reputable secondary
  source, mark it as invented in the research register.
- Quoting a model as if it were a source.
- Skipping the six questions because the model produced a
  plausible-sounding background.

The model is a brainstorming and compression tool, not a source.
Facts in the adventure that are presented as period-accurate must
trace to actual sources cited in the research register.
