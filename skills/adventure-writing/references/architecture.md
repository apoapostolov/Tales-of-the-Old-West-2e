# Architecture — The Macro Shape of an Adventure Chapter

Every chapter that survives contact with players shares the same
spine. Sections may be renamed for genre, but the function of
each section is fixed. This file lists them in canonical order
and explains what each one does.

If you skip a section, you create a known failure mode. The
failure modes are listed against each section.

## 1. Cold Open (italicized vignette)

**Function:** Teach tone in one breath. Show the world's
specific weight before any plot, name, or briefing.

**Form:** Two to five sentences in italics, set apart as a
quote-block or epigraph. Sensory. Specific to time and place.
Names one image, one sound, one smell or texture. Closes on a
hard line that hints at the rot, the tension, or the cost.

**Rules:**

- No plot summary.
- No NPC names.
- No mechanical jargon.
- No "you" or "the players."
- One concrete object the reader can see.

**Failure mode if skipped:** Players arrive cold. The chapter
reads like a wiki entry. Tone has to be re-established by the
GM in real time.

## 2. Stakes Paragraph

**Function:** Name every faction and what it wants in one dense
paragraph. Tell the reader who is fighting for what before
introducing any one character.

**Form:** One paragraph, four to eight sentences. Each sentence
names a faction and its want in plain language. Closes by
placing the PCs as variables inside the conflict, not as
protagonists above it.

**Rules:**

- No metaphor here. Plain.
- Use parallel construction: "X wants Y. A wants B."
- Close on the PCs as small, contingent, replaceable.

**Failure mode if skipped:** GM cannot answer "what's at stake"
without flipping pages. The chapter has no center of gravity.

## 3. Historical / Situational Frame

**Function:** Anchor the chapter in a specific moment of the
world. For historical adventures, name the year and the real
forces. For fantasy or sci-fi, name the equivalent: the season,
the political moment, the technological pressure.

**Form:** Two or three short paragraphs. May include a
"Historical note" call-out (see `historical-grounding.md`).

**Rules:**

- Be specific. Name the year, the season, the political body.
- Distinguish the official mission from the actual stakes.
- Earn one piece of trivia that will pay off later.

**Failure mode if skipped:** The chapter feels generic. NPCs
have nothing to push against.

## 4. The Shape of It (Arc Summary)

**Function:** Tell the GM what the adventure feels like, in
order, in two or three paragraphs. Not a scene list — a shape.

**Form:** Three to six sentences naming the inflection points.
"They start as X. A killing reveals Y. Field work splits the
camp. A native warning offers a clean path while greed demands
another. Papers split. Men kill in camp. The reckoning happens
in town."

**Rules:**

- No section numbers, no scene names.
- Name what changes, not what happens.
- Close by naming what the chapter leaves on the table for the
  next chapter.

**Failure mode if skipped:** GM has to read the whole chapter
to grasp the shape. Prep cost is doubled.

## 5. Why a Character Joins (Hook Buffet)

**Function:** Give the GM at least one reason for each player
archetype to be present. Avoid the universal "you all meet in a
tavern."

**Form:** One paragraph stating that motives come mixed, then
2–4 sentences sketching specific hooks tied to NPCs and places
that the chapter will already use. Close by encouraging the GM
to give each PC one thread.

**Rules:**

- Hooks must reference NPCs from the cast, not generic types.
- At least one hook must be a debt or grudge, not a payday.
- At least one hook must be a tie that hurts to honor.

**Failure mode if skipped:** Players have no skin in the game.
Their first instinct is to walk away.

## 6. Core Cast

**Function:** Introduce every named NPC the GM will need. Each
entry uses the same template (see `references/npcs.md`).

**Form:** A list of NPC entries. Each entry is one paragraph of
4–7 sentences in fixed structure: physical, defining quality,
mannerism, speech, view of the PCs.

**Rules:**

- Maximum 12 named NPCs per chapter. More than that and the
  GM cannot hold them.
- No two NPCs share the same defining quality.
- Each NPC has a stance toward the PCs, not just a goal.
- Stat blocks live in a separate file. This is voice and
  function only.

**Failure mode if skipped:** NPCs blur together at the table.
The GM voices everyone the same.

## 7. Rumors the Party May Already Know

**Function:** Seed the chapter with truths and lies before play
begins, so the players bring informed paranoia to the cold open.

**Form:** A D10 (or D6, D20 — system-agnostic) table. Each
entry is one sentence, one rumor. Mix verifiable truths,
slanted half-truths, and outright lies in roughly 4:4:2 ratio.

**Rules:**

- Each rumor must be actionable: it suggests something to do
  or someone to watch.
- Reference NPCs by name.
- No rumor reveals the central twist; rumors prepare suspicion,
  not solutions.

**Failure mode if skipped:** Players walk in blank. Paranoia
takes three sessions to build instead of one rumor roll.

## 8. What Is Moving Here (Faction Recap)

**Function:** State the underlying conflict in plain prose
after the cast and rumors have introduced color. This is the
sober second pass at the stakes paragraph.

**Form:** One paragraph. Names every faction's pressure on the
situation. Closes by noting why the PCs sit at a useful vantage
("nobody thinks them important enough to lie to carefully yet").

**Failure mode if skipped:** GM forgets the through-line during
prep. Improvisation drifts.

## 9. Consequence Ledger

**Function:** Define the system-agnostic counters that record
player choices. See `references/factions-and-tracks.md` for the
deep treatment.

**Form:** A list of three or four named tracks, each starting at
zero. A short paragraph explains how each track behaves at high
and low scores. Followed by a bulleted list of how each faction
acts on the tracks.

**Rules:**

- Three or four tracks. Never two; never five or more.
- Each track names a _relationship_ or _resource_, not an
  abstract virtue.
- Tracks shift `+1` or `-1` per significant choice. No big
  numbers.

**Failure mode if skipped:** Player choices feel weightless.
The reckoning is GM fiat.

## 10. What the Main Factions Want

**Function:** A bulleted recap of each faction's want, method,
and pressure on the column. This is the reference card the GM
uses mid-session.

**Form:** A bullet list, one entry per faction. Each entry: name
the faction, name the want in one sentence, name the method or
pressure in one sentence. Close by noting what the faction
offers the PCs and what it costs them.

**Failure mode if skipped:** GM forgets which NPC works for
which faction at the table.

## 11. Clue Discipline

**Function:** Tell the GM that no single roll, NPC, or place is
load-bearing. List the major facts and the multiple carriers of
each. See `references/clue-discipline.md`.

**Form:** A short paragraph stating the principle. Then a
bulleted list pairing facts with their multiple sources.

**Failure mode if skipped:** A missed roll stalls the chapter.
GM has to invent on the fly under pressure.

## 12. Camp Scenes Between Major Events

**Function:** Provide downbeat material the GM can pull off the
shelf when the players want to live in the world rather than
push the plot. See `references/scene-design.md` for the form.

**Form:** A bulleted list of 8–14 short scenes. Each scene is
one sentence of setup followed by one sentence of what the PCs
gain by being there.

**Rules:**

- Each scene reveals character, not plot.
- Each scene gives the GM permission to slow down.
- At least one scene per major NPC.

**Failure mode if skipped:** The middle of the adventure is
all push. Players burn out. NPCs never feel real.

## 13. Side Events on the Line

**Function:** Provide pressure-injection material — small
incidents the GM drops between major beats to remind the
players that the world is moving without them.

**Form:** A bulleted list of 4–8 events. Each event is two or
three sentences: setup, what it costs, what it reveals.

**Rules:**

- Each event must add tension, not exposition.
- Each event has at least two ways to resolve.
- At least one event introduces a moral anchor (a witness, a
  victim, a marker on the ground).

**Failure mode if skipped:** The middle drags. GM has nothing
to throw when energy flags.

## 14. If the Players Freeze or Break the Plan (Robustness)

**Function:** Tell the GM how the chapter survives every
obvious form of player misbehavior. This is the section that
makes the adventure replayable.

**Form:** A bulleted list of "If X" branches. Each branch
states the disruption and reroutes the consequence. At minimum:

- if a key NPC dies in the first scene
- if the party openly sides with the antagonist
- if the party refuses the central hook
- if the party tries to flee the situation
- if the party brings overwhelming evidence early

**Rules:**

- Every branch keeps the consequences intact. None say "the
  GM should restart" or "this breaks the chapter."
- Each branch names what the new shape of the adventure is.

**Failure mode if skipped:** First TPK kills the chapter. GM
abandons published material and improvises poorly.

## 15. The Arcs (Numbered Scenes)

**Function:** The body of the chapter. Two or three named arcs,
each containing 4–8 numbered scenes. See
`references/scene-design.md` and `references/pacing-and-arcs.md`.

**Form:** Each arc opens with one or two sentences of arc
intent. Each scene follows the standard scene grammar.

**Failure mode if skipped:** No body. (Obvious.)

## 16. The Reckoning (Multi-Ending)

**Function:** Cash the consequence tracks. See
`references/endings-and-sandbox.md`.

**Form:** A short setup section ("the players arrive at X, the
official reading happens"). Then 4+ end-states keyed to track
totals. Each end-state is one paragraph.

**Failure mode if skipped:** Climax feels arbitrary. Player
choices retroactively meaningless.

## 17. Pacing the Expedition

**Function:** Tell the GM how long the chapter should breathe.
Where to compress. Where to slow.

**Form:** One paragraph. Names the rhythm: slow open, dragging
middle, hammer drop, paper-work close.

**Failure mode if skipped:** GM rushes the setup, slogs the
middle, hurries the climax. Standard.

## 18. Running the Major Combats

**Function:** Tell the GM what each combat is _for_, beyond
mechanics. Variety per fight: darkness, fire, mud, panic,
exposure.

**Form:** One short paragraph. One sentence per combat naming
its texture and its function in the arc.

**Failure mode if skipped:** All fights feel the same.

## 19. Reward, Reputation, and What the Party Carries Out

**Function:** Tell the GM what the party leaves the chapter
holding — cash, contacts, scars, debts, enemies.

**Form:** Two short paragraphs. First names the tangible
rewards. Second names the intangible: who hates them now, who
owes them, what wounds last.

**Failure mode if skipped:** No bridge to the next chapter.

## 20. Carrying the Chapter into the Sandbox

**Function:** State the open threads. What surviving NPCs do
next. What unresolved questions hang over the territory.

**Form:** Short paragraph or bullet list. Each thread is one
sentence.

**Failure mode if skipped:** Sandbox loses momentum. GM has to
generate the next adventure from scratch.

## 21. Running the Dilemmas (GM Tone Notes)

**Function:** Remind the GM that dice resolve cost, not choice.
Set the moral register. See `references/dilemmas.md`.

**Form:** Two or three short paragraphs. The voice here is
confident, terse, and direct to the GM.

**Failure mode if skipped:** GM treats every fork as a skill
check. Moral weight evaporates.

## 22. Travel, Pace, and Weather

**Function:** Tell the GM how the territory itself pushes back.
What dust does to weapons. What frost does to wounds. What
distance does to stock.

**Form:** One paragraph. Concrete details only.

**Failure mode if skipped:** The setting becomes scenery
instead of pressure.

## 23. Sandbox Exits

**Function:** Name the directions the chapter leaves open for
the next session. Towns, factions, and unresolved enemies that
can pull the party.

**Form:** A short bulleted list, one direction per line.

**Failure mode if skipped:** Players ask "what now?" and the
GM has nothing.

## Section ordering and naming

The canonical order above is the order players and GMs read
naturally: tone → stakes → frame → cast → seeds → mechanics →
body → close. You may rename sections to match your genre
("The Yellowstone Line" / "The Shape of It" / "Reckoning"
versus "Setup" / "Conflict" / "Resolution"). You may not
reorder them. The cold open before the briefing before the
cast before the rumors is fixed because each section primes
the next.
