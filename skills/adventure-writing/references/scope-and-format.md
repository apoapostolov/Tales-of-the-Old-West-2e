# Scope and Format

Before the first scene is drafted, two decisions are made and
written down: what kind of adventure this is, and what the
word and session targets are. Adventures that skip this step
expand indefinitely or collapse under their own thinness.

## The three formats

### One-shot

A single self-contained session. The arc opens and closes
within that session. There is no consequence ledger spanning
forward; all threads are resolvable tonight.

Characteristics:

- **Session target**: 1 (plan for 3-4 hours at the table).
- **Word count target**: 3,000-6,000 words.
- **Cast**: 4-6 NPCs maximum. More than six and the GM cannot
  hold them; players will collapse the ensemble.
- **Factions**: one or two. Three factions is the upper limit
  if two can be resolved quickly.
- **Consequence tracks**: optional. A single pressure track
  is sufficient; do not build a multi-faction ledger.
- **Dilemma**: one central dilemma. No arc subdivisions.
- **Endings**: two to three end-states. Not four axes.
- **What carries forward**: nothing. A one-shot has optional
  sandbox exits, but the adventure works entirely without them.

The one-shot's greatest structural risk is scope creep: you
add a second faction, a third NPC with their own subplot, a
consequence that requires a second session to land. Cut to the
minimum viable adventure before drafting.

### Campaign chapter

A single chapter within a larger adventure arc. It feeds
forward (sets up future chapters) and backward (pays off
threads from earlier chapters). It does not fully resolve:
it reaches a reckoning, not a conclusion.

Characteristics:

- **Session target**: 2-4 sessions.
- **Word count target**: 6,000-12,000 words.
- **Cast**: 6-12 named NPCs.
- **Factions**: 2-3 active factions. At most two new factions
  introduced; existing factions continue.
- **Consequence tracks**: a full faction/track ledger. Carries
  forward.
- **Open threads**: the chapter closes some threads and opens
  others. At least one major thread is unresolved.

### Mini-campaign (2-4 chapter arc)

A bridging structure: a self-contained arc that can be run
as a standalone but is longer than a one-shot.

Characteristics:

- **Session target**: 3-8 sessions.
- **Word count target**: 12,000-24,000 words.
- **Chapter handoff**: each chapter opens with a short "where
  we are" block listing: active NPCs, open consequence tracks,
  and one unresolved dilemma from the previous chapter.
- **Factions**: carry through all chapters. Introduce no new
  major factions after Chapter 1.

## Format decisions

These decisions are made during scope-setting, not during
drafting.

### Pre-generated characters

Use pre-gens when:

- The adventure is a one-shot being published for convention
  play.
- The adventure is an introduction to the system and the cast
  of characters is narratively significant from minute one.

Do not use pre-gens when:

- The adventure is a campaign chapter inserted into an ongoing
  game with established characters.
- The adventure's tension depends on player investment in
  characters they have built.

If pre-gens are included, they are a separate document, not
embedded in the adventure body.

### Handouts

A handout earns its place when it carries information that is
more viscerally effective as a physical or visual object than
as read-aloud text. A forged land deed, a telegram with
redacted lines, a wanted poster.

A handout does not earn its place when it duplicates prose
that could be delivered as read-aloud, or when it requires
art the author cannot produce.

- Budget: 0-3 handouts per one-shot, 1-5 per campaign chapter.
- Each handout is listed in the chapter's materials section
  before the text begins.

### Maps

A map earns its place when spatial navigation is a meaningful
part of play: the party must make decisions about where to go,
the position of NPCs relative to each other matters, or the
location is explored in a keyed entry format.

A map does not earn its place in talk-heavy investigation
adventures where location is incidental.

If a map is included:

- It is functional, not decorative.
- It includes only locations referenced in the text.
- It has a scale or travel-time notation.
- It is referenced at the point in the text where it is first
  needed.

## Session length calibration

The number of scenes a session can hold depends on session
length and scene density.

| Session type     | Duration  | Scenes (light) | Scenes (dense) |
| ---------------- | --------- | -------------- | -------------- |
| Short session    | 2-3 hours | 3-4            | 2-3            |
| Standard session | 3-4 hours | 5-7            | 4-5            |
| Full-day session | 6-8 hours | 10-14          | 7-10           |

"Light scenes" are conversations, travel, and discovery.
"Dense scenes" are social encounters under pressure, combat
set-pieces, and dilemmas.

Plan for standard session density. If a scene list exceeds
the session target by more than 20%, cut or compress before
drafting.

## The minimum viable adventure

The smallest design that can function at the table without
collapsing. Build this before elaborating.

- **One pressure**: the situation will get worse if the party
  does nothing.
- **Two factions**: at least one has conflicting interests with
  the party.
- **Six NPCs**: each with the five-sentence template.
- **One dilemma**: a no-clean-option moment where both paths
  cost something.
- **Two endings**: at least one of which is bad for someone
  the party cares about.
- **Three clues per critical fact**: see `references/clue-discipline.md`.

If the adventure cannot be described in these terms, it is not
yet a design — it is a setting vignette. Return to the
structure before drafting.

## Scope failure modes

**Scope creep**: the adventure grows to cover every possible
contingency. New factions are added during drafting. NPCs
multiply. The consequence track spawns sub-tracks. Signs:
the word count has doubled from the target without adding
player-facing content; the author cannot summarize the
adventure in two sentences.

Fix: cut to the minimum viable adventure and rebuild outward
only if the scene you're cutting was load-bearing.

**Scope poverty**: the adventure is kept artificially small to
avoid over-writing. Dilemmas are skipped. NPCs have no depth.
Endings are all good or all bad. Signs: the word count is 30%
below target; the adventure cannot survive a player making an
unexpected choice.

Fix: add robustness, not length. A robust adventure is not
longer — it has more decision surfaces within the same word
count.

## Pre-draft scope checklist

Answer all six before moving to the research pipeline:

1. Is this a one-shot, campaign chapter, or mini-campaign?
2. What is the session count target?
3. What is the word count target?
4. How many NPCs will be in the cast (name the limit)?
5. How many endings will the adventure support (minimum two)?
6. Are pre-gens, handouts, or a map included, and why?

When all six are answered, move to `references/research-pipeline.md`.

## Convention play vs. home-table play

The scope decisions above assume a home table with a known group.
Convention play imposes additional constraints.

**Convention one-shots**:

- Hard time cap (typically 3 or 4 hours including character
  introductions and rules questions). Plan for 70% of the slot
  on actual play; the rest goes to setup, rules clarification,
  and the wrap.
- Pre-gens are mandatory, not optional. Each pre-gen has a
  one-paragraph hook into the central pressure so any player can
  pick any character and have a stake.
- The dilemma must arrive by the midpoint of the slot. A
  convention adventure that hides its dilemma until the third
  hour will not deliver the dilemma at all.
- Endings are pre-rolled: the GM should be able to call any
  ending at any point in the final 20 minutes if time runs
  short. Mark the "early-cut" ending in the text.
- Safety tools are surfaced explicitly at the start of the slot.
  See the safety section in SKILL.md.

**Home-table chapters**:

- Soft time bounds. A scene that runs long because the players
  are engaged is a feature, not a failure.
- Established characters with established relationships. Hooks
  can be more specific ("this is about Ana's brother") and less
  generic.
- The session can end on a cliffhanger and resume next week.
  Convention adventures cannot.

If an adventure is being written for both contexts, design for
convention first and add the home-table extensions in a separate
"For longer play" appendix. The reverse is much harder.

## Content declaration

At the top of every adventure, before the cold open, list:

- Content types present (violence, body horror, threat to children,
  references to specific historical atrocities, etc.).
- The recommended safety calibration (session zero, X-card, etc.).
- Any pre-game player conversation the GM should have.

This is not optional for adventures with difficult content. See
the Safety section in SKILL.md and `references/horror-design.md`
for horror-specific calibration.
