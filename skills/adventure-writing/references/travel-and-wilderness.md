# Travel and Wilderness

A journey is not dead time between scenes. The road
has its own facts: weather, terrain, the political
meaning of a landscape, the people who live at the
edges of maps. An adventure that compresses all travel
into "you ride three days and arrive" is missing a
third of its world.

## Travel as story

What the journey does that scenes inside buildings
cannot:

- Establishes the scale and texture of the world
  (how large is the territory? what does it look
  like to cross it?).
- Introduces NPCs who don't live in towns (homesteaders,
  prospectors, dispossessed people, travelers with
  information).
- Delivers consequences of earlier scenes through
  what the party finds on the road (a burned farm
  the antagonist warned about, a body, a column of
  troops moving in a direction that means something).
- Creates decisions about time, route, and risk.

A journey without any of these elements is logistics.
A journey with at least one is story.

## The journey beat

A journey beat is a specific moment within a travel
stretch that carries weight. It is not a random
encounter — it is a designed scene in a travel context.

Journey beats include:

- A body on the road.
- A rider coming fast from the direction you're
  headed.
- A farmstead that should have smoke from the
  chimney and doesn't.
- A terrain feature the party must decide whether
  to cross or go around.
- A meeting with an NPC traveling the opposite
  direction.
- A night of weather that creates cover, cost, or
  danger.
- Evidence of the antagonist's recent activity
  (tracks, a sign torn down, cattle driven into the
  wrong valley).

Plan one journey beat per day of travel for important
journeys. The beat is not required to be violent or
even eventful — it must carry information or demand
a decision.

Store journey beats in a shelf, like camp scenes:
a short list of designed moments the GM draws from
when the travel segment calls for a beat.

## Three travel structures

### Linear travel

The party moves from A to B by a known route.
The journey is not spatially open; the party is
on a road, a river, a marked trail.

Design: plan journey beats equal to the number of
days of travel. Draw from the beat shelf. The beats
are not random — the GM places them at dramatically
useful moments.

Linear travel suits campaign chapters where
the destination is the adventure, not the journey.

### Pointcrawl

Named locations connected by labeled paths. Each
path has a distance (in days or travel beats) and
a travel hazard or condition. Players navigate by
choosing which path to take at each node.

Design format per path:

```
ROUTE: Raton Pass (Cimarron → Trinidad)
Distance: 2 days
Condition: High altitude, unpredictable weather in
autumn. Spring snowmelt makes this route impassable
March–April.
Hazard: Narrow defile at the summit — a single
ambush point (Entry: Ambush at the Defile).
```

The pointcrawl produces geographic player agency:
the party is choosing routes, not just proceeding.
Different routes have different costs and different
content.

Pointcrawl suits adventures where geography is
a meaningful variable and where the party has
real choices about how to travel.

### Hexcrawl

A spatial grid where each hex contains terrain type
and optionally a keyed location or encounter. Players
navigate by direction, not by named destination.

Design: create a hex map. Define each terrain type
(plains, mountains, forest, river crossing, etc.) with
travel cost (days per hex), terrain effects, and
natural encounter probability. Key specific hexes
with locations.

Hexcrawl suits wilderness exploration adventures
where discovery is a primary engagement — the party
is mapping the unknown, not traveling to a known
destination.

## Landmark-based navigation

For adventures without a hex map or pointcrawl
diagram, use named landmarks as waypoints.

The party moves from landmark to landmark. Each
landmark has a short entry (see
`references/location-design.md` for entry format,
abbreviated for travel). The GM describes travel
time between landmarks and places journey beats
in the intervals.

Landmark names should be geographically descriptive:
"The Double Fork of the Canadian River." "The Black
Mesa with the crooked stone." "The dry well camp."
Functional names orient faster than evocative ones.

This system navigates without a map and works well
for published adventures where a full hex diagram
is not practical.

## The encounter table

A system-agnostic encounter table is a pacing tool,
not a random damage delivery mechanism.

Table construction principles:

- Include at least three non-hostile entries for
  every hostile one.
- Mix: hostile encounter / neutral NPC / environmental
  hazard / discovery / clue delivery / faction event.
- Each entry is a situation, not just a creature or
  threat. "Three riders from the Holt ranch, moving
  fast in the direction of the Reyes place, carrying
  something wrapped in canvas" is a situation. "Coyotes"
  is not.
- Each entry should have a different action-demand
  from the others: one requires combat or retreat,
  one requires social engagement, one requires
  navigation or terrain response, one delivers
  information.

Design format:

```
TRAVEL ENCOUNTERS: Cimarron River country

1-2: Dust rising to the south. (What are they going
     to find when they investigate? A Holt cattle
     drive pushing into Reyes territory. A decision.)
3: A homesteader family on foot, carrying what they
   could. Their claim burned this morning.
4: A dry creek bed with no water where the map
   indicates a ford. Someone dammed it upstream.
5: Three Ute riders at a distance, watching the
   party cross the valley. No hostile action. An
   exchange is possible if approached respectfully.
6: Weather changes fast. A cold front moving in
   from the mountains. Make camp early or push
   through for time at a cost.
```

## Journey compression

For long journeys that don't warrant beat-by-beat
coverage, use the montage paragraph: two to four
sentences of sensory travel prose that establish
the journey's character, followed by one consequence.

> The trail up from Cimarron stays dry and good
> for the first day. By the second, the altitude
> starts in on the horses. On the third evening
> you camp in a high meadow with a view of the
> Rockies filling the whole western sky. By the
> time you see Trinidad's smelter smoke, Lorca's
> wound has gone bad.

The consequence — Lorca's wound — is the cost of
the journey. It doesn't need to be dramatic.
It needs to be real.

See `references/pacing-and-arcs.md` for montage
and compression within the three-act arc.

## Terrain as encounter design

Terrain shapes tactics, costs, and opportunities in
travel. For each major travel zone, list three to
five terrain effects:

```
TERRAIN: High mountain trail (9,000+ feet)
- Weather: afternoon thunderstorms, June-August.
  Roll for cover when it hits.
- Footing: loose shale on south-facing slopes.
  Horse movement halved; ambush on high ground
  is advantageous.
- Supply: no grazing above the tree line. Carry
  all feed.
- Navigation: no landmarks above cloud cover.
  Travel blind in fog.
- Opportunity: line of sight for twenty miles on
  a clear day. The party can see who is below them.
```

Terrain effects are not punishments. They are the
world asserting its nature. A party that accounts
for terrain is better prepared. A party that ignores
terrain finds out what it costs.

For terrain effects in combat specifically, see
`references/combat-encounters.md`.

## Weather as pacing

Weather changes pace, cost, and mood. It is one
of the most cost-efficient scene tools available
because it affects everything at once.

Build a simple weather system for each chapter's
travel season. Six outcomes is sufficient:

```
WEATHER: Eastern New Mexico, October
1-2: Clear and cold, hard frost overnight.
     Good travel; horses well; stars visible.
3-4: Wind from the north. Dust and cold.
     Half movement. Tracks blow over in an hour.
5: Overcast, temperatures dropping. Move before
   it worsens.
6: Storm. Rain or early snow. No travel. Camp
   or find shelter. One supply unit consumed.
```

Roll or decide once per travel day. The weather is
not random punishment — it is atmosphere with
mechanical teeth.

## Navigation failure

Getting lost is a story generator, not a punishment.

If the party makes a navigation error — misreads
the terrain, follows a dry creek in the wrong
direction, trusts a map that's twenty years old —
they arrive somewhere they didn't intend.

That somewhere has content.

Write two to three unscheduled locations in the
adventure's geographic area: places the party might
reach by accident, each with at least one story
element (a discovery, a junction, a person, evidence
of recent activity).

A party that gets lost and finds something is better
off than a party that never gets lost and finds
exactly what they planned.

## Resupply and logistics

A travel-heavy adventure must address resupply explicitly
or the question will dominate play unproductively. The
players will track ammunition, food, water, horse feed,
fuel, medicine. Either the adventure makes resupply easy
and assumes it offstage, or it makes resupply a mechanical
element of pacing.

**Offstage resupply** (default for short adventures):

- The party returns to a town or trading post between major
  travel segments and resupplies abstractly.
- The author notes one line in the GM section: "Assume
  resupply at any settled location. Ammunition and food
  are not tracked by individual unit."

**Onstage resupply** (for survival or hardship adventures):

- Resources are tracked by named unit (rations, rounds,
  doses, water-skins).
- Each travel day consumes a stated number of units.
- Resupply requires a specific scene at a specific location
  — a settlement, a cache, a generous homesteader, a fallen
  enemy's gear.
- The depletion of resources is itself a clock; see
  `references/clocks-and-timers.md`.

A travel adventure that splits the difference — sometimes
tracking, sometimes not — will produce arguments at the
table. Pick a discipline and state it.

## NPC travel companions

If the party has NPC companions on the road (a guide, a
witness being escorted, a wounded survivor, an unwanted
tag-along), each one is a constraint and an opportunity.

For each travelling NPC, document:

- **Pace constraint**: do they slow the party? By how
  much? (A wounded NPC moves at half pace; a child can
  walk five hours a day, not ten.)
- **Resource cost**: do they consume an extra supply
  unit per day?
- **Conversation surface**: what do they reveal about
  themselves over the days of travel? Plan two or three
  beats per travel companion — one early conversation, one
  midpoint disclosure, one late surprise.
- **Vulnerability**: what happens to them if the party is
  ambushed, lost, or runs out of resources?

A travelling NPC without these four documented becomes a
drag on play — the GM has to invent them on the fly during
every travel segment.

## Mounted vs. unmounted travel

For period and historical adventures where the difference
matters, document the assumption. A mounted party covers
30–40 miles a day in fair conditions on good ground; an
unmounted party covers 15–20. A party on horseback can
flee or pursue most threats; an unmounted party cannot.

If the adventure assumes the party is mounted, make it
explicit in the scope block. If it assumes unmounted travel,
budget the slower distances into the journey beats and the
clock advances. Mixed parties (one rider, three on foot)
move at the slowest pace — plan accordingly.

For non-historical adventures with equivalents (vehicles,
mounts, magical movement), the same discipline applies.
State the assumed mode of travel; budget time and
resources to match.
