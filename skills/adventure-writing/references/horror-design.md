# Horror Design

Horror requires investment before it requires threat.
The player must care about the character — or the world,
or the people in it — before the threat arrives. Horror
without investment is spectacle. Spectacle does not
unsettle. It distracts.

## The three modes of horror

### Gothic horror

The horror is old, personal, and architectural. The
house is wrong. The family is cursed. The bloodline
carries something that does not end. The threat is
intimate: it knows the characters because it has always
known them, or because it lives where they live.

Characteristics:

- The setting is a character: the estate, the mine,
  the ship, the institution. It has history.
- The horror is entangled with the players' characters
  or the NPC cast. It is not arriving from outside —
  it was already here.
- Knowledge reveals complicity. The truth is not
  comforting. It implicates someone the players trust.
- The ending is frequently not triumphant. The gothic
  resolution is survival, departure, or partial
  containment — rarely destruction.

Design notes for gothic: lean on location design.
The estate or mine is the adventure's primary
character. See `references/location-design.md`.

### Cosmic horror

The horror is inhuman, vast, and indifferent. It does
not hate. It does not notice. Knowledge is the wound:
to learn what is true about the universe is to be
damaged by the knowing.

Characteristics:

- The threat cannot be defeated, only avoided or
  delayed.
- The investigation structure drives the adventure.
  Every clue is also a cost. See
  `references/investigation-structure.md`.
- The NPCs who know the truth are broken by it. The
  players' characters will be, too.
- Human institutions (law, the church, medicine)
  are useless or actively suppressing the truth.
- The ending options are: survive and know, survive
  and don't know, don't survive, or contain it
  temporarily at great cost.

Design notes for cosmic: the investigation structure
is mandatory. The perpetrator is not a person — it
is a situation, an entity, or a process. The "crime"
is the contact between the human world and the
inhuman one.

### Survival horror

The horror is immediate, mortal, and material. The
threat can be killed — or at least driven back —
but not easily. Resources are limited. Safety is
temporary. Every decision has a cost in blood or
material.

Characteristics:

- The players' characters are vulnerable. Their
  resources (ammunition, medicine, light sources,
  allies) deplete and do not reliably replenish.
- The threat is physically present and active. It
  hunts. It responds to noise, light, or movement.
- The pacing is faster than gothic or cosmic. There
  is less contemplation and more decision under
  pressure.
- The locations are constrained: the players cannot
  simply leave. They are trapped, surrounded, or
  dependent on something at the site.

Design notes for survival: treat the resource economy
as a design element. Name the resources that matter,
their starting amounts, their depletion triggers, and
their replenishment conditions (or the absence of
replenishment). The depletion of resources is the
adventure's clock.

## The unseen principle

Fear is maximized when the threat is implied, not shown.

The adventure supplies:

- Evidence of the threat's passage (a trail, an absence,
  a sound, a smell, a behavioral change in NPCs).
- Aftermath (what the threat did, not what it looks
  like while it does it).
- The reactions of other characters.

The adventure does not supply:

- A complete description of the threat's appearance.
- An explanation of how the threat works.
- A name for the threat early in the adventure.

The players' imaginations construct the monster from
the evidence. The constructed monster is always more
disturbing than the described one.

**Revealing the monster too early is the most common
horror design error.** Once the monster is described
completely, the players' imaginations stop constructing.
The threat becomes a problem to solve, not a source
of dread.

## The reveal economy

A horror adventure has a limited number of effective
reveals. Each major reveal costs some of the accumulated
dread. Budget them.

Rule: front-load dread; back-load reveals.

Arc I supplies evidence and implication. The players
know something is wrong. They don't know what.

Arc II supplies escalation and partial revelation.
The shape of the threat becomes visible. The players
know more, and knowing more is worse.

Arc III supplies the full reveal. By this point the
dread is so high that the reveal amplifies rather
than releases it.

A horror adventure that reveals too much too early
exhausts its dread budget. The players spend the rest
of the adventure managing a known quantity rather
than dreading an unknown one.

## Tension architecture

Dread is not a flat plateau. An adventure at constant
maximum tension produces numbness, not dread.

Build a tension arc that rises and falls:

- A period of apparent safety after the initial
  disturbance — this safety is false, but its
  falseness is not yet visible.
- A shock or escalation that breaks the safety.
- A second apparent safety, shorter and less
  convincing than the first.
- The final escalation.

The "apparent safety" periods are not wasted time —
they are the mechanism that makes the next horror
beat land harder. A camp scene before the monster
returns is more valuable than a continuous-threat
scene of the same length.

## Cost mechanics (system-agnostic)

Horror adventures impose costs for proximity to the
unnatural. These costs must be _felt_ in the fiction,
not only tracked on a sheet.

When a character takes a sanity hit, corruption
point, or emotional wound, the GM describes what that
looks like in the world:

> Vickers stares at his hands for a moment longer
> than necessary. When he finally speaks, the words
> are correct but slightly too flat.

Write the fiction of the cost in the GM notes for
each major horror encounter. What does a damaged
character look like? How does the damage manifest
in behavior? What does it cost the other players
to watch?

The fiction of the cost is the horror. The number
on the sheet is a record-keeping tool.

## The human antagonist in horror

The most frightening horror adventures layer a human
threat beneath the supernatural one.

A cultist with a recognizable motive — protecting
their family, preserving their town's prosperity,
keeping a promise made in grief — is more disturbing
than a faceless monster because the players understand
how someone becomes that. The path from human to
collaborator is visible and familiar.

The human antagonist in a horror adventure is not
the final threat. They are the bridge between the
players' world and the inhuman one. Designing them
with real motivation makes the horror more proximate.
See `references/antagonist-design.md` for deep
antagonist construction.

## Dread versus shock

Dread accumulates slowly. It is the product of
evidence, implication, and the players' anticipation
of something they cannot name yet.

Shock is momentary. A sudden revelation, a discovery
of violence, an NPC death. Shock amplifies dread but
cannot replace it.

An adventure built on shocks with no dread becomes
a series of unpleasant surprises. It is exhausting
rather than frightening. The players brace for the
next shock instead of living in the uncertainty.

Build dread. Let shocks serve the dread.

## Horror pacing

Horror pacing is slower than action pacing.

More silence. More time spent with NPCs who will suffer.
More description of the mundane before the rupture.
More room between beats.

The breath scenes in `references/pacing-and-arcs.md`
are longer in horror than in action. The camp scene
is not just a rest beat — it is a sustained period
of false safety that the players invest in.

A horror adventure that matches the pacing of an
action adventure has made a category error.

## The safety toolkit

Horror content must be introduced carefully.

Before running a horror adventure:

- List the specific types of content in the adventure
  (body horror, psychological horror, violence against
  specific demographics, etc.) in the GM notes.
- Recommend session zero calibration: the GM asks
  the players which content types they are willing
  to encounter.
- Note the X-card or equivalent safety tool and
  confirm all players know how to use it.

This is not optional in horror. It is a structural
responsibility of the adventure author.

## Horror read-aloud

Horror read-aloud is tighter than action read-aloud.

- Shorter sentences.
- More specific sensory details.
- Longer silence in the room after the read-aloud
  ends. Don't rush to the next move.
- No mood-naming. "Dread hung over the room" is
  comedy in a horror context. Name the sensory fact
  that produces the dread, not the dread itself.

See `references/read-aloud.md`. All of its anti-
patterns apply with greater force in horror. The
mood-naming anti-pattern is fatal in horror, not
merely weak.

## Anti-patterns

**Jump scares in text form**: a sudden reveal in
prose. Ineffective because the effect depends on
timing, and the GM cannot reliably reproduce the
author's timing. Design dread instead.

**Gore as substitute for dread**: detailed violence
without implication. Gore produces disgust, not fear.
Reserve visceral detail for moments where it serves
the horror's emotional logic, not for spectacle.

**Revealing the monster too early**: see The Unseen
Principle above. The single most common error.

**The clean solution**: giving the players a weapon,
a spell, or an action that cleanly and satisfyingly
destroys the threat undoes all accumulated dread.
Horror resolution is survival, not victory. The
players escape, contain, or sacrifice — rarely
triumph.

**Punishing curiosity**: players who investigate
horror are doing the right thing. If investigation
always leads to immediate harm, players will stop
investigating. Dread is earned by discovering things
that are wrong. Harm comes from engaging the wrong
directly, not from discovering it.

## The horror session zero

Horror is the one genre where session zero is structurally
required by the adventure, not optional based on group
preference. Treat it as part of the design.

The horror session zero (10–20 minutes before play, or in a
separate brief session):

1. **Declare the content**. Read aloud the adventure's content
   list (body horror, intimate threat, harm to specific
   character types, real historical horrors referenced, etc.).
2. **Calibrate**. Ask each player which content they are
   willing to encounter, which they want kept off-page, and
   which is hard-no. Do not require justification for any
   answer.
3. **Confirm the safety tool**. Confirm whichever tool the
   table will use (X-card, Lines and Veils, Script Change,
   Open Door). Demonstrate use. Confirm everyone can invoke
   it without explanation.
4. **Set the contract**. The author/GM commits to staying
   inside the calibrated content. The players commit to
   playing in good faith and using the tool when needed.

This is not therapy. It is design. Horror that lands does
so because the table has agreed in advance to be moved.
Without that agreement, dread becomes discomfort or
refusal, and the adventure misfires.

## The dread budget worksheet

When drafting a horror adventure, sketch the dread budget
before writing scenes:

```
DREAD BUDGET WORKSHEET

Arc I beats:
- [Beat 1: evidence type, sense engaged, what is implied]
- [Beat 2: ...]
- [Beat 3: ...]
Dread accumulated by end of Arc I: [low / medium / high]
Reveals spent: 0 (no major reveal in Arc I)

Arc II beats:
- [Beat 1: ...]
- [Beat 2: ...]
Dread accumulated by mid-Arc II: [should be high]
Reveals spent: 1 partial (the shape of the threat is
  visible, not its full nature)

Arc III beats:
- [Confrontation / final reveal: what remains hidden, what
  is finally seen]
Reveals spent: 2-3 (full reveal, optional secondary reveals)
```

The worksheet exposes whether reveals are front-loaded
(common author error) or back-loaded (the goal). It also
surfaces dead arcs — segments with no dread accumulation
or no escalation — before they reach the table.
