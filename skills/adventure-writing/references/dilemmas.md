# Dilemmas — Choice Without Clean Answers

A dilemma is the unit of moral weight in an adventure. Every
chapter needs at least three. They are not puzzles. They are
not skill checks. They are choices the players make as
themselves, where every road costs something they value, and
where the GM does not know in advance which road they will
take.

This file defines the no-clean-option principle, the design
templates for dilemmas, and the rules for keeping them off the
dice.

## The no-clean-option principle

> **A dilemma in which one option is clearly correct is not a
> dilemma. It is a test of attention.**

A dilemma forces the players to choose between mutually
exclusive goods, or between costs they do not want to pay.

Bad dilemma: _"Do you save the wounded soldier or shoot the
fleeing thief?"_ — Most parties save the wounded soldier.
There is no genuine tension.

Good dilemma: _"The wounded soldier and the fleeing thief
are connected. The thief is carrying the only document that
would clear the wounded soldier's family of the killing
they're about to be hanged for. The soldier dies if you
chase the thief. The thief escapes if you save the soldier.
You cannot do both."_

The good dilemma forces the players to choose what they
care about more, and to live with the cost.

## The four shapes of dilemma

Almost every good dilemma fits one of four shapes.

### 1. Mutually exclusive goods

Two outcomes the players want, only one achievable. The
classic form.

> _Save the survey wagon (which has the route notes) or save
> Reed (whose memory holds the same notes), but not both?_

### 2. The poisoned alliance

The party can advance their goal, but only by aligning with
someone they distrust, hate, or whose victory will hurt
others later.

> _Vane offers cash and protection in exchange for one
> warning when the Army moves. The cash is real. So is the
> long-term cost: every wagon Vane saves is a wagon that
> crushes a smaller business in Bozeman next spring._

### 3. The known sacrifice

The party can win, but only at a cost they have to choose to
pay. Often a named NPC, a relationship, a resource, a
principle.

> _Hannah Calder will testify to Pike's fraud. The testimony
> will succeed. The retaliation will burn her ranch within
> the month. Do you bring her in?_

### 4. The closed door

The party must accept a defeat, choosing which version. Lose
the paper and save the people; lose the people and save the
paper; or split and lose both partly.

> _The column is breaking. Reed wants the books. Rowe wants
> the wounded. Vane will buy the map. Pike will dump the
> liability. You have one wagon and four loads. Choose._

## Designing a dilemma

Use this five-step template.

### 1. Name the values in tension

Two or three values the party has expressed caring about.
Justice. Survival. Loyalty to an NPC. Loyalty to a faction.
Money. Their own reputation. The greater good.

### 2. Name the choice

State the choice in one sentence. The sentence must contain
the word "or" and must name two concrete options.

> _Surrender Keene to Rowe, or hide him from the Army?_

### 3. Name the cost of each option

For each option, name what the party loses by choosing it.
Be specific: which NPC's trust, which faction's standing,
which downstream opportunity.

> _Surrender him: Vane's network shifts to deniable hands.
> The Army knows Keene's testimony, but the network closes
> ranks._
>
> _Hide him: The Army loses the witness. The party gains a
> dependent. Mercer plots betrayal once he realizes Keene
> is alive._

### 4. Name the track shifts

Make the cost mechanical:

> _Surrender: `Army Respect +1`, `Survey Leverage -1`._
>
> _Hide: `Survey Leverage +1`, `Army Respect -1`._

### 5. Name the residue

What does the party carry forward regardless of choice?
Often the dilemma itself: the dead NPC, the ruined
opportunity, the named enemy.

## Keeping dilemmas off the dice

A dilemma is not a roll. The roll resolves the cost or the
execution; the choice is the player's.

### Rolls before the choice

Sometimes a roll surfaces information that changes the
shape of the dilemma. _"On a successful Insight, you realize
Hannah's testimony will not just risk her ranch — it will
get her killed."_ Now the choice is heavier.

### Rolls after the choice

A roll can resolve whether the choice was _executed cleanly_.
_"You decide to hide Keene. Make a Stealth check. On a
failure, Harlan saw you take him into the brush."_ The
choice stands; the consequences sharpen.

### No roll determines the choice

The choice itself is never rolled. No "Persuasion check to
decide what the party does." The dilemma belongs to the
players, not the dice.

## When to plant dilemmas

One per arc, three per chapter. The placements:

- **Arc I dilemma:** small-stakes, locks the party in. Often
  a question of _complicity_. Do they take the contract,
  knowing what they suspect?
- **Arc II dilemma:** mid-stakes, defines the party's
  alliances. Often a question of _betrayal_. Do they side
  with the legitimate authority or the underground interest?
- **Arc III dilemma:** high-stakes, defines the ending.
  Often a question of _cost_. What do they save? Whom do
  they sacrifice?

The Arc I dilemma seeds the conditions for the Arc III
dilemma. Be deliberate: the Keene-handling choice in Arc I
sets up the testimony-handling choice in Arc III.

## Dilemma craft notes

### Keep the bad option seductive

If one option is obviously bad and the other obviously good,
it is not a dilemma. The bad option must offer something
real. Money. Safety. A shorter road home.

Vane's offers of cash for warnings are written carefully:
the money is clean and the warnings are small. The seduction
is the point.

### Keep the good option costly

If one option is obviously cheap and the other obviously
expensive, it is also not a dilemma. The good option must
cost something the party values.

Helping Hannah is "good" — but it forces the party to take a
public stand, mark themselves as her allies, and inherit her
enemies.

### Avoid sermon dilemmas

A sermon dilemma is one where the chapter clearly endorses
one choice. The text condemns the bad option, hides costs
from the good option, or stacks the rewards in favor of one
side.

> Bad: _"If the players take Vane's money, they show their
> true colors as corrupt. Reduce all NPC trust by 2."_
>
> Good: _"If the players take Vane's money, `Survey
Leverage +2`, `Army Respect -1`. Vane treats them as
> peers; Rowe begins to watch. Both reactions are real."_

The chapter must not punish a choice for being morally
ugly. It must register the choice's costs in the fiction
and let the players live with them.

### Avoid trolley dilemmas

The classic "five people on one track, one person on the
other" forces a clean utilitarian calculation that most
parties solve in thirty seconds. The dilemma evaporates.

Instead, use _qualitative_ dilemmas: one option preserves
something the party cares about specifically (a person, a
relationship, a principle), the other preserves something
abstract (justice, the greater good, the future). The
qualitative tension does not resolve to math.

### Use named NPCs

A dilemma about "the troops" is weaker than a dilemma about
"Sergeant Duffy." Named NPCs give the choice weight. The
party has shared meals with Duffy. They know his Irish
accent and his dry remarks.

If your dilemma asks the party to weigh costs against
unnamed people, replace at least one of those costs with a
named person from the cast.

### Use timing pressure

A dilemma considered in calm produces lawyerly weighing.
Drop the dilemma in a moment when the party is already
pressured: after a hammer beat, in the dark, with someone
bleeding nearby.

The pressure does not change the choice. It changes the
character of the choice — what kind of person the player
becomes when forced to decide quickly.

### Allow the third option

Players will invent options the chapter did not list. Allow
them, but require them to _cost_ in the same currency as
the listed options.

> _If the players invent a "find another testifier"
> approach, allow it: tell them which NPC is plausible, name
> the new cost, shift a different track._

The dilemma's _function_ is to extract a track shift, not to
funnel the party into one of two prepared options. As long
as a track shifts, the dilemma has done its work.

## The dilemma as character revelation

The dilemma is the moment a character reveals who they are.
That is its primary function — not the consequence ledger,
not the plot branching. The track shift is the bookkeeping;
the revelation is the point.

Write the dilemma so that whatever the party chooses, the
choice _says something about them_ that the rest of the
chapter remembers.

> _"The party that sold Keene to Vane became, retroactively,
> the kind of party that sold Keene to Vane. Every NPC the
> chapter writes after that scene knows it."_

The chapter does not need to spell this out. The track
shifts and the NPC reactions handle it. But the writer must
know it. Every dilemma is a question the chapter asks the
players about themselves.

## When the chapter ends without a dilemma

If your chapter has set-pieces, combat, branching, and a
multi-ending reckoning, but no genuine dilemmas, you have
written a tactical scenario, not an adventure. It will play
fine. It will not be remembered.

Add at least one dilemma per arc. The chapter will become
something the players quote later.
