# Pacing and Arcs — Breath, Hammer, Reckoning

A chapter has a heartbeat. It opens slow, drags through a
dangerous middle, drops a hammer, and pays out at a desk in
town. Get the rhythm wrong and the chapter feels rushed,
sluggish, or anticlimactic — even if every scene is good.

This file defines the three-act rhythm, the breath/hammer
discipline within each act, and the diagnostics for sagging
middles and rushed climaxes.

## The three-act adventure rhythm

Every chapter has three arcs. The function of each is fixed.

### Arc I — Setup (Breath)

**Length:** 25–30% of total chapter.

**Function:** Introduce the situation. Establish the cast.
Plant the seeds of the conflict. The players' first
significant choice happens at the end of this arc.

**Tone:** Slow. Observational. Sensory. Players are
spectators turning into participants.

**Set-pieces:** One. Usually a small violence (a killing, a
theft, a betrayal) that proves the situation is rotten before
the central pressure even applies.

**Closing beat:** A choice that commits the party to the line
of action. Not "do they go on the expedition" — they already
agreed. Rather: "do they protect the killer, surrender him,
or sell him?" The choice that locks them in.

### Arc II — Pressure (Hammer)

**Length:** 40–50% of total chapter.

**Function:** Apply the central pressure. Force the factions
into open conflict. Compel the players to take measurable
sides. The middle act is where the consequence ledger does
most of its work.

**Tone:** Pressured. Material. Sensory in a different key —
the small comforts of Arc I curdle. Food turns short.
Tempers turn ugly. Distance closes in.

**Set-pieces:** Two or three. At least one major combat
encounter. At least one moral dilemma without a clean answer.
At least one outsider intervention (a Native warning, a
foreign emissary, a religious witness — depending on genre).

**Closing beat:** A breach. The expedition shatters, the
heist is blown, the cult moves to the open. The thing that
was held together comes apart.

### Arc III — Reckoning (Cash-out)

**Length:** 20–30% of total chapter.

**Function:** Translate the consequence ledger into a final
state. The players spend their accumulated standing for the
ending they want. Loose threads consolidate into the sandbox.

**Tone:** Quiet, then sharp. Often shifts setting to a
_paper venue_ — a town, an office, a courtroom, a bank, a
boarding-house room. The fight that decides the chapter is
often more about who reads the document first than who shot
whom.

**Set-pieces:** One or two. Usually one final standoff
(public or private) and one paper-resolution scene.

**Closing beat:** Multi-ending text keyed to the tracks.
Sandbox exits. What hunts them now.

## Breath and hammer

Within each arc, beats alternate between **breath** (low-
pressure observation, character, color) and **hammer**
(high-pressure decision, violence, crisis).

A typical arc beat sequence:

> breath → breath → hammer → breath → hammer

Two breath beats setting up tone, then a hammer that
disrupts. A breath beat to absorb the disruption. A second
hammer that closes the arc.

### Breath beats

- A camp scene
- A side event with low stakes
- A travel sequence
- An NPC interlude
- An overheard conversation
- A weather change observed
- A small repair or chore

### Hammer beats

- A killing
- A raid
- An ambush
- A confrontation
- A revelation that changes faction stances
- A forced choice with track consequences

### The breath rule

> **Never run two hammer beats back-to-back.**

Players cannot absorb continuous crisis. Without a breath
beat between hammers, the second hammer lands flat. They are
exhausted, not engaged.

If your draft has two hammer beats in a row, insert a breath
between them. A camp scene. A march. A meal. A repair. The
pressure restores.

### The breath ceiling

> **Never run more than three breath beats in a row.**

Players notice when nothing is happening. Three breaths is
the ceiling; the fourth is a sag. If you have a fourth
breath, either compress two of them or fire a side event to
inject pressure.

## Camp scenes as the breath shelf

The "Camp Scenes Between Major Events" section of your
chapter (see `references/architecture.md`) is the GM's
breath-beat shelf. When the table needs a breath, the GM
pulls a camp scene off the shelf.

Provide enough camp scenes that the GM never runs out: 8–14
per chapter. Each camp scene is short (2–4 sentences in the
chapter text). Each one humanizes a named NPC, seeds a clue,
or tints the consequence tracks.

## Side events as hammer reserves

The "Side Events on the Line" section is the GM's hammer
reserve. When energy flags or a faction needs to be felt,
the GM throws a side event into the next breath beat.

Provide 4–8 side events per chapter. Each is small enough to
slot anywhere in Arc II. Each escalates pressure without
consuming a major scene.

## Pacing diagnostics

### The sagging-middle test

Read Arc II. Count the hammer beats. Count the breath beats.
The ratio should be roughly 1:1.

If it is 1:2 or worse (more breath than hammer), the middle
sags. Compress breath beats or promote a side event to a
hammer.

If it is 2:1 (more hammer than breath), the middle exhausts.
Insert breath beats or demote a hammer to a side event.

### The rushed-climax test

Read Arc III. Count the words. Compare to Arc I.

If Arc III is shorter than Arc I in word count, the climax
is rushed. Expand the reckoning. Add the paper-venue scene.
Add the final standoff with named NPCs in real space.

A rushed climax is the most common pacing fault in
published adventures. Designers spend their energy on the
setup and run out by the end. Resist this. The reckoning is
where the chapter pays out.

### The compression test

Read Arc I aloud. Time it.

If you cannot get through Arc I in 15 minutes of read-aloud
plus GM color, it is over-built. Cut the cast. Cut the
exposition. Save details for emergence in Arc II.

The opening should _feel_ slow but _play_ fast. The slowness
is in the prose register, not in the page count.

## Travel and time compression

Long-distance journeys present a pacing trap. Real travel is
slow; play time is precious.

Use these compressions:

### The montage paragraph

Three or four sentences in italics summarizing days of
travel. Names one image per day, one weather change, one
small incident. Closes on arrival.

> _Three days. The valley narrowed. The stock thinned. A
> mule went lame and had to be shot. The captain's gloves
> stayed clean while everyone else's shirts turned brown._

### The sample-day technique

Pick one day of the journey to play in detail. Compress the
others. The played day is one breath and one hammer; the
others pass in a paragraph.

### The travel side event

Replace a generic travel sequence with a single side event.
A wrong grave by the creek. A rider under white cloth. A
missing screw. The event compresses time by giving it shape.

## Set-piece spacing

A set-piece scene (one full scene grammar, often with
combat) takes 60–90 minutes of table time. Plan accordingly.

Per arc:

- Arc I: 1 set-piece
- Arc II: 2–3 set-pieces
- Arc III: 1–2 set-pieces

For a four-session chapter, that is 4–6 set-pieces total,
roughly one per session with a half-session set-piece in
the longer middle.

If your chapter has 8+ set-pieces, you have written two
chapters. Split it.

## The breath that turns

The strongest single beat in a well-paced chapter is **the
breath that turns**. A camp scene that begins as observation
and ends as revelation. A meal that begins as relief and
ends as ambush. A repair that begins as competence and ends
as discovery.

Plant at least one of these per arc. They are the moments
players quote in retrospect.

In the Yellowstone Line: _"the bad coffee fire"_ — a camp
scene that starts as troopers arguing over coffee and ends
with the players learning who trusts the work and who
understands the rot. Same scene; the temperature changes
mid-paragraph.

## Pacing the dilemmas

One major moral dilemma per arc, three per chapter. They are
the spine of the consequence ledger. See
`references/dilemmas.md` for design.

Dilemmas land best in the breath beats _immediately after_ a
hammer. The players are off-balance from violence; the
choice cuts deeper.

Worst placement: a dilemma immediately before a hammer.
Players see the hammer coming and pre-rationalize the choice.

## Closing the arc

Each arc ends with one of:

- A choice (most common)
- A revelation
- A loss

Never a victory. A victory at the end of an arc dissipates
the energy needed for the next arc. Players celebrate, the
table relaxes, the next session opens cold.

A choice, a revelation, or a loss carries forward. Players
end the session leaning into the next one.

## The ending after the ending

The chapter's last beat is not the climax. It is the
**residue**. One scene after the final standoff that names
what the chapter left behind. A wound that won't close. A
debt called in. A name in a notebook. A grave nobody marked.

The residue is what the players remember a month later. Make
it specific. Make it small. Let the bigger stakes resolve in
the climax; let the smaller ones haunt the sandbox.

> _"Vane requests one dark meeting. Calder warns that
> hunters probe her barn. LaRoche delivers native memory.
> The sandbox tears open."_

That is residue. The chapter is over; the territory is not.
