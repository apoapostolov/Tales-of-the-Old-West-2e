# Antagonist Design

The antagonist is not necessarily the villain.

The antagonist is the character or force whose interest
most directly opposes the party's current action. A
railroad company can be an antagonist without a named
villain at its head. A drought is an antagonist. So
is a town council doing something legal that the party
finds monstrous.

This file covers the antagonists who are _characters_:
individuals and institutions with motivation, method,
and history. For faction-level antagonism, see
`references/factions-and-tracks.md`.

## Motivation archaeology

Work backward from the antagonist's current action
to the chain of decisions that made it inevitable.
The chain must be at least three steps deep.

If you cannot answer "why are they doing this _now_?"
the antagonist is arbitrary. An arbitrary antagonist
is a cardboard enemy. They are menacing in principle
but empty when the players press them.

The archaeology model:

1. **What are they doing now?** The observable action
   the party will encounter.
2. **What decision led to this action?** The specific
   choice, not a vague disposition.
3. **What situation forced that decision?** External
   pressure, personal history, or failed alternative.
4. **What value are they defending?** At the base of
   every motivated antagonist is a value — security,
   survival, loyalty, justice by their own definition.

Example archaeology:

1. Holt is burning the Navarro family off their land.
2. He signed a contract with the rail company giving
   them right-of-way before verifying his own title.
3. He needed the cash immediately — his debts to
   the bank were 90 days past due.
4. He is protecting his idea of himself as a man who
   keeps his word. The contract is signed. He cannot
   afford to default on it.

Now Holt has depth. He isn't burning the Navarros
because he is evil. He is burning them because he
made a bad decision and cannot face undoing it.

## The mirror-protagonist principle

The most resonant antagonists share a core value or
desire with the party but pursue it through destructive
means.

Both the party and the antagonist want security. Only
one of them is burning homes for it. Both want justice.
Only one of them has decided that outside the law is
faster. Both want to protect their family. One of them
has decided that other families are acceptable costs.

The mirror-protagonist is disturbing because the
path from party-member to antagonist is visible.
The players can see how someone becomes this. That
visibility is what produces moral weight, not the
antagonist's power or brutality.

When designing the antagonist, ask: what do they
share with the party? Then ask: what is the specific
decision, failure, or circumstance that made them
cross the line the party has not yet crossed?

## Antagonist competence

A competent antagonist adapts.

If the party exposes the antagonist's agent in Act I,
a competent antagonist deploys a different approach
by Act II. They do not simply proceed with the same
plan after the party has dismantled it. They recalibrate.

Design the antagonist's response tree:

- **If the party finds the evidence**: the antagonist
  does [specific action to suppress or reframe it].
- **If the party confronts the antagonist's agent**:
  the antagonist does [specific action].
- **If the party exposes the antagonist publicly**:
  the antagonist does [specific action].
- **If the party offers a deal**: the antagonist
  responds with [their terms, which serve their
  interests].

The response tree is in the GM notes, not the
adventure text. The GM uses it as a guide for
how the antagonist reacts to player actions. It
is not a script — it is a characterization tool.

## Antagonist hierarchy

Most adventures have three tiers of antagonism:

1. **The instrument**: the hired gun, the enforcer,
   the agent. This is who the party fights directly
   in Act I and II. They have their own limited
   motivation (usually material: they're paid, or
   afraid of the organizing interest).

2. **The organizing interest**: the faction, the
   boss, the institution funding the instrument.
   This is who the party traces the instrument back
   to. They have a strategic motivation.

3. **The structural force**: the economic or political
   pressure that makes the organizing interest's
   action inevitable or at least comprehensible. The
   drought. The railroad's arrival. The federal policy.
   The debt cycle.

All three tiers should be visible by Arc III. An
adventure that presents only the instrument produces
a thin narrative — the party defeats the gun, but
the organizing interest hires another one. An adventure
that presents all three invites the party to engage
at the structural level.

Not every adventure requires all three tiers to be
confrontable. The structural force may be outside the
party's reach. That constraint is itself meaningful.

## Antagonist presence before confrontation

The antagonist's work should be visible in the
adventure's world long before the party meets them.

The evidence of the antagonist:

- A consequence that happened before the adventure
  opened (a burned ranch, a missing person, a forged
  document).
- A consequence that happens during the adventure
  in response to a previous advance (the antagonist
  moves when the party threatens their plan).
- An NPC who was changed by contact with the antagonist
  (afraid, bought off, grieving, compromised).

The party assembles the antagonist's profile from
their work, not from a revelation scene. They should
be able to describe the antagonist's method and
motivation before they meet them in person.

## The antagonist's perspective paragraph

Write one paragraph from the antagonist's point
of view explaining why what they are doing is
necessary and justified.

This paragraph is not in the adventure text. It is
in the GM notes. It is never read aloud.

Its purpose: it makes the antagonist real to the GM.
A GM who understands the antagonist's internal
justification will portray them as a human being,
not as a menacing obstacle. Players will feel the
difference.

The paragraph should end with the antagonist's
self-assessment: "I don't want it to be this way.
But if I don't see this through, everything I have
built for twenty years collapses."

## Sympathetic antagonists and the moment of lost sympathy

A sympathetic antagonist is understandable through
Act I, still understandable if uncomfortable through
Act II, and unforgivable by the end of Act III.

The transition from sympathetic to unforgivable
requires a specific moment — a decision the
antagonist makes that crosses a line the players
can identify. Before the moment: "I understand why
they're doing this." After the moment: "No. That's
not something I can accept."

Design the moment deliberately. It is usually
a choice the antagonist makes under pressure when
a less costly option was available. The party may
witness it or discover it. Its effect is to complete
the antagonist's arc: from comprehensible actor
to moral failure.

## When the antagonist wins

Some end-states require the antagonist to succeed
fully or partially. Build these.

An adventure that has no "antagonist wins" end-state
has removed a structural element that carries enormous
narrative weight. A world where the antagonist always
fails is not the world the players are playing in.

The "antagonist wins" end-state should be:

- Possible through player choices or inaction
  (not forced on the players by the text).
- Consequential — the sandbox continues with the
  new reality.
- Not permanent — the players can still act in the
  world created by the antagonist's victory.

See `references/endings-and-sandbox.md`.

## The antagonist's exit

Four possible exits. All four should be possible;
none should be forced:

1. **Arrest**: the legal system handles the antagonist.
   The organizing interest may be protected or may
   not be reached.
2. **Death**: the antagonist is killed, in combat or
   otherwise. This removes the person, not necessarily
   the structural force.
3. **Escape**: the antagonist evades. They remain in
   the world. A future adventure is now in debt to
   this one.
4. **Conversion**: the antagonist changes. The
   motivation archaeology, if fully revealed to the
   antagonist, may create a crack — a moment where
   they see themselves clearly. This is rare and
   should be expensive. A converted antagonist is
   not reformed; they are broken and choosing
   differently.

Each exit has different sandbox consequences. All four
should be possible. The players' actions determine which.
