# Endings and Sandbox — The Reckoning and What Comes After

The reckoning is where player choice cashes out. It is also
where most published adventures fail. The setup is strong,
the middle is tense, and then the chapter resolves with a
single scripted finale that reads the same regardless of what
the players did.

This file teaches the multi-ending discipline: four or more
distinct end-states keyed to the consequence ledger, each
written as a paragraph the GM reads after totaling the tracks.

It also teaches the sandbox-exit discipline: the chapter does
not end at the climax. It ends at the threshold of the next
chapter.

## The four-ending minimum

> **Every chapter must have at least four distinct end-states
> determined by the consequence ledger.**

Three is too few — players can predict and game the system.
Five or more is fine but rapidly diminishing returns.

The four end-states are not "good," "bad," "neutral," and
"secret." They are four distinct _configurations of who won
what_, each with its own residue.

### The four-axis template

A clean four-ending design uses two axes from the consequence
tracks. The combinations produce four end-states.

For example, with `Army Respect` and `Survey Leverage` as
the cash-out axes:

|                          | Army Respect HIGH       | Army Respect LOW           |
| ------------------------ | ----------------------- | -------------------------- |
| **Survey Leverage HIGH** | "Army Victory"          | "Railroad Shadow Victory"  |
| **Survey Leverage LOW**  | "Local Montana Victory" | "Bad Ending, Good Sandbox" |

Each cell is one end-state. Each end-state is a paragraph in
the chapter.

The other tracks (`Crow Trust`, `Local Goodwill` in this
example) modify the end-state, opening sub-variants and
seeding the sandbox.

### Naming the end-states

Name them clearly and concretely. The Yellowstone Line uses:

- **Army Victory** — packet lands clean, Rowe's reputation
  intact, future Army contracts.
- **Railroad Shadow Victory** — players hold or sold copies,
  rich and corrupted, hunted.
- **Local Montana Victory** — fraud exposed, ranchers gain
  breathing room, party becomes local heroes.
- **Crow Understanding** — sub-ending, available to any of
  the above with high `Crow Trust`.
- **Bad Ending, Good Sandbox** — liars prosper, party flees
  with proof, no rest.

Avoid fantasy-RPG clichés ("Good Ending," "Evil Ending").
Name what _actually happens_ in the world.

### Writing each end-state

One paragraph per end-state. Three to five sentences. Cover:

1. **What the public outcome is.** Who reads the documents
   first. Whose version becomes official.
2. **What the party gets.** Cash, contracts, contacts,
   reputation — concrete.
3. **What it costs.** Enemies, debts, scars, missed
   opportunities.
4. **What carries forward.** The sandbox seed.

Example (good):

> **Bad ending, good sandbox.** Liars prosper. The party
> flees carrying proof under a heavy coat. The territory
> offers no rest.

Three sentences. Names the public outcome (liars prosper),
what the party gets (proof), what it costs (no rest). The
sandbox seed (the proof becomes leverage in the next chapter)
is implicit but clear.

## End-state branching from the tracks

Write the cash-out logic explicitly. The GM should not have
to guess.

> The cash-out:
>
> - **Highest track** determines which end-state plays.
> - **Second-highest track** determines a sub-variant.
> - **Lowest track** determines the dominant cost.

So a party with `Army Respect +3`, `Survey Leverage +1`,
`Crow Trust 0`, `Local Goodwill -2` plays:

- Dominant: Army Victory (highest track).
- Sub-variant: with the survey notes formally delivered
  (Survey Leverage positive).
- Cost: locals turn cold; Hannah Calder's funeral happens
  off-page; merchants in Bozeman do not greet the party.

The GM combines these into the actual narration. The chapter
provides the components.

## Multiple venues

Where the reckoning _happens_ is part of the ending. Different
venues yield different end-states.

> Players pick the venue: Army barracks, a newspaper floor,
> a banked vault, a church holding, or a boarded room above
> a saloon. The room anchors the ending.

Provide three to five possible venues. Each one biases the
end-state. The barracks favor `Army Respect`. The newspaper
favors `Survey Leverage` (publication is leverage). The bank
favors money. The church favors `Local Goodwill`. The
boarding house favors private violence.

The venue choice is the players' last consequential decision
before the cash-out. Make it explicit.

## The residue beat

After the multi-ending paragraphs, write one short scene that
plays _regardless of which end-state landed_. The residue.

> Vane requests one dark meeting. Calder warns that hunters
> probe her barn. LaRoche delivers native memory. The
> sandbox tears open.

The residue does three jobs:

1. Proves the chapter does not "end" — the territory keeps
   moving.
2. Plants the hooks for the next chapter.
3. Names which surviving NPCs are now _active_ in the
   sandbox versus passive.

## Sandbox exits

The "Sandbox Exits" section at the end of the chapter names
the directions the chapter leaves open. This is the GM's
launch list for the next session.

### Form

A bulleted list, one direction per line. Each direction names:

- **A place** the party can reach
- **A faction or NPC** there
- **A pressure** that draws the party

> - Bozeman and Fort Ellis offer military runs.
> - Helena demands paper and blackmail.
> - Virginia City demands law.
> - Fort Benton demands river freight.
> - Southern boundaries demand Crow promises fulfilled.

Each line is a possible next chapter. The party chooses by
where they go and what they pursue.

### Number of exits

Three to seven exits. Fewer than three feels restrictive.
More than seven is paralyzing.

The exits should be a mix:

- One or two **safe-harbor exits** (friendly territory,
  recovery)
- Two or three **opportunity exits** (paying work,
  alliances)
- One or two **threat exits** (an enemy will find the party
  if they don't move first)
- One or two **mystery exits** (unfinished business from
  this chapter)

## What hunts them now

A sandbox exit is what _the party can do_. There is also the
question of _what does the party_. Write a short section
naming the surviving enemies and what they intend.

> If Vane survived: he leaves the territory but his network
> remembers names. Expect a hired killer in any town the
> party visits within six months.
>
> If Pike survived: he stays in Bozeman and begins squeezing
> any merchant who supported the party. Local Goodwill
> erodes by `1` per month unless the party intervenes.
>
> If Iron Hail survived: he carries the memory of any party
> member he saw. Future Lakota encounters in the basin will
> be informed by what he reports.

This section is short — three to five named threats — but it
gives the GM a campaign-level memory bank.

## Reward, reputation, and what the party carries out

A short section naming the tangible and intangible takeaways.
See `references/architecture.md` for the section spec.

### Tangible

- Cash, by approximate amount in your system's currency.
- Equipment (a captured rifle, a horse, a wagon).
- Documents (the survey copies, the ledger pages, a
  letter).
- Contracts (Army escort work, ranching contracts, freight
  deals).

### Intangible

- Reputation in named towns.
- Relationships with named NPCs (allies, enemies, debtors).
- Scars (a wound that won't close, a horse that will spook
  forever, a name the party will hate).
- Knowledge (one true thing about the territory the party
  now knows that they didn't before).

The intangible takeaways outlast the cash. A weak lung from
a winter in the Yellowstone basin marks a character forever.
Make the intangibles specific.

## Carrying the chapter into the sandbox

A final short section that names _open threads_. Things the
party did not resolve. Questions the chapter raised and did
not answer.

This is distinct from sandbox exits. Exits are
_opportunities_. Open threads are _unfinished business_.

> The end breaks three lanes open. Army court jobs. Paper
> chases in Helena. Local Gallatin resistance. Crow native
> tasks. One surviving enemy stalks the player trail.

Five lanes. Each one is an open thread. Some will become next
chapters; some will become season-long arcs; some will be
resolved off-page if the party never returns to them.

Open threads make the chapter feel like part of a world,
not a self-contained module.

## The last beat is residue, not resolution

Recall from `references/pacing-and-arcs.md`: the chapter's
last paragraph is residue, not climax.

> _"GM Notes on Tone: Men demand cash, horses, contracts,
> and power. No one speaks their theme. They demand the
> payload. The rest bleeds out later."_

The last paragraph reminds the GM how to _speak_ the world
after the climax has resolved. Tone is the residue. The
chapter is over; the voice continues.

## Common ending failures

### The locked ending

The chapter scripts a single climax. Player choices
flavor it but do not change it. _"Vane is arrested by Rowe in
the alley."_ This is a railroad's terminal station.

Symptom: only one end-state paragraph in the reckoning.
Fix: write three more, keyed to the tracks.

### The dice ending

The chapter resolves the climax with a single rolled
sequence. _"If the party defeats Vane in combat, good
ending. If they don't, bad ending."_ This wastes the
consequence ledger.

Symptom: the end-state depends on a roll, not on the
tracks.
Fix: rewrite so the tracks determine the end-state. Combat
modifies texture; choices over many sessions determine
outcome.

### The therapy ending

The chapter explains, post-climax, what the players should
have learned. _"In this chapter, the party discovered that
loyalty matters more than money."_ Insulting.

Symptom: the chapter speaks its theme aloud.
Fix: cut all explicit themes from the ending. The events
speak. (See `references/anti-railroad.md` on theme-as-rail.)

### The clean ending

The chapter ties off every thread. Nothing carries forward.
The territory rests.

Symptom: no residue, no open threads, no surviving enemies.
Fix: leave at least three things unresolved. The party's
victory should never be total.

## The reckoning checklist

Before publishing the chapter, run this:

1. **Four end-states minimum.** Each one a paragraph.
2. **Each end-state names public outcome, party gain, cost,
   and sandbox seed.**
3. **Cash-out logic explicit.** The GM can total the tracks
   and predict the end-state without GM-fiat.
4. **Multiple venues for the reckoning.** Players choose where
   the climax happens.
5. **Residue beat.** One scene that plays regardless.
6. **Sandbox exits.** Three to seven directions out.
7. **What hunts them now.** Surviving enemies named.
8. **Open threads.** At least three unresolved.
9. **Tangible and intangible rewards listed.**
10. **No theme spoken aloud.**

If all ten land, the chapter is whole.
