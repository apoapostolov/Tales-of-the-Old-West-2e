# Historical Grounding — Real-World Weight

Adventures with grounding feel heavier than adventures without
it. This is true of historical settings, where the grounding is
literal, but it is equally true of fantasy, sci-fi, modern, and
horror. The principle:

> **The world feels real when the chapter knows things the
> players don't need to know.**

A chapter set in 1873 Montana is grounded by knowing what the
Northern Pacific actually did that year. A chapter set on a
fantasy frontier is grounded by knowing what fur prices are,
how long a hide takes to cure, who controls the trade routes,
and what the local nobility taxed last harvest.

This file teaches the research register: what to research, how
much to put on the page, the historical-note device, and the
period-anchor discipline.

## What grounding is and isn't

### Grounding is

- Knowing the year, season, prices, distances, technologies,
  and social pressures of the world the chapter inhabits.
- Choosing details that *could only have existed in this
  specific moment*.
- Letting the chapter's antagonists pursue plausible interests
  using plausible methods.
- Naming real institutions, real treaties, real prices, real
  professions.

### Grounding is not

- Lecture. The chapter is not a history class.
- Showing your research. The reader does not need your
  bibliography.
- Period purity. A chapter that refuses to invent because
  "that didn't happen" is a textbook.
- Excluding fiction. Real grounding makes invention possible
  by establishing what the invention is contrasted against.

## The historical-note device

The most efficient way to inject grounding into a chapter is
the in-text historical note. A short call-out, set as a quote-
block or distinct paragraph, that tells the reader (the GM)
what the real thing was without breaking the chapter's voice.

### Form

```
> Historical note: In `1873`, railroad pressure in Montana
> remained mostly survey stakes. Men fought over routes,
> grades, and paper before locomotives crossed the
> territory.
```

Two or three sentences. Names the year (or equivalent
anchor). States a fact. Implies why the fact matters to the
chapter without explaining it.

### Placement

- **Once per major scene that touches a historical reality.**
  Not every scene. Three to six per chapter.
- **Right after the scene's establishing prose**, never
  inside the read-aloud (which is fictional voice).
- **Just before the consequence beat.** The note primes the
  GM to handle the moment with grounded weight.

### Content selection

A good historical note tells the GM something they can use
*right now*. Not background trivia.

> Bad: _"Historical note: The Northern Pacific Railway was
> chartered in 1864 by the United States Congress."_
>
> Good: _"Historical note: In 1873, railroad pressure in
> Montana remained mostly survey stakes. Men fought over
> routes, grades, and paper before locomotives crossed the
> territory."_

The bad note is encyclopedia. The good note tells the GM
that the players are arriving in a moment when the fight is
about *paper, not steel* — and that shapes the chapter.

### Tone

The historical note speaks in the chapter's voice, not in
academic register. Plain, declarative, present in the
chapter's vocabulary.

> _"Brand registration locked ranches onto paper in `1873`.
> Ink, hide, and county memory formalized ownership."_

That sentence is a historical note. It is also prose that
fits the chapter's voice. The two registers are not
separated.

## Period anchors

A period anchor is a specific, dated, named element that
appears in the chapter and could only exist in the chapter's
moment.

Anchors include:

- **A year, named explicitly.** "Late summer, 1873." The
  year goes on the page.
- **A specific institution.** "Fort Ellis, planted in 1867."
  Named, dated.
- **A specific technology.** "Survey stakes" not "magic
  detection devices." If the chapter is fantasy, name the
  fantasy equivalent with the same specificity: "the
  Conclave's silver chains" not "magic surveying tools."
- **A specific price.** "High nails, doubled teamsters."
  The complaint is dated by the things being overpriced.
- **A specific role.** "Mule skinner," "remuda cutter,"
  "draughtsman." Period-specific job titles tell the reader
  what world they are in.

### Density

A grounded chapter uses three to seven anchors per scene. Not
in a list — woven into the prose. The reader feels them as
texture, not as lecture.

## The research register

Before drafting a chapter, do the research that the chapter
demands. The minimum:

### For historical chapters

1. **The year and season.** What happened, what was just
   ending, what was about to begin.
2. **The political situation.** Federal, state, local. Who
   was in charge. Who was about to lose power.
3. **The economic pressure.** What were people buying,
   selling, fighting over.
4. **The technology of work.** What tools did people use.
   What was new. What was about to become obsolete.
5. **The transportation reality.** How long did distances
   take. What modes were available. What was reliable.
6. **The communication reality.** How did news travel. How
   long did letters take. Were there telegraphs.
7. **The Native, racial, and labor situation.** Who was
   being displaced, exploited, recruited, or organizing.

### For fantasy/sci-fi chapters

The same questions, with the world's specifics. The principle
is the same: know the *moment* the chapter inhabits, not just
the *setting*.

A fantasy frontier chapter set "in the third year of the
Conclave's withdrawal from the southern hills" needs:
- What the Conclave was doing there.
- Who is filling the power vacuum.
- What products of the withdrawal are still in circulation
  (abandoned outposts, surplus weapons, pensioned soldiers,
  unredeemed promises).

The grounding makes the chapter *unrepeatable*. Set it five
years earlier or later and it would be a different chapter.

### Where to research

For real-world chapters: primary sources where possible.
Period newspapers, government reports, contemporary diaries.
The textures of speech, complaint, and aspiration come from
contemporary writing. Modern histories are summaries; primary
sources are voice.

For invented worlds: your own setting bible. If the bible is
thin, this is the chapter that thickens it. Take notes on
what the chapter establishes; feed them back to the bible.

## Specific over general

The single most important grounding rule:

> **Choose the specific instead of the general.**

| General | Specific |
|---------|----------|
| "horses" | "a remuda of fourteen mules and six saddle horses, the bay too eager and the sorrel mare with a quarter-crack hoof" |
| "a fort" | "Fort Ellis, planted in 1867 to cover the Gallatin Valley" |
| "an officer" | "Captain Nathaniel Rowe, Army to the bone" |
| "supplies" | "high nails, damp flour, doubled teamsters" |
| "the railroad" | "the Northern Pacific, still mostly survey stakes" |
| "a weapon" | "an Army-issue trapdoor Springfield, the cartridge stamped with the wrong year" |

The specific does the grounding work. The general is
generic. Always choose the specific, even when the chapter
does not need that detail for the plot.

The reader (the GM) absorbs the specific and trusts the
chapter knows the world. The reader who hits a "general" pulls
back.

## What grounding earns

A grounded chapter buys you:

- **NPC plausibility.** A captain whose pay is documented,
  whose rank is reflected in his quarters, whose vocabulary
  is period-appropriate, *feels* like a captain. The reader
  forgives the chapter's other choices.
- **Faction plausibility.** Real institutions had real
  motives. The Northern Pacific wanted route control because
  route control was money. A fictional faction with a
  similarly documented motive feels equally real.
- **Consequence weight.** When players fail, the cost lands
  in real-world currency. Not "you lose 50 gold pieces" but
  "you lose three months of escort wages, which is the
  difference between buying a winter horse and walking."
- **Memorability.** Grounded chapters get quoted. Generic
  chapters fade.

## What grounding is not allowed to do

### It cannot become the chapter

The grounding serves the chapter. The chapter does not serve
the grounding. If a historical detail blocks an interesting
scene, change the detail or finesse the period.

The Yellowstone Line takes some liberties with the
1873 expedition's exact composition because the chapter is
better with them. The historical note acknowledges this:
"Officially, it measures crossings… privately, its uses turn
dirty." The privately is invented; the officially is real.

### It cannot become preachy

Chapters set in periods with documented atrocities (Native
genocide, slavery, child labor, sexual violence, racial
pogrom) must handle the material honestly without becoming
a lecture. The grounding is in the *texture*, not in the
moralizing.

> Good: _"A laundress kneels by Bell. She speaks hard
> truth over him. Bell had a sister waiting on cash."_
>
> Bad: _"A laundress kneels by Bell, reminding the players
> of the human cost of the railroad's relentless westward
> expansion."_

The good version grounds. The bad version sermonizes. (See
also `references/anti-railroad.md` on theme-as-rail and any
companion writing skill on uncomfortable truths.)

### It cannot exclude

Period accuracy is not an excuse to exclude characters from
underrepresented populations. Real history was full of women,
non-white people, queer people, and people with disabilities
in every period. A chapter that "for accuracy" features only
white men is making a choice, not honoring history.

The grounded chapter includes the full range of people the
period actually held — the laundress, the mixed-blood
scout, the Crow leader, the woman rancher, the Chinese rail
worker, the freedman teamster — because they were there.

## Grounding for fantasy and sci-fi

The same rules apply with the source changed.

A fantasy chapter is grounded by:
- The setting's calendar and the specific year/season.
- The local political and economic moment.
- The specific institutions and their procedures.
- The technology and magic of work.
- The transportation reality.
- The communication reality.
- The peoples and their displaced, exploited, organizing
  populations.

A sci-fi chapter is grounded by:
- The decade or year within the setting's chronology.
- The specific corporate, political, or factional moment.
- The specific tech generation in use (not "computers" but
  "fourth-gen wetware decks, three years past warranty").
- Travel and comms reality.
- The labor, racial, and class situation.

The principle is the same. **Know the moment.**

## When to research vs. when to invent

A practical rule:

- **Research the texture.** What things look like, how they
  smell, what they cost, who used them. Always research the
  texture.
- **Invent the events.** The killings, betrayals, confrontations,
  and reckonings of the chapter are yours to invent.
- **Adjust events to fit texture, not vice versa.** If your
  invented event would require an impossibly anachronistic
  technology or institution, change the event. If your
  research would force a boring event, the research is wrong
  or you are using it wrong.

## The grounding pass

When auditing a chapter for grounding, run this pass:

1. **Open any random page.** Could this scene have happened
   in another year, another setting, another world?
2. **If yes, find the line that anchors it to this moment.**
   If there isn't one, add one.
3. **Read every NPC entry.** Does each NPC's vocabulary, role,
   and concerns belong to this period?
4. **Check the historical notes.** Three to six per chapter.
   Each one tells the GM something usable.
5. **Check the prices, distances, and timelines.** All
   anchored to period reality.

If the pass surfaces ungrounded scenes, do not delete them —
add anchors. A ten-word adjustment ("Late summer, 1873," "by
the Northern Pacific charter," "with a percussion-cap
Colt") is usually enough to land a scene in time.

## The closing principle

Grounding is invisible when it works and conspicuous when it
fails. A reader who notices the grounding is reading a
textbook; a reader who notices its absence is reading a
generic. The work is in the middle: dense enough to feel real,
quiet enough to feel like prose.

When the chapter knows things the players don't need to know,
the players feel the world's weight.
