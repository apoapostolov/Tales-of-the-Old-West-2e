# Scene Design — The Meso Shape

A scene is the unit of play. Get the scene grammar right and
the chapter runs itself. Get it wrong and even a strong arc
collapses into a tour.

This file defines the scene grammar, the scene types, and the
patterns that make a scene resilient to player choice.

## The standard scene grammar

Every scene in the body of an adventure has the same shape.
Sections may be absent for short scenes, but the order is fixed.

### 1. Scene title

A short noun phrase naming the event, not the location. "The
picket-line killing" not "Fort Ellis stables." "Supper with
liars" not "Officer's mess."

### 2. Read-aloud (optional, italicized)

Two to four sentences, sensory, in italics, set off as a
quote-block. Functions as a tonal handoff from prep to play.
See `references/read-aloud.md` for the full treatment.

Not every scene needs read-aloud. Use it for:

- the opening of an arc
- the moment a body is found
- the moment a fight begins
- the moment the weather turns
- any scene where the GM needs a tonal anchor

Skip it for:

- transitional scenes
- scenes that primarily exist for player initiative

### 3. Ground-truth paragraph

One paragraph in the GM voice. States what is _actually_
happening, regardless of what the players know. Names the
NPCs present, what they want in this scene, and what truth
the scene contains.

This is the GM's compass. If the players go off-script, the
GM rereads this paragraph and improvises from it.

### 4. NPC behavior

One or two short paragraphs naming what each present NPC is
doing, watching, hiding, or pushing. Use the cast template's
voice: present-tense, observational, behavioral.

> _"Vane never hurries. He lowers his voice and steps close to
> make an ally of whoever listens."_

### 5. Player levers

A short paragraph or list naming the things the players can
_see, ask, search, or lean on_ in this scene. Distinguish:

- what is visible without rolls
- what a roll might surface
- what is hidden until they push

This is the GM's improvisation toolkit. Each lever should
suggest at least one branch.

### 6. Branches (4+)

A bulleted list of at least four genuine player responses,
each with its consequence. These are not the only branches —
players will invent more — but they ensure the GM has
prepared at least four roads.

Format each branch as:

> - **If they comply:** [what happens, which track shifts, what
>   > the next scene inherits]
> - **If they refuse:** [same]
> - **If they subvert:** [same]
> - **If they exploit:** [same]

Other useful axes:

- comply / refuse / negotiate / sabotage
- believe / doubt / test / report
- save the people / save the goods / save themselves
- public / private / silent / explosive

### 7. Track shifts

A short line naming which consequence tracks shift on which
choices. Bare and explicit.

> _Helping Hannah: `Local Goodwill +1`. Burning her hay to
> deny the raiders cover: `Local Goodwill -2`._

### 8. What this scene leaves behind

One or two sentences naming the residue. A new clue. A new
debt. A wounded NPC. A burned bridge. A name in a notebook.
Every scene must leave something that the next scene
inherits, or it was a tour.

### 9. Combat block (if applicable)

See `references/combat-encounters.md`. Combat blocks are
their own form, attached to scenes that contain a fight.

## The three scene types

Adventures use three distinct scene types. Each has its own
grammar variant. Mix all three in every arc.

### Set-piece scenes

The major beats. Killings, raids, route forks, ambushes,
reckonings. Use the full grammar above. These are the scenes
players will remember.

**Pacing rule:** No more than one set-piece per session of
play. Two set-pieces back-to-back exhaust the table.

### Camp scenes

Low-stakes color and character moments. Use a compressed
grammar:

> **Title.** One or two sentences of setup naming the moment
> and the NPC(s) present. One sentence naming what the players
> learn or gain by being present.

Provide 8–14 of these per chapter. The GM pulls them off the
shelf when:

- a session opens slow
- a player wants downtime
- a key NPC has gone unvoiced for too long
- the GM needs to seed a clue without a roll

**Function:** Camp scenes humanize the cast. They are where
NPCs become people. They are also where players overhear
what the official channels won't tell them.

### Side-event scenes

Pressure-injection beats. Small incidents that remind the
players the world is moving. Use a medium grammar:

> **Title.** Two or three sentences of setup naming the
> incident. One sentence naming the cost or moral weight. One
> sentence naming what it reveals.

Provide 4–8 per chapter. The GM throws one in when:

- the table's energy flags
- the players have been comfortable too long
- a faction needs to remind everyone it exists
- a clue needs delivery without a set-piece

## The four-branch test (applied)

Every set-piece must pass it. Open the scene. Without rereading,
name four player responses you have prepared for. If you can
name only "fight" and "flee," redesign.

Useful framings to force four:

- **Resource axis:** save the goods / save the people / save
  the secret / save themselves
- **Allegiance axis:** comply / negotiate / betray / refuse
- **Visibility axis:** public confrontation / private deal /
  silent observation / proxy action
- **Time axis:** strike now / wait for the next turn / set a
  trap / let it happen and harvest the wreckage

## Read-aloud anti-patterns

See `references/read-aloud.md` for the full list. The five
worst:

1. "You see a body on the ground." — second-person, flat,
   tells the players what they perceive instead of showing
   the world.
2. "Captain Rowe approaches and explains the mission." —
   read-aloud as exposition delivery.
3. "The atmosphere is tense." — naming the mood instead of
   showing the cause of the mood.
4. "What do you do?" — read-aloud should not contain a
   prompt. Stop the read-aloud and ask in your own voice.
5. Anything in second person ("you ride," "you smell,"
   "you see"). Read-aloud is third-person sensory observation
   of the world. Players translate it into perception.

## NPC behavior must be observable

When you write what an NPC does in a scene, write what the
players can _see them doing_, not their internal state.

> Bad: _"Mercer is anxious about the missing packet."_
>
> Good: _"Mercer trims his sentences and touches his coat
> pocket twice while answering."_

The first is GM telepathy. The second is something the players
can roll Insight on.

## Track shifts: when and how

A track shifts when a player makes a choice that visibly costs
or earns them standing with the named faction. Rules:

- One choice, one shift. Never `+2` from a single choice
  unless the choice is genuinely catastrophic.
- The shift is in the fiction. The NPC's behavior changes in
  the next scene. Do not announce shifts in mechanical
  language to the players.
- Tracks are public to the GM, semi-private to the players.
  Players may sense their standing through NPC reactions, but
  they don't see the score.
- Tracks shift on choice, not on roll outcome. A player who
  decides to betray Hannah loses goodwill regardless of
  whether the betrayal succeeds.

## What every scene must do

Run this checklist on every scene before publishing:

1. **Does it cost something?** Time, blood, paper, trust,
   ammunition, sleep. If nothing is spent, cut it.
2. **Does it reveal something?** A clue, a character truth,
   a faction stance. If it reveals nothing, cut it.
3. **Does it change the standing of one or more tracks?** If
   not, demote it from set-piece to camp scene.
4. **Does it end with something the next scene inherits?**
   If not, you wrote a tour, not a scene.
5. **Could the players genuinely refuse this scene?** If not,
   you wrote a railroad station.

## Scene density per arc

A typical arc holds:

- 1 cold-open / arc-opener scene
- 2–3 set-piece scenes
- 1–2 side-event scenes (optional)
- camp scenes available on shelf

A typical chapter (three arcs) holds 6–10 numbered scenes
plus a shelf of 8–14 camp scenes plus 4–8 side events.

If your chapter has 20+ numbered scenes, you have written a
campaign or a railroad. Cut.
