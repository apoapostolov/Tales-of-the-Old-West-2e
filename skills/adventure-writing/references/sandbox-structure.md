# Sandbox Structure — A Fourth Adventure Shape

This file teaches the **sandbox** as a distinct adventure structure, sitting alongside the three-act chapter arc (`pacing-and-arcs.md`) and the investigation (`investigation-structure.md`). It is not a synonym for "branching." A branching chapter still moves the party toward a climax. A sandbox is **a country the party moves through freely, where the story is whatever the party chooses to chase, and where the country moves whether the party acts or not.**

`node-based-design.md` covers the _scene_ (how one location connects to the next). `endings-and-sandbox.md` covers the _exit_ (what survives the chapter). This file covers the **whole**: how to design an adventure whose primary engine is not a plot but a living situation.

The sandbox is the hardest structure to write and the one that most rewards being written well. It is also the structure that most punishes a monolithic design. Build it as a country, not as a track.

## What makes a sandbox a sandbox

Four properties, all required. If any one is missing, what you have written is a branching chapter with sandbox _flavor_, not a sandbox.

1. **Multiple independent levers, not one critical path.** The party can win, lose, or change the ending through more than one route. There is no single document, NPC, or confrontation the adventure cannot function without. If you can point to one object and say "the whole adventure is about getting this," you have written a heist, not a sandbox.
2. **Faction clocks that advance without the party.** The world has a schedule. Whether the party engages or not, the Company tightens its screw, the camp gathers guns, the water judge buries another grandchild. The party rides inside a moving country, not in front of a paused one.
3. **Multiple entry points and multiple resolutions.** The party can begin in any of several places and reach a reckoning by any of several roads. The opening is a door the party picks, not a briefing the party receives.
4. **Shortcut answers, not patches.** When the party does the clever thing the author did not plan for — kills the villain early, grabs the lever in week one, rides for the capital — the adventure answers with the next true consequence, never with a reset.

If those four hold, the adventure is a sandbox. If they do not, it is something else wearing the word.

## The fixed-node sandbox

The most playable sandbox shape for a single published adventure is the **fixed-node sandbox**: a small number of named locations (three to six) the party moves between freely, each a hub with its own cast, economy, and pressure, connected by travel that is itself content. The party is not free to go _anywhere_ — that is a campaign setting, not an adventure. The party is free to move between the named nodes in any order, at any pace, pursuing any of the levers, while the clocks run.

The _Quiet Title_ sandbox is the model: four fixed nodes (the plaza, the gold camp, the courtroom town, the cañón), each a complete scene-set with its own NPCs and economy, connected by roads that carry their own events. The party can open play in any of the four and reach the hearing by any road. The _Yellowstone Line_ is the contrast: it is a three-act chapter whose middle is a moving column, not a sandbox, however much it branches inside its track.

### Designing the fixed nodes

Each fixed node must be able to sustain a session on its own. For each node, answer:

- **Who lives here.** The named NPCs whose home this is. (The plaza: Don Ignacio, Eufemia, the priest, Tomás. The camp: Strand, Ouida, Pell. The town: the judge, the sheriff, the lawyer, the Ring man.)
- **What the economy is.** What changes hands here, and at what price. (The plaza's flour at six cents; the camp's gold dust on a heavy scale; the town's paper and bribes.)
- **What the pressure is.** What is squeezing this node while the party is elsewhere. (The flour clock; the royalty tenth; the court calendar.)
- **What the node wants from the party.** Every node has a job it would hire the party to do, a secret it would sell, and a line it would punish the party for crossing.

A node that cannot answer all four is not a fixed node — it is a set-piece. Demote it to a scene inside another node.

### The connective travel

The roads between nodes are not dead time. Each road is content: a journey beat, a side event, an encounter that carries its own consequence. In a fixed-node sandbox, the party's _choice of road_ is a meaningful decision, because each road carries different pressure. The road to the gold camp means passing the Company's chain crew. The road to the courtroom town means passing the toll the Company doubled. The road into the cañón means passing the Jicarilla hunting ground.

Write one to three journey beats per road, drawn from the same shelf as camp scenes and side events. The party that takes the same road twice should not find it identical the second time — the clocks have moved it.

## Three sandbox substyles

The fixed-node sandbox is the family. Within it, three substyles differ on a single axis: **what the nodes are made of, and therefore what the party's primary engagement is.** Pick the substyle by answering one question — _what is the party doing most of the time between reckonings?_

- **Social sandbox** — the party is talking, bargaining, reading, and maneuvering between people and interests.
- **Pointcrawl sandbox** — the party is choosing routes between named places, and the route is the content.
- **Hexcrawl sandbox** — the party is crossing unmapped country, and the discovery of what is in the next hex is the content.

The rest of this file's machinery (levers, clocks, entry points, shortcut doctrine, the reckoning) applies to all three. What changes between them is the node, the clock's register, the lever's shape, and the sensory palette. The sections below name the differences precisely. Read the one that matches your adventure; do not try to write all three at once.

### The social sandbox — nodes are people and interests

In the social sandbox, the nodes are **factions, patrons, and secrets**, not places. The party is stationary or near-stationary — a court, a town, a council, a corporate station, a starbase, a military camp. Travel between nodes is trivial or abstracted ("you cross the common to the Archuleta store," "you take the shuttle to deck nine"). The map the party actually navigates is a **relationship web**: who talks to whom, who owes whom, who is sleeping with whom, who is blackmailing whom, who would betray whom for the right price. The geographic map, if any, is a mood-setter, not a navigation tool.

The defining engagement is **social**. The party's primary action between reckonings is deciding whom to talk to, what to reveal to them, what to ask of them, and what to offer. The levers are alliances, secrets, debts, promises, and reputations. The clocks track relationships and standing — `Comunidad Trust`, `Company Pressure`, court favor, board confidence, regiment reputation — and they advance on the party's social choices, not on distance traveled. _Quiet Title_ is a social sandbox at its core: the four "fixed nodes" (plaza, camp, court, cañón) are really four clusters of people and interests, and the party could be dropped into a corporate station or a ducal court with the same architecture intact.

**Node design for the social sandbox.** Each node is a person or faction with a Want, a secret, a price, and a stance toward the party. The "geography" of the sandbox is who will talk to whom: Eufemia will not speak freely in front of the Company's rider; Ouida will not speak freely in front of anyone who has not earned it; the priest will not speak freely in front of his bishop's letter. Draw the relationship web before drafting the scenes, because the scenes _are_ the web.

**Clocks for the social sandbox.** Clocks track patience, favor, and exposure: the patron's patience running out, the rival's influence rising, the secret's half-life before someone else tells it. A social sandbox typically runs four to seven relationship clocks, one per major faction or patron, each visible or hidden according to who would know it.

**Levers for the social sandbox.** The lever is a piece of social ammunition: a secret that ruins a rival, a debt that buys a vote, a promise that binds a faction, a witness whose testimony swings a verdict. The cost of a social lever is almost always another relationship — promising Ouida her family's claim costs you the comunidad's simplicity; buying the plaza's debt costs you the priest's trust. Cost-distinctness (`levers-and-fallbacks.md`) is especially load-bearing here, because social levers all look alike until you name what each one costs a different person.

**Sensory register.** Rooms, clothes, ink, paper, the smell of someone's drink, the quality of a silence when a name is spoken. The social sandbox lives in interiors and in the small physical tells that betray a stance.

**Failure mode.** The social sandbox collapses into a conversation log if the author forgets that relationships must _change on their own clock._ If every faction waits politely for the party to speak to them, the sandbox is a menu of dialogue trees, not a living court. The clocks must move: the rival makes a move while the party is elsewhere; the patron's patience expires; the secret someone was sitting on gets told by a third party. The party is a variable in a moving game, not the only player.

### The pointcrawl sandbox — nodes are places on routes

In the pointcrawl sandbox, the nodes are **named places connected by labeled routes**, and the route between nodes is itself content. The party is mobile, crossing real distance, and the primary engagement is **route choice**: which path to take, at what cost, through what hazard, toward which destination. The map the party navigates is a point-and-line diagram — towns, camps, passes, river crossings, way-stations — and the choice of route is a strategic decision, not a triviality.

This is the substyle `travel-and-wilderness.md` covers at the travel-structure level; here we cover it as a whole-adventure structure. The difference is one of emphasis: a pointcrawl _travel structure_ can sit inside any adventure, but a pointcrawl _sandbox_ is an adventure whose entire pleasure is the party deciding how to move through a geography of meaningful routes — a stagecoach line contested by road agents, a frontier of forts and fords, a river system of trading posts, a chain of stations across a hostile moon.

**Node design for the pointcrawl sandbox.** Each node is a place with an entry (what you see arriving), a cast (who is there), an economy (what changes hands), and a pressure (what is squeezing it). The routes between nodes are designed content, not abstracted distance: each route has a distance, a condition (season, weather, political control), and a hazard or opportunity. _Quiet Title_'s four nodes connected by the Cimarron road, the Baldy trail, and the cañón approach are a small pointcrawl; a cattle-drive or stagecoach-line sandbox would lean on this substyle harder.

**Clocks for the pointcrawl sandbox.** Clocks track _route conditions and faction positions_: the pass closes with the season, the road agent gang moves its ambush point, the fort's garrison rotates out, the river ford silts shut, the Company's toll doubles on the main road. The party's route choices interact with the clocks — the route that was safe last week is contested this week because a clock filled.

**Levers for the pointcrawl sandbox.** The lever is a piece of geographic or logistic ammunition: control of a chokepoint, a cached supply, a forged road permit, a shortcut known only to a guide, seized high ground, a burned bridge that denies a route to the enemy. The cost of a pointcrawl lever is usually time, supplies, or exposure — taking the high pass costs you horses; caching supplies costs you a trip; seizing the chokepoint costs you the faction that held it.

**Sensory register.** The road, the weather, the footing, the horseflesh, the distance, the light at the time of day you crest the pass. The pointcrawl sandbox lives in motion and in the country between nodes.

**Failure mode.** The pointcrawl sandbox collapses into a flowchart if the routes are flavorless connectors. If every route is just "two days, roll for an encounter," the party's route choice is meaningless. Each route must carry a distinct cost, a distinct hazard, and a distinct piece of content the other routes do not have — or the choice is decorative. See `travel-and-wilderness.md` on the route-entry format.

### The hexcrawl sandbox — nodes are hexes of unmapped country

In the hexcrawl sandbox, the nodes are **hexes of a spatial grid**, each containing terrain and optionally a keyed location, and the primary engagement is **discovery**: what is in the next hex, what is over the next ridge, what the blank space on the map turns out to hold. The party is exploring unmapped or poorly-mapped country, navigating by direction rather than by named destination. The map the party navigates is one they are drawing as they go.

This is the substyle for wilderness exploration, frontier survey, dungeon-as-region, and any adventure whose pleasure is the party pushing into the unknown and finding what is there. A survey expedition into the Yellowstone basin, a cavalry column mapping the Llano, a party of fur trappers working a new watershed, a salvage crew clearing an uncharted derelict — all are hexcrawl sandboxes.

`travel-and-wilderness.md` covers the hexcrawl as a travel structure; here we cover it as the adventure's spine. The hexcrawl sandbox is the most mechanically demanding of the three because it requires a map, a terrain key, a wandering-threat table, and a supply discipline — but its payoff is the strongest sense of _discovering a real place_ of the three substyles.

**Node design for the hexcrawl sandbox.** The "node" is the hex. Each hex has a terrain type (with travel cost, terrain effects, and encounter profile) and, for some hexes, a keyed location (a lair, a ruin, a spring, a landmark, a homestead, an ambush site). Design the hex map before drafting scenes; the scenes are the keyed locations, and the terrain between them is the adventure's pacing material. Key roughly one hex in three or four; leave the rest as terrain and encounter space so the party can be surprised.

**Clocks for the hexcrawl sandbox.** Clocks track _environmental and supply pressure_: the season turning (the pass closes in six weeks), the supply Situation (rations, horse feed, water, ammunition), the weather front moving in, the wandering threat's awareness of the party (the war band has found their trail; the predators have learned the party's camp pattern). The hexcrawl's clocks are the most likely to be felt as _survival pressure_, and they are the substyle where onstage resource tracking (`travel-and-wilderness.md`) is most often earned.

**Levers for the hexcrawl sandbox.** The lever is a discovered asset or territorial advantage: a found pass that flanks the enemy, a rich strike that funds the whole expedition, a map to a lost site, an alliance with the people who actually know the country, control of the only water. The cost of a hexcrawl lever is usually information (you had to find it), risk (you had to survive getting it), or time (the season advanced while you searched).

**Sensory register.** The country itself, region by region, hex by hex. The hexcrawl sandbox lives in landscape prose and in the physical cost of crossing it. Load `knowledge/regional-landscapes.md` (in the western-writing skill) or its genre equivalent; the hexcrawl is where the landscape writing earns its keep, because the landscape is the content.

**Failure mode.** The hexcrawl sandbox collapses into a slog if the terrain is undifferentiated and the wandering table is a damage delivery mechanism. If every hex is "plains, roll for coyotes," the party will dread travel instead of craving discovery. The fix is in `travel-and-wilderness.md`: terrain types must have distinct effects, the encounter table must be mostly _situations_ rather than _threats_, and roughly a third of hexes must be keyed with real discoveries. A hexcrawl with no discoveries is a death march; a hexcrawl with nothing but discoveries is a theme park. Both fail.

### Choosing between the substyles

Pick by the adventure's **primary engagement**, not by its genre.

| Question | Social | Pointcrawl | Hexcrawl |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| What is the party doing most between reckonings? | Talking, bargaining, reading people | Choosing routes between named places | Crossing unmapped country |
| What are the nodes? | People, factions, secrets | Places on labeled routes | Hexes of terrain + keyed sites |
| What does the map look like? | A relationship web | A point-and-line diagram | A hex grid the party fills in |
| What do clocks track? | Favor, patience, exposure | Route conditions, faction positions | Season, supplies, weather, threat awareness |
| What is the lever's shape? | A secret, a debt, a promise, a witness | A chokepoint, a cache, a permit, a shortcut | A found pass, a strike, a map, an alliance |
| What is the sensory register? | Rooms, ink, silences, tells | Roads, weather, footing, distance | Region-by-region landscape |
| Genre example | Court intrigue, town faction war, station politics | Stagecoach line, frontier of forts, river trade | Survey expedition, wilderness trapping, derelict salvage |

Most published sandboxes lean on one substyle but borrow from the others. _Quiet Title_ is a social sandbox whose connective tissue (the Cimarron road, the cañón) is a small pointcrawl, and whose climax (the hearing) plays out socially. A cattle-drive campaign might be a pointcrawl sandbox that opens with a social sandbox at the home ranch and closes with one at the railhead. A survey expedition is a hexcrawl sandbox that lands in a social sandbox whenever it reaches a fort or an agency. Design the primary substyle first; borrow from the others where the adventure needs them.

### The hybrid warning

A sandbox that tries to be all three substyles at once is usually none of them. If the party is simultaneously running a relationship web _and_ choosing routes _and_ mapping hexes, the author has three sandboxes stacked and the clocks, levers, and sensory registers will fight each other for table time. Pick a primary. Let the other two substyles appear as _connective tissue_ — the social sandbox with a pointcrawl road between its nodes; the pointcrawl with a relationship web at each stop; the hexcrawl that lands, periodically, in a settlement that runs socially. The primary substyle is where the party lives. The others are where the party visits.

## The lever menu

The sandbox's engine is the **lever**: a route to winning, losing, or changing the ending that does not depend on the other routes. A real sandbox has three to nine levers. _Quiet Title_ runs nine: the field book, the surveyor's affidavit, the grant language, the cemetery register, the bought witness, the intercepted mail, the railroad's money, the bought debt, and the range war. The party needs at most two or three to reach a reckoning, which means the party can ignore six and still win — and the author has to have designed all nine because the party will not tell the author which three it plans to use.

This is the core difference from a branching chapter. A branching chapter has one prize with multiple paths to it. A sandbox has multiple prizes, each sufficient, and the party decides which prize the story is "about."

### Lever design rules

- **Each lever is sufficient on its own.** If the party holds only this one lever, the adventure can still reach a reckoning — a different reckoning than another lever would produce, but a real one.
- **Each lever is held by someone.** A lever is never loose in the world. It belongs to an NPC who will trade it, hide it, burn it, or die for it. The lever's holder is the scene.
- **Each lever has a cost distinct from the others.** The field book costs the party the most danger. The grant language costs the party a promise to a difficult ally. The bought debt costs the party cash and years. The railroad's money costs the party their independence. No two levers should cost the same thing, or the "choice" between them is decorative.
- **Levers can combine, but no combination is required.** The field book plus the affidavit plus the grant language is the strongest possible case in court. The party can also bring only one, or two, or none, and reach a reckoning on the strength of what it brought. Design the reckoning to read _any_ combination, not only the full set.

### The lever audit

Before calling the sandbox designed, run this: pick the loudest lever (the one the adventure's title seems to promise). Now ask — _if the party refuses this lever entirely, can the adventure still reach a reckoning?_ If the answer is no, you do not have a sandbox. You have a heist. Add at least two independent levers before drafting further.

## The entry-point menu

A sandbox does not have a cold open that funnels into Scene 1. It has a **cold open that establishes the country**, then an **entry-point menu** — three to five doors into the sandbox, each a real first session, each with its own tone. The party picks the door by where it goes and what it does first.

The _Quiet Title_ menu: open in the plaza (dense, slow, the supper), open in the gold camp (loud, fast, Strand sizing up strangers), open in the courtroom (cold, the party watches the suit as paper and understands the stakes), open on the road (classic western cold open, the country teaching the plot), open in the high country (disorienting, the party meets the line before it meets the suit).

### Writing each entry point

For each entry point, write:

- **The opening image.** The sensory ground the party lands on.
- **Who the party meets first.** The NPC who orients them — and note that orienting through different NPCs produces different first impressions of the same sandbox.
- **What the party is doing here.** The job, errand, or trouble that brought them through this door.
- **What the other doors look like from here.** The party should be able to _see_ the other nodes from whichever one it landed in, even if it does not visit them yet. The menu is visible from inside any single choice.

A sandbox whose entry points all funnel into the same Scene 2 is not a sandbox. The roads out of each entry point must lead somewhere meaningfully different.

## Faction clocks in the sandbox

`clocks-and-timers.md` covers the mechanic. In a sandbox, clocks do more work than in any other structure, because the sandbox's primary pressure is _the country moving while the party chooses_.

Run **four to seven clocks** in a fixed-node sandbox, one or two per node. Each clock is a faction's patience, ambition, or endurance running down. _Quiet Title_ runs six: the Company's patience, the gold camp's ambition, the comunidad's endurance (the flour clock), the mountain people's patience, the guide's nerve, and the railroad's cold patience.

### Sandbox clock rules

- **Clocks advance on time, not on triggers.** In a chapter, a clock fires when the party does a specific thing. In a sandbox, a clock advances each in-game week (or day, or season — pick the unit and hold it) whether the party does anything or not. The party's choices _change the rate_, not the existence, of the advance.
- **When two clocks collide, run the collision.** A week where the Company's crew re-marks the line _and_ the camp raids an assay wagon is a real session. Do not serialize the collisions. Let them land together and let the party choose which fire to fight.
- **A clock that fills fires whether the party is present or not.** The party hears about it after — sometimes too late. This is the sandbox teaching the party that it is not the protagonist. It is a variable.
- **Some clocks are visible to the party; some are not.** The flour price is visible (Eufemia posts it). The railroad's patience is hidden until it moves. Mix visible and hidden. See `clocks-and-timers.md`.

## When the party short-cuts the plot

This is the sandbox's defining test. A branching chapter treats the unplanned move as a failure to patch. A sandbox treats it as the design working as intended. Write a **"When the Party Shortcuts the Plot"** section that answers, honestly, the common shortcuts.

For each shortcut, give: the shortcut, the _true consequence_ (never a reset), and the road it opens. The shortcuts you must answer, drawn from real tables:

- The party ignores the loudest lever entirely.
- The party grabs the loudest lever in week one.
- The party rides for the capital / higher authority to skip the local reckoning.
- The party kills the primary antagonist early.
- The party kills any named NPC.
- The party tries to win by economics (buying the debt, cornering the market) instead of by the designed conflict.
- The party burns the central evidence to deny it to everyone.
- The party splits up.
- The party sits still and waits for the plot to come to it.
- The party tries to unite incompatible factions against a common enemy.
- The party triple-deals every faction.
- The party does something nobody planned for.

The last one is the most important. Write the doctrine in one line: **say yes, find the true consequence, trust the country to answer.** The country always has an answer. A dynamited road means federal troops. A kidnapped judge's wife means the army and a noose. A burned saloon means the camp at the party's throat. The shortcut is not the problem. The next true problem the shortcut creates is the adventure.

### The reset is banned

The single gravest sin in sandbox design is the **reset**: the author-built mechanism that undoes the party's cleverness to put them back on the intended track. The field book that is stolen back no matter what. The antagonist who survives any attempt on his life because the climax is scripted. The capital that refuses to hear the case because the local reckoning is where the author wanted the scene. Each of these tells the party their choices do not matter, and a sandbox whose choices do not matter is not a sandbox.

If the party's shortcut genuinely breaks the adventure you planned, the fix is never to undo the shortcut. The fix is to design the next true problem the shortcut creates, and run that. The party that killed the villain in week one has not ended the adventure. It has loaded the adventure into the consequences of the killing.

## The sandbox reckoning

`endings-and-sandbox.md` covers the reckoning in general. The sandbox reckoning has two special properties.

First, the reckoning is **where the clocks cash out**, not where a climax is staged. In a chapter, the reckoning is a confrontation the party drives toward. In a sandbox, the reckoning is the moment the clocks' totals become the state of the country — the hearing convenes, the season turns, the company's patience expires, the camp's guns arrive — and the party's accumulated levers and standing determine what happens in that moment.

Second, the sandbox reckoning is **more forgiving of partial engagement**. A chapter reckoning assumes the party brought the goods. A sandbox reckoning reads whatever the party actually brought — including nothing — and plays the country's verdict on that. A party that ignored every lever and sat in the saloon for six weeks still gets a reckoning: the country's verdict on people who did nothing while it burned.

## Sandbox vs. chapter — when to pick which

Pick the **sandbox** when:

- The adventure's pleasure is the party choosing _what to care about_ in a rich situation.
- The situation is a place (a county, a valley, a station) more than an event (a march, a pursuit, a siege).
- The party's freedom of movement and order of operations is the primary engagement.
- You want replayability — different parties opening different doors produce different stories.

Pick the **chapter** (three-act arc) when:

- The adventure's pleasure is the party's escalating response to a single mounting pressure.
- The situation is an event more than a place.
- Pacing cohesion and a rising tension curve matter more than free order of operations.
- The adventure is a one-shot or the opening of a campaign where the party must be delivered to a specific threshold.

Combine them when both apply: use the chapter's three-act rhythm (breath, hammer, reckoning) as the _macro_ pacing, and use the sandbox's fixed nodes and lever menu as the _scene_ structure inside each act. The _Quiet Title_ adventure does this — it has four named arcs (which give the macro rhythm) but inside each arc the party moves freely between nodes and levers.

## The sandbox audit

Before calling the sandbox designed, run all of these.

1. **The lever test.** Name your loudest lever. Can the adventure reach a reckoning if the party refuses it? If not, add two more independent levers.
2. **The clock test.** Do at least four faction clocks advance on time, not on triggers? If the country pauses when the party pauses, it is not a sandbox.
3. **The entry-point test.** Pick any of your entry points. Does opening there produce a meaningfully different first session than opening in any other? If they all funnel to the same Scene 2, rewrite the doors.
4. **The shortcut test.** Open your "When the Party Shortcuts" section. Does every shortcut have a true consequence, not a reset? Is there a line for "the party does something nobody planned for"?
5. **The dead-NPC test** (from the SKILL.md acid tests). Kill the most important NPC in scene one. Does the sandbox survive? It must.
6. **The sit-still test.** If the party holes up and does nothing for three in-game weeks, what has happened? The answer must be "the country has changed in ways the party now has to live with," not "nothing."
7. **The split-party test.** The party divides into two groups going to different nodes. Can you run it? The sandbox must support it; this is the campaign's natural shape.

If all seven land, the sandbox is whole.

## What this file is not

- **Not a hexcrawl or pointcrawl manual.** For overland travel mechanics, see `travel-and-wilderness.md`. This file is about adventure structure, not movement rules.
- **Not a replacement for node-based design.** Nodes are the _scene_ layer inside a sandbox. This file is the _adventure_ layer above it.
- **Not a license to underwrite.** A sandbox is not "here is a map, make your own fun." Every node, lever, clock, and shortcut answer is written content. The sandbox takes more writing than a chapter, not less, because the author is writing for branches the party will never take.
