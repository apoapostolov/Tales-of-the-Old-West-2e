# Read-Aloud — Sensory Openers and the Italic Voice

Read-aloud text is the chapter's tonal handoff. It is the
moment the GM stops paraphrasing and reads. The players hear
the world in a different register — usually italicized, often
set apart as a quote-block — and the table tunes to it.

Get read-aloud right and players carry the world's weight in
their bodies. Get it wrong and the read-aloud becomes a
narration interruption that the table tolerates rather than
absorbs.

This file teaches what goes in the italic voice, what stays
in the GM voice, and the kill list for read-aloud anti-
patterns.

## What read-aloud is for

Read-aloud delivers **sensory tone** in a sustained register
the GM cannot sustain alone. The italics signal: pay
attention to _texture_. Smell, light, sound, weather, posture,
the specific quality of dirt and brass.

Read-aloud is _not_ for:

- Plot delivery
- Character introduction
- Mechanical setup
- Dialogue (unless very short, very pointed)
- Information the players need to act on
- Asking the players what they do

Plot, characters, mechanics, and information are the GM's
job in their own voice. Read-aloud is the chapter painting
the scene's air.

## When to use read-aloud

Use it for:

1. **Arc openings.** The first scene of an arc benefits from
   a tonal anchor.
2. **Set-piece scene openings.** Major beats deserve the
   weight.
3. **Discovery moments.** A body found, a fire seen at the
   wrong distance, a door opened on the wrong room.
4. **Combat openings.** The first beat of a fight, before
   initiative.
5. **Weather turns.** When the world's mood shifts.
6. **Travel arrivals.** The first sight of a new town, fort,
   river, range.

Don't use it for:

1. Transitional scenes
2. Scenes that exist primarily for player initiative
3. Dialogue-driven scenes
4. Scenes the players have asked to skip
5. Quick camp scenes

A good rule: one to three read-aloud blocks per arc. More
and the GM stops reading them; less and the chapter loses
its tonal hooks.

## The form

```
> _Late summer hangs dry over Fort Ellis. The mountains look
> blue in the south. The parade ground churns under hooves
> and wagon wheels. Soldiers haul crates. Laborers bicker.
> A survey tripod sits near stacked grain. Nobody rests. Too
> many men draw pay from the same table._
```

- Set as a blockquote with `>`
- Italicized with `_..._` (or `*...*`)
- Two to five sentences
- Third-person, present tense or simple past
- Sensory details before any abstract observation
- Closes on a hard line that hints at the rot, the tension,
  or the cost

## What the sentences do

Each sentence in a read-aloud has a job. The standard
structure:

1. **Establish the moment.** Time of day, weather, season.
   "Late summer hangs dry over Fort Ellis."
2. **Anchor the space.** A wide visual. "The mountains look
   blue in the south."
3. **Add motion.** Verbs of work or weather. "The parade
   ground churns under hooves and wagon wheels."
4. **Add bodies.** Specific people doing specific work.
   "Soldiers haul crates. Laborers bicker."
5. **Land the closing image.** The hard line. "Too many men
   draw pay from the same table."

Not every read-aloud has all five. Short read-alouds (two
sentences) compress to _establish_ and _land_. Long ones
(five sentences) include all five. The order is fixed:
moment → space → motion → bodies → land.

## The closing line

The last line of a read-aloud is the most important. It is
what the players carry into the scene.

The closing line should:

- Refer to a specific object, body, or pressure.
- Hint at conflict, rot, or cost without naming the plot.
- Have a hard cadence — short syllables, declarative.

Examples (from the Yellowstone Line):

> _"It took one dead teamster and a saddle cut loose in the
> dark to show what the country thought of that."_

> _"Too many men draw pay from the same table."_

> _"A horse watches the body and rolls one eye white."_

Each closes on a concrete image and a hard cadence. None
explains the plot. Each plants a question.

## Sensory specifics

The body of the read-aloud is sensory. Lean on:

- **Sight that includes light quality.** "Pale and mean."
  "Yellow lamps over tin plates." "Blue mountains in the
  south."
- **Sound that includes texture.** "Hooves and wagon wheels."
  "A mule jerks a tether." "Brown current pushes against the
  wheels."
- **Smell that includes specifics.** "Wet wool, stove heat,
  and drying paper." "Hay and horse sweat."
- **Touch that includes weight.** "Slick clay lines the near
  bank." "Cold turns the air to glass."
- **Weather as character.** "Morning hits the valley pale
  and mean." "The dark rips open."

Avoid generic "the air is tense" or "the smell of danger."
The senses must be _specific_ enough that players can
visualize them.

## What stays in the GM voice

After the read-aloud, the GM speaks in their own voice. This
voice handles:

- NPC dialogue and behavior
- Plot information
- Mechanical prompts ("roll Insight")
- Player questions and answers
- Description of consequences

The shift from italics to plain text is itself a signal.
Players hear "the chapter is done painting the scene; now we
play."

If a chapter has _too much_ read-aloud (five paragraphs of
italics before any GM voice), the players get tonal fatigue.
Two to five sentences, then the GM takes over.

## The kill list

These read-aloud patterns destroy tone. Search and destroy.

### Second person

> Bad: _"You ride into the fort and see soldiers everywhere.
> You smell horse sweat and old smoke."_

**Diagnosis:** Second person tells the players what they
perceive. It collapses the distance between the world and
the perceiver. It also presumes the players are physically
present in a particular posture and approach.

**Rewrite:** Third person, observational. _"The fort sits
under the high pale sky. Soldiers move between buildings.
The yard smells of horse sweat and old smoke."_ The players
locate themselves in the scene as they enter.

### "You see / you hear / you feel"

Same problem, more pernicious.

**Rewrite:** Drop the "you." Render the sense directly.
"Hooves and wagon wheels in the yard. Horse sweat in the
air. The captain's voice cutting across the parade ground."

### Plot in italics

> Bad: _"Captain Rowe approaches and explains that the
> Northern Pacific has commissioned a survey of the
> Yellowstone basin and that he needs your help to escort
> it. The pay is forty dollars and the term is six weeks."_

**Diagnosis:** Plot delivery dressed as tone.

**Rewrite:** End the read-aloud after the sensory section.
Have Rowe deliver the plot in his own voice, in scene.

### Mood-naming

> Bad: _"The atmosphere is tense. Everyone is on edge."_

**Diagnosis:** Naming the mood instead of showing the cause.

**Rewrite:** Show the cause. _"A trooper drops a tin plate.
Three men reach for sidearms before they catch themselves.
Nobody laughs."_

### Question prompts

> Bad: _"What do you do?"_

**Diagnosis:** Read-aloud is not interactive. The italic
voice does not address the players directly.

**Rewrite:** End the read-aloud. Then ask in your own voice:
"What do you do?"

### Future tense

> Bad: _"You will need to investigate the killing before the
> column moves out tomorrow."_

**Diagnosis:** Future tense breaks the read-aloud's
present-immediate spell. It also tells the players what
they will do (railroad symptom).

**Rewrite:** Cut. The present situation establishes the
pressure without prophecy.

### Adjective stacks

> Bad: _"The dark, foreboding, ancient fort looms ominously
> in the distance, casting its long, sinister shadow over
> the dusty, unforgiving plains."_

**Diagnosis:** Adjective overdose. Each adjective dilutes the
others. The reader skims.

**Rewrite:** One specific adjective per noun. Better, no
adjectives — let the noun do the work. _"The fort sits at
the river bend. Long shadow over the flat country."_

### "Suddenly"

The word "suddenly" is a tell that the writer didn't trust
the prose to carry the surprise. Cut it. The shock comes from
the structure: a calm sentence followed by a hard one.

> Bad: _"Suddenly, a shot rings out."_
> Good: _"A man is laughing by the wagons. A shot."_

### Quoted dialogue inside read-aloud

Mostly avoid. Quoted lines belong to NPCs, not the italic
voice. If you must include a line, set it tight and
attribution-free.

> Acceptable: _"Somebody shouts for the satchel and prays
> to God in the dark."_

The voice is reported speech, not quotation. The shouting
is part of the texture, not a delivered line.

### Direct exposition

> Bad: _"Fort Ellis was established in 1867 and houses the
> Second Cavalry."_

**Diagnosis:** Encyclopedia voice in italics. Wrong register.

**Rewrite:** Move historical facts to a Historical Note
call-out (see `references/historical-grounding.md`) or to
GM voice. Read-aloud carries no exposition.

## Cadence and rhythm

Read aloud the read-aloud. If your tongue stumbles, the GM's
will too at the table.

Useful cadence rules:

- **Short sentences land harder than long ones.** Especially
  the closing line.
- **Vary length.** A read-aloud of all-short sentences feels
  staccato and tense. A read-aloud of all-long sentences feels
  drifting. Mix.
- **End on a hard syllable.** "Dawn" lands. "Morning" drifts.
- **Avoid clauses that trail.** "...which made the men
  uneasy." Trailing qualifiers weaken the line. End on the
  noun or the hard verb.

## When to skip read-aloud entirely

Some scenes don't need it. The chapter should not feel
obligated to provide italics for every set-piece. Skip when:

- The previous scene established the tone clearly enough.
- The players are returning to a known location.
- The scene's strength is in dialogue or NPC behavior, not
  atmosphere.
- The pacing demands quick movement.

A chapter where every scene opens with italics develops a
predictable rhythm that the players tune out. Skip
sometimes. The italics that _are_ used will land harder.

## Genre note

The standards here come from a hard, realistic register —
the western, the noir, the war novel. Other genres tune
differently:

- **Comic fantasy** uses lighter, looser sentences in
  read-aloud. The closing line lands on a joke or an
  unexpected detail rather than a grim image.
- **Cosmic horror** uses longer, more clausal sentences
  building to dread. The closing line names what should not
  be there.
- **Pulp adventure** uses sharper, more cinematic verbs.
  The closing line is often a hook or a question.
- **Modern thriller** strips ornament further. Sentences
  are blunt and procedural.

The principles (sensory, third-person, no plot, hard close)
apply across genres. The cadence varies.

## The acid test

Read your read-aloud aloud, in front of someone who has
never seen the chapter. Watch their face.

If they tilt forward and listen — it works.

If they zone out, ask "wait, what?" or look at their phone —
the read-aloud is too long, too generic, or carrying too
much exposition.

A working read-aloud changes the listener's posture. It
lowers the voice in the room. It is the chapter's most
direct contact with the table.
