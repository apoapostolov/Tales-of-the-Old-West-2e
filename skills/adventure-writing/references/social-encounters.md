# Social Encounters

A social encounter is any scene where the players'
primary agency is verbal or relational rather than
physical. The party's choices are who to talk to,
what to reveal, what to ask, and what to offer.

Social encounters are not filler between combats.
They are the mechanism by which the adventure's
truth is assembled: NPCs carry information, and
information moves through conversation.

## Social encounter grammar

The scene grammar from `references/scene-design.md`
applies with adjustments.

**Read-aloud**: shorter than action read-aloud. The
NPC's opening posture — what they are doing when
the party arrives, and their first words or silence.
This establishes the NPC's stance before the
conversation begins.

> Aldermoor doesn't look up when you enter. He
> finishes writing a line in his ledger, caps the
> ink, and turns in his chair. "The land office is
> closed on Fridays," he says. He has not stood.

**Ground truth** (GM-only): what the NPC knows,
what they want from this conversation, what they
are afraid of, and the specific things that will
move them.

**NPC behavior entry**: the NPC's opening stance
and two or three **pivot points** — thresholds that
shift their stance when crossed.

## Pivot points

A pivot point is a specific thing the party can
say, reveal, offer, or demonstrate that changes
an NPC's willingness to engage, cooperate, or
resist.

Pivot points are thresholds, not single correct
answers. Multiple approaches can reach the same
pivot.

Wrong: "If the player says 'I know about the Reyes
contract,' Aldermoor opens up." (This is a password.
It punishes players who don't guess the magic phrase.)

Right: "If the party demonstrates knowledge of the
land-deed irregularity — by producing any relevant
document, naming a specific year, or revealing that
they spoke with Reyes — Aldermoor's defensive
posture cracks."

The threshold is knowledge of the land-deed
irregularity. Multiple ways in: document, year,
name, a relevant NPC reference. The player has
agency. The GM has a clear signal.

Every social encounter entry should have two to
three pivot points. At least one should be from an
approach category the author did not design for
— physical, environmental, or emotional — with a
rough threshold rather than a specific trigger.

"If the party uses the environment against
Aldermoor — his vulnerable position, a visible
threat outside the window, evidence of someone
watching — he adjusts."

## Stakes

A social encounter with no consequence is filler.
Every social encounter must:

- Shift at least one consequence track, OR
- Reveal at least one piece of truth the party
  didn't have before, OR
- Open or close at least one forward path in the
  adventure.

If the encounter does none of these three things,
cut it or merge it with an encounter that does.

## Pressure as design tool

Social encounters under time pressure produce the
same tension as combat. Time limits enforce decision.

A social encounter's time pressure:

- A guard patrol due to pass in ten minutes.
- An NPC who has a train to catch.
- Someone who will overhear if the conversation
  runs longer than five minutes.
- An NPC who will leave the room once a specific
  event happens.

The time pressure is in the scene's setup, not
mentioned mid-scene as a GM intervention. The players
should know the limit when the scene begins.

## The social roll and fail-forward

When a social roll fails, the NPC does not simply
refuse. They respond with:

- A counteroffer (what they want in exchange).
- A demand (a cost before they'll engage).
- A warning (what happens if the party pushes
  further).
- An escalation (they call for help, or become
  hostile, or leave).

Social failure is never a dead end. It changes the
available approaches. The scene may become more
costly to navigate, but the path does not close.

Write the fail-forward response in the NPC behavior
entry: "If the party fails to move Aldermoor: he
calls for his assistant and has the party escorted
to the street. The back window of the office is
now visible from the alley."

The failure response opens a new angle or raises
a cost. Both outcomes are valid.

## NPCs with competing social agendas

A scene with three NPCs who want different things
from the party is richer than a one-on-one interview.
Each NPC reacts to what the others say or reveal.

When designing multi-NPC social scenes:

- Define each NPC's social goal in this specific
  conversation (not their general motivation — what
  do they want to walk away with from _this_ meeting?).
- Define at least one NPC pair with conflicting
  goals: A wants the party to accept their version;
  B wants the party to hear something A would suppress.
- Define what each NPC will do if another NPC
  contradicts them in front of the party.

The party in a multi-NPC social scene has social
terrain to navigate. They can play NPCs against
each other, reveal information to one that changes
another's behavior, or withdraw from one NPC's
faction to strengthen another's trust.

## Interrogation scenes

An interrogation is a social encounter where the party
holds positional power and the NPC has information.
The design challenge: what is the limit of coercion?

Write three things for an interrogation scene:

1. **What the NPC will say freely**: usually less
   than the party expects.
2. **What the NPC will say under pressure** (social
   pressure, evidence presented, appeals to interest
   or fear): this is the core exchange of the
   interrogation.
3. **What the NPC will not say regardless**: usually
   protected by a fear greater than the party
   represents. To extract this requires crossing a
   line. Name the line.

Then write: **what crossing the line costs**. An
NPC broken by force or coercion beyond a threshold
will say something — but it may change the adventure's
faction relationships, create a witness, or produce
information that is wrong because the NPC is giving
the party what they want to hear.

An interrogation that produces always-reliable
information regardless of method is not an
interrogation — it is a locked door with a required
key.

## Social dilemmas

Every major social encounter should contain at least
one no-clean-option moment. See `references/dilemmas.md`.

For social encounters specifically: the dilemma is
often about what to reveal. The party cannot both
tell NPC A the truth and protect NPC B. They cannot
both leverage what they know and keep it secret.
Demonstrating their evidence to move one NPC may
alert another.

Information asymmetry is the material of social
dilemmas. The party knows things NPCs don't.
NPCs know things the party doesn't. What to reveal,
to whom, and when — these are the decisions.

## Anti-patterns

**The monologue dump**: an NPC gives a speech while
the players listen. The players have no agency during
the information delivery. Cut the monologue. Convert
the information to a series of responsive exchanges:
the NPC gives a fragment, the players react, the NPC
continues.

**The single correct answer**: only one phrase,
only one approach unlocks the NPC. This is a
password. Replace with a threshold (see Pivot Points
above).

**The automatic failure**: an NPC who will never
cooperate regardless of what the party does. This
is valid only if the NPC is designed to be a wall
that the party must go around (and the "around" is
visible). If the party is supposed to get information
from this NPC, automatic failure is a dead end.

**The performative social roll**: a social roll
made without stakes, where success and failure
produce no meaningful difference in outcome. If
it doesn't matter whether the roll succeeds, don't
ask for the roll.

**Emotion-telling**: the NPC entry tells the GM
that the NPC is "nervous" without specifying how
that nervousness manifests in behavior. An NPC who
is nervous does something with their hands, looks
at an exit, answers the last question before the
current one. Behavior, not labels.

## The leverage inventory

Before drafting a major social encounter, write a leverage
inventory: the specific things the party can bring into
this conversation that affect the NPC.

```
LEVERAGE INVENTORY — Aldermoor at the land office

What the party may possess by this point:
- Knowledge of the Reyes contract irregularity
- The forged signature page (physical evidence)
- Testimony from the stable hand placing Holt at
  the north pasture
- Aldermoor's outstanding debt to the bank (rumour
  level, unproven)
- A relationship with Sheriff Carver (positive or
  negative depending on prior scenes)

What the NPC has heard about the party:
- That they were asking questions in town (low risk)
- That they spoke with Reyes (medium risk — Aldermoor
  becomes guarded)
- That they recovered the deed (high risk — Aldermoor
  prepares to leave town)
```

The inventory exposes which scenes have abundant leverage
(the party has many tools and many angles — the scene is
rich) and which are leverage-poor (the party has nothing
to bring — the scene will be flat or short). Adjust scene
placement and prerequisite design based on what the
inventory reveals.

## NPC stance shifts as visible signals

When an NPC's stance changes during a conversation, the
shift should be visible to the players, not just tracked
in the GM's notes.

Signal vocabulary:

- A physical change: they sit down, they uncross their arms,
  they pour themselves a drink, they close the ledger.
- A change in address: they switch from "sir" to a name, or
  the reverse.
- A change in pace: they speak faster or slower, more
  formally or more colloquially.
- A small admission: a fact they would not have shared
  before the pivot.

When the GM signals the shift, players read it. They
recognize that something they did just worked. The
conversation becomes a system the players can engage with,
not a black box that returns a result.

Write one signal per pivot in the NPC behavior entry.
