# Annotated Yellowstone Line — A Worked Example

This file pairs excerpts from the Yellowstone Line chapter
(`supplement-2-montana-campaign/book-content/02-the-yellowstone-line.md`)
with annotations showing the patterns from this skill at
work. Read it as a tour of how the principles look on the
page.

Each section quotes the chapter, then names what it is doing
and which reference module covers it.

---

## 1. Cold open

**Chapter text:**

> _"The flies were thick on the horse lines. A mule stamped
> and rattled its chain. Farther off, by the parade ground,
> somebody was laughing too loud over a bottle. Fort Ellis
> looked steady in the late light. Whitewashed walls. Flag
> limp in the heat. Wagons in a row. Men who believed a post
> and a pay chest made a hard country obey._
>
> _It took one dead teamster and a saddle cut loose in the
> dark to show what the country thought of that."_

**What it does:**

- Opens on **sensory specifics** (flies, mule chain, the
  laugh "too loud"). No exposition.
- Establishes **moment** (late light), **space** (Fort Ellis,
  parade ground), **motion** (mule stamping, men laughing),
  **bodies** (whoever is laughing, the soldiers implied
  behind the order).
- Closes on the **hard line** that names the chapter's rot
  without naming the plot: "what the country thought of
  that." The country has an opinion. The chapter is about
  what that opinion costs.
- Two italic paragraphs. Roughly five-and-two sentences.
  Cadence varies. Final sentence is short, declarative,
  ends on a hard image.

**See:** `references/read-aloud.md`. This is the model
read-aloud.

## 2. Stakes paragraph

**Chapter text:**

> The Yellowstone basin forces an `1873` Montana campaign
> open.
>
> Nothing in it sits quiet. The Army wants prestige and
> safe roads. Surveyors want a route other men will turn
> into stock, town lots, and contracts. Gallatin merchants
> want the rails bent toward their stores. Crow scouts want
> the Army deaf to everything but Crow interests. Lakota
> riders mean to bleed the white line before it crosses
> good hunting ground. The margins wait to see which ruin
> arrives first. The player characters drop into that
> violence.

**What it does:**

- One paragraph after the cold open. Names the era (`1873`),
  the place (Yellowstone basin, Montana), and the rot (five
  factions with incompatible wants).
- Each faction gets one sentence naming **want + method**.
  The Army wants prestige _and_ safe roads. Surveyors want
  routes _that other men will turn into property_. The
  method is in the want.
- The final sentence drops the players into the situation
  without prescribing their role.

**See:** `references/architecture.md` (stakes paragraph
section), `references/factions-and-tracks.md` (faction
want/method/view).

## 3. Historical note

**Chapter text:**

> Historical note: In `1873`, railroad pressure in Montana
> remained mostly survey stakes. Men fought over routes,
> grades, and paper before locomotives crossed the
> territory.

**What it does:**

- Two sentences. Anchors the chapter to a specific year and
  a specific condition.
- Tells the GM something **immediately usable**: this is a
  fight over _paper, not steel_. The Army has not yet
  arrived in force. The killings are done by hired men.
- Stays in the chapter's voice. No academic register. The
  word "stakes" carries double weight (survey stakes and
  the colloquial sense).

**See:** `references/historical-grounding.md`.

## 4. The Shape of It

**Chapter text:**

> The party joins the column at Fort Ellis. A killing and a
> night raid prove the rot started before the wagons moved.
> Field work turns political. A Crow warning offers a clean
> path while greed and Army pride demand another. The papers
> split. Men shoot one another in camp. The expedition
> shatters into frightened men, wounded stock, and rival
> truths. What survives the ride back becomes the first
> leverage players hold in Montana.

**What it does:**

- Six sentences. Names the three arcs: opening (Fort Ellis +
  raid), middle (field work + Crow warning + papers split),
  closing (camp violence + reckoning).
- Tells the GM the **rhythm**: where the breath is (the
  joining), where the hammer falls (the killing, the raid,
  the camp shootout), where the reckoning sits (the ride
  back).
- Spoils nothing. Says "papers split" without saying which
  faction wins. Says "men shoot one another" without
  naming who dies.

**See:** `references/architecture.md` (shape-of-it section),
`references/pacing-and-arcs.md` (three-act rhythm).

## 5. The five-sentence NPC

**Chapter text:**

> `Captain Nathaniel Rowe` is a white man in his early
> forties, Army to the bone, narrow through the waist, and
> hard in the saddle. He is order held up by temper. He
> keeps gloves clean in dirt and looks past men before they
> finish speaking. He talks in clipped sentences. He sees
> players as irregular tools: necessary for a mean country,
> but not people he trusts before they hold their nerve.

**What it does:**

- Sentence 1: **Body and presence.** Age, race, posture,
  service identity. Concrete: "narrow through the waist,"
  "hard in the saddle."
- Sentence 2: **Defining quality.** "Order held up by
  temper." A short phrase that compresses the character.
- Sentence 3: **Mannerism, observable.** Gloves clean in
  dirt. Looking past men before they finish speaking. Both
  things you could see at a table. No interior states.
- Sentence 4: **Speech register.** "Clipped sentences." The
  GM now knows how to play him.
- Sentence 5: **Stance toward the party.** "Irregular
  tools" + the condition that flips the stance ("before
  they hold their nerve"). Players know what they need to
  prove to shift Rowe.

**See:** `references/npcs.md`. This is the canonical
template.

## 6. The rumor table

**Chapter text:**

> | D10 | Rumor                                             |
> | --- | ------------------------------------------------- |
> | 1   | Rowe asked for regular troops. Washington refused |
>
>       him. Washington wants it small, or somebody bought
>       the refusal. |
>
> | 2 | Silas Vane holds no rank but officers clear their
> tables for him. He carries money for men who stay
> off the paper. |
> | 4 | Professor Reed measures ground honestly. In Montana,
> honest measurement ruins expensive plans. |
> | 10 | If the expedition breaks, one story blames Indians,
> one blames weather, and the truth sells to the
> highest bidder. |

**What it does:**

- D10 table. Each rumor one or two sentences.
- Mix of registers: some are intelligence (#1), some are
  character (#2), some are interpretive (#4), some are
  meta-commentary on the chapter's own ambiguity (#10).
- Each rumor seeds at least one **track** before the players
  know the tracks exist. #1 seeds Army Respect. #2 seeds
  Survey Leverage. #4 seeds the Reed/Vane fault line. #10
  primes the players to read the chapter as a story being
  written _around_ them.
- Some rumors are partly false ("Washington wants it small,
  or somebody bought the refusal" — both, neither, or one).

**See:** `references/architecture.md` (rumors section),
`references/clue-discipline.md` (rumors as carriers).

## 7. Consequence ledger — the four tracks

**Chapter text:**

> Track party standing with four groups: `Army Respect`,
> `Survey Leverage`, `Crow Trust`, and `Local Goodwill`.
> Start each at `0`. When players make a hard choice, shift
> the tracks `+1` or `-1`. The ride back into settlement
> cashes the totals.

**What it does:**

- Four tracks. Not two (binary), not five-plus (unmanageable).
- Each track names a **relationship or a resource**, never a
  virtue. "Crow Trust" not "honor." "Survey Leverage" not
  "ambition."
- Tracks shift on **decision**, not outcome. ("When players
  make a hard choice.")
- Cash-out logic explicit: "the ride back into settlement
  cashes the totals."
- Coverage rule satisfied: every named NPC and every faction
  in the chapter touches at least one track.

**See:** `references/factions-and-tracks.md`.

## 8. Faction Want / Method / View

**Chapter text:**

> - **Vane and the private line:** Vane takes route
>   knowledge before public release. He only needs paper to
>   sell certainty to the next buyer.

**What it does:**

- One paragraph, two sentences.
- **Want:** Route knowledge before public release.
- **Method:** Paper acquisition.
- **View** (implied across his NPC entry): Sees players as
  purchased assets or obstacles requiring division.

The faction entry compresses to three components without
naming them as such. The reader absorbs Vane's interest
shape.

**See:** `references/factions-and-tracks.md`.

## 9. Clue carriers — the three-clue rule

**Chapter text:**

> One failed roll does not end the chapter.
>
> Spread facts across layers.
>
> - Bell's killing points to corruption.
> - Keene points to copied notes.
> - Mercer points to divided packets.
> - Vane points to route dealing.
> - Pike points to freight theft.
> - Shows-the-Ford points to the cost of the push.
>
> If players miss a clue, move the truth into another
> mouth, pocket, or shallow grave.

**What it does:**

- States the principle ("one failed roll does not end the
  chapter") then enumerates the carriers.
- Six carriers for the central truth that the expedition is
  rotten: a body, two living liars, two factional
  antagonists, and a cultural witness.
- Closing line institutes the **fail-forward rule**: if a
  carrier dies or is missed, the truth moves to a new
  carrier ("another mouth, pocket, or shallow grave").

**See:** `references/clue-discipline.md`.

## 10. Camp scenes as a shelf

**Chapter text:**

> - **The bad coffee fire:** Troopers and a survey hand
>   argue over axle-grease coffee. A man says nobody rides
>   here for the pay. Players learn who trusts the work and
>   who understands the rot.
> - **A prayer over Bell:** A laundress says hard words over
>   Bell's body. The dead stop abstracting. The group
>   remembers Bell fed kin before he bled out.

**What it does:**

- Each camp scene one to three sentences.
- **Sensory hook + NPC anchor + what the scene offers** in
  one compact entry.
- Used as a **breath shelf** between hammer beats. The GM
  selects what fits the table's mood.
- The Bell prayer scene is the chapter's conscience: the
  dead character becomes a person _after_ death, through
  someone else's mouth. The mechanic that powers the
  chapter's grief is built into a one-line camp scene.

**See:** `references/pacing-and-arcs.md` (camp scenes as
breath), `references/architecture.md` (camp shelf section).

## 11. Robustness clauses

**Chapter text:**

> - **If Keene dies early:** Move his copy to Mercer, Bell's
>   satchel, or Reed's spare case. The copied-paper rot
>   survives Keene.
> - **If Mercer dies early:** Players find his cipher marks,
>   account slips, or letter drafts. Mercer breathes to be
>   useful; the chapter does not need his lungs.
> - **If Vane drops dead in the field:** Good. Dead men
>   holding paper still warp counties.

**What it does:**

- Explicit "if X" failsafes for every fragile NPC.
- The voice is dry and confident: "Mercer breathes to be
  useful; the chapter does not need his lungs." This tells
  the GM the writer planned for the death.
- The chapter is **anti-railroad** by construction. No NPC
  is essential. The function survives the body.

**See:** `references/anti-railroad.md`,
`references/npcs.md` (robustness clauses).

## 12. Scene grammar — Scene 3 ("The picket-line killing")

**Chapter text (compressed):**

> Near dark, mule skinner Joe Bell bleeds out by the
> remuda.
>
> Read or paraphrase:
> _"Bell lies dead in the dirt, one arm crushed under his
> weight. A cut rope trails over his boot. A horse watches
> the body and rolls one eye white."_
>
> A heavy blow struck him. A blade cut his jaw to finish it.
> One hitch rope snapped. A saddle blanket and a dispatch
> satchel vanished.
>
> The ground tracks trouble. Army issue heels cross civilian
> boots. A survey pencil shaving rests near Bell's hand. Bell
> drank with Mercer. Earlier, Bell watched Thomas Keene dig
> through a chest. Players push Mercer, Keene, Pike's men,
> and the guards.
>
> Players escalate. If they clap Keene in irons, Vane
> squeezes hard elsewhere. If they beat Mercer, he panics
> and plots to run. If they force Rowe to freeze the
> column, he does it and carries the fury into the field.
>
> A laundress kneels by Bell. She speaks hard truth over
> him. Bell had a sister waiting on cash.

**What it does:**

- **Read-aloud.** Three sentences. Sensory only. Closes on
  the hard image (the horse with the white eye).
- **Ground truth.** What is actually true at the scene:
  blow, blade, missing satchel.
- **Player levers.** The physical evidence (heels, pencil
  shaving) and the social evidence (Bell drank with Mercer,
  watched Keene). Players can investigate either.
- **Branches.** Three named branches with consequences.
  Each leads somewhere different. None of them ends the
  chapter.
- **Residue.** The laundress and the sister. The dead
  character becomes a person.

This scene contains every part of the scene grammar.

**See:** `references/scene-design.md`.

## 13. Combat block — sample

**Chapter text:**

> **Combat Encounter: Raid on the Remuda**
>
> - **Enemies:** `1 REMUDA CUTTER` inside the line, `4-6
HIRED RIDERS`, and `1-2 HIRED WHITE SABOTEURS`.
> - **Area:** The Fort Ellis horse yard: picket ropes, hitch
>   rails, feed crates, wagons, and packed animals.
> - **Terrain and Effects:** Darkness blurs sightlines.
>   Loose horses knock men down, foul firing lines, and
>   force `MOVE` and `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` tests. Stakes and
>   dragged ropes foul footing. Lit yards make targets
>   visible to everyone.

**What it does:**

- Three lines. **Enemies / Area / Terrain and Effects.**
- Enemies are tag-only. No stat blocks in the chapter; those
  live in the appendix.
- Area is one evocative line — a list of objects that paints
  the place.
- Terrain and Effects is three to four sentences. Each
  sentence names a **tactical element**:
  - Darkness (visibility)
  - Loose horses (movement / NPC chaos)
  - Stakes and ropes (footing)
  - Lit yards (moral pressure: light yourself to see, become
    a target)

**See:** `references/combat-encounters.md`.

## 14. The dilemma — Scene 6 (Thomas Keene)

**Chapter text (compressed):**

> Players surrender Keene to Rowe, hide him, sell him to
> Vane, or strip him and run him off. Army surrender spikes
> `Army Respect`. Holding copies builds `Survey Leverage`.
> Selling him kills `Local Goodwill`.
>
> - Army delivery gives Rowe a grip on the rot. Vane shifts
>   to deniable hands.
> - Hiding Keene grants truth. Mercer plots betrayal before
>   the ford.
> - Selling Keene brings him back later, wounded or offering
>   testimony for life.
> - Letting him run dulls the evidence but plants him in
>   Bozeman down the line.

**What it does:**

- Four named options.
- Each option costs something. None is clean. Surrendering
  Keene helps the Army but hardens Vane. Hiding him gets
  truth but invites betrayal. Selling him is profitable and
  damning. Letting him go is the cleanest but loses
  leverage.
- Each option **shifts a track**. Mechanical consequences
  match narrative consequences.
- The dilemma is **per-arc** (one major one in Arc I) and
  about a **named NPC** the players have spoken to.

**See:** `references/dilemmas.md`.

## 15. The dialogue test

**Chapter text:**

> _"You ride, watch, and keep your mouths shut. If you
> cannot do the first two, save us the waste and walk away
> now."_

That is Rowe.

> _"A line belongs where the ground permits it. Not where a
> merchant dreams it."_

That is Reed.

> _"Dreams are what build towns, Professor. Your
> measurements only tell the rest of us where to spend the
> money."_

That is Vane.

> _"You put stakes in the ground, and the rest follows
> behind. Tax men. Claim men. Men saying your creek was
> theirs on paper before you ever drank from it. I have
> seen the breed."_

That is Hannah Calder.

**What it does:**

- Each character is identifiable from one quoted line.
- Rowe: imperative, transactional, uses "us" for the unit.
- Reed: declarative, opposes "ground" to "dream," won't
  argue with merchants on their terms.
- Vane: condescending ("Professor"), reframes the issue,
  ends on money.
- Hannah: enumerative ("Tax men. Claim men. Men saying..."),
  speaks from observation ("I have seen the breed"),
  refuses generality.

The chapter passes the dialogue test: strip the
attributions, the speakers remain identifiable.

**See:** `references/npcs.md` (dialogue test), `references/read-aloud.md`
(speech vs. read-aloud).

## 16. Multi-ending reckoning

**Chapter text:**

> #### Army victory
>
> The packet lands clean. Rowe holds reputation. The Army
> uses players for ugly jobs.
>
> #### Railroad shadow victory
>
> The players sell or hold copies. They stand rich,
> corrupted, and hunted.
>
> #### Local Montana victory
>
> Fraud hits the light. Ranchers win a breath. The party
> gains local friends.
>
> #### Crow understanding
>
> Respected alliances adjust Crow hostility. Safe passage
> requires maintenance.
>
> #### Bad ending, good sandbox
>
> Liars prosper. The party flees carrying proof under a
> heavy coat. The territory offers no rest.

**What it does:**

- Five end-states. One per dominant track plus a "no
  dominant track" failure mode.
- Each ending is two or three sentences. None is described
  as "good" or "bad" — each has a cost and an opening.
- The "Bad ending, good sandbox" entry is the chapter's
  honesty: when players fail at every track, the chapter
  hands them a _campaign_. The failure becomes fuel.
- Each ending **opens the sandbox** with specific hooks:
  Army uses them for ugly jobs / they're hunted / they have
  local friends / they need to maintain Crow alliances /
  they carry proof and have nowhere to rest.

**See:** `references/endings-and-sandbox.md`.

## 17. Sandbox exits

**Chapter text:**

> Bozeman and Fort Ellis offer military runs. Helena demands
> paper and blackmail. Virginia City demands law. Fort
> Benton demands river freight. Southern boundaries demand
> Crow promises fulfilled.

**What it does:**

- Five directions. Each is one sentence.
- Each direction names a **place** and a **kind of work**.
- Mix of registers: military (Bozeman/Ellis), political
  (Helena), legal (Virginia City), commercial (Fort
  Benton), diplomatic (southern boundaries).
- The chapter ends with the country opening up, not closing
  down.

**See:** `references/endings-and-sandbox.md` (sandbox
exits).

## 18. The closing GM note

**Chapter text:**

> ## GM Notes on Tone
>
> Men demand cash, horses, contracts, and power. No one
> speaks their theme. They demand the payload. The rest
> bleeds out later.

**What it does:**

- Three sentences. Tells the GM the chapter's central
  craft rule: **no one speaks their theme**. Characters
  speak about their immediate wants. Theme emerges from the
  events.
- This is the **anti-railroad theme rule** (see
  `references/anti-railroad.md`) given as a closing
  instruction.
- "The rest bleeds out later" both names the chapter's tone
  (violent, slow, residual) and instructs the GM on pacing
  (don't rush the consequences; let them surface across
  sessions).

**See:** `references/anti-railroad.md` (theme as rail),
`references/architecture.md` (GM notes).

## What this chapter doesn't do

Equally instructive. The Yellowstone Line never:

- Tells the players what they should feel about the
  expedition.
- Names a "main villain" in the cast list.
- Provides a "boss fight" that any single player decision
  unlocks.
- Includes a scene the players cannot refuse.
- Contains a clue the chapter cannot survive losing.
- Lectures on the historical injustice of westward
  expansion. (The injustice is enacted by Pike's billing,
  Hannah's brand register, the laundress's hard words, the
  Crow warning ignored. The reader reaches the theme without
  being told it.)
- Closes on a clean victory.

These absences are choices. They are what makes the chapter
robust, replayable, and weighty.

## How to use this annotation

When drafting a new chapter, return to this file and locate
the section that matches what you are writing. The chapter
under review is a working model. The principles in the
references are abstracted from it. Reading the reference
plus the annotation gives you both the rule and a pattern
of the rule in practice.

When auditing a draft, the inverse: identify what your
draft is missing, find the corresponding annotation, and
study the move the chapter makes.
