# Comparative Patterns — The Same Craft, Two Adventures

This file is the agnostic tour. It takes single design
decisions and shows how two different adventures — the
_Yellowstone Line_ (a three-act chapter) and _Quiet Title_
(a fixed-node sandbox) — solved the same problem in ways
that serve their different structures. Read it to learn
that the architecture is portable and the shape it takes
should serve the situation, not the template.

The principle: **no design decision has one correct answer.
It has an answer that fits the structure you chose.** A
cold open that works for a chapter will fail a sandbox, and
the reverse. This file shows the fit.

---

## Pattern 1 — The cold open

**_Yellowstone Line_ (chapter):** opens on Fort Ellis at
late light — the post the party will depart from. Establishes
the place the action begins and the rot inside it. The party
arrives next and is hired into the column.

**_Quiet Title_ (sandbox):** opens on a dead surveyor in a
creek, eight inches deep, with the Company's report calling
it drowning. No post, no party, no briefing. Establishes the
_country_ and the _corruption_ before the party exists in the
scene, because the party will choose its own entry.

**The craft rule:** a chapter cold open establishes the
_departure point_ (the party is about to go somewhere from
here). A sandbox cold open establishes the _country_ (the
party is about to go _into_ here). The first must orient
toward a single trajectory. The second must orient toward a
field of possibility.

See `references/read-aloud.md` and
`references/sandbox-structure.md`.

## Pattern 2 — The central prize

**_Yellowstone Line_ (chapter):** one prize — the survey
packet. Multiple paths to it, multiple factions wanting it,
but one object the whole chapter's tension orbits. The party
is always, eventually, carrying or chasing the packet.

**_Quiet Title_ (sandbox):** nine levers, none mandatory.
The field book is the loudest, but the adventure is
explicitly designed to reach a reckoning if the party
_refuses the field book entirely_ and wins through the
grant language, the affidavit, the bought debt, or the
railroad's money.

**The craft rule:** a chapter can run on one prize because
the party is moving toward a climax and the prize is the
climax's hinge. A sandbox must run on a lever menu because
the party is moving _through a country_ and may never touch
the designed prize. One prize is a heist; many sufficient
prizes is a country.

See `references/levers-and-fallbacks.md`.

## Pattern 3 — Time pressure

**_Yellowstone Line_ (chapter):** pressure is the column's
march. The party is inside a moving expedition; the pressure
is the next day's ride, the next ambush, the supplies
dwindling. Time pressure is _kinetic_ — the party is being
carried forward.

**_Quiet Title_ (sandbox):** pressure is six faction clocks
advancing on the in-game week, whether the party acts or
not. The Company tightens its screw; the camp gathers guns;
the flour price holds at six cents and another family falls
behind. Time pressure is _environmental_ — the country
changes around the party.

**The craft rule:** chapter pressure pushes the party.
Sandbox pressure surrounds the party. The first is a
current the party rides; the second is a tide the party
navigates. Getting this backwards produces a chapter that
feels static or a sandbox that feels railroady.

See `references/clocks-and-timers.md` and
`references/sandbox-structure.md`.

## Pattern 4 — The "what if they break it" section

**_Yellowstone Line_ (chapter):** "If the Players Miss,
Freeze, or Break the Plan" — a patch list. For each major
NPC death or party deviation, names how the adventure
redistributes the function. Reactive. Necessary in a chapter
because the chapter has a plan the party can deviate from.

**_Quiet Title_ (sandbox):** "When the Party Shortcuts the
Plot" — a doctrine. Opens with "In a sandbox there is no
plan to break." Answers eleven common shortcuts with the
next true consequence, never a reset. Proactive.

**The craft rule:** a chapter _has a plan_, so it needs a
patch list. A sandbox _is a situation_, so it needs a
doctrine. The patch list keeps the chapter's track intact;
the doctrine keeps the sandbox's country answering. Writing
one when you need the other produces either a brittle
sandbox or a soggy chapter.

See `references/sandbox-structure.md` and
`references/anti-railroad.md`.

## Pattern 5 — The cast's relationship to the party

**_Yellowstone Line_ (chapter):** the cast is largely
_fixed_ to the column — Rowe, Reed, Vane, Pike, Mercer,
Keene, LaRoche all ride with or alongside the expedition.
The party meets them by being in the column. The cast's
views of the party _evolve_ as the column moves.

**_Quiet Title_ (sandbox):** the cast is _distributed_
across fixed nodes — Hadley in the town, Don Ignacio in
the plaza, Strand in the camp, Nah-leh in the high country.
The party meets them by traveling to their node. Each
principal is the _embodiment of a node_ and a _faction's
interest_.

**The craft rule:** in a chapter, the cast travels with
the party (or is encountered along a single trajectory).
In a sandbox, the cast is anchored — each principal _is_
a reason to visit a node. A sandbox cast that travels
freely collapses the node structure; a chapter cast that
is too anchored stalls the column.

See `references/npcs.md` and `references/sandbox-structure.md`
on the fixed node.

## Pattern 6 — The reckoning

**_Yellowstone Line_ (chapter):** the reckoning is a
_convergence_ — the party reaches settlement space and
everyone wants the packet first. The party's choice of
venue and ally determines the end-state. The reckoning is
driven _toward_.

**_Quiet Title_ (sandbox):** the reckoning is a _convening_
— the hearing convenes on the first Monday of June whether
the party is ready or not, and the clocks' totals plus the
party's accumulated levers determine what happens in that
room. The reckoning is driven _toward the party_.

**The craft rule:** a chapter reckoning is the climax the
party drives toward and shapes. A sandbox reckoning is the
moment the country's clocks cash out and the party must
live with the result. The first rewards aggressive play;
the second rewards attentive play.

See `references/endings-and-sandbox.md` and
`references/sandbox-structure.md`.

## Pattern 7 — Historical grounding

**_Yellowstone Line_ (chapter):** historical notes are
_inline inserts_ — short bracketed paragraphs setting the
real context for a fictional scene ("Historical note: Fort
Ellis was a real post east of Bozeman, founded in `1867`...").
The grounding decorates and deepens without leaving the
flow.

**_Quiet Title_ (sandbox):** historical grounding is a
_separate companion document_ (`01B-historical-facts-and-
authenticity.md`) — an eight-part treatise covering the
real Maxwell Grant history, the surveyor's trade, the
plaza's life, and an explicit ledger of what is invented
vs. real. The adventure stays playable; the treatise goes
deep.

**The craft rule:** light grounding (a real event behind
a fictional scene) earns inline notes. Deep grounding (a
whole political economy behind the adventure) earns a
companion document. The choice is about _weight_, not
preference. A treatise attached to a light adventure
overwhelms it; inline notes on a deeply-grounded adventure
under-explain it.

See `references/historical-grounding.md`.

## Pattern 8 — The consequence ledger

**_Yellowstone Line_ (chapter):** four tracks — `Army
Respect`, `Survey Leverage`, `Crow Trust`, `Local Goodwill`.
Each shifts on major choices. The reckoning reads the four
totals.

**_Quiet Title_ (sandbox):** four tracks — `Comunidad
Trust`, `Company Pressure`, `Gold Camp Standing`,
`Mountain Trust` — _plus_ six faction clocks that advance
on time. The tracks record relationships; the clocks
advance situations. The sandbox uses both because the
country has both a state (who likes the party) and a
trajectory (what is happening regardless).

**The craft rule:** a chapter needs tracks because the
party's choices are the main input. A sandbox needs tracks
_and_ clocks because the country's motion is also an input.
Tracks without clocks produce a static sandbox. Clocks
without tracks produce a sandbox that happens _to_ the
party without regard for what the party did.

See `references/factions-and-tracks.md` and
`references/clocks-and-timers.md`.

## Pattern 9 — Robustness / the dead-NPC test

**_Yellowstone Line_ (chapter):** robustness handled in
prose. "If Keene dies too soon: move his truth into Mercer,
Bell's missing satchel, or a copy hidden in Reed's spare
instrument case." Each major NPC has an inline fallback
clause.

**_Quiet Title_ (sandbox):** robustness handled in a table.
The "Truth Survives the Holder" table names, for every
load-bearing NPC, where the truth migrates on death,
flight, or bribery. Faster to read at the table; covers
more NPCs.

**The craft rule:** both work. Prose fallbacks suit a
chapter (fewer NPCs, more integrated text). Tabular
fallbacks suit a sandbox (more NPCs, more moving parts).
The artifact should match the structure's density.

See `references/levers-and-fallbacks.md`, "The
truth-survives-the-holder table."

## Pattern 10 — Read-aloud sensory registers

**_Yellowstone Line_ (chapter):** the sensory register is
the _expedition_ — horse lines, mule chains, parade
ground, bacon, harness grease. The country is experienced
_through the column's logistics_.

**_Quiet Title_ (sandbox):** the sensory register is
_node-specific_ — the plaza smells of piñon smoke and
chile ristras; the camp smells of mercury and powder; the
courtroom smells of kerosene and chew tobacco; the cañón
smells of vanilla-bark ponderosa and snowmelt. Each node
has its own palette because each node is a different
world.

**The craft rule:** a chapter's sensory register can be
singular because the party moves through one continuous
experience. A sandbox's sensory register must be plural
because the party moves between distinct worlds. The node
pallettes are how the party knows, in its body, that it
has arrived somewhere new.

See `references/read-aloud.md` and
`references/location-design.md`.

## How to use this file

When you sit down to design an adventure, first decide:
chapter or sandbox? Then read the matching column above.
The patterns are not rules; they are _demonstrations of
fit_. Your adventure will find its own fit, but it should
find it deliberately, not by accident.

If you find yourself applying a chapter pattern to a
sandbox (or vice versa) and the result feels wrong, come
back to this file. The mistake is almost always a
structure mismatch — a chapter cold open on a sandbox,
a single prize in a country, a patch list where a doctrine
should be. The fix is to swap to the pattern that matches
the structure you actually chose.
