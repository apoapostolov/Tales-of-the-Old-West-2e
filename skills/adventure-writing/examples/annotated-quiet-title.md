# Annotated Quiet Title — A Worked Sandbox Example

This file pairs excerpts from the _Quiet Title_ adventure
(`03-adventures/01-quiet-title.md`) with annotations showing
the patterns from this skill at work. Where
`annotated-yellowstone.md` tours the three-act chapter,
this file tours the **fixed-node sandbox** — the fourth
adventure shape this skill teaches.

Read the two annotated files as a pair. They show the same
craft applied to two different structures, which is the
point: the architecture is portable; the shape it takes
serves the situation.

---

## 1. Cold open that establishes country, not plot

**Adventure text:**

> The water of the Arroyo de las Vacas ran shallow over
> gravel and the surveyor's boots were still laced. He lay
> on his face in the willows with his field book under him
> and the back of his skull gone. The Company's return said
> he had drowned. The creek at that bend was not eight inches
> deep.
>
> That was the spring of `1873`. By the first cutting of hay
> the plaza was in the suit and the book had not been found.

**What it does:**

- Opens on a **body in a place**, not on the party. The
  country is established before the player characters exist
  in it. This is the sandbox cold open's job: teach the
  _world_ first, because the party will choose its own entry.
- The **mystery and the corruption arrive together** in two
  facts: the back of the skull is gone, and the official
  report said drowning. The eight-inch creek is the rebuke.
  No exposition; the contradiction does the work.
- The second paragraph names the **era and the trajectory**
  in two sentences: spring to first cutting of hay (a
  seasonal clock the party can feel), and the book unfound
  (the loudest lever, named as missing, not as goal).

**See:** `references/read-aloud.md` and
`references/sandbox-structure.md` on the cold open that
establishes country. Contrast the _Yellowstone_ open, which
establishes a _post_ the party will depart from. Both work;
they serve different structures.

## 2. The entry-point menu, not a briefing

**Adventure text:**

> The party can open play in any of five places and find a
> real first session there. Each is a door, not a station
> on a track... The plaza. The gold camp at Baldy. The
> Cimarron court. The cañón and the high country. The road
> between.

**What it does:**

- Names **five distinct entry points**, each with its own
  tone (dense/slow, loud/fast, cold/bureaucratic,
  disorienting, classic-cold-open). The party picks the door
  by where it goes, not by a briefing it receives.
- Each door produces a **different first impression** of the
  same sandbox. Entering through the plaza, the party meets
  the comunidad first. Entering through the camp, it meets
  Strand first and arrives at the plaza already aligned.
  The sandbox's meaning shifts with the entry.

**See:** `references/sandbox-structure.md`, "The entry-point
menu." This is the structural feature that most distinguishes
the sandbox from the chapter. _Yellowstone_ has one entry
(muster at the fort) because it is a chapter; _Quiet Title_
has five because it is a sandbox.

## 3. The lever menu, stated as design doctrine

**Adventure text:**

> The field book is the loudest lever. It is not the only
> one. Each lever below can break the Company's case, win
> the suit, or change the ending on its own... The field
> book. Pell's affidavit. The grant language. The cemetery
> register. The bought witness. The Company's own mail. The
> railroad's money. The plaza's debt. A range war.

**What it does:**

- Names **nine independent levers**, each sufficient on its
  own, each held by a different NPC, each costing something
  different. The party needs two or three to reach the
  strongest reckoning, but can reach _a_ reckoning with one.
- The list is **addressed to the GM, not the party**. The
  party never sees this list. It discovers levers by playing.
  The list exists so the author always has somewhere for the
  party's road to go.
- One lever (the railroad's money) **sidesteps the central
  conflict entirely**. That is the sign of a rich menu: a
  route that changes the game rather than winning the
  designed game.

**See:** `references/levers-and-fallbacks.md`. This is the
sandbox's engine room. Contrast _Yellowstone_, which has one
prize (the packet) with multiple paths to it — a chapter
design, not a sandbox design.

## 4. Faction clocks that advance without the party

**Adventure text:**

> Time moves. Track these clocks across the weeks of the
> spring court term. When a clock fills, the event happens
> whether the party is present, absent, or the cause of
> it... The Company's patience. Strand's ambition. The
> comunidad's endurance. Nah-leh's patience. Tomás Luján's
> nerve. The railroad's patience.

**What it does:**

- Names **six independent clocks**, each advancing on _time_
  (the in-game week), not on party triggers. The country
  moves whether the party watches or not.
- The clocks are **of different speeds and visibilities**.
  The flour clock is visible (it is posted at the store).
  The railroad's patience is hidden until it moves all at
  once. The mix produces both dread and surprise.
- The doctrine line — "When two clocks collide, let them" —
  is the sandbox's pacing rule. A week where two clocks fire
  is a real session; do not serialize the collisions.

**See:** `references/clocks-and-timers.md` for the mechanic,
and `references/sandbox-structure.md` for the sandbox's
specific use of clocks (advance on time, collide, fire
whether the party is present or not). _Yellowstone_ has no
faction clocks; its pressure is the column's march. This is
the structural difference.

## 5. The shortcut doctrine — say yes, find the consequence

**Adventure text:**

> In a sandbox there is no plan to break. There are only the
> next true problems the party's cleverness creates... The
> party kills Hadley... Hadley is a man; the Company is a
> machine. Killing Hadley in week two means the Company
> wires London and Santa Fe and a new superintendent rides
> up the Cimarron road by week six, leaner and colder and
> forewarnen.

**What it does:**

- Reframes the "what if they break it" section as
  **doctrine, not patch list**. The line "there is no plan
  to break" is the sandbox's creed.
- The Hadley-killed answer is the model: the shortcut does
  not end the adventure, it loads the adventure into the
  _consequences of the killing_ (a new superintendent, a
  murder investigation, federal attention, the party now
  wanted). The true next problem becomes the adventure.
- Eleven shortcuts are answered, ending with: **"The party
  does something nobody planned for. Say yes. Find the true
  consequence."** This is the line to print above the desk.

**See:** `references/sandbox-structure.md`, "When the party
short-cuts the plot," and the "reset is banned" rule. This
is the section most missing from bad modules, which patch
shortcuts with resets instead of answering them.

## 6. The truth-survives-the-holder table

**Adventure text:**

> No single NPC's death should end the chapter... If Tomás
> dies or is lost: the truth moves to Celso's dying breath;
> Halladay's letters cached with the priest; Nah-leh's
> memory of his cousin on the ridge. If Aldous Pell dies:
> his partial survey notes; his draft affidavit; his letters
> to Hadley in Hadley's safe...

**What it does:**

- Names, for **every load-bearing NPC**, where their truth
  migrates when they die, run, or are bought. This is the
  action-layer equivalent of the three-clue rule for
  information.
- The closing line — "Remove one person and the knot
  tightens elsewhere. Remove the wrong person and the knot
  tightens around the party" — names the design principle:
  death is not a dead end, it is a redirection.

**See:** `references/levers-and-fallbacks.md`, "The
truth-survives-the-holder table." This artifact belongs in
every adventure, chapter or sandbox. _Yellowstone_ carries
the same discipline in prose ("If Keene dies too soon...");
_Quiet Title_ formalizes it as a table for fast table use.

## 7. The split-party as design assumption

**Adventure text:**

> A sandbox party will divide. Two ride for Baldy, two ride
> for Cimarron, one stays in the plaza. Run it. Cut between
> the groups the way a western cuts between riders on
> different trails. Let each group's choices land on the
> other group's table.

**What it does:**

- Treats the **split party as the campaign's natural shape**,
  not an edge case to discourage. A sandbox whose nodes are
  real can sustain a split; a chapter whose track is single
  cannot.
- Names the technique: **cut between groups like a western
  cuts between riders on different trails**. This is film
  editing applied to tabletop pacing.

**See:** `references/sandbox-structure.md`, "The split-party
test." The sandbox must support the split because the party
will eventually do it; designing for it is cheaper than
fighting it.

## 8. The social scene with per-character branching

**Adventure text (the supper):**

> Don Ignacio is won by patience and by the small show of
> knowing the country... Intuit him by asking after the
> graves on the ridge rather than the lawsuit in Cimarron...
> Offend him by pulling the Company's notices off the
> doorpost before the meal, by raising your voice in his
> kitchen, or by treating Pilar as a servant.

**What it does:**

- For each principal in a social scene, names three distinct
  player approaches — **please, intuit, offend** — each with
  its own consequence-track shift. The scene is not a single
  social roll; it is four weather systems the party moves
  through.
- The "**intuit**" tier is the key innovation: a deeper read
  than "please," unlocked by understanding the NPC's actual
  concern (graves, not lawsuits). It rewards players who
  listen, not just players who roll.

**See:** `references/social-encounters.md` for the pivot-
point mechanic. The please/intuit/offend branching is a
concrete implementation of three pivot tiers per NPC, drawn
from real social play.

## 9. The historical grounding as a separate document

**Adventure text (from `01B-historical-facts-and-authenticity.md`):**

> This document is for the Game Master who wants to know,
> exactly, where the history in _Quiet Title_ ends and the
> fiction begins. It is a stamp... The adventure's spine is
> real history wearing a thin coat of invented names. The
> coat is listed at the end. Everything else is on the
> record.

**What it does:**

- Separates the **authenticity treatise** from the adventure
  itself, so the adventure stays playable and the treatise
  can be long. The GM reads the treatise once; the adventure
  is run from repeatedly.
- Includes an explicit **"where history ends and fiction
  begins" ledger**: a "real, and used accurately" column and
  an "invented, and standing in for" column. The reader can
  check any claim.

**See:** `references/historical-grounding.md`. The treatise
is the deepest form of the historical-note insert: not a
sidebar, but a companion volume. It works for any adventure
built on real history, real politics, or real science.

## What this exemplar teaches that the Yellowstone exemplar cannot

The _Yellowstone_ exemplar teaches the **chapter**: cold
open to briefing to escalating track to reckoning, all
moving the party toward a climax. It is the model for the
three-act arc.

This exemplar teaches the **sandbox**: a country of fixed
nodes the party moves between, a menu of levers none
mandatory, clocks that run without the party, shortcuts
answered with consequences, and a reckoning that reads
whatever the party actually brought. It is the model for the
fixed-node sandbox.

Read both before drafting. The adventure you are writing is
almost always one or the other, and the discipline of each
is different. The chapter asks "how do I pace the party's
drive toward the climax?" The sandbox asks "how do I make
the country rich enough that the party's choices are the
story?"
