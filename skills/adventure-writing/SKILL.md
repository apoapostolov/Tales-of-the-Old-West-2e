# Adventure Writing — A Bible for Branching, Replayable Scenarios

This skill exists because most adventures collapse into railroads,
puzzle-locked clues, or scripted heroics the moment players touch
them. The published modules that survive contact with the table
share a common architecture: **conflicting factions with stated
wants, distributed truth, consequence tracks, dilemmas without
clean answers, and endings conditional on player history.**

This bible teaches that architecture, system-agnostic. It works
for d20, PbtA, FitD, year zero, OSR, GUMSHOE, Free League, or
custom systems. It does not teach mechanics; it teaches **the
shape of an adventure that resists collapse**.

The skill is long because the job is large. Read this file first.
Then load the modules you need.

## The non-negotiables

These rules are absolute. Violate them and the adventure becomes
a railroad, a quiz, or a tour.

1. **Every faction has a Want, a Method, and a View of the PCs.**
   No faction is "the bad guys." Every faction acts on its own
   interest. The party is a variable, not the protagonist.

2. **No single roll, NPC, or clue is load-bearing.** Every
   important fact lives in at least three mouths, pockets, or
   places. Failure rerouts; it does not stall.

3. **Every scene offers four or more genuine player branches.**
   Comply, refuse, subvert, exploit, ignore. If a scene only
   tests "did they roll high enough", rewrite it.

4. **Dilemmas precede dice.** The hard moment in a scene is a
   moral or practical choice the players make as themselves. The
   roll resolves the cost, not the decision.

5. **Consequences are tracked, not narrated.** Adventures use a
   small number of named, system-agnostic counters (`+1` / `-1`)
   that cash out at the ending. The numbers shift in fiction,
   not in handwave.

6. **Every ending is conditional.** The reckoning has at least
   four distinct end-states determined by the tracks, not by GM
   decree. "Bad endings" must still seed the next adventure.

7. **The cold open shows tone, not exposition.** Open on a
   sensory image of the world's specific weight. Save the
   briefing for the second beat.

8. **Every NPC has a stance toward the PCs.** Not just "what
   they want" — what they think the PCs _are_ (tools, threats,
   marks, witnesses, peers). That stance evolves.

9. **Robustness is a feature.** Every important NPC has a "what
   if they die early" branch. Every clue has a fallback carrier.
   The adventure must survive a TPK of any single NPC.

10. **End on weight.** The closing beat of every scene is a cost,
    a decision, or an image — never a summary.

## The workflow

Use this order when drafting a fresh adventure. Skipping a step
produces the failure modes you will recognize in bad modules:
plot holes, dead clues, single-point failures, scripted endings.

### Phase 0 — Scope and Research

Do not draft a single scene until these two steps are complete.

0a. **Set scope before drafting.** Decide: one-shot / campaign
chapter / mini-campaign. Set a word-count target and a session-
count target. Scope failures (too big, too small) produce
unpublishable drafts. See `references/scope-and-format.md`.
0b. **Run the research pipeline.** Answer six research questions
about the world's political situation, economy, technology,
displaced people, daily work, and recent history. Do not draft
until all six can be answered in two sentences each. See
`references/research-pipeline.md`.

### Phase 1 — Design

1. **Pick a pressure, not a plot.** State the central pressure
   in one sentence: "A railroad survey forces a wagon column
   across hunting ground." Pressure is what makes choices hurt.
2. **Cast the factions.** Three to six. Each gets one line for
   Want, one for Method, one for View of PCs. See
   `references/factions-and-tracks.md`.
3. **Define the consequence tracks.** Three or four named
   counters, one per major faction or stake. See
   `references/factions-and-tracks.md`.
4. **Cast the named NPCs.** Use the NPC template in
   `references/npcs.md`. Each entry: physical, defining quality,
   mannerism, speech register, view of PCs.
5. **Write the cold open.** A sensory paragraph in italics.
   Then the stakes paragraph. Then the historical/situational
   frame. See `references/architecture.md` and
   `references/read-aloud.md`.
6. **Lay the arcs.** Three acts: setup (breath), pressure
   (hammer), reckoning (cash-out). See
   `references/pacing-and-arcs.md`.
7. **Design scenes as branches, not steps.** Each scene: read-
   aloud, ground-truth, NPC stance, four+ branches, track
   shifts. See `references/scene-design.md`.
8. **Distribute the truth.** No fact lives in one place. Use
   the three-clue rule and the carrier-redundancy table. See
   `references/clue-discipline.md`.
9. **Write the combat blocks.** Enemies / area / terrain effects
   / how the fight ends in fiction. See
   `references/combat-encounters.md`.
10. **Write the dilemmas.** One genuine no-clean-option choice
    per arc. See `references/dilemmas.md`.
11. **Write the multi-ending reckoning.** Four+ end-states
    keyed to track totals. See `references/endings-and-sandbox.md`.
12. **Add the failure-mode section.** "If X dies early," "If
    party sides with the antagonist," "If they refuse the
    hook." See `references/architecture.md`.
13. **Run the anti-railroad pass.** Cut every "the players
    must," "the GM should ensure," and scripted outcome. See
    `references/anti-railroad.md`.
14. **Add sandbox exits.** What threads survive into the next
    chapter. See `references/endings-and-sandbox.md`.

### Phase 3 — Playtest and Feedback Loop

Run these steps after every live session of the adventure.

15. **Playtest.** Run the adventure at a live table or arrange a
    controlled playtest session. Your job during play is to observe
    and take notes — not to explain the text or fill gaps. Gaps and
    stalls are data. See `references/playtesting.md`.
16. **Collect structured feedback.** Run the post-session debrief
    12-24 hours after the game, not immediately after. Use the
    five-question form. See `references/feedback-collection.md`
    and `templates/feedback-form.md`.
17. **Triage and file learnings.** Sort feedback into three buckets:
    (A) structural fixes for this adventure, (B) author learnings
    to prevent future failures, (C) preference mismatches (no fix
    required). File bucket-B learnings in `learnings/`. Apply
    bucket-A fixes before publishing. See `learnings/README.md`.

## How to use this bible

1. **Always read this file first.** It contains the
   non-negotiables and the workflow. Everything else supports.
2. **For a fresh adventure**, run Phase 0 first: set scope
   (`scope-and-format.md`), then run the research pipeline
   (`research-pipeline.md`). Then load `architecture.md`,
   `factions-and-tracks.md`, and `npcs.md` before drafting any
   prose. Then `scene-design.md` and `clue-discipline.md` for
   the body. Then `endings-and-sandbox.md` for the close.
3. **For a scene-level draft**, load `scene-design.md`,
   `read-aloud.md`, and `dilemmas.md`.
4. **For a combat set-piece**, load `combat-encounters.md` and
   `scene-design.md`.
5. **For an audit or revision pass**, load `anti-railroad.md`
   and `clue-discipline.md`. Run the kill list.
6. **For a historical, sci-fi, or fantasy adventure that needs
   weight**, load `historical-grounding.md`. The patterns are
   identical; only the source material changes.
7. **For an investigation or mystery adventure**, load
   `investigation-structure.md` and `node-based-design.md`.
   Use `templates/investigation-skeleton.md` for the scaffold.
8. **For a horror adventure**, load `horror-design.md`.
   Adjust pacing and read-aloud registers accordingly.
9. **For a one-shot**, load `scope-and-format.md` first.
   Use `templates/one-shot-skeleton.md` for the scaffold.
10. **For a villain or antagonist with depth**, load
    `antagonist-design.md`. Write the antagonist's perspective
    paragraph before drafting Arc I.
11. **For travel sequences or wilderness adventures**, load
    `travel-and-wilderness.md` and `location-design.md`.
12. **Pull two or three exemplars** from
    `examples/annotated-yellowstone.md` before drafting any
    section. Match the cadence.
13. **Draft, then run the anti-railroad pass.** Cut anything it
    catches. Rewrite.
14. **End on weight.** The last beat of every section is a cost,
    a decision, or an image — never a summary.
15. **After every live session**, run Phase 3 (steps 15-17).
    Observe without explaining. Debrief 24 hours later. File
    learnings before starting the next adventure.
16. **For an adventure with difficult content**, declare content
    types in the GM notes and recommend a session zero. See the
    Safety and content discipline section above. For horror
    specifically, the safety toolkit in `horror-design.md` is
    mandatory, not optional.

## The acid tests

Before you call an adventure done, run all six.

1. **The four-branch test.** Open a random scene. Can you name
   four genuine player responses, each with a different
   downstream effect? If not, redesign.
2. **The dead-NPC test.** Pick the most important NPC. Kill
   them in the first scene they appear. Does the chapter still
   reach a reckoning? If not, redistribute their function.
3. **The single-roll test.** Search the chapter for any moment
   where a missed roll halts the adventure. There should be
   none. Every gate has at least three keys.
4. **The four-ending test.** Read your reckoning. Can a careful
   reader predict the end-state from the track totals without
   GM intervention? If not, the conditions are fuzzy.
5. **The cold-open test.** Read the first paragraph aloud.
   Does it teach the world's specific weight in three sentences,
   or does it summarize the plot? If the latter, rewrite.
6. **The blind-GM test.** Hand a single scene to a GM who has
   never seen the adventure. Can they run it from the text alone
   without asking you a question? If they need clarification, the
   scene's instructions, NPC entries, or exit descriptions are
   underwritten. Fix before publication.

## A note on iteration

The workflow is presented in order, but no draft survives first
contact with this order on the first pass. Expect to:

- Draft a thin Arc I before factions and tracks are finalised,
  then loop back and revise the cold open once the tracks are
  named.
- Discover during scene drafting that an NPC needs a different
  motivation; revise the cast entry rather than forcing the scene.
- Find during the anti-railroad pass that an entire arc is
  load-bearing on a single NPC; redistribute, then re-audit.

Iteration is not failure. It is how the adventure assembles.
The order in the workflow is the order of the _final_ pass, not
the only pass.

## Safety and content discipline

Adventures touching difficult content (violence against specific
groups, body horror, intimate threat, real historical atrocities)
carry a structural responsibility to the GMs and players who will
run them.

- Declare content types in the GM notes at the top of the
  adventure, before the cold open. Use a short bracketed list
  (e.g., "Contains: lethal violence, references to historical
  ethnic displacement, brief on-page death of a child NPC").
- Recommend session zero calibration for any adventure that
  contains content beyond the system's baseline assumptions.
- Reference an in-play safety tool the table can use (X-card,
  Lines and Veils, Script Change). Do not require a specific
  one. Confirm players know how to use whichever the table picks.
- For horror specifically, see `references/horror-design.md` —
  safety calibration is not optional in horror, it is part of
  the design contract.

## What this skill is not

- **Not a system.** No mechanics, no stat blocks, no resolution
  rules. The combat-encounter block is a structural template,
  not a ruleset. Plug in your system's numbers.
- **Not a setting.** No genre commitments. Western, fantasy,
  sci-fi, modern, horror, post-apocalyptic — the architecture
  is the same.
- **Not a writing-style guide.** For prose voice, load a
  separate writing skill. This bible governs structure,
  branching, and design — not sentence-level craft.
- **Not a tournament module template.** Tournament adventures
  optimize for completion in a fixed time. Sandbox openers
  optimize for replayability and downstream consequence. This
  bible serves the latter.
