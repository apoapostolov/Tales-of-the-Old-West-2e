# Chapter 24 — The Dark Frontier

> _“THEY FOUND HER AT THE SPRING AT FIRST LIGHT. SHE HAD CRAWLED FROM WHERE THE MEN HAD LEFT HER TO THE WATER AND SHE WAS STILL TRYING TO PULL HER DRESS AROUND HER WITH THE HAND SHE COULD MOVE. THE BOY WHO FOUND HER RAN FOR HIS MOTHER AND DID NOT COME BACK TO THE SPRING FOR A YEAR.”_

The country contained much that the dime novels and the moving pictures left out. The matter of this chapter is on the historical record: sexual violence, lynching and pogrom, madness, torture, cannibalism, the drug economies, domestic killing, the abuse of children, slavery beyond the Civil War, the starving homestead, the institutional cruelty to animals. None of it was rare. All of it shaped the country the rest of the book describes.

---

## 1. Sexual Violence

The frontier was not safer for women than the city was. It was less safe in specific ways: fewer witnesses, no police, weaker law, isolation, the captivity economy on both sides of the Native–Anglo line.

The categories the period contained:

- **Marital and domestic rape.** Not named as a crime in any western jurisdiction. A husband had legal access to his wife's body. A woman had no recourse beyond the husband himself.
- **Rape of women on the line.** The cribs, the dance halls, the parlor houses. Coercion was the economic floor; a woman who refused a customer the madam wanted served could be beaten or thrown out, which on the line meant the ditch.
- **Rape on the road and the trail.** Stage stations, freight camps, the open road between settlements. A woman traveling alone was understood to be at risk and was usually warned not to travel alone.
- **Rape in captivity, both directions.** Anglo women taken by raiding parties; Native women taken by Anglo soldiers, hunters, and freighters; Mexican women in the borderlands taken by Anglo Texans and by Mexican bandits.
- **Rape of children.** The orphan, the bound-out girl, the child of the line woman, the Native child taken from the reservation school dormitory. Predator clergy, predator schoolmasters, predator employers. The period had few words for this and almost no remedy.
- **Sex slavery.** The Chinese sing-song houses of San Francisco, Portland, and the smaller Pacific cities ran on girls bought in Guangdong, shipped under the name of laundry workers, and sold into brothels for terms of contracted years from which most did not survive. New Mexico's debt peonage system held women as well as men.

The aftermath stayed with the woman through her body's marks — the limp she kept, the silence she kept, the wound that did not close properly — and through the marriage that did or did not survive, the way the town remembered, the daughter who heard the story when she was old enough.

The captivity that the captive did not want to be rescued from was its own story. Cynthia Ann Parker, taken at nine, became Comanche, married, bore Quanah, was forcibly taken back at thirty-four, and starved herself to death in her brother's house in Texas.

## 2. Lynching, Mob Violence, and the Pogrom

The vigilance committee, the lynch mob, and the racial pogrom were institutions of the West. They were not aberrations. In many western towns they were the operating mode of community self-defense and racial enforcement.

- **The vigilance committee.** A formalized extralegal body. San Francisco's was the most famous (1851 and 1856), but they appeared in any western town where the citizens decided the law was inadequate. The membership was the town's commercial elite. The targets were the local criminal element, sometimes a corrupt official, often a man whose only crime was that he stood in the way of the committee's business interests. The committee held a trial. The trial was a formality. The hanging took place at the committee's chosen tree, often at night, often with the town watching. The bodies were left up overnight as a notice.
- **The lynch mob.** Less organized than the committee. A man accused of horse theft, of murder, of cattle stealing, of an assault on a white woman — taken from the jail by the town's men, walked to the nearest tree, hanged. The sheriff sometimes resisted and was sometimes complicit. The men of the mob went home and ate supper and were not prosecuted.

The targets in the West, by frequency:

- **Suspected horse thieves.** The largest single category. Every horse thief lynched was understood to be a deterrent to the next.
- **Black men accused of any crime against a white person.** The post-Reconstruction lynching pattern that defined the South was practiced in the West too. The numbers were smaller because the Black population was smaller. The ratio was comparable.
- **Mexicans.** The 1850s and 1860s in California saw a sustained anti-Mexican lynching campaign that historians estimate killed at least 350 men. The period's Anglo newspapers reported the killings with approval.
- **Chinese.** The Rock Springs massacre (Wyoming, 1885) killed at least twenty-eight Chinese miners. The Hells Canyon massacre (Oregon, 1887) killed at least thirty-four. Smaller pogroms against Chinese workers happened in dozens of western towns through the 1870s and 1880s.
- **Native Americans.** Often killed without trial in any context: the trader who killed a man who came to his post to barter, the rancher who killed a Native who crossed his land, the scouting party that found a small camp.
- **Italians, Irish, Slavs, anarchists.** Less frequent but documented. The 1891 New Orleans lynching of eleven Italian immigrants was the largest single mass lynching in U.S. history and was widely celebrated in the press.

**The pogrom.** When the mob acted against an entire community rather than an accused individual, the event was a pogrom. The Chinese quarters of dozens of western towns were burned and their inhabitants driven out: Tacoma (1885), Seattle (1886), Eureka (1885), and many smaller. Property destroyed. Residents killed or run out of town with what they could carry.

The lynching did not balance an account. The crowd did not learn anything. The marshal who had not been there when the men came for the jail was sometimes asleep at his sister's place outside of town and sometimes saying he had not known. The town that had a lynching last year had another one this year.

## 3. Madness on the Frontier

The West produced madness at a rate the East did not. The combination of isolation, deprivation, weather, displacement, loss, and the absence of community structures broke many people. The period's word was _mania_ or _brain fever_ or _nostalgia._ The institutional response was the territorial asylum: Yuma in Arizona, Pueblo in Colorado, Stockton in California, the federal asylum at St. Elizabeths in Washington for federal employees including soldiers.

**Prairie madness.** The form of breakdown specific to the homestead wife on the high plains. Women who had been transplanted from a town in Ohio or Iowa to a soddy in western Nebraska or Dakota Territory went strange under the combined weight of isolation, the ceaseless wind, the unbroken horizon, and the absence of other women. The husband worked the field or rode the line. The wife was alone in the soddy with the wind and the children for days at a time.

The progression: first a withdrawal from speech, then a fixation on a small object or small task, then a refusal to go outside or a compulsion to walk on the prairie at night, then, in the worst cases, the wife found by the husband seated by the cold stove with the children unfed and the fire out, or walking on the prairie in the snow without a shawl and not knowing why.

- **The line shack rider.** The cowhand sent to ride a line shack alone for the winter, checking fence and pushing strays back to the home pasture. The shack: one room, a stove, a bunk, a dirt floor, a window with the glass packed in newspaper against the cold. No other human within twenty miles for three months. The forms the breakdown took: talking to the horse, talking to the dog, talking to a person who was not there, the fixation on a single sound (a loose board, a wind-shake), the fear of the dark inside the shack, the long ride to a neighbor's place at the wrong time of day for no reason, the sudden refusal to ride out. Some men were suited to it. Others broke.
- **The prospector alone.** The man who went into the high country with a burro and worked a placer claim by himself for years. The hoarding of small objects, the conversation with the burro that was the most important relationship in the man's life, the development of long elaborate theories about claim-jumpers who were not there, the periodic descent into a town where the man could not function around other people. Most died in their cabins and were found by the next prospector who came up the draw in spring.
- **The religious mania.** The frontier produced and attracted men whose religious condition had moved past devotion into something stranger. The wandering preacher who had heard a voice and followed it into the wilderness. The visionary who could not stop preaching even when the camp had asked him to leave. The self-flagellant. The Penitente in the New Mexico borderlands whose practice the Anglo could not understand and could sometimes mistake for madness.
- **The Civil War legacy.** The veteran who came home from Shiloh or Vicksburg or the Wilderness was often broken in ways the period had no name for. Some of the worst cases ended up west: in cabins, in mining camps, on the road. The man who startled at the campfire crackling, who screamed in his sleep, who could not sit with his back to a door, who drank until he could sleep and not before.

## 4. Torture

The frontier contained torture as a practice, on every side.

- **Comanche and Apache practice.** Both nations practiced ritual torture of captured enemies. The practice was part of the warrior culture and was understood by both sides in the conflict. The methods included staking out, slow burning, mutilation, and the use of the captive's own family as audience when the family had also been captured. The Comanche burning of captured Texans is documented in many period sources. The Apache slow killing of captured Mexican peasants and Anglo soldiers is documented in others.
- **Anglo and Mexican vigilante and military practice.** The Texas Rangers in the 1850s and 1860s were known for the slow killing of captured Mexicans and Comanches. The Sand Creek massacre (Colorado Volunteer Cavalry under Chivington, 1864) included the mutilation of women's bodies as trophies that were displayed afterward in Denver. The Wounded Knee dead were left on the field for days in a blizzard.
- **Vigilante torture.** The lynch mob sometimes did more than hang. The whipping post, the dragging behind the horse, the burning of the victim alive (rare in the West, more common in the Reconstruction South). The Bisbee massacre (Arizona, 1883) saw five men hanged after a botched holdup and a sixth man lynched separately a few weeks later when the mob took him from the jail.

The witness afterward carried the smell that did not leave his clothes.

## 5. Cannibalism

The West contained a small number of documented cases. The Donner-Reed party (Sierra Nevada, 1846–47) is the famous one. Alferd Packer (Colorado, 1874) killed and ate five prospecting companions and was tried twice and convicted. The smaller cases — prospectors and emigrants caught by snow, men shipwrecked off the coast, families starved on failed homesteads — left fewer records.

Cannibalism in survival situations was not common but was not unknown, and the surviving participants almost always understood what they had done as the failure that ended their old lives. The Donner survivors included people who never spoke of it and people who became famous for having survived it. The shame did not pass.

The Donner party's leadership had made decisions in August that put the party in the position by November. The cannibalism in the snow was the consequence of the route choice in summer.
