# Quiet Title — Towns, Trails, Road to Cimarron

This companion is the sandbox infrastructure for [01-quiet-title.md](01-quiet-title.md). The main adventure carries the pressure, the factions, the scenes, and the central struggle over the survey and the suit. This file carries the ground those things happen on: the settlements, the road between them, the little places where men talk too freely, and the route procedures that make travel feel like travel rather than a page turn.

Run the chapter as a **pointcrawl**. The party moves from named place to named place, and each move costs time, reveals intention, and risks weather, witnesses, trouble, or opportunity.

Use this file when:

- when the party wants to know what is in town and where it can go
- when the party rides between nodes and the country should answer
- when the party lingers in one settlement long enough for the place itself to start pushing back

## How to run the country

The playable region is small enough to know and large enough to punish mistakes. The party will spend most of its time moving between three public settlements and a handful of exposed field sites:

- **San Esteban**, the plaza
- **Elizabethtown**, the gold camp at Baldy
- **Cimarron**, the Company town and court seat
- **the lower pasture and the willows**
- **the upper sheep camp**
- **the cañón mouth and trail**
- **Halladay's blazed line**
- **the true corner cairn**

Treat each of these as a node. The road or trail between them is where time passes, supplies run down, tempers rise, and strangers are seen.

### Travel time

Use these travel times as the default in fair spring weather with ordinary loads.

| Route | Approximate distance | Horseback | Wagon or pack train | On foot |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| San Esteban to lower pasture and willows | 2 miles | under 1 hour | under 1 hour | 45 minutes |
| San Esteban to upper sheep camp | 6 miles | 2 hours | 3 hours | half day |
| San Esteban to cañón mouth | 3 miles | 1 hour | 1.5 hours | 1 hour |
| Cañón mouth to Halladay's blazed line | 10 to 12 miles of rising trail | hard half day | not wagon passable | long day |
| Halladay's blazed line to the true corner cairn | 6 to 8 miles of high country | half day | not wagon passable | hard half day |
| San Esteban to Elizabethtown | 18 to 20 miles by mountain road | long day | impossible as a quick freight run; 1.5 to 2 days with loaded wagons | 2 days |
| San Esteban to Cimarron | 22 to 25 miles by settled road | long day or hard half day | full day | 2 days |
| Cimarron to Elizabethtown | 28 to 32 miles by Company road | long day | long day to 2 days depending on freight | 2 days |

These are adventure distances, not surveyor's certainties. They are chosen to feel like the real country around Cimarron and Baldy Mountain in `1873`: close enough that every faction can reach the others, far enough that every ride exposes intention.

### Watches and movement

Divide a travel day into three watches:

- **morning**
- **afternoon**
- **night**

A cautious ride usually covers one leg in one or two watches. A hard push can compress time, but every hard push costs a price: blown horses, lost sleep, sloppy camp, a missed sign, or a higher chance of an encounter going badly.

If the party pushes a horse or mule through a second hard day in a row, call for the game's ordinary tests for horsemanship, endurance, or labor as appropriate. If they fail, tack comes loose, an animal goes lame, or the pace drops whether they like it or not.

### When to roll for travel events

Roll only when the country has room to answer. Do not roll every mile.

- **Settled road:** Roll once per watch of meaningful travel. An event occurs on `1` on `1d6`.
- **Company road between Cimarron and Baldy:** Roll once per watch. An event occurs on `1-2` on `1d6`.
- **Mountain road or upper bench country:** Roll once per watch. An event occurs on `1-2` on `1d6`.
- **Cañón and high line country:** Roll once per watch and once again when the party makes camp. An event occurs on `1-2` on `1d6`.
- **Short local ride within the plaza's immediate orbit** such as to the lower pasture or upper camp: Roll only if clocks are already hot, the party is being watched, or it travels by night. An event occurs on `1` on `1d6`.

If the party is traveling openly with lights, freight, prisoners, or visible paper, shift any event roll one step worse: `1-2` on `1d6` becomes `1-3`, and `1` becomes `1-2`.

If the party is traveling carefully, using local guides, avoiding the obvious road, or has strong faction cover, shift one step better.

### When to roll for in-town events

Do not make towns random every time the party crosses a street. Roll a town event when one of the following is true:

- the party spends a full watch working the town
- the party makes **three meaningful stops** without leaving town
- the party is hiding, shadowing, or being shadowed
- a clock is already hot and the town ought to answer

In a calm town, roll `1d6`; an event occurs on `1`.
In a tense town, an event occurs on `1-2`.
In a town on the edge of violence, an event occurs on `1-3`.

When an event occurs, use the town's own event table rather than a wilderness table.

## San Esteban gazetteer

San Esteban is the chapter's heart and should be playable like a real plaza, not merely like an opening scene. Most parties should be able to spend a full session here before they ever ride to Baldy or Cimarron.

The first thing a stranger notices is not poverty, though the plaza has that. It is age. The adobe is old enough to have rounded at the corners. The acequia has worn its own argument into the dirt. The cemetery stands above the houses the way memory stands above talk, always visible and never entirely silent. San Esteban does not feel temporary. That is what makes the Company's paper obscene here. This is a place built by people who expected grandchildren, not lawsuits.

### 1. The common and the acequia

The common is the plaza's public room. Children run beside the acequia. Women wash. Men mend tack in the shade and watch strangers without seeming to.

By noon the ditch throws back enough light to make every face plain. Chickens scratch under wagon tongues, a mule switches flies beside the post, and the whole plaza seems to conduct its business here without admitting it is conducting anything. A Game Master should use the common whenever the party needs to feel the settlement looking back at it. Private words can be spoken in San Esteban, but public judgment is formed here.

**Usually here:** plaza families, idle boys, one or two sheep hands, Don Ignacio if he means to be seen.

**What happens here:**

- Learn which family is being squeezed hardest this week.
- Notice who crosses the common without greeting whom.
- Be publicly asked to read an English notice aloud, forcing the party to choose whether to inflame, soften, or expose the Company's meaning in front of everyone.

**What it offers play:** easy rumor gathering, public speeches, intimidation, silent social pressure, and the risk of making a scene everyone will remember.

If the Company decides to make its authority felt in public, this is also where the first eviction turns into a fight worth remembering. Wagons, household goods, ditch water, rooflines, and the whole watching plaza make violence here politically expensive, which is exactly why Hadley may choose it.

### 2. Eufemia's store

The store is half mercantile, half ledger, and all memory. Flour, coffee, beans, lamp oil, harness parts, nails, cartridges, and cloth stand on the shelves. The real goods are in the books behind the counter.

It smells of coffee burlap, axle grease, damp wool, and the paper-dust of accounts kept too long and too carefully to be innocent. Nothing in the place is wasted, not even courtesy. Eufemia's counter is where hard necessity dresses itself in the language of purchase and credit, and it is one of the best places in the chapter to show that conquest in New Mexico often comes item by item, line by line, until a family wakes to discover its ruin is already written down.

**Usually here:** Eufemia, a store boy, women buying on credit, one Anglo trader, a ranch hand pretending not to ask for more time.

**What happens here:**

- Read the pressure of debt in plain arithmetic.
- Hear who is buying too much powder, too little flour, or both.
- Be asked to carry, hide, or collect on a note Eufemia does not trust to an ordinary messenger.

**What it offers play:** access to prices, debt notes, quiet gossip, letters waiting collection, and the first practical evidence of the Company's squeeze.

### 3. The Montoya house

This is Don Ignacio's seat, though he would not call it that. Meals are taken here. Decisions begin here. Outsiders are admitted carefully and remembered forever.

The house holds warmth the way an old church holds incense. There are work tools in the corners, saints on the wall, and a long memory in the table itself, scarred by plates, knives, elbows, and years. If the party is welcomed here, it is being admitted into the plaza's version of statecraft: food, silence, measured questions, and the sense that a family's life is itself a legal claim no American court has learned how to read.

**Usually here:** Don Ignacio, Pilar, visiting kin, Nacho the grandson, one or two trusted vaqueros.

**What happens here:**

- Hear the old possession claim spoken in family terms instead of legal ones.
- Get judged by how you sit, eat, and wait.
- Be drawn into a family request that sounds domestic at first and turns out to touch inheritance, marriage, or which kin can still be trusted with news.

**What it offers play:** alliance building, requests for labor, the supper scene's aftershocks, and access to the plaza's internal politics.

### 4. Father Vigil's church and vestry

The church is plain adobe, cool in the nave and colder in the vestry. The register is here, and so are the sacramental records that double as a quiet archive of who has lived and died in the place.

The floor is worn by knees as much as by boots. Candles leave their own sweet smoke in the corners. The saints are humble images, not fine ones, and the whole church has the look of a place maintained by devotion rather than by cash. The vestry, by contrast, feels like a guarded mind. Books, linen, and locked drawers make it one of the few rooms in the chapter where paper carries almost as much moral authority as blood.

**Usually here:** Father Vigil, one old sacristan, women at prayer, children drilling catechism.

**What happens here:**

- Ask after the dead, the graves, baptisms, marriages, and old family ties.
- Hide a paper, witness, or exhausted man for a night if Vigil is persuaded.
- Receive moral sanction or moral condemnation in a form harder to shrug off than law.

**What it offers play:** access to the cemetery register, quiet interviews, refuge, and one of the few places in the plaza where men lower their voices.

### 5. The cemetery ridge

White crosses and weathered markers stand over the plaza like a second settlement. The wind is harder up here and talk comes plain.

This is one of the most important pieces of landscape in the whole module, and a Game Master should treat it as such. The graves are not only graves. They are continuity made visible. They let the party understand, without speechifying, why the plaza keeps insisting that possession is older than title and why the Company, if it were wise, would fear old bones more than hot tempers.

**Usually here:** mourners, Father Vigil, Don Ignacio alone, boys avoiding work, a watcher who means not to be seen.

**What happens here:**

- Hold private meetings with a view of the whole plaza.
- Notice if someone else is using the ridge to watch movements below.
- Be shown one grave whose date, family line, or manner of burial quietly contradicts the Company's paper.

**What it offers play:** clandestine meetings, surveillance, emotional leverage, and physical proof that the community is older than the suit.

### 6. The saloon across the common

This is where Company riders drink, shearers spend foolishly, and strangers are measured fast. It is not a grand place. That makes it useful.

The room is mean enough to be honest. Cards stick a little to the tables. Somebody has spat too near the stove. Whiskey, sweat, and old smoke have made a varnish of their own over every board in the place. If the church is where the plaza remembers itself, the saloon is where it leaks. Men here boast, trim, test, threaten, and say by accident what they would deny in daylight on the common.

**Usually here:** Hadley's riders, local hands, one cardsharp, one man who has seen too much and is trying to talk around it.

**What happens here:**

- Shadow Company men without riding to Cimarron.
- Start a fight that can be read as personal instead of political.
- Hear who rode in late, paid in gold, or asked after Tomás.

**What it offers play:** eavesdropping, baiting, hired work, false confidences, and trouble that looks accidental until it does not.

### 7. The livery and stable yard

The livery is small, understocked, and painfully informative. You learn a settlement by its horses.

There is always one horse tied here that tells the truth about the week: blown, mud-splashed, new-shod, underfed, or better bred than the town has a right to expect. The stable yard is frontier bookkeeping in hair and leather. A Game Master can do a surprising amount with who rode in, how hard, on what tack, and whether the animal looks like it was ridden home or borrowed for business.

**Usually here:** the stable hand, muleteers, a boy currying horses, travelers trying to buy remounts they will not get.

**What happens here:**

- Judge which animals in town were ridden hard and which came from elsewhere.
- Learn who came in by night because the livery always knows first.
- Discover that one horse was stabled under a false name or with tack that does not match the rider claiming it.

**What it offers play:** tracks, tack signs, last-known movements, vehicle capacity, and a place to sabotage or secure mounts.

### 8. The assay shack and panning claims

The color in the creek is not enough to make a boom, but enough to sharpen tempers. The assay shack is a modest Anglo foothold in the plaza and a reminder that gold has already altered the place.

That is what makes this place valuable. It is not the diggings themselves. It is the change in imagination they represent. Men who once judged a season by hay and lambs now pause by the shack to hear what dust did in Baldy, what new claim has struck, or whether the camp is about to break with the Company. A wise Game Master will use the assay shack as the first small crack through which mining-camp appetite enters plaza life.

**Usually here:** a local assayer, hopeful men with pans, one camp intermediary, boys dreaming too large.

**What happens here:**

- Hear Baldy news before Baldy men arrive.
- Learn which claims matter and which only sustain hope.
- See how many locals are being tempted out of sheep and hay into mineral talk.

**What it offers play:** gold gossip, connections to Elizabethtown, low-level economic pressure, and clues that bridge the plaza story into the camp story.

### 9. The lower pasture and the willows

The creek bend is close enough to the plaza for grief to travel back before supper. It is where Halladay lay and where Celso dies.

No place in the module gathers more dread for so little apparent reason. In daylight it is only water, gravel, mud, and willow shadow. That is precisely why the killings here matter. Death has entered a place that ought to belong to routine labor and thirsty sheep. Once that happens, every boy sent there runs a little faster coming back, and every adult who pauses by the bank wonders what the ground is still holding.

**Usually here:** sheep hands, boys sent with messages, one man alone when he wants not to be heard.

**What happens here:**

- Examine the ground for tracks, cartridge signs, dropped paper, and false narratives.
- Hold a private talk away from town ears without going fully into the mountains.
- Notice repeated use by watchers, messengers, or killers who assume everyone else fears the place.

**What it offers play:** murder inquiry, ambush, secret meetings, and one of the chapter's strongest recurring crime scenes.

### 10. The upper sheep camp

The upper camp is labor, money, and vulnerability in one place: dogs, wagons, stacked fleece, lambing pens, and too few men to guard it all.

Run this place as the plaza's exposed purse. Sheep in a western are never merely scenery; they are a payroll, inheritance, winter flour, church candles, and marriage prospects standing on four legs in open country. That is why a raid here bites deeper than a gunfight in a saloon. It is not simply violence. It is an attack on the community's ability to remain itself through the next season.

**Usually here:** camp hands, a cook, boys too young for rifles and old enough for work, Tomás if he is being protected.

**What happens here:**

- Feel the direct cost of every raid.
- Hold rough-country conversations out from under the plaza's public eye.
- Wake to find one missing sheep matters less than the one missing saddle, cartridge belt, or camp hand that vanished with it.

**What it offers play:** defense, pursuit, economic consequence, prisoner handling, and pressure on party priorities.

If the party has made itself useful but has not kept one eye on bread and water, the acequia and headgate are where the Company's deniable hands come first. Sabotage here hurts deeper than a gunfight in the dark because the whole plaza wakes up poor.

### 11. The Archuleta yard

Where the Montoyas are stock and water, the Archuletas are trade, counting, and kin spread outward by marriage and obligation.

The yard is busy in a different key than the Montoya place. Wool gets sorted here. Harness gets argued over here. News from cousins and in-laws arrives here before it reaches the common. The Game Master should use the Archuleta yard when the party needs to feel the economic intelligence of the plaza rather than its ceremonial or patriarchal face.

**Usually here:** Eufemia's kin, freight hands, women sorting wool, visiting relations from nearby settlements.

**What happens here:**

- Learn who in the plaza can still raise cash, mules, or labor.
- Hear family grievances Don Ignacio does not voice.
- Find who resents the plaza's leadership while still needing it.

**What it offers play:** internal tension, logistical help, marriage ties, and softer pressure than the Montoya house gives.

### 12. The cañón mouth

This is where settlement ends and the country begins to narrow. Once a party rides past this point, intention becomes legible.

That is the moral importance of the place. Inside the plaza a man can still pretend he is only walking, visiting, or waiting. At the cañón mouth he is going somewhere. It is therefore the natural place for second thoughts, warnings, shadowing, and the last look back at civilization before rock, timber, and weather begin making decisions of their own.

**Usually here:** Tomás, scouts, boys fishing, riders making one last decision whether to go on.

**What happens here:**

- Detect whether someone is shadowing the trail.
- Judge weather in the high country before you commit.
- Receive a last warning, plea, or secret from someone unwilling to carry it farther up the trail.

**What it offers play:** departure scenes, surveillance, guide negotiations, and the psychological threshold between civilized dispute and mountain danger.

## Elizabethtown gazetteer

Elizabethtown is not a town in the plaza sense. It is an appetite with buildings around it. Run it as a place where every conversation has an audience and every audience has a price.

The camp should feel as though it was built in a hurry by men who expected either to leave rich or die before the timber warped. Nothing sits square for long. The slopes have already been cut and recut. Canvas gives way to boards, boards to patched boards, patched boards to ruins somebody still rents out. It is a place with no old dignity in it and an enormous amount of immediate life. That contrast is what makes Baldy useful in play. San Esteban asks what can be preserved. Elizabethtown asks what can be taken before the taking stops.

### 1. The Old Abe

The Old Abe is saloon, meeting hall, unofficial court, and hiring floor. If the party means to deal with Strand, this is where it happens.

The room wants noise and keeps getting it. Pine knots spit in the stove. Glassware clouds, chips, and survives. Men laugh too loudly because they are tired, hopeful, cornered, or lying. The Old Abe is the sort of place where a man can be ruined socially, financially, or physically before his coffee cools. That makes it one of the chapter's best stages for rough alliances and worse first impressions.

**Usually here:** Strand, miners, Brubaker's people, gamblers, men waiting to hear assay returns.

**What happens here:**

- Receive jobs, offers, tests, threats, and rumors in the same hour.
- Watch who buys whom drinks and who refuses them.
- Start a saloon peace, a saloon war, or an alliance too rough for the law.

### 2. The assay office

The camp's real altar. Men believe the assayer's number even when they mistrust the man.

There are camps where the church or the flagpole tells the truth of the settlement. Elizabethtown is not one of them. Here the truth is written in ounces. The line outside the assay office should feel like a line outside judgment itself: hope rolled tight in dirty fists, armed caution disguised as patience, and every man pretending he will take good or bad news the same way.

**Usually here:** the assayer, armed miners, claim holders, nervous laborers with tiny pans and enormous hopes.

**What happens here:**

- Learn whose claim is rising and whose is dying.
- Spot theft, false weights, and panic before it becomes public.
- See which men are worth following because they have just been paid.

If the chapter needs a midnight fight in Baldy that is about more than pride, the assay office is one of the best rooms for it. Ledgers, dust, scale weights, and one low lamp are enough to turn argument into armed theft or Company reprisal fast.

### 3. The royalty table

Whether it sits in the Old Abe or under a canvas awning in fair weather, this is where the Company turns dust into obedience.

The table is worth dwelling on because it makes empire small enough to fit under a clerk's hand. A lockbox, a scale, a ledger, and a witness or two are all the Company needs to make a miner feel the whole weight of foreign capital pressing on his week's labor. If the Game Master wants to show why anti-Company feeling in Baldy is not rhetorical but lived, this is the place to do it.

**Usually here:** Brubaker or his deputy, a lockbox, ledger copies, armed witnesses, furious miners trying not to look weak.

**What happens here:**

- Track the Company's daily skim from the camp.
- Intercept the duplicate ledger route toward Cimarron.
- Hear the one miner who will swear Brubaker shaves the weights, but only if somebody richer or braver stands beside him.

This is also where Brubaker's authority is most visible and therefore most vulnerable. A public humiliation here can drive the camp toward riot; a private strike here can turn the next morning's bookkeeping into a gun matter.

### 4. Ouida's room above the store

A private room in a public camp, and therefore never fully private.

What gives the room its charge is contrast. Outside is the camp's rough democracy of dust and appetite. Inside is paper, memory, education, and a woman who has been forced to become her own archive because no one else would preserve her claim honestly. The room should inspire a different kind of attention from the saloon: lower voices, sharper shame, and the possibility that ideas, not merely gunmen, might crack the Company's case.

**Usually here:** Ouida, occasionally one trusted visitor, never enough warmth.

**What happens here:**

- Read the grant language.
- Hear the Beaubien claim in its own voice.
- Turn an intellectual ally into a dangerous enemy by condescension or theft.

### 5. Pell's room above the Old Abe

A drunk surveyor's room: transit in the corner, papers in disorder, brass gone green, self-disgust thick in the air.

The best way to run the room is as a bad conscience made visible. The instruments are real. The skill was real. The ruin is moral before it is physical. The empty glass matters because it sits beside the transit and the field bag, making clear that a man trained to draw exact lines has become one more uneven thing in a camp full of broken prospects.

**Usually here:** Pell, the bottle, no one who would admit visiting.

**What happens here:**

- Press for an affidavit.
- Steal notes, drafts, or personal letters if he is absent or insensible.
- Decide whether to rescue, use, ruin, or sell the man.

### 6. The stamp mill and arrastres

Noise, mud, mercury, and labor. The romance of gold has long since worn off here.

This is where the camp stops pretending it is about luck. Here it becomes work, fuel, poison, machinery, and the ugly patience of reduction. A Game Master should linger on the sound and the filth. Baldy is not only cards, whiskey, and stakes. It is also men feeding stone to iron and breathing what the process gives back.

**Usually here:** laborers, mule drivers, foremen, men whose hands shake too much.

**What happens here:**

- Learn which operators are desperate enough to deal.
- Track the flow of ore, dust, and Company oversight.
- Meet a laborer too poisoned, shaken, or underpaid to stay loyal if somebody offers him a cleaner road out.

### 7. Freight corral and mule lines

Everything that enters or leaves the camp bottlenecks here sooner or later.

That makes the corral more than a logistics site. It is the camp's throat. Ore, supplies, whiskey, news, ledgers, and fugitives all pass through some version of this yard. If the party wants to interfere with movement rather than argument, this is one of the strongest places in the module to do it.

**Usually here:** teamsters, camp guards, drifters hoping to hire on, one man always listening too closely.

**What happens here:**

- Follow ledgers, dust, or people headed toward Cimarron.
- Buy passage, hide in freight, or sabotage a departure.
- Notice which outgoing loads matter enough to guard.

### 8. The played-out cuts

The upper cuts are where optimism goes to die. They are good for secret talks precisely because no one chooses to linger there.

They are also one of the best places to remind the table that mining booms are graveyards of expectation long before they become ghost towns. Broken timbers, old spoil, half-collapsed trenches, and tools left where they ceased to be worth carrying all give the Game Master a visual language for failure. Men talk more honestly here because the ground itself has stopped boasting.

**Usually here:** failed miners, claim jumpers scouting opportunity, men meeting out of sight of Strand.

**What happens here:**

- Hold clandestine negotiations.
- Find discarded tools, hidden dust, or a body no one rushed to claim.
- Hear the camp's ugliest truths from men who have stopped needing to posture.

### 9. Board shanties and tent row

This is where the nameless camp lives: cookfires, cards, sickness, dust, and unsteady dreams.

Published adventures too often give the saloon and forget the sleeping quarters. That is a mistake here. Tent row is where the camp acquires moral density. If the party wants to know what Baldy costs ordinary people, this is where it learns: wet blankets, shared coffee, mercury coughs, unpaid tabs, women making a living out of endurance, and boys learning camp manners before they have learned much else.

**Usually here:** laborers, camp women, boarders, drunks, a child or two no one admits responsibility for.

**What happens here:**

- Find a witness who saw too much and has no protection.
- Learn what the camp says about the party when the party is not there.
- Be asked to settle a small ugly matter of theft, sickness, or vanished wages that reveals who in camp can be bought for very little.

## Cimarron gazetteer

Cimarron is law as property. It should feel tighter, more watched, and harder to breathe in than either the plaza or the camp.

If San Esteban feels old and Elizabethtown feels hungry, Cimarron should feel arranged. The boards are straighter here because money wanted them straight. The mud is no less muddy, the whiskey no less raw, the men no less dangerous, but the whole town carries itself like a place that expects paperwork to do what violence elsewhere must do face to face. That is why it is unsettling. Cimarron does not look like a battlefield until the party learns how much can be killed by receipts, warrants, juries, and signatures.

Two recurring habitation nodes do matter enough to rate their own short statblocks because parties return to them and because they can be damaged, defended, or used as pressure points.

### 1. The Company's store and counting room

The biggest building in town and the true center of gravity. Goods in front, paper and control in back.

Run the front as order and the back as appetite refined into method. Flour, coffee, harness leather, lamp oil, and cloth are the visible business. The invisible business is leverage. Men enter to buy goods and leave having revealed who is flush, who is late, who is nervous, and who cannot afford to offend the wrong clerk.

**Usually here:** clerks, a Company bookkeeper, guards after dark, Hadley or Pike by appointment.

**What happens here:**

- Trace debt, mail, duplicate accounts, and freight priorities.
- Break in for the most useful papers in the whole chapter if you dare the consequences.
- Notice one transaction the clerks treat as ordinary but which plainly involves hurried concealment, false names, or panic from above.

### 2. The courtroom stair and upper room

The law lives upstairs and creaks on the way up.

The stair is a threshold worth staging. Below it, men can still talk as if this were a town like any other. Above it, every exchange acquires record, consequence, or performance. The room itself should feel second-rate in material and first-rate in danger: a frontier chamber whose furniture is plain but whose decisions can reorder land, debt, legitimacy, and memory for a whole country.

**Usually here:** lawyers, messengers, jurors, witnesses, loafers pretending they have business.

**What happens here:**

- Judge who is expected in court and who is being kept away.
- See who speaks to whom before the hearing while pretending not to.
- Stage a public confrontation that becomes part of the record whether anyone wants it to or not.

### 3. The hotel

Travelers, clerks, jurors, out-of-county witnesses, Baca's overworked lamp, and walls too thin for comfort.

Hotels in western adventures are often treated as neutral lodging. This one should not be. It is a sorting house for alliances. Every trunk, every hallway conversation, every late knock, and every breakfast silence can matter. If the saloon is where rumor becomes heat, the hotel is where intention learns to wear a collar.

**Usually here:** Baca, one tired innkeeper, commercial travelers, witnesses, men who plan to leave at dawn and do not.

**What happens here:**

- Hide or guard witnesses.
- Steal, overhear, or copy legal papers.
- Work the halls as a slow-burn social battlefield.

If a witness is to be taken quietly before dawn, or if Pell is to be moved under another name, these halls are one of the likeliest places for it. Hotels make good western battlegrounds because everyone is half-dressed, badly placed, and pretending not to hear.

### 4. The sheriff's office and jail

A badge, a desk, two cells, and the knowledge that lawful custody can be as dangerous as outlaw violence.

That is the room's real flavor: not dramatic brutality, but shabby official danger. A prisoner here is not necessarily doomed, only exposed to paperwork, delay, and the kind of procedural handling that can ruin a witness without ever looking like murder. The office should smell of dust, lampblack, and stale authority.

**Usually here:** Sheriff Tatum, one deputy if the Company is worried, drunks, prisoners, a clerk when there is paper to serve.

**What happens here:**

- Deliver or rescue prisoners.
- Serve or dodge warrants.
- Learn whether Tatum is being lazy, frightened, or useful on any given day.

### 5. Lowe's newspaper office

Ink, type, patience, and a grudge sharper than any bowie knife in town.

The office is small enough to be broken and important enough to matter if it is. That makes it perfect for western politics. A press in a frontier town is never merely literary. It is an engine for deciding which private thing becomes a public thing, and at what cost. The room should feel cramped, purposeful, and just one hard shove away from wreckage.

**Usually here:** Thaddeus Lowe, a printer's devil, one informant who came by the back way.

**What happens here:**

- Turn evidence into public danger.
- Ask what has been printed, suppressed, or held for timing.
- Use the press as leverage without fully trusting the editor.

If the party wagers on scandal, this office becomes one of the chapter's natural flashpoints. A broken press, burning copy, or protected forme can decide whether truth reaches the boardwalk before Company force reaches the truth.

### 6. The livery and stage stand

Where exits are bought, arrivals are noted, and men leaving town can still be stopped if you are quick.

This is one of the module's best suspense locations because it combines motion with visibility. Everyone is either about to become absent or has just become newly present, and both states are dangerous. A horse saddled at the right hour, a stage delayed in mud, or a trunk loaded too fast can do more for play here than a whole exchange of exposition elsewhere.

**Usually here:** stage hands, grooms, departing merchants, armed escorts, one useless loafer watching everything.

**What happens here:**

- Pull Pell off a coach.
- Shadow a courier without entering the Company's store.
- Discover a last-minute change of escort, destination, or passenger list that means someone important is being moved under cover of routine travel.

It is also the natural stage for an abduction, witness seizure, or last-second interception of copied papers. One delayed coach and one spooked team can do more to the case than a full morning of argument upstairs.

### 7. Boardwalk saloons

Cimarron's saloons are less honest than the Old Abe because they pretend to be calmer. Jurors drink here. So do deputies, clerks, and men with city coats.

That pretense matters. These rooms are where legal respectability loosens its collar after dark. Men who would never brawl in court may whisper in corners, lend influence, test the price of silence, or decide that civic duty and private advantage can still be made to shake hands. The GM should use these saloons less for raw chaos than for polished corruption beginning to show its seams.

**Usually here:** jurors, Company men, lawyers' runners, gamblers, one bartender who knows every lie in town.

**What happens here:**

- Work jurors or hear who already is.
- Start a street problem that becomes a legal problem by morning.
- Catch one respectable man saying in drink what he will deny under oath at noon.

### 8. Freight yard and river crossing

The public face of Company logistics. Wagons, draft animals, barrels, crates, and the constant chance to lose something in movement.

There is always mud somewhere here, and somebody always swears the load was counted right before it vanished. That is why freight yards are so good in a western. They make theft, delay, bureaucracy, exhaustion, and violence occupy the same ground. If the party wants to affect the Company's body rather than its courtroom face, this is where the body shows.

**Usually here:** teamsters, laborers, guards, drifters seeking work, one clerk checking loads against paper.

**What happens here:**

- Track freight and correspondence.
- Hide in outbound loads or intercept inbound ones.
- Notice when the Company is moving too much money or too much fear at once.

### 9. The edge of town

Where the respectable story of Cimarron gives way to camps, shacks, and the people the Company uses without housing too close.

No company town is entirely respectable even when it tries to be. The edge of town is where the human remainder gathers: the badly paid, the disreputable, the exhausted, the bought, the frightened, and the observant. A great many truths can be found here, but almost none of them come in a form that would satisfy a judge without work.

**Usually here:** laborers, prostitutes, drifters, half-employed toughs, a witness who does not want to be found at the hotel.

**What happens here:**

- Buy silence, cheap information, or rough hands.
- Find the kind of witness that never steps into court unless forced.
- Learn the town's underside without Company varnish.

## The pointcrawl routes

These are the lines the party will most often travel. They are not abstract. Each should carry its own mood and kind of trouble.

### San Esteban to the lower pasture

This is close country. You do not roll for travel here unless the clocks are hot, the ride is at night, or the party is already under watch.

The ride leaves the plaza by ground everybody knows: trampled edges of field, ditch water slipping beside the path, stray chickens, barking dogs, and then the open smell of grass and creek mud. The country is low and workable here, the sort of land men glance over while talking because they think they have already mastered it. That confidence is part of what makes the willows so unsettling. A killing this near the houses feels like a hand laid on the table during supper.

**Travel time:** under 1 hour on horseback or by wagon; about 45 minutes on foot. For practical play, this is a same-watch errand unless something goes wrong.

**Use this route for:** checking the willows, shadowing locals, moving quietly, testing whether danger has come close enough to touch the plaza.

### San Esteban to the upper sheep camp

This is still the plaza's country, but less protected by being seen.

The trail climbs in slow practical bends above the worked ground. Adobe and acequia give way to bunchgrass, low sage, scattered pinyon, and the bleached look of country used hard by stock and weather. From the right rise you can look back and see the church, the common, and the white crosses on the ridge all in one glance, which is useful because this is the last stretch of country where the plaza still feels close enough to answer a rifle shot or a fire in the dark.

**Travel time:** about 2 hours on horseback, 3 hours by wagon, or half a day on foot. In play terms, it is usually an out-and-back within one long day unless night, weather, or fighting intervenes.

**Roll cadence:** one roll for the outward trip by day; two rolls if by night or under active pressure.

**Use this route for:** guarding stock, prisoner handling, night rides, returning after dark, or letting the plaza's economic vulnerability become physical.

### San Esteban to Cimarron

The settled road east is the chapter's legal artery. Freight, notices, couriers, lawyers, and deputies all use it.

The road east opens the country rather than closing it. The party leaves the shelter of the cañón mouth and rides into broader grass, sage flats, low swells, and a sky that feels bigger with every mile. Cottonwoods mark what water there is. Wagon ruts harden and split and rejoin. By the time Cimarron is near, the land has taken on that company-town look western roads get when money has used them harder than weather: more freight, more dung, more cut banks, more signs that every useful thing is being drawn toward one counting room.

**Travel time:** a hard half day to a long day on horseback, a full day by wagon, or roughly 2 days on foot. In practical campaign terms, most mounted parties can leave after dawn and reach Cimarron by dusk if they push.

**Roll cadence:** one roll each watch of travel; one more if camping on the road.

**Use this route for:** summons, witness transport, debt play, mail interception, and the long psychological ride from community ground into Company ground.

### San Esteban to Elizabethtown

This is a harder road than the mileage implies. The country rises, the weather turns quicker, and open appetite replaces communal memory.

This road teaches the party the mountain by degrees. First come the pinyon and juniper above the worked valley, then the darker timber, then the aspen where the trunks stand pale as bone against the slope. Water gets colder, air thinner, and the ground more expensive under hoof. Tailings begin to appear before the camp itself does, pale fans of broken rock cast down the hillside like spoil from some patient animal eating the mountain from within.

**Travel time:** a long day on horseback in fair weather, `1.5 to 2 days` for loaded wagons, or about `2 days` on foot. In play, a fast mounted party can make it in one punishing day, but should feel the punishment.

**Roll cadence:** one roll each watch; one more at camp. In bad weather, treat every roll as one step worse.

**Use this route for:** carrying stories between plaza and camp, taking wool or dust, pursuing riders who think the mountain covers them, and making the transition from land fight to mineral fight.

### Cimarron to Elizabethtown

The Company road is the cleanest road in the chapter and therefore the most watched.

The difference between this road and the San Esteban road is one of intention. This one has been cut, improved, used, and worried over by men who mean to carry value on it. The grades feel more deliberate, the bends more worked, the bridges and culverts more purposeful where they exist at all. The party rides through country that has already been translated into freight logic, and as the climb steepens the road begins to show the same raw compromise every western road shows when capital insists on speed through bad terrain.

**Travel time:** a long day on horseback, a long day to `2 days` for freight, or about `2 days` on foot. This is the route for parties who want speed without wilderness, and it pays them back in visibility.

**Roll cadence:** one roll each watch; one more if carrying visible freight, papers, or prisoners.

**Use this route for:** duplicate ledger interception, courier pursuit, legal pressure, Company countermoves, and showing how tightly the camp and court are tied by transport.

### The cañón trail

The cañón is not a road. It is a test.

The trail west into the cañón narrows almost at once. Spruce and ponderosa close in. The creek falls over ledges in green-black water and the air cools enough that sweat goes tacky under a shirt. Higher up, the switchbacks begin, and with them the country changes from worked New Mexico to hard mountain ground: bare aspen trunks, snow lying late in the gullies, rock shelves, blind turns, and those long ridge views that make a rider feel both lucky and exposed. By the time the party reaches the blazed line and the cairn country beyond it, the land has thinned into wind, stone, and distance.

**Travel time:** from San Esteban to the cañón mouth is about 1 hour on horseback; from the mouth to Halladay's blazed line is a hard half day mounted or a long day on foot; from the blazed line to the true corner cairn is another half day in high country. Taken whole, the ride from the plaza to the cairn is usually `1.5 days` for a capable mounted party and `2 hard days` for anyone slower, burdened, or cautious.

**Roll cadence:** one roll each watch; one roll at camp; one extra roll if the party blunders, makes smoke, rides with too much noise, or travels without trusted guides.

**Use this route for:** blazes, scouts, Nah-leh, hidden movement, exposure, exhaustion, and the felt difference between a public trail and a country that does not consent to being crossed.

## What each settlement is still good for after the first visit

One danger in sandbox writing is that a place becomes "used up" after its first key scene. Do not let that happen here.

- **San Esteban** remains useful for debt, grief, family politics, food pressure, witness protection, and public legitimacy.
- **Elizabethtown** remains useful for affidavits, duplicate ledgers, extra guns, rumor, and anti-Company alliances that stop well short of virtue.
- **Cimarron** remains useful for paper, warrants, witness custody, publication, jury pressure, and the dangerous transformation of truth into public fact.

If the party revisits a town after a major success or betrayal, change no more than two things at first and let those two things speak loudly. A shuttered store. A rider now greeted by name. A juror now unfriendly. A priest who no longer offers shelter. A saloon that goes quiet when the party enters. A sandbox lives by such changes.
