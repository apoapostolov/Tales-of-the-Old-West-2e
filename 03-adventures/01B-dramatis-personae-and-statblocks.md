# Dramatis Personae and Statblocks

This appendix covers the named NPCs and the most useful generic profiles from [01-quiet-title.md](01-quiet-title.md). The main chapter gives each principal in a quick first look. This file carries the rest: backstory, wants, needs, fears, table use, and full statblocks.

Every named figure the party is likely to talk to, fight, guard, chase, or bargain with appears here. The chapter is built so that no single NPC's death ends it — see _Truth Survives the Holder_ in the main file — but each of these people is a load-bearing wall all the same. Read them before you run them.

## Named NPCs

### CORNELIUS V. HADLEY

Hadley is a white man in his middle forties, Yale-educated, tall, narrow through the face, and dressed too well for the country he works in. He gives the impression of a man who has made patience into a weapon. He never raises his voice. He folds his hands when he listens. He writes names in a small leather book without seeming to and answers hard questions with softer ones that turn the matter back on the asker.

**Backstory.** Cornelius Van Arsdale Hadley came west in `1866` with a Yale degree, a small inheritance, and a letter of introduction to a Maxwell Company director who had been his father's roommate. He was meant to be a clerk. He was a superintendent inside four years because he understood, earlier than the men he replaced, that the grant's value was no longer in cattle or sheep but in paper — the survey return, the quiet-title decree, the mineral deed, the right-of-way survey. He built the eastern line fraud on that understanding. He stood in the willows in March and watched Halladay die on it. He has not lost a night's sleep over the surveyor, only over the field book.

**Wants.** The eastern line upheld. The quiet title signed. The gold under royalty. The right of way sold to the railroad at full value. A seat on the Company's London board before he is fifty.

**Needs.** To keep the fraud off any paper a court can read. To recover or destroy Halladay's field book. To keep Pell sober enough to be useful and quiet enough to be safe. To make an example of anyone who forces the suit into open scandal.

**Fears.** A field book in open court. A letter from the Land Office in Washington. His own London directors deciding he is more expensive than the scandal he is covering. The man in the good coat being named in a courtroom by a witness who will not be bought.

**How to play him.** Hadley does not threaten in plain words. He makes offers, keeps notes, and lets his manners do the unsettling. He is most dangerous when he is most courteous, because that is the moment he is deciding whether to buy you, remove you, or let the country do it for him. Play him as a man who already knows the outcome he wants and is only calculating the price of getting there.

### DON IGNACIO MONTOYA

Don Ignacio is a Hispano man of sixty-four, sun-cut, slow in the saddle, his hands gone permanent around the shape of a rope. He has the kind of weight some men carry without trying. He stands like a man who has hauled his own water, buried his own dead, and expects to go on doing both. He speaks Spanish at home and slow careful English to Anglos, and in the plaza he has no reason to raise his voice.

**Backstory.** Ignacio Montoya was eighteen when his father Teodoro brought the sheep up out of the Taos country in `1847`, fleeing the rebellion and the hanging that followed it. He helped dig the first acequia. He laid the first adobe of the church. He married a girl from the Archuleta family and buried her in `1859` in the cemetery he had helped his father clear on the ridge. He buried two sons — one to fever, one to a Comanche lance on the lower pasture in `1868`. His daughter Pilar runs his house now and his grandson, also Ignacio but called Nacho, is the boy most often sent to fetch the strangers. He has been the plaza's _juez de agua_, the water judge, for twenty years, and the acequia turns on his word. He has never held a piece of paper that said the land was his. He has the ridge full of graves that say it anyway.

**Wants.** The plaza's twenty-six years of possession recognized in law. The acequia to run clean. The cemetery left undisturbed. No more sons buried.

**Needs.** The field book, because the court will not hear possession without it. A lawyer the Company cannot buy. Allies who know the difference between a man's land and a man's paper.

**Fears.** That the plaza will be starved out before the suit is tried. That Pilar will be left with nothing. That the cemetery will be plowed. That he will die in a country his father's bones are already in, with the country belonging to strangers.

**How to play him.** Don Ignacio answers slowly, in English to Anglos and in Spanish to anyone who will have it. He measures strangers by whether they can bear a silence without rushing to fill it. He rarely commands outright; he suggests, and the plaza takes the suggestion seriously. Play him as the slow center the whole comunidad turns around.

### EUFEMIA ARCHULETA

Eufemia is a Hispano woman of fifty-eight, widow of the founder's son, who runs the Archuleta trade in wool, hides, and dry goods out of the long adobe across the common from the Montoya house. She looks like command the moment you see her. She stands behind the counter with a pencil behind her ear and counts faster than the men buying from her. She speaks Spanish, English, and enough Ute to trade, and she changes languages without warning to see who is really listening.

**Backstory.** Eufemia Archuleta née Trujillo married into the plaza's founding family at sixteen and was widowed at thirty-six when a Comanche raiding party caught her husband on the lower pasture in `1861`. She did not remarry. She turned the Archuleta store into the plaza's bank, its post office, its clearing house for news, and its memory of debt. She lost her eldest son to the diphtheria year and her middle son to the Baldy Mountain collapse in `1870` — the Company's mine, the Company's timber, the Company's refusal to shore the drift. Her youngest, Rafael, rides drag and drinks, and she keeps him on the outfit because he is all she has left and because firing him would be admitting what he has become. She keeps the store's ledger in Spanish because the law reads English and she does not want the law reading her arithmetic.

**Wants.** The Company's freight road broken so the plaza can buy flour at three cents again. The wool sold at a fair price. Rafael steady. The ledger balanced before she dies.

**Needs.** A second freight route to Taos. Cash the Company cannot undercut. Someone to read the English notices to the families who cannot.

**Fears.** The flour at six cents until the families are gone. Rafael in the creek. The ledger closing red for the last time.

**How to play her.** Eufemia counts everything: money, loyalty, slights, and the days since her son died. She prices the party within the first hour and seldom revises that judgment. She uses language as another tool of control. She is not sentimental, but she is often right. Let her be the hardest bargainer in the chapter and one of its best friends at the same time.

### "COL." EPHRAIM STRAND

Strand is a white man of fifty, heavy in the chest and the gut, loud, and never actually a colonel of anything. He lands on people as force first and fraud second, and most men never get past the first part. He bought into the Baldy diggings in `1868` with money of unclear origin and has run the camp's rougher end ever since.

**Backstory.** Ephraim Strand was born Ephraim Strnad in a Bohemian neighborhood of Cleveland, ran away from his father's shoe shop at fifteen, and drifted west by way of St. Louis and Denver. In Denver he kept a saloon on Larimer Street, ran a faro bank that was cleaner than it looked, and killed a man over a debt in `1865` — a killing that was ruled self-defense because the dead man's gun was found at the scene, which Strand had put there. The heat drove him south. He bought into the Baldy diggings in `1868` with the Denver money and a story about a Confederate cavalry past that would not survive five minutes with anyone who had actually served. He calls himself Colonel because the title gets him the first drink free in every camp between here and Santa Fe.

**Wants.** The diggings free of the Company. The camp as his own kingdom. The first pan a stranger is offered to be the pan that buys him.

**Needs.** Guns he can trust. A deal with anyone who breaks the Company's mineral claim. To not be in the same room as the plaza's lawyer when the creek's ownership comes up.

**Fears.** The plaza winning its case, because the plaza's claim to the creek is older than the camp's. The Denver killing catching up. A sober morning.

**How to play him.** Strand is a blowhard, but he is a capable one. He buys the first drink, sizes up a stranger over the rim, and decides fast whether he is looking at muscle, a mark, or trouble. The volume is real. So is the danger. Let the fraud show first and the competence reveal itself underneath.

### NAH-LEH

Nah-leh ("He Who Counts Coup") is a Jicarilla Apache man in mature adulthood, lean, quiet-faced, and harder to read than Hadley. What he gives off first is stillness. He sits a horse as if horse and rider settled matters long ago. He does not fidget. He does not explain himself. He watches the men watching him and lets that do the work.

**Backstory.** Nah-leh was born in the cañón above the plaza in a year his people count by the elk moon and the territorial census counts as roughly `1838`. He hunted these parks before the Montoyas dug the first acequia, traded with them when they came, fought the Comanche alongside them once, and watched the survey chain arrive in `1871` with the same expression he watches everything. His cousin **Tsa-leh** was on the ridge above the willows the night Halladay died, hunting elk, and saw what he saw. Nah-leh has kept that sight the way a man keeps a final cartridge — held in reserve, spent at the moment it matters most. He has a wife and two daughters at the Cimarron Creek agency and a son who will not go to the agency and hunts with him in the high parks.

**Wants.** The cañón road left open for the hunt. The survey chain out of the high parks. The men who came up the cañón with transit and sextant to explain themselves or to leave.

**Needs.** The Jicarilla claim to the high country heard, in court or out of it. Allies who understand that a line on paper in Santa Fe does not move the country out from under the people who have lived in it for ten thousand years.

**Fears.** The high parks fenced. His son killed on the line by a chainman who will not be punished. His cousin's sight spent for nothing.

**How to play him.** Nah-leh uses English last and least, and when he does, it is spare and exact. He does not explain himself twice. He waits. He watches. He speaks only when speech will do work silence cannot. Play him as the chapter's truest witness and the least hurried man in it.

### JUDGE MORTIMER VAIL

Judge Vail is a white man in his late fifties, appointed from Ohio, owlish behind spectacles, soft-handed, and owned. The first impression he gives is of competence in service of a verdict already arrived at. He hears a case the way a clerk files a letter: with attention to form and no attention at all to the body on the floor.

**Backstory.** Mortimer Vail read law in Cleveland, failed at politics in Ohio, and was rewarded for his party loyalty with a territorial judgeship in `1869` — the kind of patronage appointment that fills the western bench. He came to New Mexico with his wife and his spectacles and his conviction that the territory's job was to become a state, and the state's job was to look like Ohio, and his job was to make both happen without anybody important being inconvenienced. The Ring found him useful because he was intelligent enough to rule cleanly in their favor without being told how. He is not a wicked man. He is a comfortable man who has arranged his comfort so that he never has to notice what his comfort costs.

**Wants.** The spring term over without a scandal that reaches Santa Fe or Washington. His appointment renewed. A quiet life.

**Needs.** To rule in the Company's favor without the ruling being obviously corrupt. A way to exclude any paper that would force him to rule otherwise.

**Fears.** A field book in open court. A federal inquiry. His own name in a newspaper beside the word _fraud_.

**How to play him.** Vail cleans his spectacles when he does not want to be read, and he cleans them often. He rules on procedure before he rules on merit, because procedure is where a corrupt judge does his cleanest work. He bends with the paper if the paper is overwhelming and public, because a scandal in open court is the one thing his owners cannot forgive. He bends less for partial paper, and not at all for none. Play him as a man who has already decided the verdict and is now managing the cost of arriving at it legally — which is, in its way, exactly what Hadley is doing, only Hadley is honest with himself about it.

### TOMÁS LUJÁN

Tomás Luján is a mixed-blood man of thirty-two, Hispano and Jicarilla, born in the cañón above the plaza. The first impression he gives is of a man trying to disappear into his own quiet. He is lean, dark, watchful, and he carries the surveyor's old haversack as if it were sewn to him.

**Backstory.** Tomás Luján is the son of a Hispano vaquero and a Jicarilla woman who died birthing him, raised half in the plaza and half in the cañón, belonging fully to neither. He learned the country as a boy learns a language — by living in it — and by twenty he was the best guide between the Sangre de Cristos and the Cimarron. Halladay hired him in the fall of `1872` for the eastern line survey because Tomás was the only man who could take a transit through the cañón in any season and bring it back. They worked six weeks together. Tomás came to admire the surveyor's stubborn honesty, the first Anglo he had ever admired. He was in the willows the night Halladay died because Halladay had sent him for the horses and he came back too late, or hid too well, or both. He has not slept through a night since.

**Wants.** To speak. To be safe. To stop seeing the willows when he closes his eyes.

**Needs.** A reason to trust the party that is stronger than his fear of the Company. A way out of the country, or a way to stay in it alive.

**Fears.** The Company's riders. The dark. His own voice, which he believes will kill him if he uses it.

**How to play him.** Tomás is the chapter's broken hinge. He speaks in bursts, with excuses and corrections outrunning sense, and he tries to guess from one breath to the next whether the party are rescuers, buyers, or executioners. Show him kindness and he clings. Show greed and he bargains. Show violence and he breaks. He carries the haversack because it was Halladay's and because letting go of it would be letting go of the only honest man he ever knew. The party that gives him a reason to speak, a way to be safe, or a guard for his back unlocks the chapter's truest witness. The party that frightens him locks the hinge forever.

### FATHER SABINO VIGIL

Father Vigil is a Hispano priest of forty-five, the plaza's pastor for nine years, a Penitente-country man with a hard theology and a soft heart he keeps hidden behind the office. The first impression he gives is of a man who has heard everything and forgiven none of it and buried most of it.

**Backstory.** Sabino Vigil was ordained in Durango and sent north to the Sangre de Cristos parishes in `1860`, to a country where the Penitente brotherhoods were older than the diocese and the parish priest was often a guest in his own church. He has buried four hundred of his parishioners in nine years — fever, raid, mine, drink, childbed, old age — and he has written every one of them into the cemetery register, with the date and the cause and, where he knows it, the family line. The register is the plaza's true title, and he knows it, and he has begun to keep a second copy in the vestry because he does not trust the Company not to burn the first.

**Wants.** The cemetery register safe. The plaza's dead left undisturbed. A confession from the man who killed Halladay, even if the confession never reaches a court.

**Needs.** A second copy of the register somewhere the Company cannot find it. An ally who will carry the register to Taos or Santa Fe if the plaza falls.

**Fears.** The church burned. The register burned. His own drinking, which his bishop does not know about and which he cannot stop.

**How to play him.** Vigil speaks Spanish in the pulpit and English to Anglos, and his English is harder than his Spanish because the English is the language of the suit and the suit is the enemy. He keeps the register the way Hadley keeps the leather book — as a weapon, held in reserve. He drinks more than he should and he is ashamed of it and the shame makes him harder on himself and on the strangers who remind him of it. He will not bend on the cemetery. He will bend on almost anything else if the bend protects the dead.

### SHERIFF BUD TATUM

Sheriff Tatum is a white man of thirty-eight, the Company's sheriff, seated in Cimarron, who rides up to the plaza when the Company sends him and not before. The first impression he gives is of a large man who would rather not run. He is not cruel and he is not brave and he does what Hadley tells him because Hadley signs the paper that pays him.

**Backstory.** Bud Tatum was a teamster out of Missouri who drifted south after the war, took a badge in Cimarron because the badge came with a house, and discovered that the badge was Hadley's badge and the house was Hadley's house. He has been Hadley's sheriff for four years. He has written "drowning" in his book for two men who were shot in the head. He knows what he is. He is not proud of it. He has a wife and two daughters in Cimarron and he tells himself, on the nights he can sleep, that the badge is for them.

**Wants.** To do as little as possible. To keep the house. To not be the man who writes "drowning" a third time.

**Needs.** Hadley to keep paying him. A reason, just once, to write the truth in the book.

**Fears.** Hadley. The plaza, if it ever stops being afraid of him. His own daughters finding out what their father writes in the book.

**How to play him.** Tatum is a coward, but he is a self-aware coward, which makes him more useful to the party than a stupid one. He will not defy Hadley openly. He will drag his feet, lose papers, be slow to ride, and write "drowning" one more time if the alternative is losing his house. He is a man the party can turn, slowly, if they give him a reason and a way to save his family. He is also a man the Company can turn back with a single threat to his daughters. Play him as the chapter's most miserable man and its most sympathetic villain.

### BRUBAKER

Brubaker is a white man of forty or so, Hadley's royalty collector at Baldy, thick through the shoulders, clean-shaved, and dressed better than the camp but worse than the court. He comes off like a man who has learned to make other men resent him and mistake that resentment for power. He carries a little more pomade than the mountain warrants, a little more certainty than the camp deserves, and he almost never forgets an amount or an insult.

**Backstory.** Amos Brubaker worked river freight on the Missouri, drifted into Kansas rail grading after the war, and discovered in both trades that he preferred counting another man's work to doing his own. The Maxwell Company found him useful because he could watch a scale, a strongbox, and three armed miners at once without blinking. He came into the Baldy diggings in `1871` to collect the royalty and has been hated there since the second week. He does not mind the hatred. Hatred that still pays is only another proof a man's hand is on the throat of a thing worth having.

**Wants.** The royalty collected in full. The duplicate ledgers carried down to Cimarron clean. The camp reminded that Company paper is not a matter for argument.

**Needs.** Hard men close by. A reputation for never blinking first. Proof for Hadley that every ounce of dust and every insult can still be counted.

**Fears.** A public robbery he cannot explain away. Strand catching him alone. A ledger in the wrong hands.

**How to play him.** Brubaker is not elegant like Hadley and not loud like Strand. He is the mean accountant between them. He speaks in dues, shortages, percentages, and arrears, and treats armed men like inventory. Play him as a man who thinks numbers are cleaner than blood and is wrong about it.

### ALDOUS PELL (THE SURVEYOR)

Aldous Pell is a white man of forty, Ring-connected, precise, and cold in a way that passes for competence. He ran the false return under Halladay's name and he has been drinking since. He is the one man in the chapter who knows exactly how the line was bent and exactly how much it cost, and the cost is eating him.

**Backstory.** Aldous Pell is the younger son of a Connecticut family that lost its money in the panic of `1857`, trained as a surveyor because surveying was a gentleman's trade that paid, and came west in `1864` to run township lines for the General Land Office. He was honest for six years. In `1870` the Ring found him, and the Ring paid better than the Land Office, and the work was the same work, only the lines moved where the Ring needed them to move. He met Hadley in Santa Fe, took the Maxwell contract in `1872`, ran the false eastern line under Halladay's name, and filed it. Then Halladay threatened to go to Washington, and then Halladay was in the willows, and Pell understood that the line he had drawn for money had been collected in blood, and the understanding broke something in him that the whiskey has not yet mended.

**Wants.** To stop seeing Halladay's face. To get out of the country alive. To be told, by someone whose opinion he respects, that he had no choice.

**Needs.** A way out that does not end in a Cimarron courtroom or a shallow grave. A signed guarantee of safety, from someone who can honor it.

**Fears.** Hadley's riders. The gallows. Himself, sober, in a room with no whiskey.

**How to play him.** Pell is the Company's broken hinge, just as Tomás is the plaza's. He is precise, technical, and coming apart. He retreats into the language of chain and transit because it is the only language in which he still feels competent. He wants absolution more than money. Give him a way out and he talks. Press him too hard and he bolts.

### OREN PELL (THE RAILROAD AGENT)

Oren Pell is a white man of thirty-five, quiet, well-tailored, perpetually taking notes in a shorthand nobody can read. He is in Cimarron and Elizabethtown and the plaza by turns, never quite resident anywhere, buying nothing and selling nothing in the open. He is no relation to the surveyor, though the Company cultivates the confusion because the confusion is useful.

**Backstory.** Oren Pell is a field agent for the right-of-way department of the Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe, hired out of a Boston bank in `1871` because he had a head for deeds and a stomach for the frontier. He is not a bad man. He is a function of a railroad, which is to say he is patient, exact, and incapable of caring about anything that cannot be measured in miles of laid rail. He has been in the grant country for two years, mapping the right of way in his shorthand, waiting for the boundary to settle so the railroad can buy its corridor clean. He will buy any paper that settles it. He will buy it from the Company, from the plaza, from the party, from a stray dog, if the dog holds clean paper.

**Wants.** The right of way across the grant, cleanly deeded, at a price the railroad will approve.

**Needs.** A definitive ruling on the eastern boundary, from any source the railroad's counsel will accept. A seller who can deliver clear title.

**Fears.** A boundary dispute that drags into the next decade. A scandal that reaches Washington and slows the railroad's federal land grants. Being recalled to Topeka without the corridor.

**How to play him.** Pell is the chapter's glacier. He does not hurry, threaten, or take sides. He makes offers in clean English and unreadable shorthand, and by railroad standards the offers are fair. He is the safest buyer in the chapter because he asks no questions, and the most dangerous creditor because the railroad forgets nothing. Play him as a man already living in `1883`, treating the `1873` suit as an inconvenience on the way to the rail.

### OUIDA BEAUBIEN

Ouida Beaubien is a mixed-race woman of thirty, the granddaughter of the grant's original Mexican grantee, who keeps a room above the store in Elizabethtown and a claim to a stretch of the grant that no court has ever heard. She is educated, angry, and exact.

**Backstory.** Ouida Beaubien is the granddaughter of Carlos Beaubien, the Mexican grantee of `1841`, by his son and a Taos woman whose name the family does not speak in mixed company. She was raised in the Beaubien house in Taos, sent to the Loretto Academy in Santa Fe for the schooling her sex and her color were not supposed to want, and came back to the grant country at twenty-one to press a claim no lawyer would take: that the mineral rights the `1841` grant reserved to the sovereign reverted, on the sovereign's default, to the grantee's heirs. She has been pressing it for nine years, in Elizabethtown, in a room above the store, reading the grant language aloud to anyone who will listen. Almost no one listens. The almost-no-one has curdled her into a woman who is right about the law and hard about everything else.

**Wants.** Her family's name on the gold. The grant language read into a court record, any court record, before she dies.

**Needs.** A lawyer willing to press the Beaubien mineral claim. A ride to Santa Fe and an audience with anyone in the Land Office. An ally who will listen to the Spanish without checking whether she is pretty while she reads it.

**Fears.** Dying in the room above the store with the claim unheard. The Company buying the last copy of the grant language out from under her. Being right, and the rightness meaning nothing.

**How to play her.** Ouida is the chapter's truest historian and its loneliest figure. She is not disinterested. She wants her family's name restored to the gold, and that means she wants a piece of what the camp pulls out of the creek. That self-interest makes her a harder ally than the party expects. She reads the grant language in Spanish first because the Spanish is the law and the English is the translation, and she watches faces while she reads to see who is listening to the law and who is looking at the woman. Treat her as a witness and she cracks the Company's mineral claim in three sentences. Treat her as a curiosity and she becomes a dangerous enemy who knows where every body is buried because her family buried half of them.
