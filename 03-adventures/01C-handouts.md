# Handouts

This appendix presents the chapter's most important papers in player-facing form. They are meant to be readable at the table, studied for implication, and handed across without requiring the group to parse a full legal archive.

Do not treat these as literal diplomatic editions. They are selective, playable texts: enough substance to matter, enough texture to feel period, and not so much bulk that the paper dies in the players' hands.

Use them in three ways:

- read aloud if the group finds the document under pressure and does not yet have time to study it
- hand over the text as written if the party gains custody of it
- excerpt or withhold lines if the party gets only part of the document, a hurried copy, or a damaged version

Where a document would naturally exist in Spanish first, this appendix gives the Spanish text and then an English rendering fit for table use. That way the paper can remain true to the world of the chapter without turning language into a logistical nuisance at the table.

## Core Handouts

These are the documents most likely to enter play in the main line of the adventure.

### Handout 1. Notice of Quiet Title and Eviction

**Give when:** the party reads the papers nailed at San Esteban, or when a literate character translates them aloud.

> **Territory of New Mexico**  
> **County of Colfax**  
> **In the District Court Sitting at Cimarron**
>
> **Maxwell Land Grant and Railway Company**  
> _Plaintiff_  
> **v.**  
> **The Inhabitants, Occupants, and Claimants of the Tract Commonly Called San Esteban, upon the Arroyo de las Vacas**  
> _Defendants_
>
> Notice is hereby given that the Plaintiff has filed its Bill in Equity and Petition to Quiet Title touching certain lands lying within the exterior boundaries of the Maxwell Grant as returned and surveyed in the year of Our Lord `1872`, and that all persons residing upon, cultivating, pasturing, enclosing, improving, occupying, or in any wise claiming any part thereof are required to appear and answer at the next term of the District Court, to be held at Cimarron on the first Monday of June next ensuing.
>
> Said occupants are further advised that their continued possession, absent lawful title recognized by this Court, shall be deemed trespass upon lands lawfully vested in the Plaintiff, and that failure to appear may result in decree by default, issuance of writ of possession, assessment of costs, and removal by the Sheriff of this County.
>
> **Particular Notice**
>
> The household presently occupying the adobe and attached yard known in local usage as the house of **Rafael Archuleta**, together with outbuildings, stock pen, and irrigated strip thereto adjoining, is directed to surrender possession within ten days of posting, or show written cause why the same should not be taken into the custody of the Plaintiff pending decree.
>
> By order of Counsel for the Plaintiff,  
> **Harlan Pike, Advocate**  
> Cimarron, New Mexico Territory  
> `April 29, 1873`

### Handout 2. Don Ignacio's Reply Posted Below the Notice

**Give when:** the party reads the common wall in the plaza.

> **Spanish original**
>
> **Esta tierra no se vende.**
>
> Estábamos en este arroyo antes que naciera tu jinete.  
> Enterramos a nuestros muertos en esta loma antes que tu agrimensor la subiera.  
> Abrimos esta acequia, sembramos este campo, marcamos estas sepulturas y dimos agua a estas ovejas sin pedir licencia en Cimarron.
>
> Si nos llaman invasores, díganlo en español delante de nuestras mujeres, nuestros viejos y los huesos sobre la iglesia.
>
> **Ignacio Montoya**  
> por la gente de San Esteban
>
> **Table translation**
>
> **This land is not for sale.**
>
> We were on this creek before your rider was born.  
> We buried our dead on this ridge before your surveyor climbed it.  
> We dug this ditch, planted this field, marked these graves, and watered these sheep without leave from Cimarron.
>
> If you mean to call us squatters, say it in Spanish before our women and our old men and the bones above the church.
>
> **Ignacio Montoya**  
> for the people of San Esteban

### Handout 5. Extract from the Original Grant Language

**Give when:** the party gains Ouida's trust, steals a copy, or studies the Beaubien claim.

> **Spanish original**
>
> **Extracto de una copia de la merced Beaubien-Miranda**
>
> "...se concede el tracto arriba descrito para población, cultivo y pastos, con sus aguas, maderas y demás aprovechamientos tocantes a ello; **quedando siempre reservadas las minas y minerales al dominio del soberano**, y entendiéndose la concesión sujeta a las leyes y usos que gobiernan colonización y ocupación..."
>
> Nota de Ouida debajo, en mano más firme:
>
> La Compañía vive de que se olvide esa palabra. Reservadas. Si la reserva queda en pie, el oro no fue suyo para gravarlo, sólo suyo para arrebatárselo a otros mientras nadie miraba el texto primero.
>
> **Table translation**
>
> **Extract from a Copy of the Beaubien-Miranda Grant**  
> translated by Ouida Beaubien from the Spanish text she keeps
>
> "...the tract above described is conceded for settlement, cultivation, and pasturage, with all waters, timbers, and appurtenances proper thereto belonging; **provided always that the mines and minerals are reserved under the dominion of the sovereign**, and that the concession shall be held subject to the laws and usages governing colonization and occupancy..."
>
> Ouida's note beneath, in a firmer modern hand:
>
> The Company takes comfort from deeds written later, after men had forgotten what the first grant did and did not convey. But if the original reservation stands, then the gold was never honestly theirs to tax, only theirs to seize and keep seizing until nobody remembered the wording. The wording is the wound.

### Handout 6. Halladay's Field Book, Cover and First Useful Page

**Give when:** the party finds the book but has not yet had time to collate every page.

> **P. Halladay - Private Field Book**  
> Eastern Boundary, Maxwell Grant  
> begun `October 3, 1872`  
> resumed `March 11, 1873`
>
> Brown leather, oil-darkened at the corners. Several leaves water-stained at the edge. Writing small, neat, and made for a man's own use rather than a clerk's eye.
>
> ---
>
> `March 11, 1873`  
> Returned to the east line in company with T. Luján, guide, and one Jicarilla hunter who marks the older trail north of the creek. Weather thin cold, snow in the gullies. Re-ran from the pine blaze above the second bend and found my fall notes correct within less than one chain. The line as now insisted upon by Company men cannot be reconciled with the original calls unless one means to swallow the plaza entire and a breadth of creek bottom besides.
>
> I was again pressed by Pell to accept a "fairing" of the return before filing. Refused. If I write the false line, I write theft in my own hand.

### Handout 7. Halladay's Field Book, The True Line

**Give when:** the party studies the proof itself, copies the book, or prepares to use it in court.

> **Field Notes - Eastern Boundary, True Run**
>
> From the stone corner at timberline marked by my own hand, thence  
> S. `12° 40'` E. by chain and compass to the healed ponderosa blaze above the arroyo shelf, `84` chains.  
> Thence continuing same bearing to the creek turn north of San Esteban hay ground, `136` chains.  
> Thence to the lower meadow line and old ditch crossing, `219` chains total from corner.
>
> The line claimed by the Company in the revised return lies east of this by near `12` miles at its greatest theft, taking in the San Esteban plaza, cemetery ridge, acequia head, hay meadow, and color-bearing creek cuts now worked by local men.
>
> Note: no honest reading of the earlier calls permits this enlargement.

>
> Witnessed this day, `March 12, 1873`, at the upper corner above the cañón:
>

>

### Handout 9. Aldous Pell's Draft Affidavit

**Give when:** Pell signs, when the party steals his draft, or when a partial version turns up among his things.

> **Territory of New Mexico**  
> **County of Colfax**
>
> I, **Aldous Pell**, being first duly sworn, depose and say:
>
> That I was employed in the year `1872` and continuing into `1873` in matters touching the eastern survey line of the Maxwell Grant.
>
> That the line filed under the name of Phineas Halladay was not in all particulars the line actually run by said Halladay upon the ground.
>
> That I did, at the urging of Company interests represented to me by **Cornelius V. Hadley**, assent to the preparation and filing of a return placing the eastern line substantially east of the true calls and measurements taken in the field.
>
> That I knew at the time of filing that said return embraced settlements, meadows, and creek ground not honestly included in the original calls.
>
> That Halladay refused to sign such alteration freely.
>
> I make this statement in fear for my life and with the understanding that no honest correction can now be made except by public admission.
>
> **Aldous Pell**
>
> At the bottom, in a different, shakier hand:  
> _Not to be delivered unless I am already gone, or under guard I trust._

### Handout 10. Hadley's Letter to a Ring Lawyer in Santa Fe

**Give when:** the party intercepts Company mail, opens the strongbox in Tale 2, or finds the letter in Cimarron.

> **Private**  
> Cimarron, `April 16, 1873`
>
> Pike,
>
> The difficulty is no longer the plaza itself but the possibility of contradiction from beneath the filed line. Halladay proved less manageable than represented and Pell more manageable than respectable, which in this country must suffice. The return now in hand will stand if not embarrassed by working notes or by one or two mixed creatures who were too near the thing in March.
>
> San Esteban can be squeezed by freight and debt if the Court's pace requires time. The families are poor in the ordinary way and can be made poorer in the useful one. What I do not want is noise. If the book exists, it must be recovered before June term or made incredible in advance of it.
>
> Impress upon V. the necessity of ruling first upon form and standing. Once the matter is made to look untidy, jurors will cling to the cleaner paper.
>
> Yours,  
> **C. V. Hadley**

### Handout 11. Oren Pell's Note

**Give when:** the railroad makes first contact.

> A clean copy of any paper in your possession bearing on the eastern boundary. The railroad's draft, drawn on Topeka, for an amount we may settle in the morning. No inquiry as to source. No further obligation if the copy proves legible and materially useful. This offer lapses at noon tomorrow.
>
> Send answer by the hotel clerk if discreet. Burn after reading if wise.

### Handout 12. Thaddeus Lowe's Broadside

**Give when:** the party goes public through the press.

> **EXTRA - CIMARRON NEWS AND PRESS**  
> **Read This Before Court Sits**
>
> We are informed, on documentary authority too grave to dismiss, that the eastern line lately claimed for the Maxwell Grant was not honestly run upon the ground as filed, and that settlements long held under cultivation may be swallowed by a paper enlargement amounting to many miles.
>
> If this be false, let the Company answer plainly why one surveyor lies dead, why a second has gone to drink and terror, and why every useful witness must be hidden like a thief before term.
>
> The people of this county may yet learn whether title in New Mexico follows labor, burial, and possession, or only the cleanest fraud that reaches a clerk's desk.

### Handout 17. Halladay's Last Unsent Note

**Give when:** the party searches Father Vigil's cache, Halladay's effects, or an optional hiding place tied to the willows.

> Father,
>
> If I do not come down by Thursday, I ask only that somebody honest hear this much: the line they want is not the line I ran. Mr. Pell has not the courage for the thing but has enough weakness for it. Hadley smiles too much when a man says no.

## How to Reveal the Field Book

The book is strongest when it does not arrive all at once. Use these staged reveals as needed:

- **First touch:** Handout 6 only. The party knows the book is real and dangerous.
- **Proof of fraud:** Add Handout 7. The party now understands the scale of the theft.
- **Court-ready proof:** Add Handout 8. The party now holds the pages that can force the hearing.
- **Damaged or partial copy:** Give only one of the three leaves, or strike a line through one paragraph and say water or fire took the rest.

## How to Use Damage, Copies, and Bad Copies

These texts should be altered according to circumstance.

- A hurried copy should preserve the important facts but flatten handwriting, witness marks, or margins.
- A Company copy should omit the most dangerous marginal notes if the omission itself creates play.
- A damaged paper should lose names, distances, dates, or one crucial phrase rather than become unreadable wholesale.
- A broadside should be more forceful and less precise than the source document that inspired it.

The purpose of a handout in this chapter is not to satisfy archival curiosity. It is to put weight in the players' hands.
