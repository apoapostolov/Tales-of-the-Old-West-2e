# Quiet Title — A Treatise on Authenticity

This document is the historian's companion to [01-quiet-title.md](01-quiet-title.md), with the sandbox support in [01A-towns-trails-road-to-cimarron.md](01A-towns-trails-road-to-cimarron.md) and the expanded cast in [01B-dramatis-personae-and-statblocks.md](01B-dramatis-personae-and-statblocks.md). It is for the Game Master who wants to know, exactly, where the history in _Quiet Title_ ends and the fiction begins. It is a stamp. Run any scene in the adventure against the pages here and you will find the ground under it is real — the legal machinery, the survey methods, the economics, the smell of an Hispano plaza at spring melt, the mercury in the creek, the weight of a surveyor's chain. The characters are invented. The country, the law, the instruments, and the ruin are not.

The _1870s_ sourcebook covers the broad material world of the frontier West — what firearms, food, wages, law, mining, and religion looked like across the whole territory. This file does not repeat that work. It goes deep on the one corner of the West the adventure lives in: the Maxwell Land Grant country of the Sangre de Cristos in the spring of `1873`. Read it as a historian's notes to a fiction writer, or a fiction writer's promise to a historian.

The adventure's spine is real history wearing a thin coat of invented names. The coat is listed at the end. Everything else is on the record.

## Part One — The Real Spine

### The grant, the sale, and the syndicate

The **Maxwell Land Grant** was the largest private landholding in the United States, and its history is the history of how paper turned conquered ground into private property.

In `1841` the Mexican governor of New Mexico, Manuel Armijo, granted roughly 96,000 acres (the figure the Mexican grant specified: the legal instrument is real, the acreage is debated) to **Charles Beaubien** and **Guadalupe Miranda**. The grant was a Mexican legal act, made under Mexican law, on Mexican soil. Six years later that soil was American, taken in the Mexican War, and the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (`1848`) promised that Mexican property rights in the ceded territory would be respected. They were not.

Beaubien's son-in-law **Lucien B. Maxwell**, a frontiersman who had been at Bent's Fort and had married Carlos Beaubien's daughter, bought out the other interests across the `1850s` and `1860s` and built a private empire on the grant — cattle, sheep, freighting, and the Cimarron ranch headquarters. Maxwell was, for a stretch, the largest private landowner in the United States. In `1870` he sold the entire grant to a syndicate of English and Dutch investors for roughly $1.35 million, and the **Maxwell Land Grant and Railway Company** was formed to assert title to it. Maxwell took his money and moved to Fort Sumner. He died in `1875`. The country he left behind him stayed at war for another twenty years.

That is the real history the adventure sits on. The foreign-backed company is real. The `1870` sale is real. The railway company is real. The Company's hunger to assert title across the whole grant at once is real.

> The adventure compresses the timeline for play. In real history the great boundary fights and the violent evictions came in the `1880s`, after the Supreme Court ruled in the Company's favor in `1887`. The adventure sets the same fight in `1873` so the player characters are in the country while the paper is still wet and the outcome is not yet decided. This is the largest liberty the adventure takes with the chronology, and it is taken openly. The shape of the fight is unchanged; only the year is moved.

### The eastern boundary and the survey fraud

The real engine of the Maxwell Grant conflict was the eastern boundary. The Mexican grant of `1841` had fixed the eastern line at a particular point. The Company's surveyors ran it farther — much farther. The result was that the Company claimed roughly 1.7 million acres where the Mexican grant had conveyed roughly 96,000. Eighteen miles of country, in places. Homesteads, plazas, and mineral land that had never been inside any grant were drawn inside this one.

The real survey at the center of the real dispute was the **Griffin survey** (so named in the historical record). The Supreme Court case _United States v. Maxwell Land Grant Co._, `121 U.S. 325` (`1887`), turned on whether the Griffin survey's eastern and northern boundaries were fraudulent — that is, whether the surveyors had deliberately inflated the grant to enrich the Company. The Court found that fraud had **not** been established, and upheld the boundaries as surveyed. The decision effectively validated the Company's title to the full 1.7 million acres. The settlers lost. The quiet-title and ejectment actions that followed, through the late `1880s` and the `1890s`, drove hundreds of families off ground their grandfathers had broken.

In the adventure, the fictive surveyor **Phineas Halladay** stands in for the real honest men in the GLO and the real friction between federal surveyors and Company surveyors. The fictive surveyor **Aldous Pell** stands in for the real Company-connected men whose lines moved where the money needed them to move. The fictive field book stands in for the real field notes that were, in fact, the legal heart of the matter — a survey was only as honest as the notes its deputy kept, and the notes were contestable, concealable, and forgeable. The fraud the adventure depicts is the fraud the real settlers alleged and the real Court declined to find.

> The adventure's "twelve miles east" figure is a fiction made of a real shape. The real inflation was larger and messier. Twelve miles is chosen for play because it is graspable — a man can ride it in a morning and understand what was taken. The real number was harder to ride and harder to grieve.

### The Santa Fe Ring and the courts

The **Santa Fe Ring** was real. It was a loose, durable confederation of lawyers, politicians, judges, and businessmen who controlled territorial New Mexico from roughly the Civil War to the turn of the century, and whose primary business was the acquisition and litigation of Spanish and Mexican land grants. Its leading figures — **Thomas B. Catron** and **Stephen B. Elkins** — are real men, and their method was real: buy a grant claim cheaply, run a friendly survey, litigate the title in a territorial court whose judges the Ring had appointed, win, and sell the now-clean title at a vast markup. Catron, by accumulation, became at one point the largest private landowner in the United States. He later served as a U.S. Senator from New Mexico.

The Ring's grip on the territorial bench was the reason a grant suit in `1870s` New Mexico was less a legal proceeding than a real-estate closing held in a courtroom. Federal judges in the territories were patronage appointments; the patronage came through the Ring; the judges ruled accordingly. A quiet-title suit in Cimarron in `1873` would have been heard by a judge whose appointment, salary, and prospects were wired to the same men whose name was on the plaintiff's brief.

In the adventure, the fictive Judge **Mortimer Vail** and the fictive Ring lawyer **Harlan Pike** stand in for this real machinery. The Company's "owned court" is not a cynic's flourish. It is the documented operating condition of the territorial judiciary.

The courthouse town around such a suit was also real, and it mattered. A place like **Cimarron** in `1873` was not merely where the judge sat. It was where jurors drank, where notaries and county clerks copied paper, where witnesses could be housed, bought, jailed, or frightened, and where the local press could turn private fraud into public scandal if the forms were locked before the Company broke the windows. Territorial juries were local, male, Anglo-leaning, and highly permeable to money, reputation, and saloon pressure. Frontier newspapers were partisan by default, often undercapitalized, and vulnerable to libel pressure, arson, broken presses, and advertiser withdrawal. The adventure's expanded Cimarron play — guarding witnesses, intercepting dispatch riders, printing broadsides, protecting a newspaper office, and fighting over which papers become public before the ruling lands — is not embroidery. It is how a nineteenth-century court fight actually spilled into the street.

### The railroad and the right of way

The **Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railroad** was real, chartered in `1859`, building west through the early `1870s`, and hungry for a southern transcontinental route. A clean right of way across the Maxwell Grant was worth a fortune in `1873`, and the men who held the grant's paper knew it. The Company's interest in settling the boundary was never only about cattle and gold. It was about selling a corridor to the railroad before the railroad could take it by federal eminent domain for less.

The fictive railroad agent **Oren Pell** stands in for the real right-of-way agents who crisscrossed the grant country in these years, taking notes, buying options, and waiting for the law to settle so the iron could be laid. His patience is the real patience of the railroad, which measured its ambitions in decades and its debts in continents.

### The gold camp and the mercury

The gold strike at **Baldy Mountain** in `1866` was real, and the boom camp of **Elizabethtown** (called "E-Town" by its people) was real — founded in `1866`, incorporated (the first incorporated town in New Mexico), and well into its boom by `1873`, with several thousand souls at peak and the Aztec Mine pulling ore out of the mountain. The gold was real. The creek it sat in was real. The royalty fights between the Company and the miners were real.

The mercury poisoning the adventure depicts in the Baldy creek is real technology applied accurately. The low-grade ore at Baldy was worked by **arrastres** and later by stamp mills, and the recovery method for hardrock gold in the `1870s` was **amalgamation with mercury** — the crushed ore was washed over copper plates coated with mercury, or ground with mercury in the arrastre, and the mercury bound the gold into an amalgam that was then retorted (heated to drive the mercury off as vapor, leaving the gold). The mercury that escaped — and a great deal escaped, in the tailings, in the creek, in the vapor — is a documented environmental catastrophe of the nineteenth-century West. Men who worked the amalgamation process absorbed mercury through the skin and the lungs. The symptoms are the ones the adventure gives its shaking miners: tremor (the "miner's shakes"), salivation, mood disturbance, and, eventually, neurological ruin. A party that drinks from the lower creek in `1873` is drinking the future.

> The adventure does not say "the Baldy miners have mercury poisoning." It shows a few of them shaking. The GM who reads this treatise knows what the shaking is. The players can discover it, or not. The country does not label its poisons.

## Part Two — The Surveyor's Trade

The surveyor is the adventure's hero, its villain, and its corpse. It is worth knowing what a General Land Office deputy surveyor in `1872` actually carried, did, and wrote down, because the whole plot turns on the difference between a true line and a false one.

### The instruments

A GLO deputy surveyor in the early `1870s` ran his lines with three instruments and a notebook.

The **Gunter's chain** was the workhorse. It was sixty-six feet long, made of a hundred iron or steel links, with brass tallies every ten links so the chainman could count by feel. Sixty-six feet because eighty chains made a mile and ten square chains made an acre — the chain was an arithmetic instrument as much as a measuring one, designed so a surveyor could convert distance to acreage in his head. A deputy surveyor carried spare links and a repair kit, because chains broke in the brush and a broken chain that went unrepaired shortened every line it measured. A surveyor who wanted to inflate a grant could simply run a chain that was a little long, or a little cold, or a little "repaired." The fraud lived in the metal.

The **compass** (often a **solar compass** of the Burt design, patented `1836`, which used the sun's position to find true north and so avoided the local magnetic anomaly that made plain compasses unreliable in mineral ground) set the bearing. The **transit** or **theodolite** measured angles more precisely than a compass and was used for the important corners. A deputy carried both, because the compass was fast and the transit was exact and a line run by compass could be checked by transit.

The whole kit — chain, compass or transit, tripod, flag poles, axes for blazing, stakes for corners, and the field books — rode a wagon and weighed more than a man.

### The line on the ground

A GLO survey line was a cut path. The party of axemen went first, cutting brush and blazing the bearing trees so the line could be found again. The chainmen followed, dragging the chain forward, driving a tally pin every chain, counting the links aloud. The compassman or transitman followed them, keeping the bearing. The deputy brought up the rear with the field book, recording every chain length, every bearing, every creek crossed, every corner set.

A **corner** was a legal monument. In timbered country it was a post set in a mound of earth, surrounded by bearing trees — trees the axemen blazed and notched, with the corner's township, range, and section inscribed on the blaze. In open country it was a mound of earth or a pit or a pile of stone. **A cairn of stone at a ridge corner was a standard GLO corner monument in mountainous treeless country** — which is exactly where the adventure puts Halladay's field book. The cairn in the adventure is a real corner monument, built the real way, and a real deputy surveyor would have built it exactly where Halladay built it: at the eastern corner of the grant, on a ridge, where a man with a transit would set up to shoot the line.

### The field book

The field book was the survey's legal heart. A deputy kept his field notes in a bound book, in ink, as he ran the line, and the field book was the primary record — the plat map drawn later in the office was derived from it. The field book recorded the bearings, the distances, the corners, the bearing trees, the watercourses, the terrain, and (crucially) the **witnesses**. A deputy surveyor working in disputed country who wanted his line to survive a challenge would have his chainmen and his guide sign or mark the book at the corners, because a witnessed corner was harder to repudiate than an unwitnessed one.

The field book could be filed, copied, forged, altered, "lost," or burned. The GLO's nightmare was the deputy whose field book disagreed with his filed return — the return being the clean summary he sent to Washington, the field book being the messy truth he kept in his pocket. The whole history of western land fraud turns on the gap between the return and the field book. The adventure's central document — Halladay's private field book, witnessed, hidden in a corner cairn, its filed return falsified under his dead name — is the GLO's nightmare made into a plot. It is not an invention. It is the thing the real Company men were accused of doing with the real surveys.

> The detail that Halladay's field book is "signed and witnessed by Tomás Luján and by the mark of Nah-leh's cousin" is period-accurate and load-bearing. A witnessed field book was the one document a Company lawyer could not simply call a forgery. A field book without witnesses was a love letter. A field book with witnesses was a case.

## Part Three — The Plaza and the Acequia

The plaza of San Esteban is invented. Everything in it is real.

### The Hispano plaza

A northern New Mexico Hispano plaza in `1873` was not a town in the Anglo sense. It was a **defensible cluster of adobe rooms around a central common**, built by an extended family or a small group of families, with the church, the cemetery, and the irrigated fields as its three anchors. The houses were **adobe** — sun-dried brick of earth and straw, laid in courses, plastered with mud, re-plastered every year after the rains. The walls were two feet thick because adobe that thin crumbled in a wet year. The floors were packed earth, swept damp to keep the dust down. The roofs were **vigas** (ponderosa pine beams, hewn flat on top) laid across the walls, with **latillas** (smaller saplings) laid across the vigas, and a layer of earth on top, rolled hard, that shed the snow and held the heat. A plaza house in `1873` smelled of woodsmoke, chile, pine resin from the vigas, and the wet-earth smell of the roof after rain.

The **acequia** — the irrigation ditch — was the plaza's central nervous system. It was not a convenience. It was the reason the plaza existed where it existed. The acequia ran from the creek to the fields, divided into laterals at the upper end of the common, and was governed by the **acequia rules**: each family took its water on its appointed day, in its appointed order, in its appointed amount, and the order rotated through the season so the families at the bottom of the ditch were not always last. The man who decided disputes was the **mayordomo** (in the adventure, this is folded into Don Ignacio's role as _juez de agua_, the water judge). The acequia was, in the plaza's actual daily life, far more important than any territorial court. The court could take your land on paper. The mayordomo could take your corn in July.

> The adventure's repeated image of the acequia running down the middle of the common is not a decoration. It is the plaza's legal, economic, and social structure made visible. When the Company's line moves twelve miles east, it moves across the acequia. The first thing the plaza loses is not the land. It is the water.

### The cemetery as title

The plaza cemetery on the ridge is the adventure's quietest argument and its truest. In Hispano northern New Mexico, the **campo santo** (holy field) was the oldest and most legible record of possession a community had. The graves had names and dates. The dates went back to the founding. A community that could point to forty years of its dead in the ground had a claim that predated the Company, predated the `1841` grant in many cases, and could not be burned, filed, or surveyed away. Father Vigil's cemetery register — a bound book of the dead, with dates and family lines — is a real kind of document, kept by real priests, and in a grant dispute it was a real and powerful piece of evidence. A grave was a corner monument that the Company could not move.

> The line _"a grave is the oldest title there is"_ is the adventure's thesis stated in the plaza's own terms. The courts did not agree. The courts ruled for the Company. The graves are still on the ridge.

### The Penitente context

Father Vigil's "Penitente-country" background and the cross driven into the ridge in Story 5 are real. The **Los Hermanos Penitentes** were (and are) a lay Catholic brotherhood, primarily Hispano, primarily in northern New Mexico and southern Colorado, whose practices — including processions, self-flagellation during Holy Week, and the reenactment of the crucifixion — were older than the diocese's formal authority over them and existed in an uneasy relationship with the institutional Church. By the `1870s` the Penitentes were a major social and moral force in the Sangre de Cristo plazas, often more present in a community's life than the parish priest, and a Penitente cross driven into the ground was a communication the community understood and the Company did not. The brotherhood's ideas of justice were older than the territory's. A Game Master who runs Story 5 should know that the Penitentes are not a costume. They are a real institution with a real theology and a real history of community self-governance under conditions where the official law was absent, hostile, or bought.

### The gazetteer settlements

The location file [01A-towns-trails-road-to-cimarron.md](01A-towns-trails-road-to-cimarron.md) takes three different kinds of place and treats each according to the real logic of the country.

**San Esteban** is invented in name and exact placement, but it is historically sound in type. Its adobe square, church, cemetery ridge, acequia, family houses, store, stable yard, and nearby sheep camps are built from the real architecture and social organization of the Hispano plazas in the Maxwell Grant country. The settlement statblock given to San Esteban is therefore not the statblock of one documented historical town. It is the statblock of a **composite northern New Mexico plaza in `1873`**, weighted toward irrigation, memory, stock, and weak formal law. That is exactly what such a place was.

**Elizabethtown** is real, and the gazetteer's treatment of it as a long dirty string of saloons, claim business, freight, assay work, and hardrock milling is historically accurate. The exact room placements, named interiors, and specific arrangement of Strand's sphere, Pell's room, and Ouida's quarters are invented for play. The camp functions attached to them are not. An assay office, a royalty collection point, boarding shanties, mule lines, stamp work, and played-out cuts were all ordinary features of Baldy's mining life by `1873`.

**Cimarron** is also real, and the gazetteer is accurate in treating it less as a frontier village than as a **company town with a court in it**. The Company's store as the economic center, the hotel as witness ground, the livery and stage stand as custody points, the sheriff's office as an arm of Company pressure, and the newspaper office as the one semi-independent institution in town all reflect the actual kinds of places where territorial litigation was made practical. The exact offices and the exact editor are, again, inventions standing on real functions.

### Small places, camps, and why there are not more villages

The gazetteer gives short statblocks to the **upper sheep camp** and the **lower pasture line camp** because those are recurring habitation nodes in play, not because they were "towns." That distinction matters historically.

Much of the country between Cimarron, the foothill plazas, and Baldy Mountain in `1873` was not filled with named villages every few miles. It was filled with **camps, line shacks, jacales, sheep grounds, abandoned prospect holes, freight halts, and isolated family places**. A western sandbox that invents too many tidy settlements for such country starts to feel more like a fantasy hex crawl than the Sangre de Cristo front. The new `01A` therefore does the right thing historically: it gives full treatment to the major settlements, short treatment to the recurring work sites, and leaves the rest of the country thinly peopled, hard to police, and easy to disappear in.

If a Game Master later adds one or two extra farmsteads, line cabins, or jacal clusters to suit table play, that is still historically true to the region so long as they remain **small, kin-based, poor in formal institutions, and vulnerable to road pressure**.

### The road, the trail, and the pointcrawl

The pointcrawl procedure in `01A` is not an abstraction laid over the country from outside. It is a way of running the country that matches how the country actually constrained travel in `1873`.

The distances used there are deliberately **real-world-feeling rather than survey-perfect**. Cimarron, the Baldy-Elizabethtown mining district, the foothill plaza country, and the rising cañón country all sat close enough to affect one another quickly, but far enough apart that every ride revealed intention and carried cost. A hard horseback day of roughly twenty to thirty miles on formed road, a slower mountain day into Baldy, and a punishing half day to day in the upper cañón and line country are historically plausible assumptions for the season, terrain, and loads involved.

The route treatment is also accurate in type:

- a **settled road** to Cimarron, carrying freight, notices, legal paper, and gossip
- a **harder mining road** toward Elizabethtown, shaped by grade, weather, and freight traffic
- a **narrow cañón trail** where stock, guides, weather, and visibility matter more than mileage

The event density in `01A` is intentionally higher than life in order to produce gameable pressure, but the **kind** of pressure it assumes is historical: broken freight, bad weather, road agents, process servers, witnesses in motion, laborers carrying rumor, and the simple fact that in a watched country, strangers on horseback are noticed.

## Part Four — The Peoples of the Grant

### The Jicarilla Apache and the high parks

The **Jicarilla Apache** were real and were, through the `1870s`, being pressed by the same forces the adventure depicts. The Jicarilla (whose name is sometimes given as _Haisndayin_, "the people") are an Athabaskan people, closely related to the other Apache groups and to the Navajo, whose homeland included the Sangre de Cristo and the San Juan country. They were hunters and gatherers with a strong seasonal round — the high parks of the Sangre de Cristos were summer hunting and gathering ground, the lower country winter refuge — and a trade and raid economy that connected them to both the Pueblo and the Plains worlds.

The Jicarilla's history in the `1850s-1870s` is one of displacement, failed treaty-making, starvation, and intermittent war. They were pressed onto inadequate reservation land, removed, pressed again. By `1873` many Jicarilla were at the **Cimarron Agency** or scattered in the mountains, hunting a country the survey chain was crossing. The adventure's depiction of a Jicarilla hunting party in the high parks, watching the chain come up the cañón, is on the record.

**Nah-leh** is invented. His situation is not. A Jicarilla man in `1873` whose hunting ground was being surveyed, whose people were being starved onto an agency, and who had watched the survey chain arrive would have read the chain exactly as Nah-leh reads it: as the first cut of a dispossession that would not stop at the first line. The detail that Nah-leh's cousin was on the ridge hunting elk the night the surveyor died, and saw what he saw, and said nothing, is the detail of a man who has learned that being a witness to white violence is a good way to become the next victim of it.

> The adventure's rule for Nah-leh's speech — that he uses English last and least, and that his English is spare, exact, and cutting — is not a stylistic flourish. It is the speech pattern of a multilingual man (Apache, Spanish, English) for whom English is the language of the enemy's law and is therefore used only when the enemy's law must be addressed. A Game Master who plays Nah-leh should resist the urge to make him eloquent in English. His eloquence is in what he does not say.

### The Ute

The **Ute** held the high parks and the mountain parks of the Sangre de Cristos and the San Juans. The **Muache** band of the Ute were the specific neighbors of the Maxwell Grant country. They were also being pressed, treated, and squeezed through the `1870s`, and the **Brunot Agreement** of `1873` (which ceded a vast stretch of Ute land in Colorado to the United States) was being negotiated in exactly the year the adventure is set. The Ute in the adventure — referenced in the freight-to-Taos story, in the high-country threat, in Nah-leh's politics — are the real neighbors of the real grant, being dispossessed by the real paper in the same season.

### The Hispano community and the law

The comunidad of San Esteban stands for the real Hispano communities of the Maxwell Grant country — **Rayado, Cimarron's outlying plazas, the settlements up the Purgatoire**, and the dozens of small plazas that had been farming and grazing the Sangre de Cristo foothills since the `1830s`, `1820s`, and earlier. These communities had come north out of the older Hispano settlements of the Río Arriba (the upper Rio Grande country, Santa Fe northward) in the Mexican period and the late Spanish period, and their legal tradition was **Mexican and Spanish property law**, under which possession, use, and long occupation carried weight. The transition to American sovereignty in `1848` did not, on paper, extinguish those rights. In practice it did, because the American courts that heard grant cases did not understand (or did not care to understand) the Mexican legal categories, and the Anglo lawyers and speculators who bought the grants did.

The adventure's Don Ignacio, with his notebook of acequia turns and graves, his slow English, and his conviction that possession is title, is the real voice of the real comunidad. The real communities lost. The adventure lets the player characters try to change that, which is the gift fiction can give that history could not.

## Part Five — The Technology and the Economics

These are the period-specific details a Game Master can lean on to make scenes land. They do not repeat the sourcebook's general material-culture coverage. They are specific to this adventure and this country.

### The cap-and-ball revolver and the cartridge revolver

`1873` is a hinge year in firearms, and the adventure sits on the hinge. **Strand's Colt 1860 Army** is a **cap-and-ball** percussion revolver — the standard Union sidearm of the Civil War, made `1860-1873`, sold surplus in bulk after the war, and still the most common revolver in the frontier West in `1873`. To load it, a man poured powder into each chamber, seated a ball with the lever under the barrel, capped each nipple with a percussion cap, and repeated for all six chambers. A practiced man could do it in a minute. A man under fire could not. Cap-and-ball revolvers fouled badly — the black powder residue built up in the chambers until the cylinder jammed — and after twenty or thirty rounds a man was beating the cylinder pin free with a stick. The 1860 Army in the adventure is the gun a broke saloon-keeper in `1873` actually carried: surplus, cheap, reliable in the way an old tool is reliable, and already obsolete.

The **Colt .45 Peacemaker**, which Hadley and the Company men carry, was introduced in `1873` and is the cartridge revolver that made the cap-and-ball gun obsolete overnight. It loaded six metallic cartridges through a loading gate, ejected the empties with a rod, and was back in battery in seconds. A man with a Peacemaker had a real firepower advantage over a man with an 1860 Army, and the adventure gives the Company that advantage for the reason the real Company had it: the Company could afford the new gun, and the men it was displacing could not.

> A Game Master who wants to feel the year `1873` in a combat scene should let the cap-and-ball guns foul and jam after extended shooting and let the cartridge guns keep running. That is the technology telling the story of who has money and who does not.

### The arrastre and the stamp mill

The **arrastre** is the oldest ore-grinding technology in the Spanish mining tradition — a circular pit lined with stone, into which ore was shoveled, and across which a drag-stone (often a granite or quartz boulder) was pulled by a mule or a horse walking in a circle, grinding the ore to sand. The Spanish had used it since the sixteenth century. The Hispano miners of New Mexico used it in `1873`. An arrastre could be built in a day with stone, a mule, and a man who knew the circle. It was slow. It was cheap. It was the technology of the community, not the Company.

The **stamp mill** — the heavy iron pestles driven by steam or water that crushed ore far faster than an arrastre — was the Company's technology, and its beat is the beat the adventure puts under the Elizabethtown scenes. A stamp mill cost real money, demanded real fuel, and crushed enough ore to feed an amalgamation plant that ate mercury by the flask. The camp that had a stamp mill was the camp that was owned by someone with capital. The camp that had only arrastres was the camp that was not.

### The flour arithmetic

The adventure's flour price — six cents a pound in the plaza, three in Cimarron — is the documented economics of a company town working as intended. In `1873` New Mexico, flour ran roughly three to five cents a pound at the source, depending on the harvest. A community that bought its flour from a single supplier, on a single road, controlled by a single company, paid what the company charged. The "flour clock" in the adventure's faction-clocks section is the real mechanism by which a grant company could dispossess a community without firing a shot: hold the price high enough, long enough, and the families go broke, sell their land for the back debts, and leave. The courts were not involved. The law of contract and debt did the work the lawsuits were too slow to do.

> A Game Master who runs Eufemia's ledger scene should let the arithmetic do the moral work. Three cents a pound is a price. Six cents a pound is a siege. The players will feel the difference without being told what it means.

### The dollar a day

The wage the adventure offers — a dollar a day and found (meaning board, meaning meals) — is the real working wage of the frontier West in `1873`. A vaquero drew $25-$40 a month. A ranch hand about the same. A miner working for wages drew $3-$4 a day at the good camps, less at the bad. A dollar a day and found was honest money for honest work, and it was the wage that left a man no room to refuse the extra dollar for the work that was not honest — because a man with no savings cannot afford to be choosy about whose dollar he takes. The whole moral economy of the adventure — why men ride for Hadley, why they ride for Strand, why they do not simply ride away — sits on that dollar a day. The country does not pay a man enough to be good.

## Part Six — The Psychology of the Operators

This is the part the sourcebook does not cover, because it is specific to the grant-company operator and the grant-community patriarch. It is the interior life the adventure's principals are built on.

### The Hadley type

Cornelius Hadley is the real type of the eastern-educated frontier operator who came west in the `1860s` to manage other people's capital and stayed because the managing was more lucrative than anything he could have done back east. The type is documented across the West — the Yale man running a Colorado silver syndicate, the Harvard man running a Montana copper concern, the Brown man running a New Mexico grant. Their psychology was not the psychology of the gambler or the gunfighter. It was the psychology of the **middle manager who has been given absolute authority over a distant asset and who is judged only on the return**. Such men are not villains in their own interiors. They are professionals doing a professional job under difficult conditions, and the fact that the job requires them to dispossess people and occasionally have them killed is, to them, a cost of doing business, the way a railroad engineer regards the men who die building a cut. Hadley's coldness is not cruelty. It is the emotional economy of a man who has decided that the work is too large to feel through.

> The most useful thing a Game Master can know about Hadley is that he does not hate the comunidad. He does not think about the comunidad. He thinks about the return. The comunidad is between him and the return. That is all. A villain who hates is easier to defeat than a professional who is merely doing his job.

### The Don Ignacio type

Don Ignacio is the real type of the Hispano community patriarch — the _patrón_ of a plaza, the man whose family broke the ground, who holds the acequia authority, who mediates disputes, and whose authority is personal, traditional, and older than any American law. Such men were the actual government of the northern New Mexico plazas through the whole territorial period, because the territorial government was in Santa Fe and the plaza was two weeks away and the plaza's problems were immediate. The patrón's psychology is the psychology of **custody** — he holds the community in trust for the dead who built it and the grandchildren who will inherit it, and his patience, his slowness, and his refusal to be hurried are the virtues of a man whose horizon is generational. He does not think in quarters. He thinks in cemeteries.

> The deepest conflict in the adventure is not between Hadley and Don Ignacio. It is between two different ways of holding land: Hadley holds it as an asset on a balance sheet; Don Ignacio holds it as a grave, an acequia, and a grandson's inheritance. They cannot understand each other. They are not speaking the same language even when they are both speaking English.

### The Strand type

Strand is the real type of the western camp boss — the man with a cloudy past, a loud manner, a talent for violence, and a genuine loyalty to the rough community that has become his home. The type populated every boom camp in the West: the saloon-keeper who became the camp's informal mayor, the gambler who became its banker, the brawler who became its law. Their psychology is the psychology of the **self-made man who is never quite sure he is made** — the man who has reinvented himself so many times he is not certain which version is real, and who covers the uncertainty with volume, generosity, and the willingness to shoot first. The "Colonel" title is the real western pretension; the camps were full of captains, majors, and colonels who had never served, because the title cost nothing and bought a drink.

### The Tomás type

Tomás Luján is the real type of the **genízaro** — the detribalized or mixed-blood Hispano-Indian man of the northern frontier, who belonged fully to neither world and who served as the indispensable intermediary between them: guide, interpreter, tracker, scout, messenger. The genízaros were a real social category in New Mexico, recognized in the census, often living in their own settlements, and their position between the worlds made them both essential and expendable. A guide like Tomás knew the country better than any white surveyor and most of the Apache, and his knowledge made him valuable and his knowledge made him dangerous — to whoever he guided for, and to himself. The guide who sees too much is the guide who has to be managed. Tomás's broken nerve is the nerve of a man who knows that his usefulness is the only thing keeping him alive and that his usefulness is also the thing that will get him killed.

## Part Seven — The Senses of the Grant

These are the specific sensory details a Game Master can drop into a scene to make the country real. They are chosen because they are accurate to the Sangre de Cristo in spring `1873` and because they are not the generic frontier smells (woodsmoke, horse sweat, leather) every western already has.

### Spring melt in the cañón

The creek in March-April is snowmelt, and snowmelt water is a specific thing: clear, green-black over the bed, so cold it aches the hand in three seconds, running fast and high in the afternoon when the sun is on the high snow and low and quiet at dawn. The sound of a snowmelt creek is a continuous low roar broken by the higher note of water pouring over ledges. The willows along the bank are the first thing to green — the _Salix_ species leaf out before anything else, a pale yellow-green, and the catkins come before the leaves. The ponderosa are dark and the aspen are bare and white and the spruce at the higher elevations hold snow in their branches that drops in the afternoon heat with a thump. The air in the cañón at this season smells of cold water, wet stone, the balsam of the ponderosa bark (which smells, exactly, of vanilla and butterscotch where the sun has warmed it), and the first faint green of the willow sap. A man riding up the cañón in April `1873` would smell vanilla where his horse brushed the ponderosa.

### The plaza at dusk

The plaza at dusk in spring smells of the cookfire (piñon pine, which burns hot and clean and smells of resin), of chile (the red chile ristras hanging on the south wall of every kitchen, dried the previous fall, ground into the pot), of the acequia (cold water and the faint vegetal smell of the moss growing on the ditch stones), and of the animals (the horses at the hitch rail, the chickens on the roof of the kitchen lean-to, the goats in the pen behind the Archuleta house). The smoke from the cookfires lies flat in the still spring air and turns the last light gold. The church bell rings for vespers. The dogs bark once at the bell and stop. The cemetery on the ridge is visible as white crosses against the dark timber, the last thing the light touches before the ridge goes dark.

### The boom camp

Elizabethtown in `1873` smells, in order of strength, of woodsmoke (every stove and every heap-fire burning green timber because dry timber was for the stamp mill), of mercury (the metallic, faintly sweet smell of the amalgamation plates, which a man who has worked the process carries in his skin for weeks), of unwashed men (the camps had no baths and the creek was too cold to wash in until June), of powder (the black powder smoke from the drifts, which hangs in the still air of the bowl and gives the camp a permanent haze), and, underneath it all, of money — the metallic, dirty, exciting smell of a place where gold is changing hands. The sound of the camp is the stamp mill's beat (a slow, heavy, arrhythmic clang that the men stop hearing after the first day and the visitor never stops hearing), the shout of the teamsters, the clatter of the arrastra mules, and, at night, the saloons.

### The courtroom

The Cimarron courtroom in June smells of lamp oil (the courtroom above the store is lit by kerosene lamps because the windows are small and the term runs all day), of the tobacco the Company's lawyers chew (plug tobacco, which smells of molasses and licorice and spit), of the bodies in the closed room (the courtroom is full and it is June and the building has no ventilation), of the dry goods stored on the floor below (flour, coffee, kerosene, leather, soap — the whole inventory of a frontier store rising through the floorboards), and of paper — the clean, exciting, dangerous smell of a great deal of paper being shuffled, signed, and filed. A man who has spent his life around paper knows the smell. It is the smell of the law. It is the smell of the Company.

## Part Eight — Where History Ends and Fiction Begins

This is the ledger the user asked for. Everything above this line is the record. Everything in this section is the invention. The adventure is built so that the inventions sit on top of the record the way a corner cairn sits on a ridge — the cairn is made up, the ridge is real, and the cairn could not stand without the ridge.

### Real, and used accurately

- The Maxwell Land Grant and its `1841` Mexican origin (Beaubien-Miranda).
- Lucien B. Maxwell and his `1870` sale to the English-Dutch syndicate.
- The Maxwell Land Grant and Railway Company.
- The eastern boundary dispute, the survey fraud allegations, and the inflation of the grant from ~96,000 to ~1.7 million acres.
- The `1887` Supreme Court decision (_United States v. Maxwell Land Grant Co._, 121 U.S. 325) upholding the Company's title.
- The quiet-title and ejectment actions against settlers in the `1880s-1890s`.
- The Santa Fe Ring, Thomas B. Catron, Stephen B. Elkins.
- The pattern of patronage-appointed territorial judges ruling in Ring-aligned interests.
- The Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railroad and its right-of-way ambitions.
- The `1866` Baldy Mountain gold strike, the boom camp of Elizabethtown, the Aztec Mine.
- Mercury amalgamation as the period recovery method, and mercury poisoning as its documented consequence.
- The GLO deputy-surveyor system: the chain, the compass/transit, the blazes, the corner monument (including the stone cairn), and the field book as the primary legal record.
- The Jicarilla Apache, their displacement, and the Cimarron Agency.
- The Muache Ute and the `1873` Brunot Agreement context.
- The Hispano plaza, the acequia, the mayordomo, the campo santo.
- The Penitente brotherhood.
- The genízaro as a social category.
- The dollar-a-day wage, the flour-price economics, the cap-and-ball vs. cartridge revolver hinge of `1873`.

### Invented, and standing in for real things

### The one large liberty

The adventure sets the decisive hearing in the spring of `1873`. In real history, the great Maxwell boundary fights, the Supreme Court decision, and the violent evictions came in the `1880s` and `1890s`. The adventure moves the climax up a decade so the player characters can be in the country while the outcome is still in doubt. The shape of the fight — the survey fraud, the owned court, the foreign-backed company, the dispossessed community, the railroad waiting — is unchanged. Only the year is moved, and it is moved so the table can play the version of the story where the field book reaches the courtroom in time. History did not let it. Fiction can.

That is the whole of the liberty. Everything else is on the ridge.

## A Note for the Game Master

Run this adventure with the confidence that the ground under it is real. When a player asks whether the eastern boundary dispute really happened, it did. When a player asks whether the court was really owned, it was. When a player asks whether a surveyor could really hide a true line in a corner cairn while a false line was filed under his name, he could, and men like him were accused of exactly that. When a player asks whether the flour was really six cents and the wage really a dollar, it was, and the gap between them is the gap the whole country lived in.

The characters are invented. The ruin is not. The adventure's promise to the table is that every dollar, every chain link, every blaze on every ponderosa, and every grave on every ridge is drawn from the record. The fiction is the coat. The history is the man inside it.
