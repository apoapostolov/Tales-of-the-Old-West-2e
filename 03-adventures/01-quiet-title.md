# Quiet Title

> The water of the Arroyo de las Vacas ran shallow over gravel and the surveyor's boots were still laced. He lay on his face in the willows with his field book under him and the back of his skull gone. The Company's return said he had drowned. The creek at that bend was not eight inches deep.
>
> That was the spring of `1873`. By the first cutting of hay the plaza was in the suit and the book had not been found.

The Sangre de Cristo country is the right way to open a northern New Mexico campaign in `1873`.

Nothing in it is settled. A foreign-backed company claims a million and three quarter acres by paper and sends its riders to prove it. Hispano families who broke this ground before Maxwell was born are served notice that they squat on land they have always owned. Gold men at Baldy Mountain want the diggings free of royalty. A railroad company wants a right of way across the grant and does not much care whose name is on the deed so long as the deed is clean. Jicarilla and Ute hunters are being fenced off the high country where they have always taken their meat. The courts belong to the same men who own the paper. This chapter drops the player characters into that knot. They begin low. A hired hand, a vaquero, a teamster, a mine mucker, a discharged soldier, a courthouse clerk run out of Santa Fe, a newspaperman sent up from the _Cimarron News_ to cover the suit, a bounty hunter on a paper of his own. All of those fit. By the end of the chapter they will have seen corporate greed, a community's slow dispossession, frontier law bought and paid for, the cost of a true line run through country that wants the false one, and the price of carrying a book men are willing to kill for.

The action is broadly linear. The plaza of San Esteban sits at the mouth of a cañón on the eastern slope of the Sangre de Cristos, inside the Maxwell Grant boundary as the Company draws it. A dead surveyor, a frightened guide, and a missing field book begin the trouble before the player characters understand whose side they are on. Out in the field the quiet-title suit turns political, then openly violent. A Jicarilla warning offers one way through the high country while Company riders and gold-camp toughs push toward another. By the time the book surfaces and men begin shooting one another in the courtroom town of Cimarron, the campaign is no longer about one surveyor's death. It is about who gets to name the ground and who gets driven off it. What the player characters carry out of Cimarron becomes the first real leverage they hold in New Mexico.

> **For the Game Master.** The spine of this adventure is real history. The plaza, the surveyor, and the Company are invented; the grant, the boundary fraud, the owned court, the railroad, the gold camp, and the ruin are not. [01B-historical-facts-and-authenticity.md](01B-historical-facts-and-authenticity.md) is the treatise that documents exactly where the record ends and the fiction begins, with the surveyor's trade, the plaza's life, the mercury in the creek, the psychology of the operators, and the senses of the grant country in spring `1873`. Read it before you run. Run any scene against it and the ground under the scene will hold.


## Historical Frame

This chapter uses the real pressure of the `1870s` Maxwell Land Grant fight without pretending to reenact one single trial in documentary detail.

In `1841` the Mexican government granted the Beaubien-Miranda tract to Charles Beaubien and Guadalupe Miranda. Lucien B. Maxwell, Beaubien's son-in-law, built a private empire on it and ran cattle, sheep, and freighting out of Cimarron. In `1870` Maxwell sold the grant to an English-Dutch syndicate. The **Maxwell Land Grant and Railway Company** was formed, backed by eastern and European money, and it set about asserting title to the whole of the grant at once.

The Company's eastern boundary was the trouble. The original Mexican grant had fixed lines. The Company's surveyors ran new lines and the new lines ran farther. Homesteads, plazas, and mineral land that had never been inside the grant were drawn inside it. Families who had farmed the Sangre de Cristo foothills since the `1830s` were sued as trespassers on their own places. Gold diggings at Baldy Mountain that had fed the boom camp of Elizabethtown since `1866` were claimed for the Company and a royalty laid on every pan. Quiet-title suits were filed in a territorial court whose judges were patronage men and whose bar was the Santa Fe Ring.

> Historical note: Thomas B. Catron, real Attorney General and later U.S. Senator from New Mexico, and his partner Stephen B. Elkins, built careers on the acquisition and litigation of Spanish and Mexican land grants. The Ring's hand is visible behind nearly every grant suit of the decade. The Company in this chapter is fiction, the men are fiction, the suit is fiction, but the shape of it is the real one.

The **Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railroad**, chartered in `1859`, was building west toward the Sangre de Cristos in the early `1870s`. A clean right of way across the grant was worth more than cattle or sheep or even gold, and the men who held the grant's paper knew it. Survey fraud, mineral claims, railroad right of way, and the dispossession of settled communities were not four stories. They were one story, told in four kinds of ink.

> Historical note: The Jicarilla Apache were being pressed onto inadequate reservation land through these same years and the Ute held the high parks of the Sangre de Cristos. Neither people accepted that a line drawn on paper in Santa Fe had moved the country out from under them.

## The Shape of It

The line of events is plain enough. The player characters arrive at San Esteban as the spring court term opens and the first eviction notices go up. A dead surveyor and a frightened guide show that the trouble began before the notices were ever nailed to the doors. In the field the quiet-title suit turns into a fight over a field book that proves the Company's eastern line was run twelve miles east of where it should have been. A Jicarilla warning offers one path through the high country while private greed and Company riders push toward another. By the time the book splits and men begin shooting one another in the Cimarron courthouse, the campaign is no longer about one surveyor. It is about who owns the country and who owns the lie. What survives the ride down to Cimarron becomes the first real leverage the player characters hold in New Mexico.

The shape is firm, but it should never feel locked. Parties will do what quiet-title suits never pretend they will not. They will read the survey before they are told to, ride after the wrong man in the dark, pocket a page that was meant to change hands later, threaten the judge, strike a private bargain with the Company's own surveyor, or decide the whole paper edifice can go to hell after the first child turns up burned out of a house. Let them. The strength of this chapter is not that the players obey the suit. The strength of it is that every attempt to seize control of the book changes who still trusts them, who fears them, and who thinks they can be used.

## Why a Character Comes to the Plaza

No decent person has to love either side of this suit. The best motives are mixed. A Hispano vaquero may ride for the Montoya outfit because the wages are honest and because his grandmother is buried in the cemetery above the plaza. A discharged trooper out of Fort Union may come because the Company's superintendent once bought him a drink in Las Vegas and mentioned paid work for a steady hand. A mine mucker out of Elizabethtown may be down on his luck and short of a stake and willing to ride shotgun on a wool shipment for cash. A gambler, drifter, or saloon hand may only want a wage and a roof through the spring. That is enough. New Mexico is full of men who took a side by accident and stayed because too much blood, paper, or money had already changed hands.

A better hook is often personal. One character may have kin in the plaza and know that a line drawn on paper in Santa Fe can lift one family and ruin another. Another may have freighted for the Company and know from the look of the wagons that the survey was bent. A courthouse clerk, a draughtsman, a newspaperman, or a lawyer's runner may come because paper is the nearest thing he has to power in a hard country, and because men with soft hands and neat writing are often the first to know where the real money lies. If the group wants stronger ties from the start, give each character one thread into the suit: prior work for the dead surveyor Halladay, a bad debt to a Cimarron merchant, a sister married into the Archuleta family, a night's shelter once taken at the Montoya sheep camp, a long acquaintance with the guide Tomás Luján on the road between settlements. Those ties do more than bring the party together. They make the lies personal.

## The Plaza

The player characters make their base in the plaza of **San Esteban**, a Hispano settlement on the Arroyo de las Vacas at the mouth of a cañón in the eastern Sangre de Cristos, inside the grant line as the Company draws it. The real anchors around it are the boom camp of **Elizabethtown** on Baldy Mountain to the north, the Company town of **Cimarron** and its courthouse to the east, **Fort Union** to the south, and **Santa Fe** beyond, where the Ring sits and the paper is filed.

San Esteban was founded in `1847` by two families fleeing the Taos Rebellion and the chaos after it. Don Teodoro Montoya brought sheep and the Archuletas brought cattle and between them they broke the ground, dug the acequia, raised the church, and buried their dead on the ridge above. By `1873` the plaza holds sixty families and a few hundred souls, Hispano almost to a one, with a small Anglo contingent at the store and the assay office and a handful of Jicarilla who winter in the cañón and trade in the plaza. The families have run stock, sheared wool, cut hay, and panned enough color out of the creek to live on for twenty-six years. None of it has ever been inside any grant, until the Company's new line said it was.

The plaza itself is a square of adobe around a dirt common, the church on the north side, the Montoya and Archuleta houses flanking it, the store and the saloon and the livery across the common, the acequia running down the middle. The cemetery stands on the ridge above, white crosses visible from the doors of the church. The cañón runs west into the mountains, narrow and timbered, the creek falling over ledges, the old trail up to the high parks and the Jicarilla country. East of the plaza the country opens out into the rolling sage and grass of the grant, the road running down to Cimarron and the court.

San Esteban is older than most of the towns of the territory and it shows. The adobe is weathered smooth and the acequia has run clean for a quarter century and the cemetery has more graves than the plaza has living folk. The families know one another's debts and one another's dead. A stranger who comes into the plaza is measured in the first hour by people who have been measuring strangers for a long time. The Company has no office here and never has had. The Law aspect sits at rank one because no sheriff has ever worn a badge in the plaza and the nearest court is two days east in Cimarron. The community's strength is in its Civic rank and in its long memory, and the Company's first move in the suit will be to attack exactly those things.

## What Is Moving Here

On the surface this is a lawsuit over a boundary. Under that surface, four fights are happening at once. The Company wants the eastern line upheld, the gold lands under royalty, the right of way sold clean, and the quiet title signed by a friendly judge. The comunidad wants its commons, its water, its cemetery, and its twenty-six years of possession recognized as the law has always recognized them. The gold camp at Baldy wants the diggings free of the Company and free of the plaza both, and will shoot whichever side tries to take a percentage. The Jicarilla and Ute want the high country left open for the hunt and want the men who came up the cañón with survey chain and sextant to explain themselves or to leave. The player characters begin low enough in all of it to hear truth from every direction. That is the real strength of the opening. Nobody yet thinks they are important enough to lie to carefully.

## The Paper

The central lever of the chapter is a field book.

In the fall of `1872` the General Land Office sent a deputy surveyor named **Phineas Halladay** to run the Maxwell Grant's eastern boundary for the official record. Halladay was a federal man, honest by habit, a Methodist out of Ohio who had run township lines in Kansas and Colorado and never filed a false return in his life. He was six weeks in the field. He kept two books. One was the fair copy he was meant to file with the Land Office in Santa Fe. The other was his private field book, the working pages, the true line as he ran it with chain and compass and the help of a mixed-blood guide named Tomás Luján and a Jicarilla hunter named Nah-leh.

The line Halladay ran put the eastern boundary of the grant where the Mexican grant had always put it. The line the Company wanted ran twelve miles farther east. Twelve miles of plaza, acequia, cemetery, hay meadow, and gold-bearing creek. Halladay refused to file the false fair copy. The Company's own surveyor, a Ring-connected man named **Aldous Pell** (no relation to the railroad's agent Oren Pell, though the Company likes the confusion), filed a false return under Halladay's name. Halladay threatened to go over the Company's head to the Land Office in Washington. In March of `1873` Halladay was found dead in the willows of the Arroyo de las Vacas. The Company's return said he had drowned. The back of his skull said otherwise. His private field book was never found.

Whoever holds the field book holds the line. The book can break the Company's quiet-title suit in Cimarron. It can be sold to the railroad, which wants the boundary settled cleanly one way or the other. It can blackmail the Company for more money than the plaza will see in a generation. It can be burned, and with it the only record of where the true line ever ran. The player characters will spend the chapter chasing it, hiding it, copying it, fighting over it, and learning that the book is heavier than it looks because the dead surveyor's name and a child's inheritance and a widow's dower and a nation's promise are all folded into its damp pages.

## Consequence Ledger

Track the party's standing with four groups: `Comunidad Trust`, `Company Pressure`, `Gold Camp Standing`, and `Mountain Trust`. Start each at `0`. When the party makes a major choice, move one or more tracks by `+1` or `-1`. By the time they ride down to Cimarron for the hearing, the totals should already be shaping the end of the campaign.

`Company Pressure` runs the other way from the rest. A high `Company Pressure` score means the Company has leverage over the party: blackmail, debt, a warrant, a secret the party would rather keep. Low `Company Pressure` means the party's hands are cleaner but their path is harder, because the Company fights clean hands the hardest.

These tracks should not stay abstract. Let them throw visible consequences forward.

- If `Comunidad Trust` rises early, the families open doors, share the acequia labor, hide a witness, and the guide Tomás Luján speaks more plainly.
- If `Comunidad Trust` falls, the plaza closes ranks. Doors shut. The acequia is "accidentally" turned away from party land. The priest becomes politely deaf.
- If `Company Pressure` rises, Superintendent Hadley begins to treat the party as assets he owns: small favors called in, a threat held back, a warrant produced at the worst hour.
- If `Company Pressure` falls, the Company stops negotiating and starts sending riders.
- If `Gold Camp Standing` rises, Strand's miners back the party at Baldy, lend powder and picks, and spread a story in the camp that favors the party.
- If `Gold Camp Standing` falls, the camp treats the party as Company spies and a claim-jumper's bullet comes out of the dark sooner or later.
- If `Mountain Trust` rises, Nah-leh's people send warnings, guide the party through the cañón, and tell the truth about the night Halladay died.
- If `Mountain Trust` falls, the high country goes silent. The passes close. The party rides blind into country that punishes the blind.

## What the Main Factions Want

Use this when players ask what is really going on.

- **Hadley and the Company:** Hadley wants the eastern line upheld, the quiet title signed, the gold under royalty, and the right of way sold to the railroad at full value. He is not stupid and he is not needlessly cruel. He is cold. If the party helps him keep the paper clean, he rewards them with cash, credit, and legal shelter. If they prove the survey was bent, he faces a choice between a fortune and a cell.

- **Don Ignacio and the comunidad:** Don Ignacio wants the plaza's twenty-six years of possession recognized. He does not want war. He has buried two sons and he knows what war costs. He needs the field book because the court will not hear possession without it. He will pay what he can, which is not much, and he will pay in wool, stock, loyalty, and the labor of the plaza before he pays in gold.

- **Strand and the gold camp:** Strand wants the Baldy diggings free of the Company and free of the plaza. He will ally with anyone who breaks the Company's claim and he will turn on anyone who tries to lay a royalty on a pan. He has more guns than scruple and more courage than sense.

- **Nah-leh and the mountain people:** Nah-leh wants the cañón road left open for the hunt and the survey chain out of it. He can deal. He does not beg. He knows where the surveyor died because his cousin was on the ridge that night watching the willows, and the truth of the death is the coin he will spend last.

- **The railroad (Oren Pell):** Pell wants a clean right of way. He does not care whose name is on the deed so long as the deed will hold in court and in Washington. He is the safest buyer for any paper the party holds, and the most dangerous, because the railroad collects debts across continents.

- **Judge Vail and the Ring:** Vail wants the suit settled without a scandal that reaches Santa Fe or Washington. He is owned, but he is not stupid, and a field book in open court is a scandal he cannot survive by ruling against it. He will bend where the weight of the paper bends him.

These pressures should answer later choices, not merely decorate them. If the party helps Hadley keep the false survey intact, the Company grows stronger in the last act but the party may arrive at Cimarron under Company control. If they help the comunidad prove the fraud, more of the ending belongs to the families and less to clean cash. If they answer Nah-leh honestly, the high country opens. If they answer him badly, the chapter remembers it in the passes themselves.

## Core Cast

These are the principal named figures of the chapter. What follows is the first impression each one gives — the paragraph a player reads across a crowded room or the GM reads the moment an NPC opens a door. The full profiles (backstory, want, need, fear, how to play them) and the complete statblocks for every named figure and every generic profile in the chapter live in [01A-dramatis-personae-and-statblocks.md](01A-dramatis-personae-and-statblocks.md). Run the social scenes below with both files open.

`Cornelius V. Hadley` is a white man in his middle forties, Yale-educated, tall, narrow through the face, and dressed too well for the country he works in. The first impression he gives is of patience used as a weapon. He never raises his voice. He folds his hands when he listens. He writes down names in a small leather book without seeming to and he has the habit of answering a hard question with a softer question of his own that turns the matter back on the asker. He speaks in measured educated sentences and wastes no western color, which itself puts frontier men off their stride. In his general view, the player characters are labor and risk to be priced; if they prove themselves above their price he promotes them, and if they prove themselves below it he has them removed with paperwork rather than lead. He is the Company.

`Don Ignacio Montoya` is a Hispano man of sixty-four, sun-cut, slow in the saddle, his hands gone permanent around the shape of a rope. The first impression he gives is weight. He stands like a man who has carried his own water and buried his own dead and intends to go on doing both. He speaks Spanish at home and slow careful English to Anglos, and he does not raise his voice either, because in the plaza his voice does not need raising. He carries a small notebook of the acequia turns, the brands, the debts, and the graves, because the law of the plaza is memory and memory must be written down or the courts will say it never happened. In his general view, the player characters are strangers to be measured; if they prove they know the difference between a man's land and a man's paper he feeds them at his table, and if they do not he is still polite and the door is still shut.

`Eufemia Archuleta` is a Hispano woman of fifty-eight, widow of the founder's son, who runs the Archuleta trade in wool, hides, and dry goods out of the long adobe across the common from the Montoya house. The first impression she gives is command. She stands behind the counter with a pencil behind her ear and she counts faster than the men who buy from her. She speaks Spanish, English, and enough Ute to trade, and she switches between them without warning to see who is listening. She lost her husband to a Comanche raid in `1861` and two of her three sons to fever and to a mine collapse at Baldy, and the third son, **Rafael**, rides drag for the outfit and drinks. In her general view, the player characters are either customers or trouble, and she prices both the same way.

`"Col." Ephraim Strand` is a white man of fifty, heavy in the chest and the gut, loud, and never actually a colonel of anything. The first impression he gives is force and the second is fraud, and most men stop at the first. He bought into the Baldy diggings in `1868` with money of unclear origin and he has run the camp's rougher end ever since. He speaks in the idiom of the mining camp and the saloon and he numbers everything in ounces and dollars. He carries a loaded Colt 1860 Army in a half-flap holster, the old cap-and-ball service piece he bought surplus in Denver, and he has used it. In his general view, the player characters are either allies against the Company or claim jumpers in service to it, and he decides which by how they treat the first pan he offers them.

`Nah-leh` ("He Who Counts Coup") is a Jicarilla Apache man in mature adulthood, lean, quiet-faced, and harder to read than Hadley. The first impression he gives is stillness. He sits a horse as if horse and rider had settled the matter years ago. He does not fidget. He does not explain himself. He watches the men who watch him and he lets the watching do the work. He speaks Apache, Spanish, and accented English, and he uses the English last and the least. In his general view, the player characters are not yet important, but unlike the Company men they may still be worth the trouble of measuring correctly.

`Judge Mortimer Vail` is a white man in his late fifties, appointed from Ohio, owlish behind spectacles, soft-handed, and owned. The first impression he gives is of competence in service of a verdict already arrived at. He hears a case the way a clerk files a letter: with attention to form and no attention at all to the body on the floor. He speaks in the measured sentences of the bench and he has the habit of cleaning his spectacles when he does not want to be read. In his general view, the player characters are either useful witnesses to be shaped or dangerous ones to be discredited, and he will know which by the second day of the hearing.

`Tomás Luján` is a mixed-blood man of thirty-two, Hispano and Jicarilla, born in the cañón above the plaza. The first impression he gives is of a man trying to disappear into his own quiet. He is lean, dark, watchful, and he carries the surveyor's old haversack as if it were sewn to him. He guided Halladay for six weeks and he was in the willows the night Halladay died, and what he saw has made him afraid of the dark and afraid of his own shadow and afraid, above all, of speaking. He speaks English with Halladay's Ohio flatness laid over his Spanish and his Apache. In his general view, the player characters are possible rescuers, possible buyers, and possible executioners, and he will try to guess which from one breath to the next.

`Father Sabino Vigil` is a Hispano priest of forty-five, the plaza's pastor for nine years, a Penitente-country man with a hard theology and a soft heart he keeps hidden behind the office. The first impression he gives is of a man who has heard everything and forgiven none of it and buried most of it. He is the one who keeps the cemetery register, which is also a register of possession, because a grave is the oldest title there is. He speaks Spanish in the pulpit and English to Anglos and he drinks more than his bishop would like. In his general view, the player characters are souls to be measured by what they do for the widows and the orphans of the plaza, and he has a long memory for the measuring.

`Sheriff Bud Tatum` is a white man of thirty-eight, the Company's sheriff, seated in Cimarron, who rides up to the plaza when the Company sends him and not before. The first impression he gives is of a large man who would rather not run. He is not cruel and he is not brave and he does what Hadley tells him because Hadley signs the paper that pays him. He speaks little. He carries a Winchester in the scabbard and a star on his vest and he is honestly not certain which one is heavier.

`Aldous Pell` (the surveyor, not the agent) is a white man of forty, Ring-connected, precise, and cold in a way that passes for competence. He ran the false return under Halladay's name and he has been drinking since. He is the one man in the chapter who knows exactly how the line was bent and exactly how much it cost, and the cost is eating him. He speaks in the technical jargon of the chain and the transit and he wants, more than anything, to be told he had no choice. He did.

`Oren Pell` (the railroad agent, no relation) is a white man of thirty-five, quiet, well-tailored, perpetually taking notes in a shorthand nobody can read. He is in Cimarron and Elizabethtown and the plaza by turns, never quite resident anywhere, buying nothing and selling nothing in the open. He wants the right of way. He will buy any clean paper that settles the boundary. He is the safest and the most dangerous man the party will meet, because the railroad forgets nothing and forgives less.

`Ouida Beaubien` is a mixed-race woman of thirty, the granddaughter of the grant's original Mexican grantee, who keeps a room above the store in Elizabethtown and a claim to a stretch of the grant that no court has ever heard. She is educated, angry, and exact. She holds a copy of the original `1841` grant language and she reads it aloud in Spanish to anyone who will listen, which is almost no one. She is the chapter's truest historian and its loneliest figure, and if the party treats her as a witness rather than a curiosity she can crack the Company's case in three sentences.

| 9 | Nah-leh's cousin was on the ridge the night the surveyor died. Nah-leh has not said what his cousin saw. He is holding it the way a man holds his last cartridge. |
| 10 | Father Vigil's cemetery register is also a property register. A grave is the oldest title there is. If the Company burns the plaza's paper, the graves will still be on the ridge. |

## How This Sandbox Runs

The arcs below are scenery, not track. They exist so the game master always has a ready scene for any direction the party turns. They are not the order in which the tale must unfold. The party can ride for Baldy before it ever walks the plaza, ride into Cimarron on day one to read the suit, climb the cañón in the first week, or sit in the saloon and run a long con on Hadley's riders without ever drawing iron. None of those is wrong. The job here is not to move the party through a sequence. The job is to make the country and its factions push back, no matter where the party goes and no matter how cleverly it tries to cut the knot.

Three rules hold the sandbox up.

First, the field book is the loudest lever, not the only one. A party that refuses the book, burns it, or never finds it still has four other ways to break the Company's case (the grant language, the affidavit, the cemetery register, the bought witness) and four other ways to win without breaking the case at all (buy the plaza's debt, sell the right of way, start a range war, or simply outlast the Company's London patience). A party that grabs the book in week one and rides for Santa Fe should find that Santa Fe has its own ideas about whose paper wins. Do not collapse the table because the party skipped a step. Step sideways to a different lever and let the country answer.

Second, the factions have their own clocks and they keep them whether the party watches or not. Hadley does not wait for the party to find the book. Strand does not wait for the party to broker a deal. Don Ignacio does not wait for the party to defend the plaza. The world advances on its own schedule and the party rides inside it. The next section gives the clocks.

Third, give the party the consequences of its own competence. The players who shortcut a plot are usually the players most engaged with it. Reward the shortcut by making it mean something — it skips a fight and starts a different one, it wins a scene and loses a friend, it breaks the Company's line and brings the railroad's full attention down on the plaza. Never punish ingenuity with a reset. Punish it, if at all, with the next true problem the ingenuity created.

## Faction Clocks

Time moves. Track these clocks across the weeks of the spring court term. When a clock fills, the event happens whether the party is present, absent, or the cause of it. Read the clocks out loud at the end of each session if your table likes pressure. Let the party hear the Company's patience running down.

**The Company's patience (12 weeks to the hearing).** Each week Hadley tightens a screw: a survey crew re-marks a mile of the eastern line; an eviction notice goes up; the freight road toll doubles; a plaza hand is found dead in a ditch and ruled drowned; a Company lawyer rides down from Santa Fe. By the hearing the plaza should be squeezed to the edge of ruin. The party can slow this clock by making Hadley's life expensive (a burned survey wagon, a public affidavit, a dead crew) or speed it by provoking him.

**Strand's ambition (gold-camp escalation).** Strand wants the diggings free and he is willing to shoot for them. Each week he gathers another two or three hard men to the Old Abe. After three weeks he has enough to raid a Company assay wagon. After five he has enough to make a move on the survey crew itself. If the party does not engage the gold camp, the gold camp engages the Company on its own, and the party arrives at the hearing to find the creek already running with blood that is not theirs.

**The comunidad's endurance (the flour clock).** Eufemia's ledger is a clock. Each week the flour price holds at six cents, another family falls behind on the store account. After four weeks a family is evicted by debt rather than by lawsuit — quieter, cheaper, and harder to fight in court. After six the first families begin to talk about leaving. After eight the plaza's population starts to thin. The party can break this clock by opening a second freight road, by running the wool to Taos (Story 4), by buying the debt, or by making the Company's freight too expensive to run.

**Nah-leh's patience (the mountain clock).** Nah-leh watches the survey chain come up the cañón. Each week the chain moves, his patience drops. After four weeks of survey incursion, a Jicarilla party shoots a Company chainman on the line. After six, they burn a transit. After eight, if the party has not made themselves useful or honest to the mountain people, Nah-leh stops warning anyone about anything and the high country closes. A party that earns `Mountain Trust` slows this clock. A party that earns the Company's pay on the survey crew speeds it.

**Tomás Luján's nerve (the witness clock).** Tomás wants to speak and he wants to live and the two wants are tearing him in half. Each week he stays silent, his nerve erodes. After three weeks he tries to run for Taos. After five, if he has not been protected or befriended, the Company catches him running and he is found in another shallow creek. A party that gives him a reason to trust, a way out, or a guard slows this clock. A party that frightens him speeds it to the breaking point.

**The railroad's patience (the right-of-way clock).** Oren Pell's patience is the longest and the coldest. It does not move week to week. It moves when the party hands him paper, when the hearing's outcome becomes clear, or when Hadley fails for too long. When it moves, it moves all at once. The railroad buys the Company's debt, or the Company's silence, or the Company itself. A party that sells Pell a clean copy of the field book sets this clock to its end and gets paid. A party that ignores Pell finds, at the hearing, that Pell has found another way and the party is no longer necessary.

When two clocks collide, let them. Strand's raid on the assay wagon and the Company's survey crew may arrive in the same week, and the party has to choose which fire to fight. Tomás running for Taos and a Company eviction happening on the same morning is a real session. The clocks are the engine of the sandbox. Wind them.

## Multiple Ways In, Multiple Ways Through

There is more than one way to win the chapter, and most of them are not "find the book and take it to court." Lay these out for yourself even if you never lay them out for the players. The party will find its own road through the menu; the menu exists so you always have somewhere for that road to go.

### Goals the party may set for itself

The party does not have to share a goal. Different characters may want different things, and the tension between those wants is the campaign.

- **Win the suit for the comunidad.** Get the eastern line thrown back to its true position and the plaza's title confirmed.
- **Free the diggings.** Break the Company's mineral claim so Strand's camp, and any prospector, can pan without royalty.
- **Get paid.** Sell whatever paper the party can lay hands on to the highest bidder, walk away rich, and let the country sort itself.
- **Hold the high country.** Keep the survey chain and the railroad out of the cañón and the Jicarilla hunting parks.
- **Survive Hadley.** The party backed the wrong horse early and now just wants to get out of the grant alive and unbought.
- **Settle an old score.** A character's hook runs through Strand, through Hadley, through the dead surveyor, through a grave on the ridge. The suit is scenery; the score is the game.
- **Build something.** The party wants a stake — land, a saloon, a freight line, a mine — and the chapter is the capital they mean to earn to buy it.

### Levers, not one lever

The field book is the chapter's loudest lever. It is not the only one. Each lever below can break the Company's case, win the suit, or change the ending on its own. A clever party will stack two or three; a brutal party will use one and ride the consequences. Keep all of them live in your head so that when the party ignores the book, you are not stuck.

- **The field book.** Halladay's true line, witnessed, in his hand. Strongest in court, most dangerous to carry.
- **Pell's affidavit.** The false surveyor's sworn confession to filing the crooked return. Almost as strong as the book, weaker if coerced, gone if Pell runs.
- **The grant language.** The original `1841` Spanish, which reserved the mineral to the sovereign. Ouida Beaubien holds the only copy. Breaks the gold claim without touching the boundary.
- **The cemetery register.** Father Vigil's record of the plaza's dead back to `1847`. The oldest title there is. Slow in court, unanswerable in the plaza, useless for the gold.
- **The bought witness.** Tomás Luján, alive and willing to swear what he saw in the willows in March. Powerful if the court will hear an Indian-Mexican mixed-blood, which Vail's court may not.
- **The Company's own mail.** Intercept one of Hadley's letters to his Santa Fe lawyer naming the line a fiction, and the Company hangs itself on its own postage.
- **The railroad's money.** Oren Pell will pay for any paper that settles the boundary cleanly. Selling to the railroad wins without a court at all — and binds the party to a debt across a continent.
- **The plaza's debt.** Buy what the plaza owes Eufemia and the Cimarron store and the plaza owes the party. Title by debt is slow, ugly, and very period.
- **A range war.** Make the grant too expensive and too bloody for London to keep funding. The Company's directors do not care about one dead surveyor; they care about the quarterly return. Bleed the return and the Company folds the grant.

A party that refuses to touch the book can still win through any of the last seven. A party that grabs the book in the first session can still lose to a Company that buys the judge, buys Pell's retraction, or simply kills the witnesses before the hearing. No single lever is the key. The levers are a tool kit.

## Where the Party Can Go First

The party can open play in any of five places and find a real first session there. Each is a door, not a station on a track. Give the party the door it walks through and let the other four wait, advancing on their clocks, until the party turns toward them.

**The plaza.** Hire on with the comunidad or with the Company's local crew. Walk into the supper, the willows killing, the night raid. The densest first session, the most NPCs up front, the slowest burn. Good for a table that wants to know everyone before the shooting.

**The gold camp at Baldy.** Open at the Old Abe with Strand buying drinks and sizing up strangers. The party hears the suit as saloon talk, meets Strand first, and rides down to the plaza as his people rather than as neutral hands. Faster, louder, and the party enters the plaza already aligned, which the plaza notices and remembers.

**The Cimarron court.** Open on the first day of the spring term, with the party in the gallery watching Filomeno Baca outgunned by the Ring's lawyers. The party meets the suit as paper, sees the judge yawn, and understands immediately that the courtroom is where the chapter ends, not where it begins. Good for a table that wants to know the stakes cold before it picks a side.

**The cañón and the high country.** Open on the trail above the plaza with the party guiding, hunting, or running from something. The party meets Nah-leh before it meets Hadley, meets the line on the ground before it meets the line in the suit, and may find the cairn and the book before it knows what either is. Dangerous and disorienting and very good for a table that trusts the country to teach the plot.

**The road between.** Open on the freight road, with the party riding shotgun on a wool shipment, a Company freight wagon, or a stage. The party meets the chapter in motion, gets robbed, gets lost, finds a body, or fetches up at whichever end of the road the trouble sends it. The classic western cold open. Good for a table that wants to discover the country before it discovers the plot.

## When the Party Shortcuts the Plot

This section replaces the old "if the players break the plan" patch list. In a sandbox there is no plan to break. There are only the next true problems the party's cleverness creates. Below are the common shortcuts and the honest answers to each. The answers are not punishments. They are the country answering back.

### The party ignores the book entirely

Let it. The book stays in the cairn, or Hadley's men find it in week eight and the eastern line is confirmed on paper forever, or Nah-leh quietly takes it down the mountain to the Indian agent at Cimarron Creek and it becomes a federal problem the Company did not see coming. Meanwhile the party wins through the grant language, the affidavit, the bought debt, the range war, or the railroad's money. The country does not require the party to care about the book. The book requires someone to care about it, and if the party will not, the NPC who does becomes the next problem.

### The party finds the book in week one

Good. Now the book is a problem, not a quest. Everyone wants it and the party cannot file it, sell it, copy it, or even read it near a window without changing who trusts them. Hadley offers money, then threats, then riders. Pell wants it destroyed to save his own skin. Oren Pell wants a copy. Strand wants it used and then burned. Don Ignacio wants it in court. Nah-leh wants the offensive pages burned. The party that finds the book fast spends the rest of the chapter defending, copying, hiding, and bargaining over a thing every faction will kill for. The shortcut does not end the chapter. It loads the chapter into the book and lets the party carry the weight.

### The party rides straight for Santa Fe with the paper

Santa Fe is not a vending machine that accepts paper and dispenses justice. The Ring owns the territorial courts, the territorial press, and the territorial governor's ear. A party that rides to Santa Fe finds that the Attorney General's office is staffed by men who eat with Catron, the newspaper editor owes the Ring his press, and the governor's aide is a Ring man named (in this fiction) Williamson. The party can still win in Santa Fe, but it wins by finding the one faction in the capital that wants to embarrass the Company — a rival in the Ring, a federal land-office man with a grudge, a newspaper with nothing to lose — and that faction has its own price. Santa Fe is a harder road than Cimarron, not an easier one.

### The party kills Hadley

Hadley is a man; the Company is a machine. Killing Hadley in week two means the Company wires London and Santa Fe and a new superintendent rides up the Cimarron road by week six, leaner and colder and forewarned. It also means the quiet-title suit becomes a murder investigation that Tatum cannot bury, the plaza comes under federal attention it did not want, and the party is now the most wanted men in Colfax County. Hadley's death does not break the Company. It breaks the party's cover. Play the consequences straight and let the party ride them.

### The party kills Strand, or Pell, or Tomás, or Vail

Every named death moves the chapter, not ends it. Strand dead means the gold camp fragments into three smaller factions that the party now has to deal with separately. Pell dead means the affidavit is gone but his private notes survive in his room above the Old Abe, and his letters to Hadley survive in Hadley's safe, and the party can find either. Tomás dead means the willows truth moves to Celso's dying breath, to Halladay's cached letters, or to Nah-leh's memory. Vail dead means the spring term collapses, the suit is postponed to the fall, and the Company's lawyers file for a change of venue to Santa Fe, where the Ring's grip is tighter. No death is a dead end. Each is a door to the next true problem.

### The party tries to buy the plaza's debt and win by ledger

Eufemia will help with the arithmetic and she will charge for the help. The Cimarron store's books are the Company's, and the Company will not sell the debt to strangers without a premium and a reason. The party that wants to win by debt needs cash (a lot of it), needs Eufemia's contacts in Taos, and needs to survive the weeks it takes to move the paper. Meanwhile the flour clock runs and the Company's patience clock runs and a party that wins by ledger can still arrive at a hearing where the Company has burned the plaza's flour stores out of spite. Title by debt is real and period and it is not a shortcut past the chapter. It is the chapter, told in ink.

### The party burns the field book

Then the eastern line stays where the false survey put it, on paper, unless the party can prove the fraud another way. The grant language still breaks the gold claim. The cemetery register still proves possession. The affidavit, if Pell signed one before the burning, still names the line a fiction. A party that burns the book to deny it to everyone has chosen to win without it. Honor that choice by making the other levers harder, not impossible. The burned book is gone. The truth of the line is still in the ground, in the blazes on the ponderosa, in Nah-leh's memory, and in the plaza's grandfathers under the ridge.

### The party splits

A sandbox party will divide. Two ride for Baldy, two ride for Cimarron, one stays in the plaza. Run it. Cut between the groups the way a western cuts between riders on different trails. Let each group's choices land on the other group's table: the Baldy pair's deal with Strand reaches the plaza as a rumor that turns Eufemia cold; the Cimarron pair's questions in the courthouse reach Hadley as a report that sends riders up the road. A split party is harder to run and it is the shape the campaign will actually take. Keep the clocks running for both halves. Bring them back together at the hearing, if they all live.

### The party sits still

A party that holes up in the saloon and waits for the plot to come to it has chosen to let the clocks run. They will run. By week three Hadley's crew re-marks the line past the cemetery. By week four Strand raids an assay wagon and the road becomes unsafe. By week five a family is evicted by debt and the plaza asks, coldly, whose side the strangers are on. By week six Tomás runs for Taos and is caught or killed. A passive party does not freeze the chapter. It lets the chapter happen to the party, and a chapter that happens to a passive party is usually a chapter the party loses. That is a fair outcome. Some westerns end with the strangers riding out as the country closes behind the people they would not help.

### The party tries to unite the factions against the Company

The hardest and the most rewarding play. The comunidad, the gold camp, the mountain people, and the railroad do not want the same thing even when they all want the Company gone. The party that tries to build a coalition has to square the plaza's claim to the creek against Strand's claim to the creek against Nah-leh's claim to the cañón against Pell's need for a clean deed. The coalition is winnable — it is one of the strongest endings — but it is won by the party doing the hard diplomatic work of making four incompatible wants stand together for one season. A party that pulls it off has earned an ending nobody scripted. A party that half-pulls it off has a coalition that collapses at the hearing into three factions shooting at each other and at the Company. Both are good sessions.

### The party refuses to pick a side and plays all of them

The double- and triple-game. It works until it does not. Each faction is watching the party, and the party that sells the book to Pell, the affidavit to Don Ignacio, and a copy to Strand is one loose word from all three knowing. Play the factions as smart. Hadley has riders who follow strangers. Strand has miners who drink in the same saloons as the Company men. Nah-leh has eyes on every ridge. A triple-dealing party should get rich and then get caught, and the catching should be the chapter's climax. The most dangerous men in a sandbox are the ones no one faction trusts at the end. Let the party become those men, if it wants, and let the ending fit.

### The party does something nobody planned for

Say yes. Find the true consequence. The party dynamites the Cimarron freight road and the Company cannot move wool or flour for a month. The party kidnaps Judge Vail's wife to force a ruling. The party sets the Old Abe on fire to draw Strand's men off the line. The party prints the field book in the _Cimarron News_ and dares the Company to sue for libel. Each of these is a real western and each has a real answer. The dynamited road means federal troops out of Fort Union. The kidnapped wife means the army and a noose. The burned saloon means the gold camp at the party's throat. The printed book means the Company's stock price in London and a writ for the editor's arrest. The country answers every move. Trust the country to answer.

## Arc I: The Plaza and the Dead Surveyor

This opening arc should take time. Do not rush the player characters into the field. They need to hear the different claims men and women make for the suit before they see how little any of those claims hold under the ridge. It is the densest of the five entry points and the one the rest of this chapter assumes by default. If the party came in by a different door, fold the scenes below into whatever road the party actually walked.

## San Esteban
### 1. Arrival and hire

Read or paraphrase:

> Spring sits thin over the Sangre de Cristos. The snow is still in the high timber and the creek runs cold and full off the melt. The road up from Cimarron is mud for the first mile and dust for the rest, and the plaza appears by degrees out of the sage: first the white crosses on the ridge, then the church tower, then the long adobe wall of the Montoya place, then the common and the acequia running down the middle of it.
>
> Sixty families live here. The adobe is weathered the color of old bone. The doors are pine. The horses stand at the hitch rail in front of the store and they are all of them thin from the winter. A bell rings once in the church tower and stops.

Bring the party in by hire or by errand. Be plain about money. The comunidad pays in cash what it can, which is not much, and the rest in wool, stock, and the labor of the plaza. The Company pays better and pays in gold, but the Company pays for a verdict. A character hired by the comunidad comes to guard the spring sheep camp against Jicarilla raiding, real or staged. A character hired by the Company comes to ride shotgun on a survey crew that means to re-mark the eastern line before the court term. Both hire on at the store, where Eufemia Archuleta takes the names in her book and pencils the wage beside them. The wage is a dollar a day and found, paid at the end of the season or on the ride out, whichever comes last.

> Historical note: A vaquero or ranch hand in `1873` New Mexico drew between twenty and forty dollars a month. A dollar a day and found was honest wages for honest work and it was the wage that left a man no room to refuse the extra dollar for the work that was not honest.

Give the players time to walk the plaza and learn who belongs to which world. The comunidad wears the sun and the slow patience of people who have been here long enough to have grandfathers in the cemetery. The Company has no office here, but two of Hadley's riders drink at the saloon across the common and they wear their Colts low and their Company pay on their faces. The Church and the cemetery are the plaza's first answer to any paper. The acequia is its second. If the party looks closely, reward it. `INSIGHT` reads the strain between Eufemia behind the counter and the two Company riders who buy their whiskey and do not pay. `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` shows the horses at the hitch rail are all thin and all shod in the plaza's own fashion, which means no remounts to be had for cash. `BOOKLEARNIN'` notices that Eufemia keeps the store's ledger in Spanish and the eviction notices the Company nailed to the doorpost are in English, and that nobody in the plaza can read the English ones. `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` lets a player start choosing what kind of face he means to show these people.

The plaza itself should do some of the work. It smells of woodsmoke, wet wool, and the ditch. The acequia runs clear and cold down the middle of the common and the children play at it and the women wash at it and the horses drink from it. The Company's two notices are nailed to the store's doorpost. One is the quiet-title suit, _Maxwell Land Grant and Railway Company v. the Inhabitants of the Tract_, returnable to the Cimarron court the first Monday of June. The other is a notice of eviction. Eufemia has let them stay up because pulling them down is what the Company expects and what the Company expects is what the Company prepares for. A hand-lettered sheet below them, in Spanish, is Don Ignacio's reply: _Esta tierra no se vende._ This land is not for sale.

Do not assume the party takes employment meekly. Some groups will haggle for more pay, insist on knowing who holds legal authority over the plaza, or demand to read the suit before they ride anywhere. Good. Let Don Ignacio answer slowly in English if he is pressed in public and more fully in Spanish if he is pressed in private. Let Hadley's riders notice who wants terms instead of wages. Let Eufemia mark the practical ones and the curious ones. If the party tries to pull the Company's notices off the doorpost on the first day, the two riders in the saloon will come across the common and the scene will tighten, and the tightening is useful later. Early anger is the chapter's first currency.

Let the common itself work on the party before the chapter hurries into the willows. Father Vigil is at the church door, sweeping. If a player goes to him there he speaks more plainly than the suit allows. The line is bent, he says. The surveyor knew it and the surveyor died for it. The guide knows it and the guide is dying slower. The cemetery register is open to any man who can read a date. The dates do not lie and the dates go back to `1847`. A player who helps him with the sweeping should earn more than a nod. Later trust ought to remember it.

Eufemia's side of the counter gives a different lesson. The store sells flour at six cents a pound when the Cimarron price is three, and there is no second seller. The Company controls the freight road and the Company's store in Cimarron undercuts the plaza's wool at the buyer and overcharges the plaza's flour at the wagon. The arithmetic is the chapter in miniature. Hadley cares what the court will say. Don Ignacio cares what the acequia will carry. Strand cares what a pan will yield. Eufemia cares what the ledger will balance. Nah-leh cares what the high country will hold. The player characters walk into the middle of all four accounts on the first afternoon and they do not yet know it.

### 2. Supper at the Montoya house

That evening, Don Ignacio has the party to his table if they have come by the comunidad's hire, and Eufemia has them to hers if they have come to trade, and the priest has them to the rectory if they have come to pray. Put the party in one room and let the other two find their way to it.

Read or paraphrase:

> The Montoya kitchen is long and low and warm with the cookfire and the lamp. Tallow and chili and the wet-wool smell of the roof. Don Ignacio sits at the head and his daughter Pilar serves and the men eat with tortillas and their hands. Across the table Eufemia sits with her pencil behind her ear though she is not at the store. Father Vigil sits at the far end and does not eat much and drinks more than he should.

Let the important voices share the room and let the party move between them. Don Ignacio talks possession, acequia, graves, and the long memory of the plaza in the slow English he saves for Anglos. Eufemia talks freight, flour, wool, and the arithmetic of being undercut at both ends of the road. Father Vigil talks the cemetery register, the dates, and the dead. If you want a sharper local edge, have Tomás Luján come in late and sit by the door and not eat at all. By the end of the meal the players should know that Don Ignacio thinks the suit is lost before it is tried unless the field book surfaces, that Eufemia thinks the plaza is being starved out before it is sued out, that the priest thinks the dead are the only title the court cannot burn, and that Tomás Luján knows something he would rather die than tell and is half of the way to dying of it already. They should also know how these people carry themselves. Don Ignacio listens with his hands folded on the table. Eufemia talks with the pencil. The priest watches the door. Tomás watches the window.

Good table lines here should be easy to hear and easy to remember:

> _Twenty-six years we have planted here,_ Don Ignacio says. _My father is on the ridge. My sons are on the ridge. The Company's paper says we squat. A man does not squat on his father's grave._

> _The flour is six cents,_ Eufemia says, _because the road is the Company's road and the wagon is the Company's wagon and the store in Cimarron is the Company's store. They do not need to win the suit. They only need to make the flour six cents until we are gone._

> _The book is in the ground with him,_ the priest says, _or it is in the cañón, or it is in Hadley's safe. God knows. The court will know what God knows if God's people find it and carry it down the mountain._

Some parties will try to break the room instead of reading it. They may accuse Hadley's riders openly, force Tomás to speak before he is ready, demand the cemetery register on the table, or slip out after the meal to follow the priest or Tomás. Support all of that. A public accusation should not solve anything early, but it should sort the room fast. Don Ignacio hates disorder more than suspicion. Eufemia dislikes scandal unless it protects the ledger. Tomás is strongest when silent and weakest when pressed to answer one clean question in front of witnesses. A shadowing player can catch Tomás leaving by the back door, the priest locking the cemetery register in the vestry, or one of Hadley's riders counting the horses at the Montoya corral before the night raid. Those discoveries should come with position, not certainty.

Give the supper some heat before it breaks. Father Vigil is just angry enough to say too much if the wine is poured. Eufemia is just tired enough to forget politeness when the freight arithmetic is questioned. Don Ignacio keeps sounding like the patient grandfather at the head of the table, which is precisely how he enters a stranger's trust. If the party leaves the light and follows Tomás, the priest, or one of the riders, reward them with movement instead of revelation. Tomás walks to the edge of the common and stands looking at the cañón mouth and does not move for ten minutes. The priest kneels in the vestry over the register and turns the pages to the year `1847` and stops. A Hadley rider circles the Montoya corral counting horses more carefully than a drink requires. None of it proves enough. That is why it has value.

#### How the supper bends to the party

The supper is the chapter's first real social test, and the four principals read the party across the table as carefully as the party reads them. Run each principal as a separate weather system the party can please, intuit, or offend. The tracks they shift here move the consequence ledger before a shot is fired.

**Don Ignacio** is won by patience and by the small show of knowing the country. A player who sits through one of his silences without filling it, who eats with tortillas and the right hand, or who asks after the acequia rather than after the suit, earns his early trust. A player who speaks Spanish to him, even badly, earns more.

> _You eat like a man who has eaten tortillas before,_ Don Ignacio says, watching the hands. _Where did you learn it._

A player who intuited him — who asks about the graves on the ridge rather than the lawsuit in Cimarron — gets the line that buys the most:

> _The graves are older than the grant,_ he says. _A man who asks after the graves understands what is being asked of him. A man who asks after the suit asks only after the money._

Offend him by pulling the Company's notices off the doorpost before the meal, by raising your voice in his kitchen, or by treating Pilar as a servant. He will not say a word. The door will simply be shut the next time the party knocks, and `Comunidad Trust` drops a point. The plaza notices when Don Ignacio's door is shut.

**Eufemia** is won by competence with numbers and by an honest acknowledgment of the flour arithmetic. A player who reads the eviction notices aloud to her, in English, so the families can be told what they say, earns her instant regard — she has been waiting three weeks for someone literate and kind enough to do it.

> _You read the English,_ she says, pencil behind the ear. _Good. Read it to them in Spanish tonight. They have a right to know what is nailing them to the doorpost._

Intuit her by offering to carry the wool to Taos (Story 4) before she asks, or by asking to see the ledger rather than the suit. She will show the ledger. The ledger is the harder document and the truer one. `Comunidad Trust` rises and a useful ally is made.

Offend her by haggling down her wage offer, by flirting, or by suggesting the plaza sell out and move. She will mark the name in her book — the same book that tracks the flour debts — and the party will find the plaza's credit closed to them inside a day.

**Father Vigil** is won by respect for the dead and by sitting with him after the meal over the register. A player who helps him carry the register to the vestry, or who asks to see the oldest grave, earns the second copy's existence — the copy he keeps in the vestry because he does not trust the Company not to burn the first.

> _You want to see the oldest,_ he says. _Here. `1847`. Teodoro Montoya's wife. The first. She died in the first winter, bearing. The ground was frozen and they built a fire on it to dig it. The family has been here since the family has been dying here._

Intuit him by not mentioning his drinking, by bringing a bottle to the rectory as a gift rather than a judgment, or by asking him to say the words over Celso later. He is a man whose soft heart is hidden behind a hard office, and the party that finds the soft heart finds the cemetery register's full weight on their side.

Offend him by treating the register as evidence rather than as the dead, by mocking the Church, or by asking whether the priest is "really" neutral. He will go cold, the register will stay locked, and the party loses the oldest title in the country.

**Tomás Luján** (if he comes in late and sits by the door) is the hardest read and the highest stakes. He is won only by the long, patient, undemanding attention of a player who notices him, does not press him, and offers him a cigarette or a plate without asking anything in return. He has been waiting for someone to notice him without wanting something. A player who does that earns the first sentence he has spoken to a stranger since March.

> _You do not ask me anything,_ Tomás says, not looking up from the plate he has not touched. _That is new. Most men, the moment they are kind, they want. What is it you do not want._

Intuit him by leaving the table first, by walking out to the cañón mouth with him after the meal in silence, or by telling him, plainly, that Halladay was an honest man and that honest men deserve to have what they died for carried forward. The party that cracks Tomás at the supper cracks him without coercion, and a willing witness is worth ten coerced ones.

Offend him by grabbing his arm, by demanding he speak, by cornering him, or by mentioning the willows. He will leave by the back door, run inside three days, and the witness clock jumps forward a week.

### 3. The willows

Before the meal's unease has settled, a boy comes running from the lower pasture. Two of the Montoya ewes are down in the willows at the creek bend and there is a man in the brush with them, and the man is not one of the plaza's.

It is a dead man. He is a young Hispano sheep hand named **Celso Baca**, and he has been shot once under the arm with a heavy pistol and once across the jaw and left in the willows where the ewes found him. Celso rode night guard at the lower pasture and he was the one who first found the surveyor Halladay's body in this same bend of creek in March. He had been quiet and strange since. Now he is in the same willows, with the same kind of wound, and the acequia water runs red over his shirt for a moment and then clear again.

Read or paraphrase:

> Celso lies on his side in the mud with one arm out and the ewes backed off up the bank. The wound under the arm is the size of a half dollar and the jaw is gone. The creek takes the blood and thins it and carries it on. Celso's hat is in the water twenty yards downstream and turning in the current.

There should be enough on the ground to trouble a smart party. One set of hoofprints shows a shod horse, the plaza's fashion. Another shows a horse shod in the Company's fashion, the wide heel caulks the Elizabethtown smith puts on. A shaving of pencil lead sits in the mud near Celso's hand, the same kind Halladay used. Celso had been drinking at the saloon with one of Hadley's riders the night before, and earlier in the day he had spoken to Tomás Luján at the corral. Let the players question Tomás, the riders, the sheep hands, and the priest until the scene begins to tighten around them. If they press hard, reward them before the shooting starts. The rider lied about when he last saw Celso. Tomás has mud on his cuffs from the willows. One ewe hand admits a Company man offered two dollars to know which pasture Celso rode that night. Eufemia says Celso owed her three dollars for flour, which is true and not the point.

This is an early place where parties try to take the whole chapter by the throat. They may clap the rider in irons at once, beat Tomás for an answer, ride down to Cimarron to fetch Sheriff Tatum before dawn, or decide the killing proves the plaza itself is compromised. Let them push. If the rider is seized early, Hadley sends a cleaner crew from Elizabethtown sooner. If Tomás is beaten or humiliated in public, he becomes sloppier, more frightened, and more willing to run. If the party fetches Tatum, Tatum rides up, looks at the body, writes "drowning" in his book the way he wrote it for Halladay, and rides back down. The party should feel that strong action changes the chapter without ending it.

Let Celso's death sit in the plaza for a moment before the night raid breaks over it. Father Vigil kneels by him on the bank and says a few hard words that are not much prayer and not much accusation, only enough to remind everyone that Celso belonged to somebody before he became evidence. If a player stays, that player can learn Celso had a sister in the plaza and a mother who still writes as if her son might come home from the lower pasture whole. Small facts like that make later lies heavier.

If the party treats the willows like a murder scene and not a prelude, help them. The rider should not answer like a melodrama villain. He trims every sentence, turns the dead man meaner than he was, and tries to reduce murder to drink, debt, and bad company. The Company's men close ranks fast in the way hired crews do when they smell one of their own may be selected for punishment whether guilty or not. Don Ignacio will tolerate aggressive inquiry only so long as it still looks like the plaza's grief. Once it starts looking like Anglos running the acequia, he hardens. That is good pressure. It gives the party room without giving them ownership of the plaza.

### 4. Raid on the sheep camp

Before the killing is settled, riders hit the upper sheep camp in the dark.

Read or paraphrase:

> The first shot comes from above the camp and the second comes from inside it. Then the sheep break and a ewe screams the way a woman screams and the camp dogs go mad. Somebody in the dark is shouting for the book and somebody else is shouting for God.

Use confusion. Use sheep breaking loose. Use muzzle flashes at bad angles. Make it hard to tell whether the riders are after the flock, the panic, the guide Tomás Luján, or one particular haversack. Put `6-8` mounted riders in the dark, one man already inside the camp cutting the picket rope, and enough panic in the animals to make wool and horn more dangerous than a single pistol. Their purpose is plain even if their employer is not. They mean to scatter the flock, cover the escape of the inside man, and seize Tomás Luján if they can, because Tomás is the one living witness to what happened in the willows in March. The fight matters because lost sheep can starve the plaza before the suit is tried, because a captured rider can point toward hired interference rather than a simple night raid, and because Don Ignacio will remember who held their head when the dark broke open around them. Afterward, let one dying rider carry something wrong in his pocket: an Elizabethtown assay chit, a Company cartridge he should not have, or a marked double eagle from Hadley's strongbox. It should start suspicion, not settle it.

What happens here should travel forward. If the flock is badly scattered, the plaza's spring shearing fails and the whole comunidad grows poorer and meaner by summer. If the party captures a living rider, Hadley and the surveyor Pell both become nervous sooner. If the inside man escapes cleanly, Tomás grows bolder and the riders more willing to come back. If the party protects Tomás, later fights become more openly about the field book than about sheep.

Expect the players to do more here than trade shots in the dark. They may give chase instead of securing the camp, fire the hay stack or the wagon to make light, scatter the flock on purpose to deny the riders a target, or drag a living attacker back for questioning before the blood dries. None of those choices should be wrong. They should simply cost something else. A hard pursuit may identify whose men these are and still leave the plaza short sheep. A lit camp gives clearer fire and tells half the ridge where the weak point lay. Saving Tomás may mean letting the inside man vanish. Interrogating a captured rider before dawn may produce one broken phrase, one false name, or one item in his pockets that matters more than anything he says.

If a rider is taken alive, do not rush past him. A living attacker is not only a source of facts. He is a problem men start arranging themselves around. Don Ignacio wants the plaza's grief answered in the plaza's way. Tatum, if he is fetched, wants order and a chain of custody he can sell back to Hadley. Hadley wants the man dead before sunrise for reasons he will never state cleanly. Eufemia wants to know who paid him and how much, because the number is data. The party can hand him over, keep him under their own watch, trade him later, or use his body as proof this was paid work rather than a range quarrel. A silent prisoner is still a powerful thing in a plaza.

**Combat Encounter: Raid on the Sheep Camp**

- **Enemies:** `1 PICKET CUTTER` inside the camp, `4-6 HIRED RIDERS`, and `1-2 ELIZABETHTOWN TOUGHS` riding cover through the dark.

- **Area:** The fight runs across the upper sheep camp on the bench above the plaza: picket ropes, the lambing pen, the cook fire's banked coals, two wagons, the dog lines, stacked fleece, and too many animals packed too close together for calm work.

- **Terrain and Effects:** Darkness keeps sightlines uncertain until the fire is lit or a muzzle flashes. Loose sheep can knock men down, foul lines of fire, or force sudden `MOVE` and `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` tests. Picket stakes, cut ropes, and the lambing pen's wattling turn the ground treacherous at foot level. If the party lights the hay stack, attacks become easier to place but everyone standing in the glow becomes easier to hit in return.

By the time the party beds down, they should know which leaders were useful under pressure and which looked mean, frightened, or slippery. Hand out private impressions if you like. The character who watched the riders sees the Company man flinch at the name of Hadley. The one who guarded Tomás notices the guide reach for the haversack three times in the dark. The one who held the flock hears a rider call to the inside man in English. The one who searched the dead sees Celso's pencil shaving in the rider's pocket, wrapped in a piece of the Company's own notice.

## Arc II: Baldy Mountain and the Quiet Title

This middle arc takes the party north to the gold camp at Elizabethtown and into the Company's machinery. It is where the suit stops being a plaza quarrel and becomes a fight over mineral land, a railroad right of way, and the slow arithmetic of who can afford to bleed longest. The arc is not a track. The party may ride up the mountain for gold, for the surveyor Pell, for the grant language Ouida keeps, or simply because the plaza got too hot to sit still in. Any of those roads lead into the same knot of money and powder and the Company's patience running shorter by the week.

## The Gold Camp
### 5. Elizabethtown

The road north to Baldy climbs out of the plaza's cañón into the pinyon and juniper and then into the ponderosa and then into the aspen, and the boom camp of Elizabethtown sits in a bowl of the mountain where Willow Creek cuts the gold-bearing gravel. It is a camp of five hundred souls in the good months and a hundred and the wind in the bad, and it smells of woodsmoke and powder and the mercury the arrastres use and the cheap whiskey the saloons sell. Strand holds the rougher end of the camp from a saloon called the Old Abe and he is the man to see about the diggings.

Read or paraphrase:

> The camp runs along the creek in a long untidy line of canvas and clapboard and the tailings run down off the cuts above it in pale fans of broken rock. The stamp mill at the upper end beats a slow rhythm the whole camp has stopped hearing. A line of men waits at the assay office with their pans and their paper. Two of them are armed. All of them are.

The camp is the Company's milk cow and the Company knows it. The royalty is a tenth of the gross and the gross is what keeps the camp alive and what keeps the Company's London directors patient through the slow suits. Strand pays the royalty because he has to and hates it because he has to, and the hatred is the lever he offers anyone with a strong back and a loose notion of whose gold the creek holds.

Do not let the party walk the camp without reading its economy on the page. The assay office is the camp's bank and its church; a man's standing in Elizabethtown is the number the assayer writes on his slip. The arrastres down by the creek grind the low-grade ore with mercury and horse power and the mercury gets into the creek and the creek gets into the men who drink it and a few of them have begun to shake. The Company's royalty man sits at a table in the Old Abe every evening with a locked box and a ledger and he takes his tenth in dust, weighed on a scale that everyone in camp believes is heavy to the Company's side and nobody can prove. `INSIGHT` reads the strain in the line at the assay office and the way the armed men stand a half-step closer to the pans than the unarmed. `LABOR` shows that the cuts above the camp are played out at the top and the good dirt is deeper now and the deeper dirt costs more to move. `BOOKLEARNIN'` notices that the royalty man's ledger is kept in duplicate and the duplicate goes down the mountain to Cimarron every Sunday in a saddlebag.

Strand makes his approach the way a man like Strand always does: a drink, a story, a test of how the strangers take to being sized up. If the party comes up the mountain from the plaza he hears about it before they reach the Old Abe, because the camp hears everything that happens in the plaza within a day. If they come from Cimarron he hears about that too. Read him close to the stereotype at first and let the stereotype hide the calculating man underneath.

> _You all look like you can ride and shoot,_ Strand says, pouring before he asks. _Half the men in this camp can ride and shoot. The other half lie about it. Sit. Drink. Tell me who sent you up my mountain, and I will tell you whether they did you a favor._

The Company's counter-offer comes by way of a man named **Brubaker**, Hadley's royalty collector, who finds the party within the hour of their arrival and offers double whatever Strand offered and a Company brand on their horses. Brubaker is plain about what the brand means. It means the Company feeds them, shelters them, and pays them, and it means the Company owns the use of them. The choice between Strand's rough money and the Company's clean money is the arc's first real fork, and it is the fork the third path runs between.

#### How Strand bends to the party

Strand sizes up the party in the first five minutes and decides whether they are muscle, mark, or menace. He is won by men who can drink, shoot, and tell a story, and he is won hardest by men who can do all three without taking themselves seriously. He respects the western code of the saloon — the bought drink, the tall tale, the willingness to be sized up without taking offense.

> _You drank it,_ Strand says when the glass is empty. _That is a point in your favor. Most men sent up here by the Company drink my whiskey and then tell me they are not Company men. You drank it and said nothing. That is either honesty or good sense. In this camp they are the same thing._

Intuit him by asking about the Denver days before he offers them. Strand buried the Denver killing under the Colonel story, and a player who has heard the rumor (Rumor 5) and asks, carefully, will watch the Colonel mask slip for one second before it comes back. That one second is worth more than a dozen drinks. Strand will trust a man who could have used the Denver story as a lever and did not.

Offend him by refusing the drink, by quoting the law at him, or by being unable to shoot when he takes the party out back to test them on bottles. A man who cannot shoot is, in Strand's economy, a man who cannot earn, and Strand does not keep men who cannot earn. `Gold Camp Standing` drops and the camp closes ranks.

The deepest read on Strand is that he knows his claim to the creek is as crooked as the Company's, just older. A player who says so to his face, calmly, without judgment, earns a kind of respect Strand has not given since Denver. He will not admit the crookedness. He will buy the next round, and the round after that, and the party has an ally who will shoot for them and lie for them and never quite tell them the whole truth.

### 6. Ouida Beaubien

Ouida keeps a room above the Elizabethtown store and a copy of the original `1841` grant in a tin box under her bed. She is the granddaughter of Carlos Beaubien, the original Mexican grantee, and she has been told all her life that the grant was stolen from her family by Maxwell and from Maxwell by the Company. She is right about the second and half-right about the first. What she can give the party is the language of the original grant, which conveyed the surface for stock and agriculture but reserved the mineral to the sovereign. The Company's claim to the gold rests on a later deed that does not mention minerals. With the grant language alone, the Company's gold claim collapses without touching the boundary. With the grant language and the field book together, the whole eastern half of the Company's case goes.

Read or paraphrase:

> The stair up to her room was unplaned lumber and the room itself smelled of cold coffee and old paper. She had the tin box on the table and the lid open and she did not invite anyone to sit. She was thirty and she dressed like a woman who had stopped caring what the camp thought of how she dressed. The grant copy lay under a sheet of oiled paper and the Spanish was small and exact and the seal was old and dark.

Ouida is the arc's truest historian and its loneliest figure. She has read the grant language aloud in the store to anyone who would listen for two years, which is almost no one, and the almost-no-one has curdled her. She is not a disinterested witness. She wants her family's name restored to the gold, which means she wants a piece of what the camp pulls out of the creek, which means she is an ally with a price. Treat her as a person and not a plot device, and the scene does what it needs to.

She will hand over the grant language, or a true copy of it in her own careful hand, for a promise. The promise is that the party will speak her family's claim in the Cimarron court, alongside the comunidad's, alongside the camp's. The party can promise and mean it, in which case the hearing gains a third claimant and the complexity that comes with one. They can promise and not mean it, in which case Ouida will know by the second day of the hearing and will become a dangerous enemy who knows where every body is buried because her family buried half of them. They can refuse, in which case she keeps the grant language and the Company's gold claim survives even a field-book victory. Or they can take the language by other means, which the camp will hear about inside a day and which will mark them as the kind of people who rob a lone woman in a room above a store.

If a player treats her as a witness rather than a curiosity, give her the scene she is owed. Let her read the relevant clause in Spanish, then translate it flat:

> _The surface for pasturage and tillage. The mines are not conveyed. The sovereign keeps the mines. That is the law under which this grant was made, and the law under which it must be read. Your Mr. Maxwell understood it. Your Mr. Hadley counts on your court forgetting it._

#### How Ouida bends to the party

Ouida has been reading the grant language to the empty air of the store for two years, and the party is the first audience in that time that might actually matter. She reads the room as carefully as Strand reads his, but she reads for a different thing. Strand reads for usefulness. Ouida reads for condescension. The first player who looks at the grant instead of at her earns the conversation; the first player who looks at her instead of at the grant ends it.

She is won by respect for the law and for the Spanish. A player who reads the grant clause in the original (a `BOOKLEARNIN'` character with Spanish, or one who simply asks her to read it again, slowly) earns her instant and startled regard. She has been waiting two years for someone to treat the document as the document.

> _You read the Spanish,_ she says, and for the first time she offers a chair. _Sit. Read it again. The second clause. The word is_ reservados. _Reserved. Not conveyed, not granted, not sold. Reserved. The sovereign kept it, and when the sovereign defaulted the right reverted. To whom did it revert, do you think. Read the rest and tell me._

Intuit her by asking about her grandfather before asking about the gold. Carlos Beaubien is the name she has been trying to get into a courtroom for nine years, and the player who asks after the man rather than the metal gets the woman behind the anger. She will tell the party about the Loretto Academy, about the lawyers in Santa Fe who would not take her case, about the room above the store and how long she has been in it. The conversation costs an hour and buys an ally whose loyalty is deeper than the grant language.

Offend her by offering to buy the grant copy outright (she reads it as the party trying to own her family's name the way the Company owns the grant), by asking whether a woman can really press a claim in a territorial court, or by looking at the derringer in the tin box instead of at the seal. Any of the three and the lid closes, the chair is withdrawn, and the party leaves the room with nothing but the memory of the Spanish they did not read carefully enough.

The deepest read on Ouida is that she is right about the law and she is right to be angry and her anger has made her hard in ways that will cost the party at the hearing if they bring her. A party that promises her the courtroom and means it gets the grant language and a brilliant, difficult, third claimant who will argue her family's case alongside the comunidad's and refuse to subordinate one to the other. A party that promises and does not mean it learns, by the second day of the hearing, that Ouida has been reading the grant language to the _Cimarron News_ editor on the side, and the story breaks with or without them. She has been waiting nine years. She will not be managed.

### 7. The false surveyor

In a room above the Old Abe, behind a door that has been kicked open more than once, the surveyor **Aldous Pell** is drinking himself to a thickness that will not remember March. His transit and chain lean in the corner under a film of dust. His field bag is open on the floor and the instruments he still owns are spotted with the green of old brass. He filed the false return under Halladay's name. He knows exactly where the line was bent, exactly how much it cost, and exactly what the back of a man's skull looks like when the cost comes due. The cost is eating him.

Read or paraphrase:

> The room smelled of stale whiskey and the particular sweet-rot of a man who has not changed his shirt in a week. Pell sat on the edge of the bed with a glass in both hands and the glass was empty and he was watching the empty as if it might fill itself. His hands were the hands of a man who had once made his living by their steadiness.

Pell is the arc's broken hinge. He is the one man in the chapter who can sign an affidavit naming the false line, the false return, and the hand that paid for both, and an affidavit from the surveyor who filed the return is nearly as strong as the field book itself. The trouble is that Pell's nerve is gone and his price is not money. His price is a way out of the country that does not end in a Cimarron courtroom or a shallow grave.

Handle him gently and he will draw the false line and the true line on a piece of wrapping paper with a shaking hand and sign his name to an affidavit that names the fraud and the date and the dollar amount. That affidavit and the field book together are the strongest paper the party can carry into the hearing. Handle him roughly and he signs whatever is put in front of him, but the signing breaks something in him and he runs inside two days, and his running gives Hadley the cleanest possible argument: that the affidavit was beaten out of a drunk and signed under duress and worth nothing. The party can also sell Pell to Hadley, in which case Hadley thanks them, pays them well, and puts Pell on the next southbound stage with a fresh name, a closed mouth, and a Company pension paid through a lawyer in Las Vegas. The affidavit never existed and the witness is gone.

There is a fourth handling, which the best tables find on their own. A party that listens to Pell instead of threatening him can get the thing he actually needs to say.

> _I drew the line where he told me to draw it,_ Pell says, not looking up. _Twelve miles. You understand what twelve miles of country is, on the ground. I drew it and I filed it and I told myself the Mexican would sign it because the Mexican was dead and the dead sign anything. Then the Mexican was not dead in the way they said and he was dead in a different way, in the willows, and I had filed his name to a line drawn over his body. I am not asking you to forgive me. I am asking you to get me out of this country before the same men come for me who came for him._

The party that gets him out, by stage or by their own escort, earns a witness who will appear in Cimarron or in Santa Fe or wherever the case finally lands. The party that leaves him in the room above the Old Abe finds, by week's end, that the room is empty and the bottle is on the floor and Pell is in the creek or on a stage or simply gone, and the affidavit with him.

### 8. The freight road and the right of way

Between the camp and the court runs the road the Company built, and on that road the chapter's slowest clock is the one the railroad keeps. **Oren Pell** (no relation to the surveyor, though the Company cultivates the confusion) rides the road between Cimarron, Elizabethtown, and the plaza by turns, never resident anywhere, buying nothing and selling nothing in the open, taking notes in a shorthand nobody can read. He wants the right of way across the grant for the Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe, and he does not care whose name is on the deed so long as the deed is clean.

Read or paraphrase:

> He came into the Old Abe at the dinner hour and took a table alone and ordered coffee and drank it black and read a folded newspaper that was three weeks old and from Topeka. His coat was Eastern and his boots were new and his hands were the hands of a man who had never run a sluice box. He watched the room over the paper. When he had finished the coffee he left two bits on the table and walked out, and the bartender swept the coin into the till without comment, and the room went back to its business.

Oren Pell is the arc's safest buyer and its most dangerous creditor. He will buy any clean copy of any paper that settles the boundary. He will pay in railroad drafts cashable in Topeka, Kansas City, or New York. He will ask no questions about where the paper came from and he will remember, in a ledger in Topeka, exactly who sold it and for how much. A party that sells him a copy of the field book leaves the chapter rich and leaves the chapter owing the railroad a favor the railroad will collect, in its own season, in its own country.

Meeting him is not a scene the party has to earn. If they sit still in any of the three towns for two days, he finds them. He is patient the way a glacier is patient. Let the party decide whether to sell to him, to use him as a counter-weight against Hadley, to ignore him, or to try to read his shorthand. Each of those is a real western.

## The Court in Cimarron
### 9. Cimarron

The road down from Baldy is the road the Company built, and Cimarron is the Company's town. The court is the second story of the Company's store. Judge Vail holds the spring term the first Monday of June. The suit is _Maxwell Land Grant and Railway Company v. the Inhabitants of the Tract_, and the Company's lead counsel is a Ring man named **Harlan Pike** out of Santa Fe, smooth, well-dressed, and certain. The comunidad has one lawyer, a young Hispano out of Taos named **Filomeno Baca**, who is brave and outgunned and knows it.

Read or paraphrase:

> Cimarron ran along the river in a line of false fronts and muddy streets and it was the Company from one end to the other. The store was the biggest building on the street and the court was up the stairs at the back of it and the stairs creaked under the weight of the law. A Company sign hung over the door. The sheriff's office was across the street and the jail was behind it and both of them were Company too.

Do not let the party read Cimarron as a neutral venue. Every plank of it belongs to the suit's plaintiff. The judge is a Ring appointment. The sheriff is Hadley's man. The store is the Company's store. The hotel is the Company's hotel. The newspaper, the _Cimarron News and Press_, is the one institution in town that is not cleanly owned, and its editor, a Yankee named **Thaddeus Lowe**, hates the Company with a cold and careful hate that he has so far been too prudent to print. He is the arc's hidden lever, and a party that finds him finds a press.

Filomeno Baca is the comunidad's lawyer and he is staying in a back room at the hotel, working by lamp, drowning in paper. He is twenty-six, he read law in Taos under a man who read law in Santa Fe under a man who was hung for it, and he has taken the case for a fee the plaza can mostly pay in wool. He is brave. He is alone. If the party brings him paper he did not have, the paper walks into court on his arm. If the party brings him nothing, he walks in alone and he loses, and he knows it.

> _They have three lawyers and I have one of me,_ Baca says, not looking up from the brief. _They have a judge. I have the truth, which in this court is presently worth less than a good brief. If you have paper, bring it to me before the term opens. After the term opens it is too late for anything but theater._

## Arc III: The Eastern Line

This arc takes the party up the cañón and onto the line itself, with Tomás Luján and Nah-leh, to find the field book and to learn what really happened in the willows in March. It is the chapter's journey and its reversal. It can be played before Arc II, after it, interleaved with it, or not at all, if the party has chosen to win by another lever. If the party never climbs the cañón, the book stays in the cairn and the line stays in the ground and the truth of March stays in Nah-leh's memory, and the chapter ends however the party's other levers end it. The arc is here for the party that wants to walk the fraud with its own eyes.

### 10. The cañón

The cañón runs west out of the plaza into the Sangre de Cristos. It is narrow, timbered, and cold, the creek falling over ledges, the trail going up in switchbacks through the spruce and then out onto the high parks where the elk feed and the Jicarilla hunt and the snow stays until June. Tomás knows the cañón the way a man knows his own hallway. Nah-leh knows it the way a man knows the back of his hand and the hand of his enemy. If the party takes both of them, the cañón opens. If the party takes one, the other's knowledge is missing and the chapter remembers it. If the party takes neither, the cañón punishes them as high country punishes the proud.

Read or paraphrase:

> The cañón narrowed and the spruce closed over the trail and the light went green and thin. The creek fell over a ledge to the left and the trail switchbacked up the right and the horses picked their footing one stone at a time. Above the spruce the aspen stood white and bare and not yet leafed and above the aspen the snow still lay in the gullies. A gray jay came down out of the timber and sat on a branch at Tomás's shoulder height and watched them pass and did not fly until they were past.

The ride up is the chapter's one sustained piece of country, and it should do the work the country does in a western: it should wear the party down, sort the competent from the not, and teach the reader the ground the fraud was drawn across. Use the climb. A spring storm comes through in the afternoon and the party makes a cold camp in the spruce with the horses stamped tight against the wet. A snow bridge over a side creek gives way under a pack horse and the party works half the night to save the animal or the gear or both. The elk are up on the high parks in their calving bands and a cow bawls in the dark and the horses pull at the picket. None of this is decoration. A party that arrives at the line wet, tired, and short a horse reads the fraud differently than a party that arrives dry and fresh.

If Nah-leh rides with the party, he names the country as they climb, in the spare way he names things. This meadow is where his people took the elk in the second moon. This ridge is where the survey chain came two summers ago and the chainmen shot a colt his people had not finished breaking and left it for the ravens. This saddle is where his cousin lay in March and watched the willows. He does not editorialize. He points. The party that listens learns that the line on the ground is the latest in a long series of lines drawn across people who were already here.

### 11. The line on the ground

Where Halladay ran the true line is marked by his own blazes on the ponderosa, cut clean, at the right intervals, the bark healed over them in the year since. Where the Company's false line runs is marked by newer blazes, cut sloppier, cut over the old ones where they could be found, cut twelve miles east of where they should be. The two lines are visible on the same trees in places, the old clean blaze and the new rough one, and a man who knows what a survey blaze means can read the fraud on the bark.

Read or paraphrase:

> Halladay's blaze was a foot long and level and the cambium had healed to a brown lip around it. A foot to the right and four feet higher was Pell's blaze, ragged, the bark splintered out, the hatchet having been swung by a man who did not care whether the tree lived or died. Tomás put his hand on the clean blaze. _Here,_ he said. _He marked the true line here. They marked the false line there. Twelve miles of country between these two marks, and twelve miles of us._

A party with Tomás and the field book can walk both lines and see the fraud with their own eyes. A party with Tomás and no book can still see the two blazes and still understand what they mean, which is enough to swear to in a courtroom if the courtroom will hear a mixed-blood guide, which Vail's may not. A party with neither Tomás nor the book can still see the blazes if they know what blazes mean, which a `BOOKLEARNIN'` or `NATURE` character does.

Nah-leh's ridge is a half-day's ride higher. From it, the whole bend of the Arroyo de las Vacas is laid out below, the willows a dark line along the water, the plaza a pale square in the sage, the road to Cimarron a thread running east. This is where his cousin lay in March and watched. From here the party sees what the cousin saw.

What the cousin saw was three men and a fire. Two of the men were Company riders out of Elizabethtown, recognizable by their horses and their hats. The third was a white man in a good coat who did not do the shooting but who stood and watched and who rode away with the field book in his saddlebag. Nah-leh does not name the third man. He points to where the good coat stood, and he lets the party name him.

> _My cousin did not come down,_ Nah-leh says. _A man with a rifle who does not shoot when he should has used up his one chance to be a fool. He watched. He saw. He came home and he told me. I have kept what he told me. I have not sold it. I am telling you now because you are here and because the line is below us and because the men who drew it are not done drawing._

The party, by now, can name the man in the good coat. The only man in the chapter who wears a good coat, who does not do his own bleeding, and who had reason to want the field book off the surveyor and into a saddlebag is Hadley. The party does not need Nah-leh to say it. The party says it, and Nah-leh watches them say it, and he nods once, and that is the chapter's reversal: the man who hired some of them, or tried to, is the man who stood in the willows and watched the surveyor die.

### 12. The book

The field book is where Halladay hid it the morning he knew they were coming for him: in the cairn he built at the true eastern corner of the grant, the corner the false line moved twelve miles east. Tomás watched him build the cairn and Tomás is the only living man who knows it stands where it stands. The cairn is at the timberline on a ridge above the head of the cañón, a careful cone of stacked stone that looks, to anyone who does not know, like a surveyor's corner marker of no particular interest. Which is exactly what Halladay intended.

Read or paraphrase:

> The cairn was three feet high and built of the flat local stone and it sat on the ridgeline where a man with a transit would set up to shoot the eastern line. Tomás knelt beside it and pulled the third stone from the north face and reached in and came out with a bundle wrapped in oilcloth and then in canvas and then in a surveyor's leather sleeve stamped _P.H._ The oilcloth had kept the ink from running. The pages were the true line, run by chain and compass, signed and dated and witnessed by Tomás Luján and by the mark of Nah-leh's cousin, in their own hands, in March of `1873`.

The book is the chapter's hinge. In hand, the party has the lever. The pages show the line Halladay actually ran, the corners he actually set, the measurements that put the eastern boundary exactly where the `1841` grant put it and not where the Company's `1872` return put it. The witness signatures make it sworn testimony. The hand is Halladay's, identifiable to any man who knew his writing, which the Land Office in Santa Fe did.

What the party does with the book in the next ten minutes decides a great deal of the rest of the chapter.

- **Carry it down whole** and file it in court, and the eastern line falls on the strongest paper possible.
- **Copy it by hand on the ridge** (a long cold day's work; `BOOKLEARNIN'` and time) and carry the copy down while leaving the original in the cairn, and the party has a lever and a fallback, but the copy is disputable in a way the original is not.
- **Burn the offensive pages**, as Nah-leh may ask, and the party buys mountain peace at the cost of the strongest evidence.
- **Sell it on the ridge** to whoever can reach them there (Hadley's riders, Oren Pell's courier, Strand's miners), and the country learns inside a day that the party has the book and the party becomes the most hunted people in Colfax County.
- **Bury it deeper** and walk away, having seen the truth and decided to win or lose without it.

The ridge is not a safe place to make this choice. The Company's chainmen have been re-marking the line east of the cairn all spring, and a working party of them is within a half-day's ride most weeks. If the party lingers, the chainmen find the horse tracks and come looking, and the ridge becomes the site of the chapter's first fight over the book rather than over the line. If the party has earned `Mountain Trust`, Nah-leh's scouts warn them of the chainmen in time to choose whether to hide, run, or fight. If they have not, the chainmen crest the ridge while the party is still in the cairn.

**Combat Encounter: The Cairn on the Ridge**

- **Enemies:** `1 COMPANY CHAIN BOSS` (a hard case, ex-army, armed with a '73 Winchester) and `3-5 COMPANY CHAINMEN` (mixed arms, mostly revolvers and one shotgun). If the Company is genuinely frightened of the book, add `1 ELIZABETHTOWN TOUGH` riding as Hadley's eyes.

- **Area:** A timberline ridge, open, with the cairn at its high point, deadfall timber for cover on the north and west, the trail dropping east and west. Wind from the west. Long sightlines in every direction but no depth.

- **Terrain and Effects:** Thin air punishes any `LABOR` or extended `MOVE`. The deadfall is hard cover but it is also the only concealment, and a man behind a log is a man whose position is known. The cairn itself is cover for one crouching man. The wind pushes muzzle smoke east, blinding anyone shooting that direction after the first volley. A horse shot loose on the ridge runs and the party walks home, which is its own consequence.

## Arc IV: Quiet Title

The reckoning belongs in the courtroom town of Cimarron. There should be boots, paper, witnesses, lies, and the feeling that men are now trying to do with offices what they failed to do with carbines in the willows and on the ridge. This arc is the convergence: every clock the party has run, every lever it has pulled, every faction it has courted or offended, lands in the same second-story courtroom in the same Company town on the same Monday morning. The arc is short because the work that decides it has already been done. The hearing is where the chapter's account comes due.

### 13. The eve of term

The night before the hearing, the town is a market in paper and in fear. Everyone who wants the book, or a copy, or an affidavit, or a witness, or simply a promise, comes to the party's door or sends a note to its table. Run the eve as a single rolling scene in which the party is offered, threatened, and begged in turn, and in which the order of the offers is itself a test.

Read or paraphrase:

> The lamp at the hotel desk had been turned low and the clerk had gone to bed and the hall smelled of kerosene and the cold drafts of a building thrown up fast over a dirt floor. Boots crossed the porch below the window at intervals and did not always come up the stairs.

Hadley comes himself, in the person of a courier, with a final offer: cash for the book, cash for the affidavit, cash for Pell's silence, and a Company draft for the party's future, in exchange for a quiet morning in which none of it reaches the courtroom. It is a large offer. It is meant to look like a large offer. Hadley would rather pay than fight, because paying is cheaper than a scandal and a scandal is the one thing his owners in London cannot forgive.

> _Mr. Hadley's compliments,_ the courier says, laying a folded draft on the table. _He observes that a great deal of paper changes hands in this country and that most of it is worth more to the man who forgets he had it than to the man who carries it into a courtroom. The figure is negotiable. The morning is not._

Oren Pell comes by note, folded once, in his unreadable shorthand decoded into clean English: a railroad draft for a clean copy of any paper the party holds, no questions, no enmity, no memory. The railroad's patience has decided to move, and it has moved to the party's table.

> The note read, in a clerk's hand: _A clean copy of any document in your possession that bears on the eastern boundary. The railroad's draft, drawn on Topeka, for an amount we will agree on in the morning. No inquiry as to provenance. No further obligation. This offer lapses at noon tomorrow._

Strand comes in person, drunk and loud, with two of his miners at his back, and his offer is simpler and uglier: let him have the book for one night, he will use it to break the Company in the morning, and he will cut the party in on the camp's gross for a year. Strand's offer is the one most likely to get someone killed before dawn.

> _One night,_ Strand says, hand flat on the table. _You let me read that book into the record and the Company is done by noon and you ride out of here with a tenth of every pan that comes out of that creek for a year. One night. Or you can take Hadley's paper and starve on it._

Don Ignacio does not come. He sends Father Vigil, with the cemetery register under his arm and a single request: that the party, if it has the book, read it in open court and then hand it to the church for keeping, because the church is the one institution in the country the Company cannot buy. Father Vigil's offer is the only one that asks the party to give the paper away for nothing, and it is the one most likely to be refused, and it is the one most worth taking.

> _Don Ignacio does not come because Don Ignacio does not bargain,_ the priest says, the register under his arm. _He asks. Read the book in the court. Then give it to me, and I will put it in the altar with the communion silver, where no Company man will reach it. You will have done the right thing and you will have nothing to show for it. That is the shape the right thing takes, in this country._

Filomeno Baca comes last, alone, tired, with his brief in a carpetbag. He does not offer money. He offers the truth and the question of whether the party came to New Mexico to do anything with it.

> _I can win with the book,_ Baca says. _I can fight with the affidavit. I can argue the grant language until the court throws me out for arguing it. I cannot win with none of it. Tell me what you are carrying into that room tomorrow, and I will tell you whether I am a lawyer or a man reading a eulogy._

#### How the eve of term bends to the party

The eve is the chapter's last social test and the one with the most money on the table. The party cannot please everyone; it can barely please two. Run each offer as a pressure the party can accept, refuse, defer, or play against another.

**Hadley's courier** is won by a yes, obviously, but also by a counter-offer that shows the party understands it is being bought. Hadley respects the counter-offer because the counter-offer is a negotiation and Hadley negotiates. A party that accepts flatly is marked as cheap; a party that haggles is marked as professional. Offend the courier (or, worse, send him back with a threat) and `Company Pressure` rises and the Company stops negotiating and starts planning.

**Oren Pell's note** is won by a copy, any copy, on any paper. Pell does not care about the party's morals; he cares about the railroad's corridor. Offend him by ignoring the note — Pell reads silence as incompetence, and the railroad's next offer goes to Hadley instead.

**Strand** is won by drinking with him and refusing the deal anyway, which he respects more than a sober refusal. He is a saloon man; he understands the drink that does not buy anything. Offend him by taking Hadley's money in front of him, which he reads as betrayal, and `Gold Camp Standing` crashes and the camp's first bullet finds the party within the week.

**Father Vigil** is won by taking the register seriously, even if the party will not give him the book. A party that promises to read the book in court and means it earns his blessing; a party that promises and does not mean it earns his contempt, which is the chapter's most quietly devastating consequence, because the priest controls the cemetery register's second copy.

**Baca** is won only by the truth. Tell him what paper you have, honestly, and he becomes the lawyer the party needs. Lie to him, even slightly, and he finds out in the courtroom when the paper he expected is not there, and the betrayal breaks the one ally the party cannot replace.

> _I can win with the book,_ Baca says. _I can fight with the affidavit. I can argue the grant language until the court throws me out for arguing it. I cannot win with none of it. Tell me what you are carrying into that room tomorrow, and I will tell you whether I am a lawyer or a man reading a eulogy._

The party does not have to answer all of them. It has to answer Baca, because the morning comes regardless.

### 14. The hearing

Judge Vail's court is the second story of the Company's store and it smells of dry goods and lamp oil and the tobacco the Company's lawyers chew. The Company's lead counsel is Harlan Pike, smooth, well-dressed, and certain, with two junior Ring lawyers behind him and a paralegal whose only job is to hand him paper. Filomeno Baca sits alone at the other table with his carpetbag. The courtroom is full.

Read or paraphrase:

> The room was hot from the lamp and the bodies and the closed windows. The Company's table had carafes of water and clean paper. Baca's table had a carpetbag and a sheaf of briefs that had been read so many times the corners were soft. Vail sat behind the bench with his spectacles on and cleaned one lens as the room settled. The jury was twelve Anglo men from Cimarron and the ranches roundabout. Not one of them was from the plaza.

Who is in the room is who the party brought there or failed to bring. Hadley sits in the front row in a good coat. Don Ignacio sits in the back in his work clothes. Eufemia sits with the ledger open on her knee. Strand sits with two of his miners and he is sober, which is its own kind of warning. Father Vigil sits with the cemetery register. Tomás sits by the door, if he is still alive and not under the ground for running. Nah-leh does not come inside; he sits on the porch with his back to the wall, where he can see the street and the stair and the livery at the same time. Ouida is there if the party promised her, and she has her own copy of the grant and her own claim to press. Oren Pell is not in the room; he does not need to be. He has men in the room.

The hearing runs in three movements.

The first is the Company's case, which is paper. Pike rises and lays out the `1872` survey return, filed under Halladay's name, showing the eastern boundary twelve miles east of the old line. He produces the Maxwell-Company deed, the royalty agreements, the right-of-way surveys. It is a great deal of clean paper and it took a great deal of crooked work to make it clean, and none of the crooked work is visible in the paper itself. Pike is good. The jury is his. Baca cannot dent this case by argument alone.

The second movement is the party's, if the party has paper. This is where the field book, the affidavit, the grant language, the cemetery register, the bought witness, or the intercepted mail enters the record. Each lever lands differently.

- The **field book** enters as a survey document in the dead man's own hand, witnessed, dated, showing the true line. Pike's only answer is forgery, which he will try, and which the witness signatures make hard. If Pell is in the room and willing, the forgery claim dies on Pell's testimony.
- The **affidavit** enters as a sworn confession from the man who filed the false return. Pike's answer is duress, which is plausible if Pell is gone and stronger if the party handled him roughly.
- The **grant language** enters as the original Spanish of the `1841` grant, which reserved the mineral to the sovereign. This does not move the eastern line. It destroys the Company's gold claim outright, which is the claim that pays for the suit, which is the claim that pays Hadley.
- The **cemetery register** enters as a record of possession older than the Company, older than Maxwell, older than the `1841` grant itself in places. It is slow in court and unanswerable in the plaza and Vail will try to exclude it as hearsay, which Baca can fight.
- **Tomás Luján as witness** enters as the only man who was in the willows in March and lived. Vail may refuse to swear him. The jury may refuse to hear him. If he is heard, he names the man in the good coat, and the courtroom changes.
- The **intercepted mail** enters as the Company's own letter naming the line a fiction. This is the lever that hangs the Company on its own postage, and Pike cannot answer it without naming his client a liar, which he will not do.

The third movement is Vail's ruling, and Vail's ruling is the chapter's political question made flesh. Vail is owned. He is not stupid. A field book in open court is a scandal his owners in Santa Fe cannot survive by ruling against, because the scandal reaches the federal Land Office and the Land Office reaches Washington. Vail will bend with the paper if the paper is overwhelming and public. He will bend less if the paper is partial, and he will not bend at all if the paper is absent or if the party's witnesses are dead, bought, or excluded.

The party's choices in the witness chair and in the courtroom gallery decide how far Vail bends. A party that has stacked the field book, the affidavit, and the grant language, and that has kept its witnesses alive, can force the eastern line back toward its true position and destroy the gold claim in the same morning. A party with one lever fights for one win and concedes the rest. A party with no levers watches the eastern line confirmed on paper forever and walks out of Cimarron with the comunidad's ruin and the party's own unpopularity.

### 15. The final standoff

The hearing's outcome does not settle the country. It settles the courtroom. The country settles itself in the street afterward, and the street is where the arc's violence lives, if violence is what the party's choices have built.

The finale should fit the accumulated choices. Hadley may order the book seized by force in the alley behind the court before it can be filed. Strand's miners may decide the courtroom was a better place for a Winchester than a gavel and act on the belief. The Company's riders from Elizabethtown may ride into Cimarron at dusk looking for the party or for Pell or for anyone who spoke. Or one enemy may try to settle everything with a quiet knife behind the store while the town is at supper. The strongest ending usually forces a choice between lawful custody and practical control. One feels cleaner. The other is often safer. In New Mexico those are rarely the same thing.

Use the party's earlier choices to decide which ending is most honest.

- Strong `Comunidad Trust` and the field book in Filomeno Baca's hands lean toward a court victory, the eastern line restored, and the comunidad keeping its land. The standoff, if it comes, is the Company trying to seize the book before filing, and the plaza and the party holding the stair.
- Strong `Gold Camp Standing` and the grant language lean toward the diggings going free, the Company's mineral claim collapsing, and Strand holding the camp as his own kingdom. The standoff is Strand's men against the Company's riders in the street, with the party choosing whether to let it happen or stop it.
- Strong `Mountain Trust` and Nah-leh's testimony lean toward the high country being recognized, which satisfies no court but which changes who lives and who dies in the cañón for a generation. The standoff, if it comes, is federal, not Company: the Indian agent, the army, the line on a different map.
- High `Company Pressure` and a hidden copy lean toward bargain, blackmail, or private violence, and a dark ending in which the party leaves Cimarron rich and unclean, and the standoff is the party against the comunidad it sold out.
- Broken trust and buried evidence lean toward knife work, vanished books, and a fugitive ending into the sandbox. The standoff is the party against everyone, running south for Fort Union or west into the high country with whatever it can carry.

**Combat Encounter: Final Standoff in Cimarron**

- **Enemies:** Match the ending the party built. A lawful seizure may mean `2-4 COMPANY RIDERS` and `1 ELIZABETHTOWN TOUGH` under a Company foreman, or `1 HARLAN PIKE` protected by `2 PINKERTON MEN` the Company hired for exactly this. A wider collapse may add armed townsmen, Strand's miners, or rattled plaza folk as moving complications rather than clean enemy lines.

- **Area:** Run the final violence in a place that fits the reckoning: the alley behind the Company's store, the stair up to the courtroom, the livery at the edge of town, the boardwalk in front of the Old Abe's Cimarron branch, or the crossing of the Cimarron River where the road south runs. Settlement space should feel tighter, more public, and more dangerous for what it means afterward than for the raw body count.

- **Terrain and Effects:** This is not open-country fighting. Boardwalks, barrels, freight wagons, muddy streets, hitch rails, store windows, and the stairwell all matter. Witnesses complicate every shot. `PRESENCE`, `INSIGHT`, and intimidation may matter as much as `SHOOTIN'` if the party tries to force surrender before the whole town chooses a side.

### 16. End states

#### comunidad victory

The field book is read in open court. The eastern line falls back to its true position. The plaza keeps its land, its acequia, its cemetery, and its twenty-six years. The Company's quiet title is denied. The party becomes known in the plaza as the people who carried the true line down the mountain, and the plaza opens to them for the rest of their days.

#### gold camp victory

The grant language is read in open court. The Company's mineral claim collapses. The Baldy diggings go free. Strand holds the camp as his own. The party leaves with gold-camp credit, Strand's rough friendship, and the enmity of the Company.

#### mountain understanding

Nah-leh's testimony is heard, in court or out of it. The high country is recognized, if not by the court then by the people who live under it. The cañón road stays open. The Jicarilla hunt. The party has made a friend of a people who do not make friends lightly.

#### railroad shadow victory

The party sells a copy of the field book to Oren Pell. The railroad settles the boundary to its own satisfaction and the right of way is built. No one outside a small circle knows the whole truth. The party leaves richer, less clean, and more important than before, and the railroad remembers their names.

#### bad ending, good sandbox

The field book is burned or buried. A liar prospers. The eastern line stands as the Company drew it. The party has to leave fast with only what proof they could hide under a coat. This is still a strong sandbox opening. New Mexico is full of unfinished business.

## Standalone Tales

The following outlines are provided as tales that can be played as standalone adventures or fitted into your campaign.

##### Story 1 - The Brand Inspector

A brand inspector out of Cimarron rides into the plaza with a warrant to inspect the Montoya herd for stolen stock. The warrant is real. The inspector is Company-paid. What he is looking for is not a stolen brand but an excuse to seize the herd in lieu of the unpaid royalty the Company has just invented. How do the player characters handle a lawful warrant served by a corrupt man? Do they let him inspect, and trust the brands? Bribe him? Beat him? Ride down to Cimarron and demand the warrant be quashed? Each path costs the plaza something different.

##### Story 2 - The Freight Wagon

A Company freight wagon breaks an axle in the cañón above the plaza and the driver, wounded in the fall, is brought down to Eufemia's store. The wagon carries flour for the Company store in Cimarron, but under the flour, in a nailed crate, is a strongbox. Inside the strongbox is two thousand dollars in gold and a letter from Hadley to a Ring lawyer in Santa Fe that names the eastern line a fiction and the field book a danger. The driver is dying and the wagon is the plaza's by salvage if the party wants it. Do they take the gold and burn the letter? Take the letter and leave the gold? Send the lot down to Cimarron and let the law sort it? Or read the letter aloud in the plaza and let the comunidad decide what to do with a confession that names its own crime?

##### Story 3 - The Schoolteacher

A young Anglo schoolteacher out of Las Vegas comes to the plaza to teach the children their letters, sent by a charity the Company funds. She is earnest, well-meaning, and teaching the children to read English from a primer that calls the Hispano families squatters. Father Vigil is furious. Eufemia is calculating. Don Ignacio is patient. The teacher does not understand what she is doing. What do the player characters do with a woman whose harm is real and whose malice is absent?

##### Story 4 - The Sheep Judge

The spring sheep shearing is the plaza's one reliable cash crop and the buyer is a Company man who has come to lowball the wool. Eufemia wants the party to ride with the fleece to Taos and sell it on the open market, breaking the Company's buyer and netting the plaza three times the price. The road to Taos is two weeks and it crosses the Moreno and the range of a small band of Ute who have not been paid for the grass the flock eats. The party can run the wool, fight the Ute, strike a deal, or sell to the Company man and keep the peace. Each choice writes a different line in Eufemia's ledger.

##### Story 5 - The Penitente Cross

A wooden cross is found on the ridge above the cemetery, driven into the ground in the night, with a knotted cord hanging from it. Father Vigil knows what it means. The Penitentes of the Sangre de Cristo have taken an interest in the plaza's trouble, and the cross is both a warning and a promise. The brotherhood is old, Hispanic, and outside the Church's official blessing, and its help is real and its methods are not the court's. Do the player characters welcome a third party whose idea of justice predates the territory? Keep the plaza's fight in the courtroom? Or let the brotherhood do what brotherhoods do, and live with the story that tells?

## Side Events on the Line

Use these when the table needs one more turn of pressure, when the cañón should feel longer, or when the party's choices have made the chapter split into separate moving problems.

- **The wrong grave by the creek:** The party finds an older grave near the creek, shallow enough that the melt has started to uncover it. The dead man is not from the plaza. He wore Company boots and carried a broken transit and a strip of survey paper too weathered to read. Pell recognizes the dead if pressed and lies first. The scene lets the party feel that the line was dirty before they ever set foot on it.

- **A rider under white cloth:** A lone rider approaches the camp under a scrap of white cloth tied to a ramrod. He claims to carry a message from Hadley, an offer, or a proposal from the railroad. The rider may be honest, stalling, or simply measuring the camp. The party can receive him, strip and search him, shadow him on the way out, or shoot the parley to pieces and live with the story that tells later.

- **The missing transit screw:** One small but crucial piece of Pell's transit goes missing and the whole day turns around a thing no bigger than a thumbnail. This is a good side event when you want to show how easy it is to cripple a survey without killing a man. The party may discover sabotage, clumsy theft, or simple panic hidden under a lie.

- **A wounded horse and a faster choice:** One horse takes a bad shot or a bad break in a place where the party cannot linger. Somebody must shoot it, somebody must strip the tack, and somebody must decide whether the load it carried is worth redistributing. Give this scene to the player who has been most careful with animals so far. Let that care mean something.

- **A settler's claim marker:** The party passes one pathetic little claim marker on good ground: rough-cut wood, one name, one date, a promise written ahead of safety. It should be obvious how little such a thing weighs against a Company deed, a survey transit, or organized money. If the party remembers the place later, it becomes a moral landmark rather than a random signpost.

- **The wrong cartridges:** A handful of cartridges turn up where they do not belong: Company issue in a plaza pouch, Elizabethtown manufacture in a saddlebag that should have held only survey gear, or mixed calibers hidden under oats. The scene gives suspicious players a chance to start building chains instead of isolated clues.

## Camp Scenes Between Major Events

Use these when a session needs breathing room or when the players want to live in the cañón rather than jump from ambush to ambush.

- **The bad coffee fire:** Two of Strand's miners and one of the plaza's sheep hands are arguing over coffee boiled black as axle grease. The dispute starts over the price of flour and ends with one man saying nobody rides into this country for the wages alone. If the players sit in, they can learn who among the rank and file thinks the suit is honest and who already knows better.

- **Don Ignacio at the cemetery register:** The old man works by lantern in the vestry after everyone else has wrapped in blankets. He turns the pages of the register, the dates and the names, the slow accumulation of the plaza's dead. A respectful player can get the real history of the grant from him here. An observant one notices that the register is also a map of possession, each grave a stake in the ground older than the Company.

- **Eufemia counting the fleece:** Eufemia checks the wool, the weight, the grease, the tags herself when she thinks no one important is looking. She is not checking for discipline. She is checking where the plaza's living has gone. If a player watches long enough, the whole practical economy of the comunidad becomes plain.

- **Nah-leh listening in the dark:** Nah-leh sits where he can hear three fires at once. He does not seem to be doing anything. Then he answers a question nobody has asked yet because he heard it coming from the way voices shifted. This is a good scene to remind the players that the quietest man in the party may have the best idea of what is building around them.

- **Strand with a bottle:** Strand drinks more than his camp would approve. Not enough to disgrace himself. Enough to confess the wrong ambition to the wrong listener. He wants the diggings as his own kingdom and he knows the kingdom is built on the same kind of paper the Company uses. A player who handles him well can either stiffen his spine or steer him toward ruin.

- **Tomás trying to be believed:** Tomás offers tobacco, a story, a sketch of the cañón, anything to build the smallest bridge of trust. He is trying to buy the right to speak without speaking. The more pleasant he seems, the more useful it is when the truth comes out.

- **Horse line at midnight:** A good horse line is a society. Animals stamp, blow, pull, settle, and spook in a chain. If the players spend time there, reward them with early warning in later trouble. Men who know horses well should never feel useless in this chapter.

- **A distant fire in the wrong place:** One player on watch sees a fire where no camp should be, up on the ridge. Then it goes out. No attack comes that night. The point of the scene is not immediate action. The point is to make sleep thin and judgment worse by morning.

- **A prayer over Celso:** One of the older Hispano women of the plaza says a few words over Celso that are not much of a prayer and not much of an accusation, but enough to remind the party that the dead do not stay abstract just because the suit keeps moving. If a player stays for it, that player learns Celso had a sister expecting him home before the shearing.

- **The spoiled flour barrel:** One barrel in the Company freight has gone damp low in the staves and somebody has turned it so the leak will not show at first glance. A player who notices it can push the Company's teamster, protect the plaza hands who will be blamed, or say nothing and remember the fraud for later use.

- **Cards with Tomás:** If Tomás is alive and not under hard guard, he tries to sit into a card game as if ordinary company might make him ordinary again. He loses attention faster than money. A patient player can get him talking sideways around the truth before fear drives him back into silence.

- **A horse gone lame for no clean reason:** One valuable horse comes up sore in a way that might be bad ground, bad luck, or quiet sabotage. This gives the party a chance to inspect tack, question the order of march, and begin suspecting that not every attack in this chapter arrives on horseback with a rifle in hand.

- **Nah-leh and the weather sign:** Nah-leh studies cloud, wind, bird movement, and the smell in the air while everyone else is still talking about men. If a player joins him, he offers one dry remark that proves he is tracking more than weather. It is a good way to shift attention from intrigue back to country before the country punishes them again.

- **Eufemia's plain ledger:** If the party stays long enough at the store or comes back by it, Eufemia can show them a few hard lines in her account book: flour prices, wool prices, freight hire, a debt called in too early. It is one of the cleanest ways in the chapter to show what the Company does to people who never see a courtroom.

## Truth Survives the Holder

No single NPC's death should end the chapter. Each truth the party needs has more than one way into the record. Use this table when a holder dies, runs, or is bought off before the party gets to him.

| If the holder dies or is lost... | The truth moves to... |
| --- | --- |
| Tomás Luján (the willows) | Celso's dying breath; Halladay's letters cached with Father Vigil; Nah-leh's memory of his cousin on the ridge |
| Aldous Pell (the false return) | Pell's partial survey notes in his room above the Old Abe; his draft affidavit; his letters to Hadley in Hadley's safe |
| Cornelius Hadley (the Company's hand) | The Ring lawyer Harlan Pike in Cimarron; a Company director wired from London; Oren Pell, who was never as separate as he claimed |
| Judge Vail (the bench) | The hearing collapses and the suit moves to the fall term or to the territorial supreme court in Santa Fe; or to the _Cimarron News_ and the court of public opinion |
| Ouida Beaubien (the grant language) | The original grant copy in the County archives at Taos; a transcript filed with the parish; her own notes, if her room is searched before the Company burns it |
| Strand (the gold camp's muscle) | The camp fragments into three smaller factions the party must deal with separately; or Ouida inherits the camp's claim |
| The field book itself | The line is still in the ground, in the blazes on the ponderosa, in Nah-leh's witnessed corner-marker, and in the plaza's grandfathers under the ridge |

The chapter is a knot of truths held by fallible people. Remove one person and the knot tightens elsewhere. Remove the wrong person and the knot tightens around the party.

## Pacing the Chapter

The broad rhythm is straightforward. The opening should spend time on arrival, hire, the supper, Celso's killing, and the first night raid. The middle should breathe long enough for the party to know the plaza, the camp, the suit, and the line before the cañón ride and the hearing begin tearing the chapter apart. The last movement should feel faster and meaner: the ride down to Cimarron, the hearing, one hard standoff, then the ride out with whatever paper and whatever enmity the party has earned. However long your table likes to take over a thing, do not let the opening pass without blood and suspicion, and do not let the ending arrive before the party has had to decide what is worth carrying out.

## Running the Major Combats

Each big fight here should feel different. If they all turn into men standing in open ground rolling `SHOOTIN'`, the chapter flattens. The sheep-camp raid should feel confused, all loose wool, darkness, crossed lines of fire, and men shouting names they cannot place. Any fight in the Elizabethtown saloon should feel cramped and loud, with the stamp mill's beat underneath and a back door that may or may not be watched. The cañón ambush, if it comes, should be all verticality and broken timber, with the high ground belonging to whoever got there first. The fight at the cairn, if the Company's riders reach the party on the ridge, should feel thin-aired and cold and consequential, because the book is in hand and the shooting is over the book. The last standoff in Cimarron should feel dangerous in a different way because there are witnesses, law nearby, and one last chance to stop before the whole town chooses a side.

## Reward, Reputation, and What the Party Carries Out

Do not pay the party only in cash. Cash matters, and Don Ignacio or Hadley should pay what they can if the party lives long enough to collect, but the true rewards are rougher than coin. A party may leave this chapter with Don Ignacio's blessing and the plaza's open door, Eufemia's ledger in their favor, Strand's rough friendship and a stake at Baldy, Father Vigil's cemetery register as a witness to their good name, Nah-leh's memory and a guide in the high country, a copied field book worth money or blackmail in Santa Fe, or Ouida's grant language and a claim of their own on the mineral. They should also leave carrying damage. A lame horse. A cough from a night in the wet willows. Bad press in Cimarron. One faction convinced they stole what was rightfully theirs. A dead friend whose sister now knows the party's names. The best New Mexico rewards come with dirt on them.

## Carrying the Chapter into the Sandbox

When this chapter closes, the party should have at least three living leads into the wider book. One may come from the comunidad, with Don Ignacio or Filomeno Baca sending for them when a land, water, or stock matter turns ugly elsewhere in the grant. One may come from paper, with Hadley's survivors, Oren Pell, or a Santa Fe banker wanting the surviving field book, its copies, or its originals. One may come from the gold camp, with Strand, Ouida, or a new prospector wanting help against claim-jumpers, royalty men, or the railroad. One may come from the high country, with Nah-leh offering warning, work, or one hard chance to prove earlier trust was not wasted. One may come from an enemy who lived. That is the proper ending here. Nobody is clean. Nobody is done. The country is still there when the smoke thins.

## Sandbox Exits

The ending should throw the party into the wider New Mexico book with open hooks, not a ribbon tied around the matter. Cimarron and the Maxwell Grant are strong if the party wants court follow-up, range pressure, and the long slow work of land-title law. Santa Fe fits if they kept paper worth money, blackmail, or investment, and if they want to pull at the Ring directly. Elizabethtown and Baldy suit a group that wants the mining camp, the boom-and-bust cycle, and the rough end of the territory's economy. Fort Union and the army country to the south fit if the party's choices brought federal attention down on the grant. The Sangre de Cristo high country and the Jicarilla and Ute ranges fit best if trust or betrayal with Nah-leh was the heart of play. Taos and the Río Arriba suit a party that has made friends among the Hispano communities and wants the long game of a people who have been here three centuries and intend to be here three more.

## GM Notes on Tone

Keep this chapter hard and specific.

Men should want cash, wool, gold, paper, water, graves, and authority. They should not speak like a theme statement. Nobody in the plaza says he is resisting colonial dispossession. He says the flour is six cents, his father is on the ridge, and the creek at that bend is eight inches deep.

The community is not a symbol of innocence and the Company is not a symbol of evil. The comunidad has its own cruelties: a sheep hand beaten for a bad night, a woman silenced at the table, a Jicarilla trader cheated at the store. The Company has its own decencies: Hadley pays his men on time and does not lie to them about the danger, and the court in Cimarron is, by the low standard of territorial courts, no worse than it has to be. The weight of the chapter is that decent people and indecent people are on both sides of the line, and the line itself is what is being fought over.

> Historical note: The Maxwell Land Grant fight ran in the courts and in the country for decades after `1873`. The grant was eventually confirmed by the U.S. Supreme Court in `1887` on terms that favored the Company. The Hispano communities inside the grant lost most of what they had held. The gold camp at Elizabethtown boomed and busted and is a ghost town now. The plaza of San Esteban in this chapter is fiction, but the plazas it stands for were real, and what happened to them is a matter of record.

The larger truth will show itself. It always does, in this country. The men who drew the false line are dead and the men who fought them are dead and the creek still runs shallow over gravel in the bend where the surveyor lay, and the creek does not care whose paper said what. That is enough.
