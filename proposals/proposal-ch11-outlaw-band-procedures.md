# Proposal: Chapter 11 Outlaw Band Procedures

**Status:** Draft planning proposal  
**Target:** `Tales of the Old West 2e`, Chapter 11 `Outlaws of the Old West`  
**Scope:** Proposal only. No manuscript integration yet.

## Purpose

Chapter 11 already does a good job establishing the shape of outlaw play:

- band scale
- gang leadership
- cohesion
- refuge and fence relationships
- horsestock
- wanted pressure
- hideout life
- gang trouble

What it does not yet do is tell the table how those parts actually resolve in play.

Right now the chapter gives the GM and players a framework of band state, but it is light on procedures:

- when to roll for the gang as a group
- which property applies to which kind of action
- how success or failure changes the band state
- how a gang-level result translates into consequences for the whole party

The design goal is therefore:

- keep the chapter’s current structure
- add a small number of clear outlaw-band procedures
- make the existing properties actionable in dice checks
- ensure outcomes produce visible changes to the whole gang, not just flavor text
- avoid turning outlaw play into a separate tactical subsystem

## Core Diagnosis

Chapter 11 currently defines the band as a living criminal body, but most of its rules are descriptive rather than procedural.

That creates three gaps:

1. The gang has values, but not enough explicit tests.
2. The values have meaning, but not enough trigger points.
3. The results can change the fiction, but not always the gang state.

In practice, that means the GM has to improvise too much at the exact moment the chapter should be most useful.

## Recommended Design Shape

The chapter should gain four linked procedures:

1. `Band Action Checks`
2. `Refuge and Fence Procedures`
3. `Travel, Pursuit, and Horsestock Procedures`
4. `Seasonal Gang Resolution`

These should be written as light procedural tools, not a deep subsystem.

The chapter should answer one simple question for each procedure:

- what is the gang trying to do?
- which band property matters?
- what roll is made?
- what happens on 0, 1, 2, or 3+ successes?

## Proposal A: Add Band Action Checks

This is the main missing piece.

### Function

A `Band Action Check` is a group-level roll used when the gang acts as a band rather than as isolated individuals.

Examples:

- slipping into or out of a settlement
- finding shelter, food, or help
- moving stolen goods through a fence
- recruiting new hands
- keeping the gang together after a rough score
- surviving a chase or hard ride
- holding a hideout under pressure

### Recommended Roll Structure

Use the leader’s appropriate attribute or skill as the base roll, then add or subtract dice from the relevant band property.

Examples:

- `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` for leadership, recruitment, and social pressure
- `NATURE` or `HAWKEYE` for scouting, moving country, and finding safe ground
- `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` for horsestock-related work
- `LIGHT-FINGERED` or `MAKIN'` for theft, concealment, and stolen-goods handling

The chapter should not invent a new dice engine.

It should simply define when a band-scale roll is called for and how band state modifies it.

### Recommended Outcome Scale

Use the chapter’s familiar success bands:

- `0 successes`
- `1 success`
- `2 successes`
- `3 or more successes`

That keeps the procedures aligned with the rest of the book.

### Suggested Outcome Pattern

For band-scale actions, the outcome should usually do one or more of these:

- advance the gang’s aim
- shift a band property up or down
- create heat or delay
- create a new complication for the next scene or season

## Proposal B: Make Refuge Procedural

`Refuge` is already one of the strongest ideas in chapter 11. It should become a direct play procedure.

### When To Roll

Roll when the gang needs a settlement to provide one of the following:

- shelter from a posse
- supplies
- medical help
- a quiet stable or hiding place
- local information
- temporary silence from law or merchants

### What The Roll Uses

Use `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` plus the current `Refuge` score.

### What The Roll Should Change

The result should affect:

- whether the gang gets the help
- whether the help is clean, costly, or compromised
- whether `Refuge` rises, holds, or falls
- whether the town becomes a new source of trouble

### Recommended Result Logic

- `0 successes`: the request fails and the settlement relationship worsens
- `1 success`: the gang gets help, but at a cost or under suspicion
- `2 successes`: the gang gets the help cleanly
- `3+ successes`: the gang gets extra value, and `Refuge` may improve

### Why This Matters

This makes towns matter in outlaw play for reasons beyond flavor.

It also gives the GM a clear way to answer:

- can the gang disappear here?
- can they resupply here?
- can they recover here?
- can they trust this place again?

## Proposal C: Make Fence Access Procedural

`Fence Access` needs a concrete conversion procedure so stolen property stops being an abstract note.

### When To Roll

Roll when the gang tries to turn:

- `Hot Goods` into cash
- `Hot Stock` into cash or usable trade
- dangerous loot into a usable relationship

### What The Roll Uses

Use `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'`, modified by:

- `Fence Access`
- the town’s `Mercantile` condition or equivalent local standing
- the heat on the goods
- whether the goods are stock, goods, or cash

### What The Roll Should Change

The result should determine:

- whether the goods sell
- how much value is recovered
- whether the fence stays safe
- whether the gang gains heat, delay, or a better connection

### Recommended Result Logic

- `0 successes`: no deal, or the fence becomes a problem
- `1 success`: partial value, slow value, or a bad bargain
- `2 successes`: fair value
- `3+ successes`: better-than-fair value and a useful side effect

### Why This Matters

This is the missing bridge between robbery and campaign economy.

Without it, the chapter has loot categories but not a strong reason to care about them.

## Proposal D: Make Horsestock Procedural

`Horsestock` is the band’s mobility clock. It should directly affect movement, pursuit, and escape.

### When To Roll

Roll when the gang:

- makes a hard ride
- escapes pursuit
- moves camp under pressure
- survives winter travel
- loses mounts in a score
- tries to stay ahead of enemies over rough country

### What The Roll Uses

Use `NATURE`, `ANIMAL HANDLIN'`, or a relevant leadership roll, modified by `Horsestock`.

### What The Roll Should Change

The result should determine:

- whether the gang escapes cleanly
- whether they leave sign behind
- whether horses are lost, wounded, or exhausted
- whether the gang’s next move is delayed or endangered

### Recommended Result Logic

- `0 successes`: the gang loses ground, horses, or safety
- `1 success`: the gang escapes, but at a cost
- `2 successes`: the gang stays ahead
- `3+ successes`: the gang gains a tactical advantage, such as time, distance, or a false trail

### Why This Matters

This makes horses an actual campaign asset rather than a note on a band sheet.

## Proposal E: Add A Seasonal Gang Resolution

The chapter already uses the `Turn of the Season`. It should use that moment to resolve gang-wide consequences.

### Seasonal Procedure

At each `Turn of the Season`, the gang should resolve:

- upkeep
- hideout condition
- one hideout activity
- any major gang trouble
- any shift in `Refuge`, `Fence Access`, or `Horsestock`
- any wanted escalation or reduction

### Suggested Seasonal Questions

- Did the gang make enough money to hold together?
- Did the hideout remain safe?
- Did the gang rely on a town, a fence, or a wrangler enough to strengthen or weaken that link?
- Did the gang ride hard enough to cost horses?
- Did the gang’s reputation make pursuit worse?

### Why This Matters

It gives outlaw play a clean rhythm:

1. score
2. consequence
3. hideout management
4. seasonal pressure

That is the backbone the chapter is currently missing.

## Proposal F: Tie Results Back To The Whole Party

The chapter should explicitly say that gang-scale outcomes are not private to one character.

### Recommended Effects

Band procedures should be able to affect:

- Cohesion
- Wanted
- Refuge
- Fence Access
- Horsestock
- notable member Loyalty
- the hideout’s safety
- the gang’s ability to take the next score

### Principle

If the gang succeeds or fails as a group, the outcome should change the group.

That is the key distinction between a character action and outlaw-band play.

## Proposed Chapter Insertion Points

The strongest place to add these procedures is after the existing band-state sections, before `Scores and Criminal Work`.

Suggested order:

1. band scale
2. leadership
3. cohesion
4. refuge
5. fence access
6. horsestock
7. band action checks
8. hideout life
9. scores
10. wanted
11. consequences

That keeps the chapter readable and lets the procedural section sit directly on top of the properties it uses.

## What Should Not Be Added

The proposal is intentionally narrow.

Do not add:

- a tactical mass-combat layer
- a separate outlaw economy
- a second reputation system
- detailed daily supply accounting
- stronghold management complexity
- individual bookkeeping for every ordinary hand

The chapter should remain campaign-facing, not simulation-heavy.

## Bottom Line

Chapter 11 is already strong on structure and atmosphere.

It needs one more layer:

- explicit band-scale checks
- clear property-to-roll mappings
- outcome bands that alter gang state
- seasonal procedures that turn the framework into a usable outlaw campaign loop

That would make the chapter do what it is currently implying but not yet fully executing: support a real outlaw-band simulation in play.
