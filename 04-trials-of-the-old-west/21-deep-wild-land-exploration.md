## Chapter 21 - Deep Wild Land Exploration

> _THE TRAIL ENDS WHERE THE MAP ENDS, AND THE MAP ENDS SOONER THAN THE COUNTRY DOES. PAST THE TRAIL IS THE WILD LAND — THE RIVER NO STAGE FOLLOWS, THE TIMBER NO AX HAS TOUCHED, THE RIDGE NO SURVEYOR HAS STAKED. THE MEN WHO GO THERE COME BACK CHANGED OR THEY DO NOT COME BACK._
>
> — WEBB CALDER, TO A DUDE WHO ASKED WHAT WAS PAST THE OPEN RANGE

### The Wild Land and Its Work

The corebook's Traveling table covers the trail and the road — the country the wagon, the stage, and the rail have opened. The trade chapter covers the freighting outfit moving goods along the known ways. Neither covers the country past the known ways: the river no steamboat runs, the timber no logger has cut, the ridge no trail crosses. This is the **deep wild land**, and the party that goes there — to trap, to prospect, to survey, to hunt, to find a lost column, or simply to get out of the county's reach for a season — is a party traveling in a different medium than the road.

This chapter builds three modes of deep-wild travel — **by water, through forest, and over mountain** — as variants on the same problem: the party is moving through country that has no table row, no way-station, and no relief, and the engine must decide whether it arrives, what it costs, and what it finds. Each mode reuses the operation engine (the outlaw chapter's Progress-versus-Trouble track) with its own stages, abilities, and Trouble. Together they give the wild land the same weight the road already has.

> _Calder watched the cornishman read the river from the bank, the way the cornishman read the rock underground — by the look of the surface and the lie of the current and the color of the water where it ran over what was underneath. The river was the only road into the country they were going into, and the river was not a road. The river was a thing with its own opinion about who used it._
>
> "We run the canoe to the fork and line the loaded one through the rapids above it."
>
> "You've run this river."
>
> "I've run rivers. This one I've read." The cornishman did not look up from the water. "The fork is two days if the current holds and three if it doesn't. Past the fork is the timber, and the timber is where the map stops and the country begins."
>
> "And past the timber?"
>
> "Past the timber is why we're going, Webb. You don't pay a man to read a river to a place he's already been."
>
> _Calder did not answer that, because it was true. The wild land was the country the map did not cover, and the men who went there were the men the map could not help. The engine should make the going cost what it cost, and the finding worth what it was worth, and the country indifferent to both, the way the country was indifferent to everything except itself._

### The Wild-Land Expedition

A deep-wild expedition — up a river, through a timber, over a divide — is an operation, scaled to its length and severity. The **Progress** is the miles or the reach achieved; the **Trouble** is the country's resistance — the rapids, the blowdown, the snowfield, the lost pack animal, the wrong turning, the weather (the weather chapter), the simple attrition of bodies and gear against a country that does not care.

| Expedition Scale | Progress Needed | Trouble Limit | The Expedition |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `The Foray` | `4` | `3` | A week or two into the wild land and back — a trapping foray, a prospecting trip, a search for lost stock |
| `The Deep Trip` | `6` | `4` | A month or more into country the trail does not reach — a survey, a hunt, an expedition to a known destination |
| `The Long Journey` | `8` | `5` | A season in the deep wild — a crossing of a range, a descent of an unknown river, a flight into country the law cannot follow |

Each mode of travel — water, forest, mountain — sets the stages and the abilities. The GM chooses the mode by the country the party is in, or runs a mixed expedition (river to the fork, timber past the fork, the divide at the end) as a sequence of stages, each rolled in its own mode.

### By Water

The river is the oldest road in the West, and the one the rail and the stage never replaced. The steamboat works the navigable lower rivers (the Missouri, the Mississippi, the Red, the Colorado's slow stretches), and the canoe, the keelboat, the raft, and the mackinaw work the waters the steamboat cannot reach. Travel by water is faster downstream than up, cheaper than a wagon for heavy loads, and the only way into country the trails do not cross.

#### The Steamboat

The steamboat is the river's railroad — a mobile crossroads, a trade hub, a gamble of boiler and snag. A party booking passage on a steamboat is buying speed and relative safety; a party running one is running a mobile saloon and freight outfit that can also blow up. The steamboat's travel row on the corebook's Traveling table is `20-30 miles per Shift` downstream, `10-15` upstream (against the current), halved on a shallow or snag-choked river.

The steamboat's Trouble is the river's: the snag that opens a hull, the sandbar that grounds the boat for a day, the boiler that bursts (a non-typical-harm event, the corebook's fire/explosion rules), the ice that closes the channel, the bandit gang that learned the boat carries a payroll. A steamboat is also the river's saloon — the gambling, the drinking, the crossroads of every faction and trader on the river — and the saloon chapter's keeper and crossroads frames apply to the boat's cabin as they apply to any bar.

#### The Canoe, the Keelboat, the Raft

The smaller craft work the waters the steamboat cannot. They are the deep-wild party's river transport, and their travel rows extend the Traveling table where the corebook stops.

| Craft | Upstream | Downstream | Portage | Load |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Canoe (bark or dugout) | `6-8 miles/Shift` (poling, lining) | `15-25 miles/Shift` | light — two men can carry it | `600-800 lbs` |
| Mackinaw / flatboat | `4-6 miles/Shift` (rowed, cordelled) | `15-20 miles/Shift` | heavy — often one-way, broken up at the destination | `2,000-4,000 lbs` |
| Keelboat | `3-5 miles/Shift` (cordelled, poled, sailed) | `12-18 miles/Shift` | very heavy — a crew's work to portage | `10,000-15,000 lbs` |
| Raft | none (a raft goes down) | `8-12 miles/Shift` | none — a raft is disposable | whatever it will float |

A party traveling upstream is working for every mile — poling, lining (walking the craft up along the bank with a line), cordelling (the same, with a larger crew). A party traveling downstream is riding the current, and the current's Trouble (the rapids, the snag, the rock garden) is the price of the speed. A portage — carrying the craft and its load past an unnavigable stretch — is a `LABOR` operation in itself, a day or more of work for every rapid lined around.

#### River Stages

| Stage of the River | The Problem | Likely Ability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reading the river | Read the current, the rapids, the channels, the safe line | `HAWKEYE`, `NATURE` |
| The rapids | Run or line the craft through the broken water | `OPERATE`, `LABOR`, `RESILIENCE` |
| The portage | Carry the craft and the cargo past the unnavigable | `LABOR`, `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` (if pack animals help) |
| The camp | Find the eddy, the landing, the dry ground for the night | `NATURE`, `HAWKEYE` |
| The river's people | Pass the nations whose river it is, and the traders on it | `PRESENCE`, `INSIGHT` (the borderlands and the native-material-culture knowledge) |

The river's Trouble is the water itself — a capsize in a rapid is a swim that can drown (the corebook's drowning rules), lose the cargo, and end the expedition. The river is faster than a horse on the downstream and slower than a walk on the upstream, and the engine should make both felt.

### Through Forest

The forest is the country the axe has not opened, and it is the slowest going in the West. The party on foot or on horseback through uncut timber is a party moving a mile or two a day, climbing over blowdown, cutting a path, leading horses through country that fights every step. The forest is also the logger's country (the logging chapter) and the trapper's (the fur-trade chapter), and a deep-forest expedition is often one of those trades' work.

#### Forest Travel

Forest travel extends the Traveling table where the corebook's `hard terrain` row stops short of the deep timber.

| Forest Type | Per Shift (mounted) | Per Shift (afoot) | The Trouble |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Open pine (park-like) | `4-6 miles` | `8-10 miles` | low — the grass between the trees, the easy ground |
| Second growth / mixed | `2-4 miles` | `5-7 miles` | moderate — blowdown, deadfall, the tight stand |
| Old growth / dense | `1-2 miles` | `3-5 miles` | high — cutting a path for the pack string, the horse-busting deadfall |
| Burn / blowdown | `a mile, if that` | `2-3 miles` | extreme — the forest knocked flat, climbing over the wreck |

A mounted party in the deep forest is often a dismounted party leading the horses — the horse cannot pass what a man can climb over, and the pack string must be led, lifted, and coaxed through the blowdown a foot at a time. The engine should make the horse a cost as often as a help in the deep timber, which is the truth the mountain men knew and the dude did not.

#### Forest Stages

| Stage of the Forest | The Problem | Likely Ability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reading the forest | Find the game trail, the ridge, the way through the timber | `NATURE`, `HAWKEYE` |
| The cutting | Open a path for the string, axe-work, the deadfall cleared | `LABOR`, `MAKIN'` |
| The blowdown | Climb, lift, and coax the horses over the fallen timber | `LABOR`, `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` |
| The lost | Hold the bearing in country with no landmark, no view, no trail | `HAWKEYE`, `NATURE` |
| The forest's people | Pass the nations whose forest it is, and the animals in it | `INSIGHT`, `NATURE` (the wildlife, the predators, the sign) |

The forest's Trouble is the going itself — slow, hard, and a party spent in a day what a party on the trail spent in a week. The forest is also the season's country — the deadfall of a storm, the fire-scar of a past year, the snow that doubles the labor in winter — and the weather chapter and the winter chapter apply their pressure to the timber the way they apply it to the open range.

### Over Mountain

The mountain is the country that stands on end, and the party that crosses a divide is a party climbing into worse air, worse weather, and worse going with every hundred feet of elevation. The mountain's Trouble is the altitude (the shortness of breath, the altitude sickness), the exposure (the weather chapter's worst, on a ridge with no cover), the loose rock, the snowfield in July, and the pass that may or may not be open.

#### Mountain Travel

Mountain travel extends the Traveling table with the elevation modifier the flat-country rows do not carry.

| Mountain Travel | Per Shift | The Trouble |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Mountain trail, good | `4-6 miles` (the trail trades distance for grade) | moderate — the ledge, the rockfall, the horse that will not cross |
| Mountain trail, poor | `2-4 miles` | high — the washout, the snowfield, the lost trail |
| Cross-country (off-trail) | `1-3 miles` | extreme — the scramble, the loose rock, the cliff, the route-finding |
| The divide (the pass) | the pass is the operation, not a row | the weather, the snow, the altitude, the wrong turning |

The mountain's great modifier is the **elevation** — for every `2,000 feet` above the party's start, apply `-1 die` to `RESILIENCE` and `LABOR` rolls (the altitude, the air, the effort), and the weather chapter's conditions worsen by one step (the clear day on the flat is the storm on the ridge). The snowfield does not care that it is July; the pass does not care that the party needs to cross; the mountain does not care about the party at all.

#### Mountain Stages

| Stage of the Mountain | The Problem | Likely Ability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reading the mountain | Find the pass, the route, the line through the rock | `NATURE`, `HAWKEYE`, `BOOKLEARNIN'` (a map, a survey) |
| The climb | The grade, the loose rock, the horse on the ledge | `LABOR`, `ANIMAL HANDLIN'`, `RESILIENCE` |
| The pass | The weather window, the snow, the decision to push or to wait | `NATURE`, `INSIGHT` (the read of the weather), `RESILIENCE` |
| The altitude | The air, the sickness, the exhaustion at height | `RESILIENCE`, `DOCTORIN'` |
| The descent | The loose rock, the bad step, the ledges down | `LABOR`, `MOVE` |

The mountain's Trouble is the consequence of a wrong decision in a way the river's and the forest's Trouble is not — the wrong pass in the wrong weather is a death the engine should not soften, and a party that turns back from a pass it cannot cross is a party that made the right read, which the engine should reward with survival and the story the county tells of the expedition that came back when it did not have to.

### The Wild Land's Find

The wild land's payoff is not always a destination. A deep-wild expedition may find a prospect (the mining chapter), a fur-bearer's rich country (the fur-trade chapter), a route the railroad could use, a lost column, a nation's hunting ground, or simply the country itself, which is its own finding. The GM should let the wild land pay in what the wild land has — game, fur, mineral, view, solitude, danger — and let the party's `HAWKEYE` and `NATURE` rolls determine what is found and what is brought back, the way the prospector's rolls determine what the ground gives.

A wild-land find worth staking (a prospect, a meadow, a pass) is the seed of the next chapter's work — the mining chapter's claim, the cattle chapter's range, the trade chapter's route. The wild land is where the county's future comes from, and the party that goes there is the party that finds it first, which is worth what it is worth and costs what it costs.

### The Wild Land and the Other Rules

The wild land is the country past the rest of the book's reach.

- **The corebook's Traveling table** is the road; this chapter is the country past the road. The two share the engine's travel logic and extend it where the table stops.
- **The weather chapter** sets the wild land's conditions, harder than the settled country's — the storm on the river, the blowdown in the forest, the blizzard on the divide.
- **The sickness chapter** and the conditions (`Starving`, `Dehydrated`, `Exhausted`, `Freezing`) are the wild land's daily pressure — the party far from the doctor and the way-station pays the full cost of every condition the engine imposes.
- **The mining, fur-trade, and logging chapters** are the wild land's trades — the prospect, the trapline, the timber cruise are the reasons a party goes into the deep country, and this chapter is the going.

The trail ends where the map ends, and the country past the map is the deep wild land. A chapter that gives the river, the forest, and the mountain their own modes of travel and Trouble gives the party the country the road does not reach, and lets the engine make the going cost what it cost and the finding worth what it was worth, in the country that does not care about the party except as another thing the weather and the ground and the water can take.
