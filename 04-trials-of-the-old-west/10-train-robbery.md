## Chapter 10 - Train Robbery and the Iron Horse

> _SHE WILL DO WHAT YOU TELL HER. THAT IS THE MACHINE. THE MEN BEHIND HER ARE THE ONES YOU HAVE TO WATCH, AND THE WIRE IS FASTER THAN YOU ARE._
>
> — DUTCH MILLER, TO A FIREMAN WHO THOUGHT THE THROTTLE WAS THE HARD PART

### The Iron Horse and Its Trade

The railroad is the new thing in the West, and the new thing is money on wheels. A single train carries a payroll, a clean shirt, a family's household, a strongbox of the express company's paper, and a dozen witnesses who will remember every face they saw. The men who rob trains are the men who have decided that the money is worth the wire that will follow them — and the wire is faster than any horse, and the railroad remembers every loss until the end of its corporate life.

Bring the iron horse fully onstage when the train is the score, the set-piece, or the power that will answer after the smoke clears. A robbery uses operation rules scaled to the take; the railroad answers as a faction with schedules, detectives, reward money, political pull, and a memory longer than any posse's.

It also folds in the stagecoach, the iron horse's smaller cousin, and the road-agent holdup, which is the train robbery's older, poorer relative. The two share a shape: a moving target, a take in a lockbox, and a wire that can ruin you after.

A quick road holdup can stay one roll and a flourish. A train robbery begins when the party cases the run, buys the inside man, plans the stop, and accepts that the company will send detectives and the bench will swear warrants before the season turns.

> _Miller saw them at the obstruction on the curve a half-second before the Johnson bar would have done the work for them. Three men, two rifles, a red lantern sunk in the weeds so a man in a hurry would have read it as a stop signal and a man who knew the road would have known there was no signal at that curve. Miller was a man who knew the road. He dumped the air and reached under the coal for the shotgun and did not blow the whistle, because the whistle was for the wire and the wire was what they wanted to stop._
>
> The fireman had the shovel half up. "What in the hell, Dutch."
>
> "That's a stop," Miller said. "Keep your head down."
>
> "That ain't a stop. There's no signal at this curve."
>
> "I know it," Miller said. "That's why we're stopping."
>
> _The express messenger was already in the car with the door dogged. The passengers were beginning to look up from their papers. Miller stood in the cab with the shotgun across his thigh and waited to see whether the three men were stupid or just slow, because the difference would decide the next four minutes and possibly the rest of his life._

### The Anatomy of a Train

A train in the 1870s is a chain of cars behind a locomotive, and each car is a different problem. The GM and the planning robbers should know what is in the chain before anyone rolls.

| Car | What It Carries | What It Is to a Robber |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `Locomotive` | The engineer and the fireman, the throttle, the air brake, the coal | The lever that stops the train; the men who know the wire |
| `Tender` | The fuel and the water, and often the engineer's shotgun | The gap between the engine and the cars; the place a boarder lands |
| `Express Car` | The express company's strongboxes, the mail, the way-bills | The take; the strongest door on the train, and the messenger behind it |
| `Baggage Car` | The passengers' trunks, the freight, the coffins, the livestock | A take if the gang has time and a fence; a slow one to crack |
| `Passenger Coach` | The people, their pockets, their watches, their witness | The easy money and the worst Trouble; every face a wire |
| `Sleeper` | The well-off, the papers, the strongbox a banker is carrying | The richer take and the more dangerous witnesses |
| `Caboose` | the crew, the conductor's books, the rear guard | The back door; the car that watches the trail behind |

A mixed freight-and-passenger run — the kind that stops at Sandoval's way-station platform three times a week — might be locomotive, tender, one express car combined with the baggage, one coach, and a caboose. A through-express on the main line is locomotive, tender, express, baggage, two or three coaches, a sleeper, and a caboose, with an express messenger who carries a shotgun and a strongbox that is bolted to the floor. The payroll train for a mine or a railroad camp is an engine, a tender, an express car with a guard, and one coach full of miners going home; it is shorter, meaner, and better guarded than its size suggests.

### Stopping the Train

A moving train is a wall of iron and momentum, and a horse cannot catch one for more than a few miles. The robbery begins with the stop. There are three honest ways to do it, and one desperate one, and the choice sets the first stage of the operation.

#### The False Signal

The railroad runs on signals — lamps, flags, torpedoes strapped to the rail, the order board at a station. A robber who can present the right signal at the right place can stop a train the way the railroad itself would. This is the cleanest stop, when it works.

The acting hand makes a `HAWKEYE` or `OPERATE` roll to set the signal correctly and in time, with a bonus die if the gang has an inside man at a station or a stolen signal flag, and a penalty die if the engineer knows the road well enough to smell a wrong signal. Miller, in the vignette, smells it; that is the engineer's job. On a success, the train stops in confusion and the gang has the advantage of the fiction. On a failure, the engineer dumps the air anyway but he has seen the trick, and the express messenger dogs his door before the gang is on the platform.

#### The Obstruction

A rail pulled up, a pile of ties on the track, a horse or a wagon left across the line at a blind curve. The engineer sees the obstruction and stops, or he does not. This is the brutal stop, and it carries the risk of a derailment, which kills everyone on the train including the take.

The acting hand makes a `LABOR` roll to set the obstruction in a way that stops the train without derailing it, with `OPERATE` as the ability if the gang is working a removed rail or a broken fishplate. On a failure, the train derails; roll on the **Derailment Table** below for who lives and what is left of the take. A derailment is the gang's worst outcome short of the rope: it kills the witnesses the gang did not mean to kill, it brings the railroad's full weight down on the county, and it turns a robbery into a murder charge for every hand the marshals can name.

| D6 | Derailment Result |
| --- | --- |
| `1-2` | The engine stays upright; the cars behind jackknife. The express car is among the wreckage, and the strongbox may be cracked open or buried. `D6` passengers and crew dead. The wire goes out within the hour. |
| `3-4` | The engine rolls. The engineer and the fireman are dead or pinned. The express car is on its side; the take is reachable but so are the bodies. `2D6` dead. |
| `5` | The firebox ruptures. The wreck burns. The take goes with it, and so does anyone left in the coaches. `3D6` dead. The gang may salvage the strongbox if it was thrown clear. |
| `6` | Catastrophic. The train is a field of wreckage and fire. `4D6+` dead. The take is ash. The railroad offers a Federal bounty for the gang. |

A derailment makes the brutal stop cost what it should cost. A gang that pulls a rail is a gang that has accepted the dice, and the dice do not care about the gang's intentions.

#### The Sabotage

A blown bridge, a burned trestle, a cut telegraph wire worked into the stop. This is the premeditated version of the obstruction, done days ahead with black powder and a man who knows the road. It is the stop of a `Big Operation`, and it uses the `MAKIN'` ability (with the `Engineer` talent) for the placement and the charge, and `NATURE` or `HAWKEYE` to choose the ground where the stop is in the gang's favor. A sabotage that fails is Trouble, not a clean miss; a sabotage that succeeds too well is a derailment, read on the table above.

The sabotage is also the gang's chance to cut the wire ahead of the robbery, which is the single most important thing a train robber can do. The telegraph runs alongside the track, and a wire cut at the right pole — and the right dummy message sent from a stolen key — can buy the gang the hours it takes to be a county away before the company knows the train is late. Cutting the wire is a `LIGHT-FINGERED` or `OPERATE` roll, with the `Engineer` or `Lockpicker` talent helping where the gang has one. A failed wire cut means the company knows, and the company's detectives travel ahead by wire faster than any horse.

#### The Boarding

The desperate stop, and the one the dime novels love. The gang rides alongside the rear of the train at a gallop and boards from horseback, taking the caboose and working forward. This is the only stop that does not require the train to slow — and it is the most dangerous, because a man between a horse at speed and an iron car at speed is a man the physics will write off.

A boarding is a `MOVE` or `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` roll to make the jump, with a penalty die for every ten miles an hour the train is making over a walk. On a failure, the boarder is down — a fall, a crushed limb, a man under the wheels — and the gang is one short and has made noise the crew heard. The boarding uses the **chase range ladder** from Chapter 6 of the corebook to track the boarders' progress through the train: the caboose is the entry, the express car is the target, and each car between is a range category the boarders must close against the resistance of the crew and any armed passengers.

### The Casing Layer

A heist's pleasure is half in the plan, and the rules should reward the plan, not just narrate it. The **Casing Layer** is the gang's pre-Operation intelligence-gathering phase: the Shifts before the job, where each gang member takes one **casing action** to learn the run and bank an advantage for the stages to come.

The gang is gathering clues _about the job_, and the clues become dice. A gang that cases well enters the Operation with a pool of banked dice that reward the plan; a gang that improvises enters the Operation bare and pays for it in steeper Trouble.

#### Casing Actions

Each Shift of preparation, each gang member takes **one** casing action. Each successful casing action grants the gang a **casing die** — a bonus die banked for a specific stage of the Operation (the signal, the express car, the wire, the get clear). A casing die is spent on the stage it was banked for, adding its pool to that stage's roll.

| The Casing Action | The Pool | What It Banks |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Case the run** | `HAWKEYE` | Learn the schedule, the crew, the habitual stops, the grades where the train slows. Banks a casing die for the stop or the get-clear. |
| **Read the railroad** | `INSIGHT` | The wire traffic, the detective's pattern, the mail car's contents, the company's mood. Banks a casing die for the wire stage or the Heat Track (below). |
| **Turn the inside man** | `LIGHT-FINGERED` or `PRESENCE` | Turn the clerk, sweet-talk the fireman, buy the agent. Banks a casing die for the express car or the signal, depending on the inside man's position. |
| **Work the wire** | `OPERATE` or `LIGHT-FINGERED` | Cut the wire ahead of time, or plant the dummy message (the wire chapter, Ch.16). Banks a casing die for the wire stage, or removes the wire as a Trouble source for the whole job if fully cased. |
| **Scout the ground** | `NATURE` or `HAWKEYE` | Read the country the gang will ride into — the crossings, the blind ground, the pursuit's likely path. Banks a casing die for the get-clear and the `Hard Ride and Flight` after. |

A gang typically takes **two to four Shifts** of casing before a job. Each casing action is rolled on the standard success ladder: `0` reveals nothing (and may alert the railroad — see below), `1` banks one casing die, `2+` banks a casing die and grants `+1` to its face when spent. A gang member may case the same aspect twice to bank two dice for the same stage, or spread the casing across stages to cover the job's several problems.

**The risk of casing.** Each failed casing action (`0`) raises the railroad's awareness — roll a `D6`: on a `1`, the railroad's detective or agent notices the interest, and the gang's **Heat** (below) climbs one step before the job even begins. A gang that cases clumsily is a gang that has told the railroad a job is coming, and the railroad listens.

The Casing Layer makes the plan matter at the table. A gang that cases the run, turns the inside man, and works the wire enters the Operation with a fistful of banked dice that a gang that rides up at dusk and improvises lacks. The unprepared gang meets the express messenger cold and the wire hot.

### The Robbery as Operation

Once the train is stopped (or boarded), the robbery itself is an operation, using the outlaw chapter's Progress-versus-Trouble track. The scale is set by the run and the take.

| Operation Scale | Progress Needed | Trouble Limit | The Run |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `Wayside Hold` | `4` | `3` | A mixed run at a wayside station; one express box, a few passengers, a sleepy crew |
| `Payroll Run` | `6` | `4` | A payroll or specie shipment; an armed express messenger, a guard or two, a coach full of witnesses |
| `Through-Express` | `8` | `5` | The main-line express; a hardened messenger, Pinkerton or company detectives aboard, the wire running |

**Progress** is securing the take and getting clear. **Trouble** is everything that makes the robbery a robbery instead of a withdrawal: the express messenger behind his dogged door, the armed passenger who draws, the conductor who slips out the back, the wire getting out, a face recognized, the engineer who is less afraid of the gang than of his company.

| Stage of the Robbery | The Problem | Likely Ability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Holding the crew | Keep the engineer at the throttle or away from it; keep the fireman from the wire | `PRESENCE`, `SHOOTIN'` |
| The express car | Get the door, the messenger, the strongbox; the lock or the messenger's nerve | `LIGHT-FINGERED`, `PRESENCE`, `SHOOTIN'` |
| The passengers | The take from the pockets, and the witnesses — rob them, bind them, or leave them | `PERFORMIN'`, `LIGHT-FINGERED`, `PRESENCE` |
| The wire | Keep the wire from going out, or accept that it will and ride hard | `OPERATE`, `LIGHT-FINGERED` |
| The get clear | Be off the train and mounted before the posse or the wire brings pursuit | `ANIMAL HANDLIN'`, `MOVE`, `NATURE` |

Each stage is tagged Safe / Risky / Desperate as the outlaw chapter tags a score. Walking the length of a stopped through-express with the express messenger dogged behind a strong door and a coach full of armed passengers is Desperate. Lifting the way-bills from a sleeping wayside station at midnight is Safe.

#### The Express Messenger

The express messenger is the robbery's central NPC, and he deserves a moment of thought. He is a company man, well-paid to sit in a steel car with a shotgun and a strongbox and not open the door for anyone the company has not authorized. Some are brave. Some are not. The gang's choice is whether to talk him out (`PRESENCE`, `PERFORMIN'`, the threat of fire or dynamite), to crack the door and the box by force (`LIGHT-FINGERED` on the lock, `MAKIN'` or `OPERATE` with the right tools or the wrong ones), or to accept that the box is beyond reach and take what else the train offers.

A messenger who opens the door under threat has done his company a cost-benefit analysis the company will fire him for, and he will say so on the witness stand later. A messenger who holds the door is the robbery's worst Trouble, because the time it takes to crack him is the time the wire uses to bring the pursuit. The gang that shoots a messenger to open a door has turned a robbery into a murder, and the federal mail, if there is any in the car, has turned it into a hanging offense for every hand the marshals can name.

#### The Mail

The mail is the third rail of train robbery. A train carrying U.S. mail is under federal protection, and robbing it is a federal crime that the marshals and the company pursue with a reach no county crime earns. If the express car holds mailbags — and it often does, on a mixed run — the gang's `Wanted` rises to `Federal` on the mail alone, regardless of the take. A gang that means to keep its `Wanted` at `Regional` must know which runs carry mail and avoid them, or must leave the mailbags untouched in the car even as they take the strongbox. The wire and the mail are the two reasons a clean train robbery is harder than a clean bank job.

#### Example: The 9:14 at Cimarron Curve

The gang spends two Shifts casing the job. One rider **reads the railroad** and banks a casing die for the express car. Another **works the wire** and succeeds, banking a casing die for the wire stage. A third fails to turn the inside man, and the GM rolls for awareness; the railroad does not notice, but the failed action gives the gang no help.

The robbery is a `Medium` operation (`6` Progress, `4` Trouble). The false signal stops the train cleanly. The gang spends the express-car casing die on the messenger and wins the exchange, marking Progress without killing him. Then the conductor slips away. The gang spends the wire casing die to keep the message from going out, but the roll fails; Trouble rises, and the wire will bring pursuit by sundown.

The gang gets the strongbox and clears at `6` Progress and `3` Trouble. Heat climbs because a train was robbed, and climbs again because the wire carried enough description for the railroad's file. The take is real, but so is the next job's shadow.

### The Stagecoach and the Road Agent

The stagecoach is the iron horse's older, poorer cousin, and the road-agent holdup is the train robbery's ancestor. A stage runs a fixed route on a rutted road at six miles an hour, carrying passengers, mail, and a way-bill box of express paper or a strongbox of the company's specie. It is slower, softer, and more easily stopped than a train, and its take is correspondingly smaller.

A stage holdup is a `Small` operation (`4` Progress, `3` Trouble), run with the same procedure as a wayside train hold. The stop is easier — a log across the road, a rifle aimed at the driver, a steep grade the horses cannot hurry up — and the Trouble is correspondingly lighter, until the stage is a mail stage, at which point the federal reach applies exactly as it does to the train. The shotgun messenger beside the driver is the stage's version of the express car's guard, and he is the holdup's central problem in the same way: talk him down, shoot him, or accept his terms.

The stage is also the passenger's experience of overland travel, and the party that rides the stage rather than robbing it is using the corebook's Traveling table with the stagecoach's row. A stage attack from the passenger's side is a `Standoff or Siege` (the justice chapter) or a chase (the corebook), depending on whether the driver holds the road or breaks for it.

### The Railroad as a Faction

The railroad is a faction, and it is the faction most likely to ruin a train robber's season. Define it as you would any faction: name, purpose (_build the line, protect the freight, keep the schedule, return the shareholder's dividend_), means (_the wire, the detectives, the company's lawyers, the federal mail's reach_), reach (_Regional, pressing Federal through the mail and the land grants_), and Standing toward the party, which begins at `0` and drops by two steps the first time the gang robs a train and stays low until the gang does the railroad a service of equal weight.

The railroad's Faction Roll each season is how its memory acts. A `Pressure` result means the company's detectives have closed a town against the gang, or leaned on a fence, or put a man on the platform at the way-station. A `Bold Move` result means the company has sworn federal warrants, or brought in the Pinkertons, or leaned on the bench to set an example. A gang that robs the same railroad twice is a gang the railroad has decided to break, and the railroad's memory is longer than any horse.

The wire is the railroad's great advantage and the gang's great enemy. The gang that cannot cut the wire, or send the dummy message, is a gang the company tracks in real time. Treat the wire as a standing modifier on the gang's `Hard Ride and Flight` after a train job: if the wire got out, the pursuit begins at `Regional` and climbs toward `Federal` as the company's wires ahead of the gang assemble the picture. If the gang cut the wire or sent the dummy, the pursuit begins at `Local` and the gang has the head start the wire would have stolen.

### The Heat Track

The railroad's `Standing` drops when the gang robs it, but `Standing` is the county's general regard — it resets, it softens, it can be repaired with a service. The railroad's **memory** is different. The railroad keeps a file, and the file grows with every train job in its territory, and the file does not soften. Track the railroad's memory as a **Heat gauge** (`0`-`5`), distinct from `Wanted` and from `Standing`. Heat makes the railroad's pursuit escalate across jobs rather than reset after each score.

| The Heat Gauge | The Railroad's Response |
| --- | --- |
| `0` | **Cold.** The railroad has no file on the gang, or the file is closed. Jobs are cased and run at ease. |
| `1–2` | **Warm.** The railroad has noticed. The company's local detective reads the patterns; a rider watches the way-station platforms. Casing actions face a `-1` pool (the railroad is alert); `Wanted` after a job begins at `Regional`. |
| `3` | **Hot.** The railroad assigns a **dedicated detective** — a recurring NPC with a name and a file, who appears on the gang's trail across sessions. Casing actions face a `-1` pool; the detective adds `+1` Trouble die to any job in the territory; `Wanted` begins at `Federal` if mail was involved. |
| `4` | **Burning.** The railroad hires the Pinkertons or petitions for federal marshals. The detective is now a squad; patrols ride the line; the gang's fence and way-station contacts are leaned on. Every job in the territory adds `+1` Trouble die. |
| `5` | **Inferno.** The railroad has made the gang its specific business. Federal troops may be petitioned (the political chapter, Ch.25); the railroad offers rewards; the gang's name is on wires in three territories. Running a train job at Heat `5` is a `Desperate` operation on every stage, and the railroad will not stop until the gang is broken, hanged, or gone from the territory. |

**Heat climbs** by one step with each train job in the railroad's territory — the file grows. It climbs an additional step if the job killed passengers or crew, if the mail was taken, or if the railroad's detective was made a fool of. It **fades** by one step per season the gang lies low in that territory (no train jobs, no casing, no fence-work for the railroad's paper). The file never closes, but the urgency fades. A gang that robs one train and then rides to another railroad's territory for a year comes back to find the Heat at `1` or `0` — the file is still there, but the detective has been reassigned, and the gang is yesterday's problem.

The Heat Track is the railroad's persistence, and it is the thing that makes a gang that robs trains different from a gang that robs banks. A bank job is a local crime with a local tail; a train job is a crime against an institution that keeps files, assigns detectives, and escalates its response across the season and the campaign. A gang that robs the same railroad three times is a gang the railroad has decided to break, one step at a time, until the gang is broken or the file is the gang's epitaph.

> _Miller brought the newspapers from the platform, the way he always did, and he laid the one from Denver on Sandoval's counter with his finger on the third column. The column said the railroad had put a man on the Cimarron run — a Pinkerton, by the paper's read, which meant the company was no longer playing at detection and had decided to spend real money on whoever had hit the line three times since the spring._
>
> "Three times," Sandoval said, reading it.
>
> "Three times." Miller did not take his finger off the column. "The heat's up, Adelina. They're not sending a local man this time. They're sending a name."
>
> "Whose name does Riddle carry on this?"
>
> "Riddle's name is in the file now, if the inside man talked, which the inside man always talks. The heat doesn't cool by robbing the same road. It cools by leaving the road alone, or by leaving the road's territory, and Riddle is in no shape to leave the territory."
>
> _Sandoval read the column twice and folded the paper and put it under the ledger. The Heat gauge had climbed another notch, and the gang that had been the railroad's problem was becoming the railroad's business, and the railroad's business was the kind that ended in a wire and a troop and a rope, in that order, unless the gang found a different road to rob or a different territory to rob it in._

### The Iron Horse and the Other Rules

The train robbery ties the book's crime and commerce threads together, and it is the outlaw trade's most lucrative score — and the most likely to end the gang.

- **The Casing Layer and the Heat Track** are the robbery's two distinct layers beyond the Operation. The Casing Layer rewards the plan (banked dice for cased stages); the Heat Track makes the railroad a persistent adversary whose response escalates across jobs. The Operation itself is unchanged.
- **The wire chapter** (Ch.16) is the gang's great enemy and the Casing Layer's great tool — a wire cut ahead of time is a cased stage; a wire that gets out is the Heat Track's accelerant.
- **The justice chapter** (Ch.11) is where the mail's federal reach and the Heat Track's detective both land — the detective's file is the prosecutor's evidence.
- **The banking chapter** (Ch.9) is the train robbery's twin — the same Operation frame, the same anatomy, the different institution. A gang that robs both is a gang whose Heat and whose `Wanted` are both climbing.

A campaign that uses these rules will find that the iron horse changes the county the way it changed the actual West: it brings money, witnesses, the wire, and the long reach of a company that does not forget. A gang that robs a train has taken from the most organized interest in the country, and the country will collect, with interest, across seasons. The train robbery is the most lucrative score in the outlaw's trade, and it is the score most likely to end the gang — not in a shootout, but in a file that grew until the file was a noose.
