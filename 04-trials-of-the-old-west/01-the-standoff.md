## Chapter 1 - The Standoff

> _THE SILENCE IN THE ROOM BEFORE THE GUN IS THE LOUDEST SOUND THE WEST MAKES. IT IS THE SOUND OF THREE MEN DECIDING, ALL AT ONCE, WHAT THEIR HANDS ARE FOR._
>
> — SAM RIDDLE, TO A YOUNG HAND WHO ASKED WHY NOBODY DREW

### The Standoff and Its Work

The duel is the formal thing — two men, an agreed distance, a structured draw, the rules written down. The standoff is the informal thing, and it is the scene the West is built on. Three men at a table, or four across a bar, hands on the butts of their six-guns, nobody willing to be first and nobody willing to back down, the room holding its breath around them. The standoff is not a fight. It is the door the fight comes through, or the door the fight is pushed back from, and the engine should be able to resolve which door it is without jumping straight to the combat Round.

This chapter gives the standoff a short scene-level mechanic. It does not replace the duel, the combat Round, or the social-conflict rules. It resolves the *whether* and the *who first* — whether the standoff breaks into iron, and whose hand goes for the gun or comes away from it — and then hands off to the existing rules for whatever comes next.

> _The saloon had gone quiet the way saloons do, which is all at once and from the walls inward. Riddle had his hand on the Colt and his back to the post and his eyes on the two men at the bar who had come in out of the storm with a name in their mouths and a debt behind it. The keeper had her hand under the bar on the shotgun. The cornishman at the far end had not moved, because cornishmen in saloons with debts in their mouths do not move._
>
> _Nobody drew. The silence was the standoff, and the standoff was its own kind of work, and Riddle was doing the work with his eyes and his hand and the careful way he did not blink._
>
> "Gentlemen," the keeper said, and the word was the first stone in the jar, and the jar was the silence, and the silence broke or it did not. That was the standoff. That was what the dice were for._

### The Standoff Round

A standoff is resolved in **Standoff Rounds**, which are shorter than combat Rounds and exist only to decide the standoff itself. A Standoff Round is a single beat of tension — the held breath, the eye contact, the hand on the wood — resolved by one roll, and then the standoff either breaks or holds for another Round.

Most standoffs resolve in one to three Standoff Rounds. If it runs longer than that without breaking, it has fizzled into the uneasy peace the table can read as the standoff's natural end.

#### Who Is in the Standoff

A standoff needs at least two parties who are willing to draw but not yet willing to be first. A single gunman holding a room is not a standoff; it is a threat. The standoff is the unstable equilibrium, and it requires at least two hands on at least two guns, each hand unsure of the other.

For the rules, treat each **side** as one party — Riddle and the keeper are one side if their interests align; the two men at the bar are another side; a third party (the cornishman, a deputy in the corner, the kid at the door) may be a third side, or may be a non-combatant the standoff ignores. The GM names the sides before the first Standoff Round.

#### The Standoff Roll

Each Standoff Round, every side makes a single **Standoff Roll**. The roll is `Quick` — the attribute of the hand and the nerve — modified by the abilities and talents below. The side with the most successes controls the Round and decides what the standoff does next.

| The Side's Approach | The Ability That Modifies `Quick` |
| --- | --- |
| Cool nerve, reading the room, waiting for the other hand to flinch | `INSIGHT` |
| The threat of the gun, the reputation, the promise of what comes next | `PRESENCE` |
| The fast hand, the draw, the willingness to be first if it comes to it | `LIGHT-FINGERED` or `SHOOTIN'` |

A side chooses one approach and adds that ability's dice to the `Quick` pool. A side cannot stack two approaches in one Round; the leader of the side chooses what the side is doing this beat — reading, threatening, or readying — and rolls accordingly.

#### Standoff Modifiers

Apply the modifiers that plainly matter, to the ±3 cap, on the side's pool.

| Condition | Modifier |
| --- | --- |
| The side has the drop (the other side's hands are not on their guns) | `+2 dice` (and the standoff may be over before it begins) |
| The side has more guns in the standoff than the opposition | `+1 die` |
| The side's leader has the `Cold Blooded` talent | `+1 die` |
| The side's leader has the `Quick Draw` talent | `+1 die` to a `LIGHT-FINGERED`/`SHOOTIN'` approach |
| The side's leader has the `Authority` talent and is the lawful party | `+1 die` to a `PRESENCE` approach |
| The side's leader has `Fame` 3+ for violence | `+1 die` to a `PRESENCE` approach (the room knows the name) |
| The side's leader has `Fame` 3+ as a peacekeeper or preacher | `+1 die` to an `INSIGHT` approach (the room wants it defused) |
| The side is outmatched, cornered, or protecting non-combatants | `-1 die` |
| The side's leader is wounded, drunk, or shaken | `-1 die` to `-2 dice` |
| The side's leader has the `Man's Best Friend` or `Companion` and the companion is present and loyal | `+1 die` (the dog at the heel, the hand at the back) |

The GM should not stack every modifier. Pick the one or two that make the moment dangerous and real.

### Reading the Standoff Roll

Compare the successes of the opposing sides. The highest-rolling side controls the Round and chooses one of the following resolutions, or the dice choose for them.

| Result | What the Standoff Does |
| --- | --- |
| One side wins by `2+` successes | **That side breaks the standoff on its own terms.** It draws first (into the combat Round, with the initiative advantage), backs the other side down (de-escalation, the loser takes a `Standing`/`Reputation` cost), or imposes a demand the loser must answer or draw against. |
| One side wins by `1` success | **The standoff tips but does not break.** The winning side gains an edge — the other side's hand comes off the gun a quarter-inch, a third party speaks, the keeper's shotgun clears the bar — and the next Standoff Roll is at `+1 die` for the winner. The standoff holds for another Round. |
| A draw | **The standoff holds, tense.** Nobody blinks. The silence stretches. Roll another Standoff Round, or — if the table agrees the tension has played itself out — the standoff fizzles into the uneasy peace below. |
| All sides fail (`0` successes) | **The standoff fizzles.** Nobody draws, nobody backs down, but the moment passes. The parties disengage with their hands still on their guns and their pride intact. The standoff ends without a fight and without a winner, which is often the most dangerous outcome, because the debt is still on the page. |

#### Breaking Into the Combat Round

When a side chooses to draw (or is forced to by the losing end of a `2+` result), the standoff ends and the combat Round begins. The side that broke the standoff gains **one free opening action** before initiative is drawn — the first shot, the first lunge, the thrown chair — exactly as the corebook's surprise and ambush rules describe. The standoff was the surprise; the side that controlled it was the ambusher.

The opening action cannot be Blocked or Dodged unless the target's side had a `Cold Blooded` or `Quick Draw` talent in play, in which case the target may react as if already on edge.

#### Backing Down

When a side backs down, the standoff ends without a fight. The backing side takes the cost of the retreat: a `Standing` drop with whichever faction witnessed it (the county reads a backed-down gun as a gun that wasn't ready), a `Reputation` shift toward the cautious or the cowardly (the GM's call, by the county's temper), and the debt or the grievance that caused the standoff left unresolved, to fester into the next scene. Backing down is not defeat — it is often the smart play — but the engine should make it cost something, because in the West it did.

### The Uneasy Peace

A standoff that fizzles or holds past three Rounds resolves into the **uneasy peace**. The guns stay on the hips, the room goes back to its drinking, and the parties leave with the matter unsettled. The uneasy peace is the engine's way of saying the standoff was a draw, and the debt is still owed. The GM should note the unsettled standoff as a grudge the county remembers — the next time the parties meet, the standoff resumes with the memory of the last one on the table, which is often the thing that pushes it over into iron.

### The Standoff and the Other Rules

The standoff is a door, not a room. It ladders cleanly into the engine's other scenes.

- **Into the duel.** A standoff between two parties that agree to formalize it becomes a duel — use the corebook's duel procedure, with the standoff's tensions setting the modifiers. A standoff that breaks into a duel is a standoff that found its form.
- **Into the combat Round.** A standoff that breaks into iron hands off to the corebook's combat rules, with the breaking side's free opening action as the bridge.
- **Into social conflict.** A standoff that resolves into a demand, a threat, or a back-down ladders into the social-conflict rules for whatever is negotiated at the point of the gun. A threat made in a standoff and backed by a winning roll is a social conflict the other side begins at a `-1 die`.
- **Into the holdout (Ch.2).** A standoff that neither side breaks and neither side can leave becomes a siege — the door barred, the windows covered, the night coming on. The holdout chapter picks up where the standoff runs out of room.

### A Word on the Table

The standoff is the scene the dice are worst at and the table is best at. The rules here give the dice a way to resolve the *whether* and the *who first*, but the *feel* of a standoff — the silence, the eye contact, the hand on the wood — is the players' and the GM's to carry. Read the room before you roll. Name what each side wants and what each side is afraid of. Let the silence sit for a beat before the dice hit the table. The standoff is the scene where the engine's gauges matter least and the table's nerve matters most, and the chapter is built to get out of the way of the table when the table is doing its work.
