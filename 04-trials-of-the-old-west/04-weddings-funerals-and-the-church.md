## Chapter 4 - Weddings, Funerals, and the Church Service

> _THE COUNTY GATHERS FOR THE RITES THAT MARK A LIFE — THE WEDDING, THE FUNERAL, THE SUNDAY SERVICE, THE REVIVAL — AND IT SETTLES HALF ITS SCORES WHILE IT'S THERE. THE PREACHER IS THE WITNESS, THE CONGREGATION IS THE JURY, AND THE GRAVEYARD KEEPS THE COUNTY'S LONGEST LEDGER._
>
> — JUDGE HARLAN VOSS, TO A YOUNG LAWYER WHO ASKED WHY THE BENCH ATTENDED FUNERALS

### The Rites and Their Work

The ceremonial scenes are the county's punctuation. A wedding joins families and merges property and sets the faction map for a generation. A funeral reveals it — who came, who stayed away, who stood at the grave and who could not look the widow in the eye. A Sunday service or a camp revival gathers the county in one room and lets a preacher, a reformer, or a faction move the room's temper in a way no saloon argument can. These are the scenes the campaign returns to season after season, and they are drama engines — the wedding that surfaces the feud, the funeral that reads the will, the revival that turns the county's `Standing` on its head.

The families chapter (Ch.15) treats marriage as an allegiance tag; the faction chapter (Ch.14) treats the church as a faction. This chapter gives the rites themselves scene-level frames: short procedures (not full operations) that resolve the rite's dramatic question and move the engine's gauges. Use them when the rite is the session's centerpiece, not when it is a sentence of color.

> _Voss stood at the back of the church and watched the Davidson boy and the Sandoval girl stand up together in front of the priest, and he watched the county watch them, and the county's face was the engine the wedding was running. The Davidsons on one side, in their good coats and their bitterness, because the boy was marrying beneath the family's opinion of itself. The Sandovals on the other, in their best and their suspicion, because the girl was marrying into the name that had taken the south pasture in fifty-eight and the county had not forgotten it._
>
> _The priest said the words. The ring was a Davidson ring, which the Sandovals noted, and the dowry was a Sandoval deed to the spring's north section, which the Davidsons noted, and the county noted both and adjusted its ledgers accordingly._
>
> _Nobody objected, though the Davidson patriarch's face was the kind of objection that did not need a voice. The girl said yes. The boy said yes. The priest pronounced them, and the county went out into the sun to drink the Davidson whiskey and eat the Sandoval tamales and pretend, for an afternoon, that the wedding had healed anything at all._

### The Wedding

A wedding is the engine's strongest allegiance-builder and its most public flashpoint. Two families are joined, property is merged, a feud is papered over or set alight, and the county watches. Run the wedding as a short scene built on the social-conflict rules, with three beats: the match, the objection, and the merger.

#### The Match

The match is the social conflict that establishes whether the wedding goes forward on its terms. The family or faction proposing the match rolls `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` against the other family's `INSIGHT` (reading the match's true cost) or `PRESENCE` (resisting it). The match's modifiers are the families' `Standing` with each other, the dowry's size (a Capital transfer, below), and the county's temper (read off the town's `Civic` and the factions' positions).

- A match that succeeds cleanly goes forward with both families' blessing.
- A match that succeeds barely goes forward with conditions — a larger dowry, a property concession, a public apology for an old slight.
- A match that fails does not go forward, or goes forward as an elopement that shames both families and starts a feud the families chapter's Feud Clock runs.

#### The Objection

The wedding's dramatic beat is the objection — the moment a voice is raised in the asking. An objection is a social-conflict challenge to the match, made by a party with standing (a rival suitor, a feuding family, a faction whose interests the match crosses). The objector rolls `PRESENCE` against the officiant's `INSIGHT` and the couple's resolve.

| The Objection's Outcome | What It Means |
| --- | --- |
| The objector wins by `2+` | The wedding halts. The match is broken, or delayed until the objection is resolved (a duel, a payment, a withdrawal). The objector gains `Standing`; the couple's families lose face. |
| The objector wins by `1` | The wedding goes forward, but the objection is on the county's record. The objector's grievance is public; the couple's alliance carries the mark of it. |
| The objector fails | The wedding goes forward, and the objector is the one who loses face. The county reads a failed objection as a humiliation, and the objector's `Standing` drops. |

A wedding with no objection is a wedding the county has accepted, which is itself a `Standing` gain for the couple's families. A wedding with a failed objection is a wedding the county watched the objector lose, which is a `Standing` gain doubled. A wedding halted by an objection is a wedding the feud chapter picks up as a Feud Clock ticking.

#### The Merger

The merger is the wedding's settlement — the transfer of Capital, property, and `Standing` the match represents. The dowry (the bride's family to the groom's, or vice versa by custom), the settlement (the groom's family to the couple), and the alliance (both families' `Standing` with each other raised by one step) are the merger's mechanics, applied at the rite's conclusion. A wedding that merges two feuding families is a wedding that erases Feud Clock segments (the families chapter) and shifts the county's faction map for a generation — which is why weddings are worth a session and not a sentence.

### The Funeral

A funeral is the county's accounting. Who attends is the faction map revealed; the will read is the property transferred; the grudge surfaced is the feud's next chapter. Run the funeral as a scene that reveals and redistributes, rather than a contest.

#### Who Attends

The funeral's attendance is the county's faction map in one room. The GM should note who came, who sent regrets, and who was conspicuously absent — each is a `Standing` signal the county reads. A faction that attends a rival's funeral is a faction offering a truce or a taunt; a faction that is absent is a faction making a point. The party's attendance is itself a `Standing` move, read by every other faction present.

A funeral is also the classic scene for the revelation — the secret surfaced in a eulogy, the stranger at the back who is the dead man's other family, the letter read aloud that names a debt or a betrayal. The GM may use a funeral to deliver a plot's hidden facts in a setting the county cannot look away from.

#### The Will and the Estate

A funeral is when the will is read and the estate is settled, and the property and Capital of the dead are transferred. The will is a legal instrument (the justice chapter's courts and the future land-office chapter's procedures), and a contested will is the engine's door for the inheritance dispute, the forged codicil, the disinherited son, the second family. A will contest is a social-conflict or a trial (the justice chapter), depending on whether it is fought in the parlor or the courthouse.

A funeral with no will is a funeral the estate goes to the county's default — the widow, the eldest son, the creditor, the faction with the strongest claim — and the dispute that follows is the feud chapter's work.

#### The Grudge at the Grave

A funeral is the stage a feud uses. The old score settled at the graveside, the accusation shouted over the open hole, the shot fired from the carriage — the funeral is the scene the engine's feuds and `Standing` ladders can push into a standoff (the standoff chapter) or a murder. The GM should let the funeral be the flashpoint the campaign's long grudges reach for, because the county's dead are the county's longest memory, and the funeral is the day that memory speaks.

### The Service and the Revival

The Sunday service and the camp revival are the county's gathered social events, and they are scenes a preacher, a reformer, or a faction uses to move the room's temper. Run the service or revival as a congregation-as-crowd social conflict, using the corebook's crowd rules, with the preacher as the acting agent.

#### The Service

A Sunday service is a weekly social event the town's `Civic` aspect supports, and the preacher's sermon is a `PERFORMIN'` or `PRESENCE` roll against the congregation's `INSIGHT`. A successful sermon moves the congregation's temper — toward repentance, toward a reform, toward a faction's position — and shifts `Standing` with the religious faction the preacher represents. A failed sermon is a Sunday the congregation slept through, and the preacher's `Fame` and the church's `Civic` modifier take the small hit.

The preacher is a cast-adjacent figure — the circuit rider, the camp pastor, the priest at the Nuevomexicano mission — and the writing skill's `religion-and-faith.md` governs the register. The service is also the county's weekly social map: who sat with whom, who took communion and who abstained, who left early. The GM may use the service the way the funeral uses attendance — as a `Standing` and alliance map the county reads every Sunday.

#### The Revival

A camp revival or a circuit revivalist's visit is a county-wide event that moves `Fame` and `Reputation` more sharply than a service. The revivalist is a `PERFORMIN'` powerhouse, often a traveling figure with a talent for the crowd, and the revival is an operation-scale social event (a `Small` operation, `4` Progress, `3` Trouble) whose Progress is the county moved and whose Trouble is the resistance — the skeptic, the rival preacher, the faction that does not want the county's temper turned.

A revival that succeeds shifts the county's `Standing` with the religious faction up a step, drops it with the factions the revival's reform targets (the saloon, the brothel, the corrupt bench), and may produce conversions the family chapter reads as new allegiances. A revival that fails is a county that did not move, and the revivalist's `Fame` and the religious faction's `Civic` modifier take the hit. A revival that goes Trouble-high is a revival that produced a mob — the Committee roused, the saloon threatened, the county's temper turned to the rope, which is the justice chapter's and the atrocities chapter's door.

### The Rites as Clocks

Each rite is a seasonal anchor in the Turn of the Season, and the campaign's calendar runs on them.

| The Rite | The Season |
| --- | --- |
| The wedding | Spring or early summer, when the families can travel and the feast can be held |
| The funeral | Any season — the dead do not wait, and the funeral is held within days |
| The revival | Late summer or fall, after the harvest, when the circuit rider can travel and the county can gather |
| The Christmas service, the harvest home, the saint's day | The fixed festivals, by the church's calendar and the county's custom |

The GM may use the rites as the campaign's fixed points — the wedding the season's climax, the funeral the season's crisis, the revival the season's turn — and let the engine's other pressures (the weather, the trade, the feud, the sickness) move around them. The rites are the county's punctuation, and a campaign that uses them will find the county's life has a rhythm the engine's gauges can mark.

### The Rites and the Other Rules

The rites tie the book's social threads together, and they are the engine's densest scenes for `Standing`, `Fame`, and the faction map.

- **The families chapter** holds the marriage as an allegiance and the feud as the rite's shadow; this chapter gives the wedding and the funeral their scene-level frames.
- **The faction chapter** holds the church and the family as factions; the rites are where those factions' `Standing` is most publicly moved.
- **The social-conflict rules** (corebook Ch.5) are the engine the rites run on — the match, the objection, the sermon are all social conflicts under a ritual frame.
- **The justice chapter** is where the contested will and the funeral-day murder land, and the standoff chapter is where the graveside grudge stops short of iron.

The rites are the county's punctuation, and a chapter that gives them a frame gives the campaign its heartbeat. The wedding joins, the funeral reveals, the revival moves, and the county's life runs on the rhythm the rites set, season after season, generation after generation, in the church and at the grave and at the table where the whiskey and the tamales are served and the county pretends, for an afternoon, that the rites have healed anything at all.
