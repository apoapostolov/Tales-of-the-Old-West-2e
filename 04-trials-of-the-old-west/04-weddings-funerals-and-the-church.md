## Chapter 4 - Weddings, Funerals, and the Church Service

> _THE COUNTY GATHERS FOR THE RITES THAT MARK A LIFE — THE WEDDING, THE FUNERAL, THE SUNDAY SERVICE, THE REVIVAL — AND IT SETTLES HALF ITS SCORES WHILE IT'S THERE. THE PREACHER IS THE WITNESS, THE CONGREGATION IS THE JURY, AND THE GRAVEYARD KEEPS THE COUNTY'S LONGEST LEDGER._
>
> — JUDGE HARLAN VOSS, TO A YOUNG LAWYER WHO ASKED WHY THE BENCH ATTENDED FUNERALS

### The Rites and Their Work

The ceremonial scenes are the county's punctuation. A wedding joins families and merges property and sets the faction map for a generation. A funeral reveals it — who came, who stayed away, who stood at the grave and who could not look the widow in the eye. A Sunday service or a camp revival gathers the county in one room and lets a preacher, a reformer, or a faction move the room's temper in a way no saloon argument can. These are the scenes the campaign returns to season after season, and they are drama engines — the wedding that surfaces the feud, the funeral that reads the will, the revival that turns the county's `Standing` on its head.

The families chapter (Ch.15) treats marriage as an allegiance tag; the faction chapter (Ch.14) treats the church as a faction. This chapter gives the rites themselves scene-level frames: short procedures (not full operations) that resolve the rite's dramatic question and move the engine's gauges. Use them when the rite is the session's centerpiece, not when it is a sentence of color.

> _Voss stood at the back of the church and watched the Davidson boy and the Sandoval girl stand up together in front of the priest, and he watched the county watch them, and the county's face was the engine the wedding was running. The Davidsons on one side, in their good coats and their bitterness, because the boy was marrying beneath the family's opinion of itself. The Sandovals on the other, in their best and their suspicion, because the girl was marrying into the name that had taken the south pasture in fifty-eight and the county had not forgotten it._
>
> _The priest said the words. The ring was a Davidson ring, which the Sandovals noted, and the dowry was a Sandoval deed to the spring's north section, which the Davidsons noted, and the county noted both and adjusted its ledgers accordingly._
>
> _Nobody objected, though the Davidson patriarch's face was the kind of objection that did not need a voice. The girl said yes. The boy said yes. The priest pronounced them, and the county went out into the sun to drink the Davidson whiskey and eat the Sandoval tamales and pretend, for an afternoon, that the wedding had healed anything at all._

### Social Combat for the Rites

The rites are the county's high-stakes social scenes, and a single opposed roll cannot carry their weight. The rites use **Social Combat** — the engine's full tactical social-conflict system, the social equivalent of the combat Round. This chapter adopts Social Combat for the rites and gives each rite a distinct *scene-type* so the three feel different from each other and from a trial or a negotiation.

Social Combat uses the engine's action economy (1 slow + 1 fast action per Round), a **Composure** pool (social HP, set by `DOCITY` + `CUNNING`, typically 4–8), **leverage as consumable weapon-dice** (a secret used is a secret spent), and **social-distance bands** (Intimate / Personal / Professional / Public) that govern which moves work where. A speaker reduced to 0 Composure is **socially Broken** and rolls on a Social Fallout table — the social equivalent of the critical-injury table. The full Social Combat system — its action lists, its leverage table, its damage formula, its distance-move procedure, its Fallout table — is the engine's social-combat frame; this chapter defines the three rites' *calibration* of it, the way the combat chapter defines a duel's calibration of the combat rules.

#### The Wedding — the Fusion Scene

A wedding is the engine's strongest allegiance-builder and its most public flashpoint, and it is a **fusion scene**: the merger of two families is the goal, the objection is the opposition, and the gathered county (the audience) is whose opinion shifts the `Standing`. The wedding runs as a Social Combat in which the couple (or their champions) are one side, the objector is the other, and the audience's opinion is a tracked third side — the **audience track** — that both compete to shift.

**The distance that matters:** Public. A wedding is performed before the county, and the moves that work are the moves that land in front of witnesses. An objection whispered to the officiant at the altar (Intimate) carries less weight than an objection shouted from the back pew (Public), and the objector must decide whether to move the scene to Public (a slow action, a dramatic escalation the county cannot ignore) or to work at Personal (the direct appeal to the officiant) and risk the county not hearing. This is the Social Combat distance layer's contribution to the wedding: the *where* of the objection is half its weight.

**The leverage:** the dowry deed (D8, consumable), the family's reputation (rank, persistent armor), the rival suitor's claim (D6 or D8 by the claim's strength), the documented secret about a family (D10, consumable, exposes the secret), the public threat to disinherit (D12, consumable, shifts the scene to Public forcibly). A wedding's leverage is the county's paper and the family's name, spent publicly.

**The beats:** the match is the Social Combat's opening (the couple's case laid out); the objection is the opposition's first attack; the merger is the scene's resolution — the audience track's final state determines whether the merger is blessed (audience warmed), contested (audience split), or broken (audience turned against the match). A wedding that ends with the audience track warm erases Feud Clock segments between the families; a wedding that ends with the audience cold starts one. The dowry and the settlement (the Capital and property transfer) are the merger's mechanics, applied at the scene's conclusion, scaled by the audience's verdict.

#### The Funeral — the Revelation Scene

A funeral is the county's accounting, and it is a **revelation scene**: the funeral's tension is not *who wins an exchange* but *what comes out*. The funeral uses Social Combat's distance bands and leverage, but its engine is **information** — the secrets, the debts, the grudges, and the will's contents that surface when the county is gathered and cannot look away. The funeral is closer to the investigation primitive than to the duel.

**Who attends** is the faction map revealed (as before), and the GM should read the attendance as the scene's opening position — the audience track starts warm toward the dead man's allies and cold toward his enemies, and the revelations of the scene shift it.

**The will and the estate** are the funeral's central leverage. The will is a document (D8 or D10 leverage, depending on its contents and its contestation), and a contested will — the forged codicil, the disinherited son, the second family — is the funeral's Social Combat: the claimants deploy their evidence (the prior will, the witness, the handwriting expert's `BOOKLEARNIN'` roll) as leverage, and the audience (the gathered family and the county) is the track whose verdict decides the estate's disposition. A will contest won at the funeral may still go to the courthouse (the justice chapter and the inheritance chapter), but the funeral's verdict is the county's read, which the court ignores at its peril.

**The grudge at the grave** is the funeral's escalation to Public distance — the accusation shouted over the open hole, the shot fired from the carriage. A funeral that moves to Public is a funeral that can tip into a standoff (the standoff chapter) or a murder, and the engine should let the Public declaration carry the weight it carries in any Social Combat: it cannot be taken back. The county's dead are the county's longest memory, and the funeral is the day that memory speaks.

#### The Service — the Congregation Scene

A Sunday service is a weekly Social Combat at low stakes — the preacher works the congregation, and the congregation's temper is the audience track. The preacher's sermon is a slow action (a `PERFORMIN'` or `PRESENCE` attack roll against the congregation's collective `INSIGHT`), and a successful sermon shifts the audience track toward the preacher's position (repentance, a reform, a faction's line). A failed sermon is a Sunday the congregation slept through, and the preacher's `Fame` and the religious faction's `Civic` modifier take the small hit. The service is the Social Combat dial turned down to its lightest setting — one or two exchanges, low Composure pools, the audience track as the only real stake.

The service is also the county's weekly social map: who sat with whom, who took communion and who abstained, who left early. The GM may use the service the way the funeral uses attendance — as a `Standing` and alliance map the county reads every Sunday, and as the place the week's Social Combats (the wedding, the trial, the election) are seeded.

#### The Revival — the Audience-as-Combatant Scene

A camp revival is the Social Combat dial turned to its fullest: the **audience-as-combatant**. In a revival, the audience is not a passive track shifted by the speakers — the audience is the *prize* and the *battlefield*, and the revivalist and the opposition (the skeptic, the rival preacher, the saloon keeper whose trade is threatened) compete to move the crowd's opinion, conversion by conversion.

The revival uses Social Combat's full action economy, with the revivalist's `PERFORMIN'` as the main attack pool and the crowd's temper as the Composure pool being fought over (a collective pool, typically 10–20, representing the crowd's resistance to the message). Each successful attack by the revivalist shifts a portion of the crowd toward conversion (step the religious faction's local `Standing` up); each successful counter by the opposition shifts a portion back. The revival ends when the crowd's pool is reduced (the crowd is moved) or the revivalist's Composure is broken (the crowd is not). A revival that moves the crowd shifts the county's `Standing` with the religious faction up a step, drops it with the factions the revival's reform targets (the saloon, the brothel, the corrupt bench), and may produce conversions the family chapter reads as new allegiances. A revival that fails is a county that did not move, and the revivalist's `Fame` takes the hit. A revival where the opposition is broken and the revivalist presses too far is a revival that produced a mob — the Committee roused, the saloon threatened, the county's temper turned to the rope, which is the justice chapter's and the atrocities chapter's door.

### The Rites as Clocks

Each rite is a seasonal anchor in the Turn of the Season, and the campaign's calendar runs on them.

| The Rite | The Season |
| --- | --- |
| The wedding | Spring or early summer, when the families can travel and the feast can be held |
| The funeral | Any season — the dead do not wait, and the funeral is held within days |
| The revival | Late summer or fall, after the harvest, when the circuit rider can travel and the county can gather |
| The Christmas service, the harvest home, the saint's day | The fixed festivals, by the church's calendar and the county's custom |

The GM may use the rites as the campaign's fixed points — the wedding the season's climax, the funeral the season's crisis, the revival the season's turn — and let the engine's other pressures (the weather, the trade, the feud, the sickness) move around them. The rites are the county's punctuation, and a campaign that uses them will find the county's life has a rhythm the engine's gauges can mark.

### The Rites and the Other Rules

The rites tie the book's social threads together, and they are the engine's densest scenes for `Standing`, `Fame`, and the faction map.

- **The families chapter** holds the marriage as an allegiance and the feud as the rite's shadow; this chapter gives the wedding and the funeral their scene-level frames.
- **The faction chapter** holds the church and the family as factions; the rites are where those factions' `Standing` is most publicly moved.
- **Social Combat** — the engine's full tactical social-conflict system (Composure, leverage, distance bands, audience track, Fallout table) — is the frame the rites are built on. The wedding is a fusion scene (merger as goal, audience as prize); the funeral is a revelation scene (information as the engine, the will as leverage); the revival is an audience-as-combatant scene (the crowd is the battlefield). The three scene-types make the rites feel different from each other and from a trial, even though all use the same Social Combat engine calibrated differently.
- **The justice chapter** is where the contested will and the funeral-day murder land, and the standoff chapter is where the graveside grudge stops short of iron.
- **The inheritance chapter** is where a will contested at the funeral goes if the courthouse is the next stop; the funeral's audience-track verdict is the county's read, which the court inherits.

The rites are the county's punctuation, and a chapter that gives them a frame gives the campaign its heartbeat. The wedding joins, the funeral reveals, the revival moves, and the county's life runs on the rhythm the rites set, season after season, generation after generation, in the church and at the grave and at the table where the whiskey and the tamales are served and the county pretends, for an afternoon, that the rites have healed anything at all.
