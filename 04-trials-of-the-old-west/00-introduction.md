# Trials of the Old West

> _THE COUNTRY IS DRY AND THE COUNTRY IS WATCHED. THE LEDGER IS KEPT AT THE WAY-STATION AND THE LAW IS KEPT AT THE COUNTY SEAT AND THE WEATHER IS KEPT NOWHERE, WHICH IS WHY THE WEATHER KILLS MORE MEN THAN THE LEDGER OR THE LAW EVER WILL._
>
> — FROM A LETTER FOUND IN THE WAY-STATION'S POSTBOX, UNSIGNED

## At the Table

Sooner or later a western campaign runs past the first gunfight. The posse catches the outlaw and somebody has to try him. The drive reaches the river and the herd will not cross. The mine pays for a season, then the cough starts. A newspaper turns a private lie into county truth. The wire carries a warrant faster than a horse can run. Winter closes the pass and the pantry ledger becomes as important as a Winchester.

_Trials of the Old West_ is built for those turns. It keeps the core game's dice, Trouble, Faith, `Standing`, `Wanted`, Capital, town aspects, and Turn of the Season, then puts them to work in places the frontier campaign naturally goes: courtrooms, freight roads, ranch ledgers, train platforms, sickrooms, press rooms, winter cabins, faction councils, and country where the trail gives out.

The rules are meant to make opportunity playable. A saloon can become an information house. A herd can become the campaign's fortune and its danger. A bank can be robbed, used, ruined, or made into the reason the county goes hungry. Factions can call in debts. A baron's job can pay enough to save a party and mark them for everyone who hates him. Weather can stop being background and start deciding whether the party reaches shelter before the cold finds their hands.

The examples follow a small cast living in one hard corner of country: a trader, a trail boss, a judge, a miner, a sergeant, an engineer, and a man trying to stay clear of what he used to be. They are not the point of the book. They are hands on the tools, there to show how the procedures feel when they touch people with debts, grudges, property, and bodies that can break.

The country assumed here is high desert and grassland below the Sangres and the Cimarron, with a railhead, a mining district, a cavalry post, and a county seat within a hard ride of each other. Tejano families, Nuevomexicano villages, Pueblo neighbors, Apache and Comanche memory, Black cavalrymen, Chinese laborers, Irish railroad men, German farmers, Jewish merchants, Mexican freighters, Anglo ranchers, and women of every station all had their hands on that country, or had the country put its hands on them. The rules will ask you to keep them human. No noble savage. No flat villain. No town built from stock parts.

## How the Parts Work

Each part widens the frame. The first two chapters handle the tense scene before or during violence. The next seven make work and business into campaign play. Crime, law, factions, family, paper, mail, and press pull the party into the county's public life. Weather, deep country, encounters, and winter give the land its teeth. The final part lets the table handle battles, barons, and the national politics pressing down on the county.

Read only the chapter the next session needs, or let the whole book change the campaign's weather.

### Part I — Person and Scene

These are the chapters of the moment — the single tense scene, resolved at the table in its own frame before it hands off to the corebook's conflict rules.

**The Standoff (Ch.1).** Three hands near three guns, no one willing to be first. The roll decides who moves, who backs down, and whether the room becomes a duel, a fight, or an ugly peace.

**The Holdout (Ch.2).** A cabin siege is fought with walls, windows, ammunition, wounded people, fire, and dawn. Attackers build Breach. Defenders spend what they have left to keep the room.

### Part II — Hearth and Trade

These are the chapters of daily life and livelihood — the sickness, the crossroads, and the work that keeps the county alive. The rites of the county — the wedding, the funeral, the church-going — are not a chapter of their own here; they are the family's business, and they live in the Families chapter (Ch.14), as Quarrels between families with stakes measured in `Standing` and blood.

The six business chapters (Ch.4–9) share a seasonal order: assess the season, invest before the dice are rolled, operate through scenes or jobs, reckon the result, and reinvest what remains. Each trade has its own resource and its own trouble, so running a saloon, a mine, and a cattle outfit do not feel alike. The saloon has its Crossroads rating and the nightly Trade-Night; the freighter has the Route Ledger and the Cargo Bet; the rancher has the Herd die and the open range; the miner has the Vein die and the Dust; the wild trades have the Stand die, the Trapline Menu, and the Decline; the banker has the Reserve gauge and the bank run.

**Sickness and the Fever (Ch.3).** Cholera, smallpox, camp fever, the flux, childhood diseases, and the pox move through groups, not just single bodies. Spread and Severity let the table fight quarantine, triage, fear, and the limits of 1870s medicine.

**Saloons and the Trade (Ch.4).** A keeper who owns the place owns trouble. Crossroads, Refuge, Trade-Night, faro, rooms, workers, deputies, wanted men, and faction meetings all pass over the same bar.

**Running Trade and Resources (Ch.5).** The railhead sets the floor price. The road adds the rest. Route Ledger, Cargo Bet, supply runs, scarcity, and rail schedules make carrying goods a living instead of a shopping note.

**Cattle Drives and the Open Range (Ch.6).** The herd is money that can panic. Drives, stampedes, roundups, Herd dice, breeding, and shared grass let the ranch live season by season, until the grass runs thin and the neighbors reach for wire or rifles.

**Mining Claims and the Ground's Promise (Ch.7).** A strike is only the start. Prospecting, claim law, Vein dice, Depth, shift work, dust, cave-ins, company pressure, and union anger make the mine a place the party enters and a business that can eat them.

**Logging, Fur Trade, and Hunting (Ch.8).** Timber, pelts, meat, and hides come out of country that can be emptied. Stand dice, traplines, rendezvous, Take dice, and Decline make extraction profitable, dangerous, and visible on the map.

**Banking and the Vault (Ch.9).** The bank is a vault, a ledger, a rumor, and a promise. Reserves, loans, drafts, deposits, safe-cracks, robberies, and bank runs decide whether the county still believes its own paper.

### Part III — Crime and the Law

These are the chapters of the county's violent and judicial underside — the score, the trial, and the atrocity.

**Train Robbery and the Iron Horse (Ch.10).** The train can be stopped by signal, obstruction, sabotage, or boarding. Casing, Heat, derailment, armed messengers, federal mail, and railroad memory turn the score into more than a strongbox.

**Justice, Trial, and Incarceration (Ch.11).** Posse, arrest, trial, docket, sentence, jail, prison, jailbreak, pardon, and lynching follow the shot. Evidence becomes leverage. The jury moves. The court calendar becomes a weapon.

**Atrocities and Human Trade (Ch.12).** This material belongs only at tables willing to handle it with restraint. Atrocity marks the doer. Captives remain named people with needs and futures. Refusal, rescue, testimony, and repair all cost.

### Part IV — The County

These are the chapters of the county's web of interests, blood, paper, and communication — the social layer above the individual and below the nation.

**Factions and Standing (Ch.13).** The county has organized interests, and they remember. `Standing`, faction bonds, debts, Influence, debt calls, seasonal events, rolls, and clocks make help public and allegiance expensive.

**Families and Feuds (Ch.14).** Blood owns land, carries insult, buries its dead, marries for advantage, and teaches children who to hate. Bond, Debt, property, quarrels, weddings, funerals, church days, and generations make the family a force on the county map.

**Inheritance, Land Office, and Speculation (Ch.15).** Deeds take land as surely as rifles. Land office, probate, contested title, insurance, speculation, and the Market put paper in the same campaign space as gun smoke.

**The Wire (Ch.16).** A message outruns a horse. Costs, word counts, operators, favors, interceptions, cut lines, and wire-down time decide whether a warrant arrives today or after the party has crossed the wash.

**Mail and the Post (Ch.17).** The mail moves on a schedule the county trusts. Routes, bags, delays, riders, robberies, special deliveries, and star-route contracts let letters, deeds, payrolls, and lies travel with weight.

**The Press and the Election (Ch.18).** A story can move `Fame`, a season of editions can ruin a man, and a campaign can be fought through rallies, canvasses, fraud, suppression, and votes counted too late to save anyone.

### Part V — The Wild Country

These are the chapters of the land itself — the weather, the country past the trail, the encounter, and the season that contracts it.

**Weather and the Trail's Teeth (Ch.19).** The sky gets a procedure. Regional weather, Weather Pattern, Exposure, blizzard, prairie fire, flash flood, and drought decide when existing conditions come due.

**Deep Wild Land Exploration (Ch.20).** Past the trail, the party travels by water, through forest, or over mountain. Provisions, Feed, and Gear shrink by the Shift. Push, go careful, rest, scout, and decide what can be left behind.

**Regional Encounter Tables (Ch.21).** The country between towns is full. Six regions carry D66 tables for riders, herds, patrols, weather, water, sign, and quiet. Tension, Game, and Sign let the country change after the party passes.

**Winter and Wintering (Ch.22).** Winter counts autumn's debts. Stores, Fuel, Feed, `Horsestock`, `Cohesion`, Cabin Fever, hard rides, and spring thaw make December through March a season of play.

### Part VI — The Nation and the Decade

These are the chapters of the largest scales — the war, the great operators, and the political weather of the decade.

**Mass Combat and the Skirmish Line (Ch.23).** Quality, Strength, Cohesion, unit rolls, attached leaders, artillery, Gatlings, charges, fortifications, sieges, and running fights let battles play at a size larger than a saloon floor.

**Robber Barons and the Long Reach (Ch.24).** The baron is a faction of one with money, lawyers, railroads, and reach. Survey jobs, land jobs, dirty jobs, war jobs, patronage, and obligation put the party under men whose offices sit far east of the county.

**The Political Scene of the 1870s (Ch.25).** National parties make the weather the county lives under. Republican and Democratic coalitions, Liberal Republicans, Greenbackers, federal acts, the Panic, the Great Sioux War, the Compromise of 1877, and the silver fight press downward into local play.

## A Note on What Is Coming

The rules here do not exhaust the West. Specific river systems, survey parties, doctors as businesses, bounty hunters, holidays, and festival calendars can all be built from the same material when a campaign needs them. Start with the historical fact. Name the table choice. Charge the cost.

## The Shape of the Book

The country is dry and the country is watched. A drive does not end when the herd reaches the railhead. A robbery does not end when the gang rides clear. A trial does not end when the judge speaks. The county remembers.
