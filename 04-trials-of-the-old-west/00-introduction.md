# Trials of the Old West

> _THE COUNTRY IS DRY AND THE COUNTRY IS WATCHED. THE LEDGER IS KEPT AT THE WAY-STATION AND THE LAW IS KEPT AT THE COUNTY SEAT AND THE WEATHER IS KEPT NOWHERE, WHICH IS WHY THE WEATHER KILLS MORE MEN THAN THE LEDGER OR THE LAW EVER WILL._
>
> — FROM A LETTER FOUND IN THE WAY-STATION'S POSTBOX, UNSIGNED

## What This Book Is

This is _Trials of the Old West_, a book of new rules for the game of the same name. It does not change the game you already have. It widens it. The corebook and the 1870s supplement give you the saloon, the trail, the camp, the mine, the posse, and the gang. This book gives you the things those books reach for and do not quite close: the trial that follows the arrest, the weather that buries the drive, the iron horse on the rail, the herd on the move, the claim in the rock, the fight too large for a pistol, and the county's interests pressing on all of it at once.

Every chapter here is built from the same engine the corebook uses. You will not learn a new way to roll dice. You will find new ways to use the dice you already have, new gauges that sit beside the ones you already track, and new tables that decide what the country does to the people in it. The point is to make the West feel wider without making it feel different.

The book is built around a small company of people who live in one corner of country — a trader who keeps the ledger, a trail boss who keeps his silence, a judge who keeps the rope, a miner who keeps his cough to himself, a sergeant who keeps the line, an engineer who keeps the throttle, and a man trying to keep clear of what he used to be. They open the chapters, and they appear in every example. They are not your player characters. They are the ground the rules walk on. You will meet them in `/docs/vignettes-and-cast.md`, and you are free to use them or to people your own county with your own.

## How to Use This Book

Take any chapter on its own, or take them as a set. Each is a complete thing that plugs into the game at a place the corebook already knows. Read the chapter once, use its tables at the table, and let its pressure do its work. The prose in each chapter is not decoration. It is the reason the rules exist, and a reader who skips it for the tables alone will miss the weight the tables are built to produce.

A word on the country this book is set in. It is the high desert and grassland below the Sangres and the Cimarron country of northern New Mexico, with a railhead, a mining district, a cavalry post, and a county seat in a hard ride of each other. It is the country where every pressure in this book can be felt at once. It was not all Anglo, and neither is the cast. The honest rendering of the West's peoples is the business of the writing skill this book is built with, and that skill governs every scene in which they appear. Hold the country to the same standard the skill holds the prose: no noble savage, no flat villain, no town of stock parts, and no flinching from the uncomfortable truths the rules put on the page.

## What You Will Find

The book is organized into six parts, by the scope of the pressure each addresses — from the single tense moment, through the hearth and the work, through crime and the law, through the county's web of interests, out into the wild country, and up to the nation and the decade. The chapters within each part lean on one another, but a table may take any chapter alone, or take the parts in any order the county calls for.

### Part I — Person and Scene

These are the chapters of the moment — the single tense scene, resolved at the table in its own frame before it hands off to the corebook's conflict rules.

**The Standoff (Ch.1)** is the scene-level mechanic for the multi-party face-off: three or four hands on their six-guns, nobody willing to be first. You will find a Standoff Roll (opposed `Quick` plus a chosen ability), a break-points table that decides who draws and who backs down, and clean ladders into the duel, the combat Round, and the social conflict. It is the door the fight comes through, or the door it is pushed back from.

**The Holdout (Ch.2)** is the PC-scale homestead and cabin siege — the classic "hold the cabin until dawn." You will find a holdout operation with the polarity flipped (attackers accumulate Breach), an assault-tactics table (rush the door, fire the roof, starve them out, the parley, the breach), the room-by-room fight when the wall fails, and the dawn as the natural clock. It is distinct from the unit-scale mass combat, and it is the frontier's most personal siege.

### Part II — Hearth and Trade

These are the chapters of daily life and livelihood — the sickness, the crossroads, and the work that keeps the county alive. The rites of the county — the wedding, the funeral, the church-going — are not a chapter of their own here; they are the family's business, and they live in the Families chapter (Ch.14), as Quarrels between families with stakes measured in `Standing` and blood.

The six business chapters (Ch.4–9) share a seasonal frame — the **Investment Cycle** (assess, invest, operate, reckon, reinvest) — but each trade has its own **distinct resource and loop**, so running a saloon, a mine, and a cattle outfit feel mechanically different. The saloon's dial is its Crossroads rating and its loop is the nightly Trade-Night; the freighter's asset is the Route Ledger and its bet is the Cargo; the rancher's resource is the Herd die and its politics is the common-pool Range; the miner's mystery is the Vein die and his doom is the Dust; the wild trades run on the Stand die, the Trapline Menu, and the Decline; the banker's dial is the Reserve gauge and its risk is the run.

**Sickness and the Fever (Ch.3)** puts the country's oldest killer on the table. You will find the disease as a pressure that spreads (the Spread gauge) and kills (the Severity gauge), the named diseases of the West (cholera, smallpox, camp fever, the flux, the childhood diseases, the pox), the outbreak fought as an operation, and the period doctor's honest limits — the calomel, the lancet, and the odds that saved fewer than the modern table may expect.

**Saloons and the Trade (Ch.4)** is the chapter for the keeper who owns the place. You will find the saloon and brothel as a seasonal enterprise running on the Investment Cycle, a **Crossroads rating** (the county's traffic, information, and trouble) as the house's distinct dial alongside the `Refuge` gauge, the rooms as a revenue line with the women who work them as named figures, and the **Trade-Night Procedure** — the keeper's nightly loop of reading the room and making one move, where every drink is a `Standing` shift. The saloon is the engine's densest crossroads, and the keeper plays the faction map one drink at a time.

**Running Trade and Resources (Ch.5)** is the chapter for the moving of goods across distance. You will find the spatial economy (why flour is cheap at the railhead and dear at the snowbound mine), the freighting outfit running on the Investment Cycle with a **Route Ledger** (routes as a growing asset — a road run before is safer than one not) as its distinct dial, the **Cargo Bet** (the risk-weighted margin that sets the run's stakes) as its strategic decision, the supply run as an operation, the **Schedule metronome** (the `INSIGHT` read of the market's rhythm), and the railhead as the trade hub. It is the chapter that gives the freighter, the trader, and the way-station keeper their mechanical home.

**Cattle Drives and the Open Range (Ch.6)** is the chapter for the herd on the move. You will find the drive as an extended scene with the miles to the railhead as the goal and the trail's teeth as the cost, the stampede as a nested disaster, the ranch running on the Investment Cycle with a **Herd die** and **Quality rating** as its living asset, the **Breeding Dial** as the multi-season investment that raises the herd's grade, and the open range as a **common-pool resource** (a shared grass die all the ranchers draw down) whose tragedy is the engine of every range war. It is the chapter that makes a herd a character.

**Mining Claims and the Ground's Promise (Ch.7)** is the chapter for the diggings. You will find prospecting as a search across country, the claim under the law of 1872, the working mine running on the Investment Cycle with a **Vein die** and Depth rating as its depletable mystery (you don't know if the vein continues until you sink the shaft), the **Shift Menu** as the mine's tactical work loop (drill-and-blast, muck-and-timber, prospect-the-drift, shore-up), the **Dust Track** (silicosis as an accumulating doom in the lungs), and the mine as a place — the dungeon of the chapter. The company-vs-union web gives the mine its political layer.

**Logging, Fur Trade, and Hunting (Ch.8)** is the chapter for the wild land's extractive trades, each with a distinct take-economy. You will find the lumber camp with a **Stand die** (the timber tract that depletes) and the river drive **timed by the spring thaw** (a clock that ends the drive when the water drops); the trapline as an **activity menu** (run the line, move camp, rest and repair) and the **rendezvous** as the annual reckon-and-reinvest scene; and the market hunt's **take die** and the **Decline** — a per-region game die that steps down as the hunt succeeds, emptying the encounter table and shifting the plains nations to hostility. The buffalo's decline is the engine's honest metanarrative, made mechanical.

**Banking and the Vault (Ch.9)** is the chapter for the county's treasury and its temptation. You will find the bank's anatomy, the robbery as an operation scaled to the bank's strength, the safe-crack as the centerpiece (combination, ear, drills, powder, the time-lock's patience), deposits and the bank run, and the bank as a **financial institution** running on the Investment Cycle with a **Reserve gauge** (the fractional-reserve solvency ratio) as its distinct dial and the **Banker's Dial** (lend for profit vs hold for safety) as its strategic decision. A robbery is a **reserve shock** that can break the county's credit, not just take its cash. It is train robbery's twin, and the engine's most iconic score after the iron horse.

### Part III — Crime and the Law

These are the chapters of the county's violent and judicial underside — the score, the trial, and the atrocity.

**Train Robbery and the Iron Horse (Ch.10)** is the chapter for the iron horse as a target. You will find the train's car-by-car anatomy, the four ways to stop one (the false signal, the obstruction, the sabotage, the boarding), the robbery as an operation scaled to the run, the **Casing Layer** (the pre-Operation intelligence-gathering that banks dice for the stages a gang cased), the **Heat Track** (the railroad's persistent, escalating memory across jobs), the derailment table, the federal mail's hanging offense, and the railroad as a faction that does not forget. The stagecoach folds in as the iron horse's poorer cousin.

**Justice, Trial, and Incarceration (Ch.11)** closes the loop the outlaw chapter opens. You will find the posse and the arrest, the **trial as a phased Quarrel** (the jury as the prize both sides shift, evidence as consumable leverage, the courtroom's phases — opening, the prosecution's case, the defense's case, the summation), the **Docket** (the court's schedule as a strategic lever — timing as a faction's tool), the sentencing tables (misdemeanor, felony, capital), the jail and the territorial prison, the **jailbreak with its Casing-the-jail layer** (patience as the jailbreak's core virtue), the pardon bought from a faction, and the lynching when the law's grip is short and the rope is long. It is the chapter that makes a crime have a tail, and makes the tail bite.

**Atrocities and Human Trade (Ch.12)** is the hardest chapter, and the one most in need of being done right or not at all. You will find the atrocity as an operation whose moral weight lands on the doer (the `Doubts` and `Vexes` the engine refuses to let the act avoid) and accumulates across the campaign in the **Moral Corrosion Track** (a gauge that does not reset — the man the atrocity makes, tracked until he is undone by it or redeems it by costly atonement), the **captive as a tracked person** (the trade's cargo treated as a person with live condition tracks, not a commodity), the **refusal as a protected dial** (costly on both sides — the engine's only both-sided moral choice), the trades in people (the captive trade, debt peonage, convict leasing, the Chinese trade, the re-enslavement and terror), and the army, the Ranger, and the Committee at their worst. The chapter is optional, and it is governed by the writing skill's hardest standards.

### Part IV — The County

These are the chapters of the county's web of interests, blood, paper, and communication — the social layer above the individual and below the nation.

**Factions and Standing (Ch.13)** is the chapter to read early, because half the book reaches for it. You will find a ladder of how a faction regards the party (sworn to hunted), the **Faction Web** — bonds and debts *between* factions, with propagation (helping one faction warms its allies and chills its enemies) and a seasonal Relationship Event table that shifts the graph under the party, a roll to call on a faction for help, **Influence** (a spendable, decaying political-capital pool that makes standing a current rather than a reservoir), the debt-call procedure (past favors turned into current demands), the Faction Roll (a single die each season for each faction to see what it did with its time and money), and Faction Clocks. It is the chapter that makes the world act back, and makes every choice a commitment.

**Families and Feuds (Ch.14)** is the chapter for the blood-and-property web that runs the county. You will find a way to design a family (name, nationality, holdings, traits, generations), a table of psychological trait-tags in the period's own vocabulary, family properties as the engine's holdings, the feud on **two axes** — the **Bond** (the Feud Clock's heat) and the **Debt** (who owes whom) — and the **Feud Move Menu** (the raid, the lawsuit, the slander, the mediation, the retaliation, the truce) that makes the feud a strategic contest between families, the **family events** — the wedding, the funeral, and the church-going — as Quarrels between families, each with its own goal and its own way of moving `Standing`, and a year-by-year **Generational Clock** that grows the family (births, young'uns who help with the work, hands who join the workforce and its holdings). Marriage is the engine's strongest allegiance-builder and its most public flashpoint.

**Inheritance, Land Office, and Speculation (Ch.15)** is the chapter for the paper war — the civil-law and finance layer the criminal court does not reach. You will find the land office as a federal-reach faction (the book and the seal), the contested title and the probate as an **evidence economy** (gather evidence — the prior filing, the survey, the witness, the forged codicil — and spend it at the hearing as leverage), insurance on the cargo and the building, and speculation as a downtime gamble and a tracked **Market** (a position held across seasons, with a price die that shifts by the season's pressures). The West was taken as much by the deed as by the rifle.

**The Wire (Ch.16)** gives the telegraph the mechanical definition three other chapters lean on. You will find the wire's cost and speed, the operator as an **Influence contact** (a managed relationship, not a flat modifier), the four ways to cut or compromise a line, and the wire-down condition that slows the county's pursuit and news to horse-speed. It is the reason a clean getaway stopped being clean.

**Mail and the Post (Ch.17)** is the chapter for the county's connection to the wider world on a schedule. You will find the route table (star route, stage, rail, express), the mailbag's contents (letters, deeds, news, payroll, powder), the mail run as a vocation and operation, the **mail schedule as a metronome** (the known days the county sets its clock by, and the strategic lever timing provides), the mail robbery as a **roadside ambush** — a light, mean scene mechanically distinct from the train job's full Operation — and the star-route contract as a faction's lever.

**The Press and the Election (Ch.18)** is the chapter for the county's peaceful weapons. You will find the press as a faction's loudspeaker (owned, subscription, allied, independent, opposition), a single check to go to press that moves `Fame` and `Standing` at county scale, the press as a **reputation engine** (a sustained campaign that shifts county-wide Influence over editions), the suppression as an operation, and the election as a multi-session operation — or a **cumulative contest** with an **electorate pool** that each campaign scene (the debate, the rally, the press, the canvass, the fraud) shifts across the campaign's weeks.

### Part V — The Wild Country

These are the chapters of the land itself — the weather, the country past the trail, the encounter, and the season that contracts it.

**Weather and the Trail's Teeth (Ch.19)** puts the sky on the table. You will find a regional weather table (season by season, with travel and danger modifiers), the **Weather Pattern** (a pattern die that tracks the *trend* — a dry stretch building toward fire risk, a cold front dropping toward a blizzard — so the watchful traveler can read what the weather is building toward), the **Exposure gauge** (the cumulative cost of sustained bad weather, wearing the party down shift by shift), the country's worst weather — the blizzard, the prairie fire, the flash flood — built as operations, and the drought as a slow pressure that withers a town and breaks an outfit. The weather adds no new ways to die; it decides when the ways you already have come due.

**Deep Wild Land Exploration (Ch.20)** is the chapter for the country past the trail. You will find three modes of wilderness travel — by water (steamboat, canoe, keelboat, raft), through forest (open pine to blowdown), and over mountain (with the elevation modifier) — each as stages of an expedition operation, **Expedition Supplies** (Provisions, Feed, and Gear as resource dice that deplete in the wild land, giving the journey's Trouble a source), a **Travel-Activity Menu** (push, careful, rest, scout — the daily decision the flat Progress track abstracted away), and the wild-land find as the seed of the next chapter's work. The river alone was weak; as one mode among three, it earns its place.

**Regional Encounter Tables (Ch.21)** is the chapter for the country between the towns, which was not empty. You will find the states grouped by terrain and temper into six regions (the Great Plains, the Desert Southwest, the Rocky Mountain country, the Pacific Slope, the Great Basin, the Border Country), each with a shared D66 encounter table whose entries are mostly not combat — the lone rider, the grazing herd, the waterhole, the sign, the emptiness — and the **Region State** (Tension, Game, and Sign ratings that make the country *remember* the party: clear the bandits and the road quiets; hunt out the game and the herds thin; bring the railroad and the traffic grows).

**Winter and Wintering (Ch.22)** is the chapter for the season the country closes. You will find the Winter Quarter as an interlude on the Turn of the Season — the count of stores, the "What Winter Brought" table, the monthly attrition across `Provisions`, `Horsestock`, `Cohesion`, and the conditions — the **Wintering Activity Menu** (ration, cut wood, tend stock, hold the cabin, risk the ride — the winter as a season of decisions, not a grind), the **Cabin Fever Track** (the close air's erosion of the household's peace, the winter's distinct social cost), and the spring thaw as the campaign's annual reset. Winter is the season the rest of the book's gauges all climb at once.

### Part VI — The Nation and the Decade

These are the chapters of the largest scales — the war, the great operators, and the political weather of the decade.

**Mass Combat and the Skirmish Line (Ch.23)** is the chapter for the fight too large for the player characters' pistols. You will find the unit abstraction (Quality, Strength, Cohesion), the skirmish turn as an opposed unit roll, the player character as an attached leader, the battle wrapped in the operation framework, and the artillery, the Gatling, the charge, the fortification, the siege, and the running fight as special cases. It is the chapter that lets the Indian Wars and the county war play at their true size.

**Robber Barons and Their Work (Ch.24)** is the chapter for the great operators whose reach is territorial or federal. You will find the baron as a faction of one, a table of the work and opportunities he offers a party (the survey job, the land job, the dirty job, the war job), the price of his regard, and historical profiles — Stanford and Huntington, Gould, Catron and the Santa Fe Ring, the cattle syndicates — held to the standard of the period's uncomfortable truths.

**The Political Scene of the 1870s (Ch.25)** is the chapter for the national party layer above the factions, and the book's most ambitious. You will find the Republican and Democratic coalitions (with their internal tensions and the Liberal Republican and Greenback eruptions), the acts as national modifiers (the Enforcement Acts through the Posse Comitatus and the Chinese Exclusion), and a year-by-year timeline of the decade's political and world events, with the modifier shifts each year produces. A campaign set in any year plays under that year's weather — the Panic, the Great Sioux War, the Compromise of 1877, the silver question — and the metaplot is the engine's way of making the decade the campaign's, and not a generic West.

## A Note on What Is Coming

The book's core is twenty-five chapters of developed rules, from factions to the political scene of the decade. A short list of smaller situational gaps waits in the wings — specific river systems, the survey party as operation, the doctor as a business, the bounty-hunter vocation, the holiday and festival calendar. Each has its raw material already written in the corebook and the 1870s supplement; the work is conversion, not invention, and the chapters will be built on the same engine and held to the same standard as the ones here, when a campaign calls for them.

## The Design Bible

The understanding of the game that produced this book is written down in `/docs/design-bible.md`, at the repository root. That file is the engineering under the prose — the gauges, the tables, the resolution vocabulary, the downtime spine, and the map of how the chapters connect. A reader who wants to extend this book, audit it, or build another one beside it should read the design bible first. This book is one application of the engine. The bible is the engine itself.

The country is dry and the country is watched. The rules in this book are written to make both of those things felt, at the table, in the choices the player characters make and the consequences the county hands back. That is the whole of the book's purpose.
