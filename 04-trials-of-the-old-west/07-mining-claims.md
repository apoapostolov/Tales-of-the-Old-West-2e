## Chapter 7 - Mining Claims and the Ground's Promise

> _THE GROUND DOES NOT PROMISE. THE GROUND IS. THE MEN WHO GO DOWN INTO IT ARE THE ONES DOING THE PROMISING, AND THEY ARE PROMISING IT TO THEMSELVES._
>
> — ELI PENSINGER, TO A YOUNG MINER WHO SAID HE HAD A GOOD FEELING

### The Ground and Its Trade

The prospector and the hardrock miner are half the West's other economy, and the corebook's setting chapter and the 1870s supplement's `mining-camps.md` knowledge render the camp, the shift, the hierarchy, and the cost in the dust. What neither gives you is the claim and the strike as procedures — the act of finding color, the legal fact of staking the ground, the season's roll of a working mine, and the long, grinding Trouble of the dust in a man's lungs. This chapter is the missing half.

It is an optional framework for groups who want the diggings on the table. It builds **prospecting** as a multi-check search across country. It builds **claim staking** as a legal and factional procedure, grounded in the Mining Law of 1872. It builds the **working mine** as a seasonal enterprise parallel to the corebook's business rules, with a `Mine Yield` Bonus/Penalty table that pays in clean cash and charges in dust and cave-ins. And it folds in the mine as a place — the shaft, the drift, the stope — as a site-generator for the underground scenes the camp produces.

You do not need these rules for a prospector's one-off pan in a creek. Use them when the diggings are the campaign — when the party has a claim to defend or to jump, a mine to work, or a company town's factions pressing on the county.

> _Pensinger set the round and lit the fuse and came around the corner of the drift and waited with his palm flat on the wet wall. ### Prospecting

A prospector looks for color in country the geology suggests might hold it. The corebook's `Miner 49 Er` and `Mountain Folk` talents are the natural edges here, and `HAWKEYE`, `NATURE`, and `BOOKLEARNIN'` are the abilities, depending on whether the prospector is reading the surface, reading the country, or reading the company's library of survey reports.

Prospecting is a **search operation** — a sequence of checks across country, where the Progress is finding a prospect worth staking and the Trouble is the cost of the looking.

| Prospect Scale | Progress Needed | Trouble Limit | The Search |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `A Pan in the Creek` | `2` | `2` | A day's work in a known drainage; placer color, or nothing |
| `A Prospect Trip` | `4` | `3` | Weeks in the country; the search for a lode or a paying placer |
| `A Wildcat Venture` | `6` | `4` | A funded expedition into unproven ground; the search for a new district |

**Progress** is finding a prospect — a showing of color worth the cost of a claim. **Trouble** is the search's cost and its dangers: bad water, bad weather, a claim already staked by a man with a rifle, the company's surveyor who follows the prospector's tracks, a partner's claim-jump, the country's people whose ground the prospector is walking. Each stage is tagged Safe / Risky / Desperate.

| Stage of the Search | The Problem | Likely Ability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reading the geology | Choose the ground; read the indicator minerals, the contact, the float | `BOOKLEARNIN'`, `HAWKEYE`, `NATURE` |
| The traverse | Cover the country; find the water, the camp, the way in | `NATURE`, `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` |
| The test pits and the pans | Work the likely spots; read the color, the flour, the nuggets | `LABOR`, `HAWKEYE` |
| The showing | Find the lode or the placer that justifies a stake | `BOOKLEARNIN'` (assay), `LABOR` |
| The country | Pass through the people whose ground it is, and the company whose survey it might be | `PRESENCE`, `INSIGHT` |

A prospector who fills the Progress track has a prospect: a place worth staking. A prospector who hits the Trouble limit before finding color has spent the season for nothing, or worse — lost a partner, crossed a faction, or caught the attention of the company's detectives.

#### Placer and Lode

The distinction matters at the table. A **placer** claim is a stretch of creek or gravel worked from the surface — pans, rockers, sluices, hydraulics in the bigger operations. It is cheaper to work, faster to exhaust, and the entry point of every rush. A **lode** claim is a vein of mineral in hard rock, worked underground by the drill and the powder. It is expensive to work, slow to exhaust, and the entry point of the company. The same district often holds both: the placers that drew the rush, and the lodes the rush's survivors chased when the placers played out. The prospector's choice of which to chase sets the shape of the campaign.

### The Claim and the Law

A prospect is worth nothing legal until it is a claim. The Mining Law of 1872 — the law that governs the mineral West of this game's setting — lets a man stake a claim on the public domain, mark its corners, file its notice at the county seat, and work it, in exchange for annual assessment work and the obligation to actually mine the ground. The law is simple. The men who work under it are not.

#### Staking the Claim

Staking is a procedure of discovery, marking, and filing. Treat it as a `Small` operation (`4` Progress, `3` Trouble), with the abilities shifting by the stage.

| Stage | The Problem | Likely Ability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The discovery cut | Prove the mineral is there, in the ground, in paying or promising amount | `LABOR`, `BOOKLEARNIN'` (assay) |
| The monuments | Mark the claim's corners on the ground, to the law's shape and size | `LABOR`, `NATURE` |
| The filing | File the notice at the county seat; record the location, the date, the claimant | `BOOKLEARNIN'`, `Lawyer` |
| The assessment | Do the annual work the law requires, and record it, or lose the claim | `LABOR` |

The filing is the legal door, and it is the door the faction system walks through. A claim filed in a county whose `Civic` aspect is low may never be recorded, or may be recorded twice — once for the prospector and once for the company's man at the recorder's window. A claim in a county whose `Law` is low is a claim defended by the claimant and his rifle, not by the sheriff. A claim on ground a faction wants — the company, the land-grant operators, the reservation line — is a claim that faction will contest, by the law if it can, by other means if it must.

#### The Company and the Union

The mine is not a neutral place, and the men who work it are not neutral men. Two factions contest most hardrock districts.

- **The Company** is the faction that owns the shaft, the mill, the company store, and often the county seat's recorder. Its purpose is the dividend, and its means are the wage, the debt, the detective, and the closed room where the price of powder is set. A claim the company wants is a claim the company takes, by law or by purchase or by the simple pressure of being the only buyer and the only store.
- **The Union** (the Western Federation, the Knights, whatever form the district's organization takes) is the faction of the men in the drift. Its purpose is the wage, the eight-hour shift, and the living man over the dividend. Its means are the strike, the boycott, and the closed meeting. A prospector who becomes an owner is no longer the union's man, and a miner who works the company's drift is no longer his own.

The tension between company and union is the mine's engine at the faction level. A campaign in a hardrock district will find the party choosing a side, or being chosen by one, the way every man in the district was made to choose.

### The Working Mine as Business

A claim that is filed and worked is an enterprise, run as a seasonal business parallel to the corebook's outfit rules. The placer and the lode are both run this way, with different key abilities and different yields.

- **Key ability (placer):** `LABOR` (the work is surface, the men are few, the equipment is the rocker and the sluice). A hydraulic operation shifts the key ability to `OPERATE` and the scale up.
- **Key ability (lode):** `Miner 49 Er` or `LABOR`, the proprietor's or the shift boss's. A lode worked by a crew reads `MAKIN'` (the timber, the machinery) and `BOOKLEARNIN'` (the engineering) as help abilities.
- **Prerequisites:** a filed claim, a property (a shaft, a drift, a mill site), and stock or capital sufficient to the scale. A lone miner with a single-jack works a prospect; a company works a mine.
- **Town aspect modifiers:** the mine reads the town's `Natural Riches` (the district's grade) and `Law` (the claim's protection), in addition to the universal `Law` modifier. A mine in a `Natural Riches 1` district is a mine that plays out; a mine in a `Law 1` district is a mine the claim-jumper takes.

#### The Season Mine Roll

The mine's Season Roll is made at the Turn of the Season, like any business roll, by the proprietor or the shift boss. It cannot be pushed, and it does not generate Trouble — the mine generates its own Trouble, in the dust and the cave-in, which is what the Bonus/Penalty table is for. Read the roll on the standard business ladder: `0` a Penalty roll, `1` steady (wages and powder paid), `2+` a Bonus roll with `+1` to the Tens die per success beyond the first.

#### Mine Yield Table

| D66 | Mine Bonus | Mine Penalty |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `11-13` | A good stope. `D6 × $60` in clean cash from the clean-up. | The grade dropped. `D6 × $50` in lost yield; reassess or go deeper. |
| `14-16` | A new vein. `+1` Capital in ore or equipment, immediately realized. | A cave-in. Lose `D6 × $75` in cleanup costs and `1` to `2` weeks of work; test for the men (Trouble). |
| `21-23` | A bonanza pocket. `2D6 × $100` in specie or high-grade. | Silicosis. One named miner (a compadre, a player character, the shift boss) takes `1` permanent `Doubts` and a chronic cough; the dust is collecting. |
| `24-26` | The mill paid. `D6 × $150` from the clean-up at good rates. | A fire in the shaft or the works. `D6 × $100` in damage; production halted a season. |
| `31-33` | A deep strike. `+1` to the mine's effective `Natural Riches` next season. | The company moved in. `Standing` with the company faction drops two steps; the mine is now contested. |
| `34-36` | The union held, the wage held, the shift ran. `+1` to next season's Mine Roll. | A strike or a lockout. No yield this season; `Standing` with company and union both in flux. |
| `41-43` | A buyer at the assay office. `D6 × $200` cash for high-grade at a good price. | A claim-jump. Lose `1` Capital in ground, or fight for it in court (`Lawyer`) or out of it. |
| `44-46` | The district boomed. `+1` Capital in claims or stock, realized. | The district busted. `Natural Riches` reduced by `1` rank; the mine's future in question. |
| `51-56` | A strike of high-grade the likes of which the district has not seen. `+2` Capital. | A bad blast, a bad shaft, a bad season. `2D6 × $75` in losses; a named man dead or maimed. |
| `61-66` | The mother lode. `+1D3` Capital and the district's attention — for good and ill. | The mine played out. The claim is exhausted; the Capital is gone; the men move on. |

A mine that rolls Penalty season after season is a mine going broke or going dark, and the engine allows it. A mine that rolls Bonus season after season is a mine the faction system will notice — the company, the union, the claim-jumper, and the bench all want a piece of a paying dig, and the faction engine is how they come for it. The dust is the Trouble that does not ask permission: a Bonus roll does not stop the dust from collecting, and a campaign in a hardrock district should let the silicosis accumulate in the background, the way the actual camps let it accumulate, until the cough becomes the engine of a miner's retirement or his end.

### The Mine as a Place

The mine is also a dungeon. A working hardrock mine is a three-dimensional site — shaft, drift, stope, winze, raise — driven into the mountain by the work, and the GM who needs an underground scene has the mine's vocabulary to draw on. The `mining-camps.md` knowledge file in the writing skill is the reference for the shaft, the drift, the stope, the timber, the skip, the drill, the powder, the muck, and the smell of the drift at the end of a shift. The mine is a place the party goes into for rescue, for theft, for a meeting, or for the working of a claim, and the procedures of this chapter are what make the place live.

A cave-in is a `Trouble` result on the Mine Roll, and it is also a scene: the drift that closed on the men, the race to dig them out, the man at the face whose air is running. A rescue is a `Small` operation, with `LABOR`, `MAKIN'`, and `RESILIENCE` as the abilities and the clock of the trapped men's air as the Trouble. The mine's underground scenes use the corebook's conditions — `Exhausted`, `Dehydrated`, the disease rules — and the mine's own Trouble: the gas, the dust, the bad air that kills a man at the face before anyone knows it is there.

A campaign that uses these rules will find that the ground has a memory — of the men who worked it, the dust they breathed, and the dividend it paid or refused. The mine is the prospector's outlaw score, run across seasons instead of hours, paid in ore instead of cash, and judged by the ground rather than the county.
