## Chapter 9 - Logging, Fur Trade, and Hunting

> _THE WILD LAND'S TRADE IS THE TRADE OF TAKING WHAT GROWS AND TURNING IT INTO WHAT SELLS — THE TREE INTO THE BOARD, THE BEAVER INTO THE HAT, THE BUFFALO INTO THE ROBE. THE MEN WHO DID THIS WORK LIVED WHERE THE WORK WAS, AND WHERE THE WORK WAS WAS THE COUNTRY THE COUNTIES HAD NOT REACHED YET._
>
> — WEBB CALDER, TO A DUDE WHO ASKED WHAT MEN DID PAST THE RANGE

### The Wild Trades and Their Work

The wild land's chapter (Ch.21) gives the party the modes of travel into the country past the trail. This chapter gives the party the **trades** that took men into that country and kept them there: the logging camp that took the timber, the trapline that took the fur, and the market hunt that took the hide and the meat. These are the three extractive industries of the wild land, and they share a shape — a seasonal enterprise built on a wild resource, a hard operation to bring the take out, and a trade that emptied the country it worked as surely as the rail and the plow did.

The corebook treats these as lifepaths and salary lines — the trapper, the lumberjack, the market hunter are archetypes, not procedures. The 1870s supplement renders them in rich procedural prose (the buffalo-hide hunter's stand, the wolfer's strychnine, the cornishman's shift) with no dice. This chapter gives them their mechanics: the **logging camp** as a business and the river drive as an operation; the **trapline** as a seasonal enterprise and the rendezvous as a trade crossroads; the **market hunt** as a take-based trade and the buffalo's decline as the engine's honest accounting of the country the trades emptied.

> _The logger stood at the river's edge and watched the last of the winter's drive come through the boom, which was the logs caught in the pocket and waiting for the mill, and the mill was the year's wages and the camp's keep and the company's dividend, and the river had brought it all down from the timber in the high country. The drive had cost two men — one drowned at the jam at the narrows, one crushed by the swing of a sawlog at the take-out — and the logger counted the cost the way the company did not, because the company counted the board feet and the logger counted the names._
>
> "How much made the boom?"
>
> "The better part of the cut. We'll make the winter's wages."
>
> "And the two?"
>
> "The two don't make the boom. The two make the count at the camp and the letter to the widows. The drive's the drive, and the river takes what it takes." The logger did not look away from the water. "Next winter we go back up and we cut it again, and the river brings it down again, and the county builds its houses out of what the high country grew and the river carried and the men died for. That's the trade. That's all the trade is."

### Logging and the Lumber Camp

Logging is the trade that built the West's houses, its mines' timber, and its railroads' ties, and the lumber camp is the trade's home — a seasonal camp in the timber, a crew of axemen and teamsters and rivermen, and a company that sells the cut and pays the wages. The logging camp is a **seasonal enterprise** parallel to the cattle chapter's ranch and the mining chapter's mine, with its own key ability, its own take, and its own Trouble.

#### The Lumber Camp as Business

| The Camp | What It Is |
| --- | --- |
| Key ability | `LABOR` (the axeman's and the teamster's trade) or `MAKIN'` (the sawyer's and the millwright's) |
| Prerequisites | A timber tract (the land office chapter's claim or a company lease), a crew, the tools (axe, crosscut saw, the skidding gear), and a mill or a market |
| Town aspect modifiers | `Natural Riches` (the timber's quality and access) and `Mercantile` (the market for the cut) |
| The Season Log Roll | `0` a Penalty, `1` steady (wages and keep paid), `2+` a Bonus with `+1` to the Tens die per success beyond the first |

The logging Bonus and Penalty table follows the cattle and mining chapters' shape — the good winter's cut, the lost drive, the fire in the camp, the jam at the narrows, the market's price, the company's hand. The logging Trouble is the wild land's: the weather (the weather chapter and the winter chapter), the river drive's drownings, the falling tree's kill, the fire that takes the camp and the cut, and the simple attrition of men working in timber with sharp tools and heavy loads.

#### The River Drive as Operation

The river drive — floating the winter's cut from the timber to the mill — is logging's signature operation, and it is the logging chapter's centerpiece the way the stampede is the cattle chapter's. The drive is a `Medium` operation (`6` Progress, `4` Trouble): the Progress is the logs delivered to the boom, and the Trouble is the river — the jam (the logs' pile-up, broken by the riverman with a pike and a prayer), the drown (the man who went in and did not come up), the high water and the low, the weather, and the mill's competitor who cut the boom and sent the cut downstream.

| Stage of the Drive | The Problem | Likely Ability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The break-up | Start the logs moving with the spring thaw | `LABOR`, `NATURE` (the read of the water) |
| The run | Ride the logs down the river, pike in hand | `OPERATE`, `LABOR`, `RESILIENCE` |
| The jam | Break the pile-up before it floods the banks | `LABOR`, `MAKIN'` (the dynamite, if the jam is bad), `MOVE` (the quick work on the rolling logs) |
| The boom | Catch the cut at the mill's pocket | `LABOR`, `OPERATE` |

A river drive is one of the West's most dangerous operations, and the engine should let it kill — the drowning rules (the corebook) and the crush of the sawlog are the Trouble the drive's Troubles roll on, and a bad drive costs the camp the men the logger counted in the vignette. The drive is also the logging chapter's great set-piece, and a campaign in the timber country will find the drive is the season's climax the way the round-up is the cattle chapter's.

### The Fur Trade and the Trapline

The fur trade is the oldest of the West's trades, and by the 1870s it is the trade in decline — the beaver hat is going out, the buffalo robe is still in, and the mountain man's era is ending in the market hunter's and the reservation's. The trade is still a livelihood, however, for the trapper who runs his line in the high country and sells his take at the rendezvous or the post, and the trade is still the reason men go into the wild land and stay there.

#### The Trapline as Seasonal Enterprise

| The Trapline | What It Is |
| --- | --- |
| Key ability | `NATURE` (the read of the country and the fur-bearer) or `HAWKEYE` (the sign and the set) |
| Prerequisites | A line (the deep-wild-land chapter's country), the traps, the pack animals, and a post or a rendezvous to sell the take |
| Town aspect modifiers | `Natural Riches` (the fur-bearer's country) and `Law` (the line's protection from other trappers and the nation whose country it is) |
| The Season Trap Roll | `0` a Penalty (a poor take, a lost line, a trapped man), `1` steady (the wages of a hard season), `2+` a Bonus with `+1` to the Tens die per success beyond the first |

The trapping Bonus and Penalty table follows the cattle and mining shape — the good winter's take, the lost trapline (the rival, the nation, the weather), the Spoiled pelt, the market's price, the company post's hand. The trapping Trouble is the wild land's, plus the trade's own: the trap that takes the man (the corebook's traps and the injury table), the nation whose country the line is in (the `Standing` and the encounter), and the fur-bearer's decline (the engine's honest accounting of the country the trade emptied).

#### The Rendezvous

The rendezvous is the fur trade's crossroads — the annual gathering of the trappers, the traders, the nations, and the company at a fixed place and time, where the winter's take is traded for the next season's supplies and the year's news and gossip are exchanged. The rendezvous is a **scene** (the trade chapter's crossroads frame, scaled up): a multi-faction trade fair where the trapper's take meets the company's goods, the nations meet the traders, and the `Standing` shifts of a season are settled in trade, drink, and the occasional fight.

A rendezvous is also the engine's opportunity — the place a party meets the wild land's factions (the nations, the companies, the rival trappers), the place a wild-land expedition is fitted out, and the place the fur trade's business is done. The GM may use the rendezvous as the campaign's annual anchor the way the families chapter uses the rites — the fixed point the wild land's year turns on.

### The Market Hunt

The market hunt is the trade that took the West's game at industrial scale — the buffalo-hide hunter who took fifty beeves a day for the robe, the market hunter who took the antelope and the elk for the railhead's table, the wolfer who took the wolf for the bounty and left the strychnine-baited carcass for whatever else found it. The market hunt is the trade the 1870s supplement renders in the most detail, and the trade the engine should let empty the country it works.

#### The Market Hunt as Take-Based Trade

The market hunt is not a seasonal enterprise in the cattle and mining shape; it is a **take-based trade** whose payoff is the take multiplied by the market price, and whose Trouble is the country's decline.

| The Take | The Price (the trade chapter's spatial economy applies) | The Trouble |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The buffalo robe | `$1-3` at the rail, more finished | The herd's decline; the nation's anger (the encounter, the `Standing`); the weather; the skinning crew's labor |
| The market meat (antelope, elk, deer) | `5-10¢` a pound at the railhead | The game's decline; the spoilage; the railhead's demand |
| The wolf scalp (the bounty) | `$1-5` a scalp, by the county's bounty | The strychnine's collateral (the dead dog, the dead eagle, the dead man's stock); the wolfer's reputation |
| The hide and the fur (the smaller trade) | the fur trade's prices, above | The trapline's Trouble |

A season of market hunting is resolved as an operation (the deep-wild-land chapter's expedition frame): the Progress is the take, the Trouble is the country's resistance and decline. A market hunt that succeeds wildly (`3+`) is a market hunt that took a great deal out of the country, which is the engine's honest accounting — the take that made the trade's money is the take that emptied the country, and the campaign that runs the market hunt for several seasons will find the encounter table's game entries thinning and the nation's `Standing` dropping, which is the history the trade made.

#### The Buffalo's Decline

The buffalo's decline is the engine's metanarrative for the market hunt, and a campaign in the plains country should let it happen. Each season of heavy market hunting in a region reduces the region's buffalo-herd entries on the encounter table (the regional-encounter chapter) and shifts the `Standing` of the plains nations (the Lakota, the Cheyenne, the Comanche) toward the hostile, for the obvious and historical reason. The buffalo's decline is the engine's way of making the market hunt's success the country's loss, which is the truth the trade did not face until the country was empty and the trade was over.

### The Wild Trades and the Other Rules

The wild trades are the extractive industries of the wild land, and they tie the book's wilderness and economy threads together.

- **The deep-wild-land chapter (Ch.21)** is the country the trades work in — the modes of travel, the stages, the Trouble the wild land imposes.
- **The regional-encounter chapter (Ch.22)** is the country's life the trades take from — the game entries the market hunt thins and the fur trade traps.
- **The cattle, mining, and trade chapters** are the trades' settled-country cousins — the ranch, the mine, the freight outfit are the enterprises these trades are the wild versions of.
- **The faction engine** holds the company, the nation, and the rival trapper as the trade's factions; the robber baron chapter holds the company and the market's distant hand.

The wild land's trade is the trade of taking what grows and turning it into what sells, and a chapter that gives the log its drive, the fur its trapline, and the robe its hunt gives the campaign the country's extractive edge — the trade that lived where the counties had not reached, and that emptied the country it worked as surely as the rail and the plow did. The men who did this work lived where the work was, and the engine should let the work pay what it paid and cost what it cost, in the country that did not care about the trade except as another thing the market could take.
