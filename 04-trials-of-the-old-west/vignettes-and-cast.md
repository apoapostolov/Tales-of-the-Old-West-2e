# Vignettes and Cast — Trials of the Old West

This book is built around a small interlocking company of people who live
in one corner of country. They open the chapters, demonstrate the
mechanics in the flesh, and appear in every worked example. They are not
player characters. They are the ground the rules walk on.

The cast is a web, not a list. Each figure knows the others. Each carries
a debt, a history, or a silence where another figure is concerned. When a
chapter opens, its anchor figure is on the page, but at least one of the
others is in the room, or on the road, or owing money. That is how a
country works.

Why a recurring cast: the outlaw chapter of the corebook does this with
Jeb, Mexican Joe, and Luther Vale, and it is the reason those rules feel
like they belong to a place rather than a spreadsheet. This book
extends that method across the book so the whole shares one
populated corner of the West.

---

## The country

The corner of country where these seven lives cross is not named on the
map of the real West. It is a composite of the country the game is built
on: high desert basin and grassland below the Sangre de Cristos and the
Cimarron country of northern New Mexico, with a railhead, a mining
district, a cavalry post within a hard ride, and a county seat where the
circuit judge comes twice a year. The river is small and runs bitter in
late summer. The grass is good in wet years and gone in dry ones. It is
the kind of country where the law, the railroad, the mine, the post, and
the open range all press on the same few hundred people, which is why it
is the right place to show every rule in this book.

Adelina Sandoval's way-station sits at the hinge of it: the stage road,
the cattle trail, the track to the diggings, and the military road all
pass within a mile of her spring. Everyone waters at her place. That is
how a Nuevomexicana trader with a ledger and a long memory ends up at the
center of every tale.

## The cast

### Adelina Sandoval — the way-station

Nuevomexicana, forty-one, widow of a teamster killed on the Rayado years
ago. Runs the way-station and trading post at the county's hinge: a
store, a kitchen that feeds whoever pays or works, a few rooms, a stable,
and the only reliable spring between the river and the mountains. Keeps
the ledger in Spanish and English both. Educated at the Loretto Academy
in Santa Fe before her father died and the money died with him. Reads the
newspapers a month late and remembers what they said. Sharp tongue, dry
wit, a Catholic's sense of account that does not stop at the church door.

She is the moral center and the bookkeeper of every debt. Nothing moves
through her counter that she does not note. She knows what a man earns,
what he owes, who he rides with, and what his horse is worth, because all
of it crosses her counter in goods or paper. She does not judge quickly.
She judges completely.

**Voice fingerprint.** The educated woman who has come down in the world
and come out harder. Sentences are complete and direct. Vocabulary is
bilingual when she is angry or counting (_pidón_, _ayudante_, _ni modo_)
but never performed. She does not raise her voice. She does not soften a
number. Her refusals are quiet: she will not say _please_ to a man who
owes her, and she will not say _I am worried_ when the ledger will say it
for her. Her beat is the pencil behind the ear, the hand turning the
ledger, the eyes on the door while she talks. The laconic register
borrowed from the hands she feeds, but with the grammar whole.

**Anchors.** Chapter 2 (Factions & Standing) and Chapter 3 (Weather).
Appears in justice, mining, and cattle vignettes — her counter is where
the county's business gets done.

**What she wants.** To keep the place through the dry years and the hard
ones, and to leave it whole to her sister's boy.

**What she fears.** That the drought, the Ring, or the railroad will take
the spring the way they took her husband.

### Webb Calder — the trail boss

Anglo, mid-forties, born in burnet County, Texas. Trail boss on the
northern drives, before that a top hand, before that a teenager riding
with a bad crowd he will not name. Married once; the wife and a daughter
died of fever in `72 while he was on the trail. Has not been back to the
house. Drives stock because it is the only work that fills the silence.
Laconic, competent, fair to his crew, dangerous when crossed, and
carrying a guilt he will never name because the trail does not trade in
names for that kind of thing.

**Voice fingerprint.** The laconic ranch hand, but aged into authority.
Short declarative sentences. Trade vocabulary, Anglo-Saxon, biblical only
in the rare oath. Idiom: _I reckon, I expect, I figure, looks like, suit
yourself, the hell you say, that's how she lays._ Refuses to name
feeling. Will say _I'd hate to leave that horse_ and mean everything
else. The pause before a hard answer is its own line. Builds a smoke when
the question is hard. Looks at the herd, not the man.

**Anchors.** Chapter 6 (Cattle Drives). Appears in mass combat (he has
seen the herds stampede at the smell of powder) and in the weather
chapter (he has been caught on the high plains by a blue norther and
marked by it).

**What he wants.** To bring the herd in and sit quiet through the winter.

**What he fears.** Another house emptied while he was on the trail.

### Judge Harlan Voss — the circuit

Anglo, fifty-eight, appointed by the territorial bench, circuit judge for
three counties that between them support one courtroom and a half. Reads
law in Indiana before the war, rides with the Union, comes west in `68
with a commission and a bad knee that forecasts the weather better than
any barometer. Orotund, weary, unmarried, convinced of the necessity of
law and clear-eyed about its reach. Holds court in saloons, in sod
courthouses, on hotel porches; once in a barn because the only witness
was too shot through the lung to be moved. Has sentenced nine men to hang
and slept after every one of them, badly.

**Voice fingerprint.** The orotund judge, but worn down by distance and
use. Long periodic sentences in the courtroom (_Be that as it may, the
territory having seen fit…_); short, plain ones off the bench. Latinate
vocabulary in the charge, Anglo-Saxon in the glass afterward. Idiom: _be
that as it may, the court is of the view, I will hear the man, let us
have done._ Refuses to bluster. Refuses to pretend the law is more than
it is. His beat is the spectacles off and on, the hand on the bad knee,
the long look at the man in the dock before he speaks.

**Anchors.** Chapter 4 (Justice, Trial & Incarceration). Appears in the
factions chapter (the bench as an institution) and in mass combat (the
law at the edge of a county that cannot keep order by itself).

**What he wants.** To leave the bench with the country a shade less mean
than he found it.

**What he fears.** That he has hanged an innocent man and will never know
which one.

### Eli Pensinger — the hardrock

Cornish, thirty-seven, come to the district by way of Michigan's copper
country and before that the tin at home. Hardrock miner, shift boss when
the superintendent will have him, single-jack double-jack hand always.
Half-deaf from the drills; watches your mouth when you talk. Sits with
his back to the wall. Precise with tools, with timber, with the blast, with
the assay. The drills took his hearing; the dust has begun to take his
breath. He has not told the company doctor. He has not told anyone. The
men in the district trust him because he sets a timber that holds and
names a bad round before anyone lights it.

**Voice fingerprint.** The silent competent man, made more silent by the
deafness. Fragments. _Aye. No. Set it there._ Cornish words for the work
(_kibble, skip, stope, winze, bal_). Watches the mouth. Does not raise
his voice because he cannot gauge it. Writes a clear hand when the words
will not come. His beat is the palm flat on the rock to feel what the ear
cannot hear, the chew tucked in the cheek, the eyes on the ceiling. He
refuses to guess about ground. _I'll go look,_ he says, and goes.

**Anchors.** Chapter 7 (Mining Claims & the Ground's Promise). Appears in
the factions chapter (the miners' union and the company) and in weather
(what the rains do to the tailings and the timbers).

**What he wants.** One good pocket of his own, enough to go home to
Cornwall with the breath he has left.

**What he fears.** The dust. He will not say the word.

### Sergeant Moses Carver — the Tenth

Black, thirty-four, born free in Newbern before the war, enlisted in `66
into the Ninth Cavalry and transferred to the Tenth the season after. Has
ridden every column the regiment has run in the district and a few the
records do not show. Professional in the saddle and bitter off it in equal
measure, the bitterness held down to a low steady pressure that surfaces
only as a flatness in the voice when a white officer is wrong and everyone
in earshot knows it. Writes a clean report. Reads everything he can steal
off the post. Married in the abstract to a woman in Leavenworth he sees
six weeks in two years. Has saved the lives of two lieutenants who never
thanked him and one settler family who did, in writing, to the
commanding officer, who filed it.

**Voice fingerprint.** The soldier, with the Black freedman's careful
public register layered under it. Short sentences. Trade and army
vocabulary (_detachment, requisition, dismounted, stand-to_). Publicly,
the grammar is exact and the anger is not in the words; the anger is in
what the words refuse. _Yessir. The sergeant's view, sir._ Privately,
with Pensinger or Sandoval or Calder, the sentences lengthen and the
Latinate drops and the humor comes through, dry and aimed upward. His
beat is the hand on the carbine, the eyes on the country, the unhurried
way he sits a horse. He refuses to be hurried by a man who outranks him.

**Anchors.** Chapter 8 (Mass Combat & the Skirmish Line). Appears in
weather (campaigning in the blue norther), justice (the army as the long
arm of a court that cannot keep order), and factions (the post and the
people it patrols).

**What he wants.** The honorable discharge, the pension, the woman in
Leavenworth, and to ride out of the country one time without a wire
calling him back.

**What he fears.** That he dies in a fight he was ordered into by a fool,
and the record calls it something it was not.

### Dutch Miller — the throttle

German-American, twenty-nine, born Heinrich Möller in Allegheny, ran away
to the fireman's gang at fifteen and worked his way up to engineer. Runs
the mixed freight-and-passenger on the district's single track, the one
that stops at the way-station platform three times a week. OPERATE
incarnate: knows his engine the way Calder knows a horse, by sound and
smell and the way she holds the grade. Twice carried an express box of
payroll and once been looked at hard by men who wanted it; both times the
look decided against. Carries a shotgun under the coal. Reads the
railroad's mood by the wire traffic and knows trouble is coming before
the agent does.

**Voice fingerprint.** The working-class operator, German syntax under
the English when he is tired (_Ach, she will hold it_). Sentences run with
the machine: clipped when the work is on, long and fond when the engine
is the subject. Trade vocabulary (_throttle, Johnson bar,injector, blower,
firebox, pop valves_). Idiom: _she'll take it, hold her steady, we'll make
the water, that's a good girl._ Watches the gauge glass the way Sandoval
watches the ledger. Refuses to be hurried by a conductor; will be
hurried by a low water level. His beat is the hand on the Johnson bar, the
coal shovel, the wipe of the face with the rag.

**Anchors.** Chapter 5 (Train Robbery & the Iron Horse). Appears in
factions (the railroad as a power) and in the cast web (he is the one who
brings the newspapers to Sandoval and the mail to Voss).

**What he wants.** The seniority run to the coast and a house in
Albuquerque with a porch.

**What he fears.** A wreck he caused by pride or haste, and the men in
the cars behind him.

### Sam Riddle — the through-line

Anglo, thirty-one, former outlaw, trying to stay straight, failing. Rode
with the worst of the local gangs through his twenties, walked away after
a job in Lincoln County went ugly and a man who should have lived did
not. Works now as a rider for Sandoval's freight concern and as an extra
hand on Calder's drives, which is how a man with a price on his head
somewhere east of here ends up watering horses at the spring of a woman
who knows exactly what he is. The ledger is what keeps him: Sandoval
holds his wages against the fine he will owe if the past catches him, and
the past is always catching him. Connects this book to the outlaw chapter
of the corebook; he is the man for whom Wanted, Justice, and the faction
pressures of the railroad and the Ring all have a face.

**Voice fingerprint.** The quiet man who used to be loud. Short
sentences, careful. The outlaw slang surfaces under pressure (_score,
heat, the split, the fence_) but he has trained himself out of it and the
effort shows. Watches doors. Sits where he can see the room. Idiom: _I
just work here. That ain't my business. I'd as soon not._ Refuses to brag.
Refuses to talk about Lincoln. His beat is the hand resting easy near the
pistol, the eyes that measure distance to every exit, the way he eats with
his back to the wall. When Sandoval counts his debt out loud he says
nothing and pays it.

**Anchors.** The through-line character. Opens or appears in the
factions, justice, train robbery, and mass combat vignettes. He is the
figure who feels every rule in this book on his own skin, because every
rule in this book is the kind of rule that falls hardest on a man trying
to outrun what he was.

**What he wants.** To be the man Sandoval's ledger says he is trying to
become, and to live long enough to become him.

**What he fears.** That the man he was is the man he is, and the ledger
is wishful accounting.

## The web

The cast is not seven strangers. It is one corner of country.

- **Voss lodges at Sandoval's** when court is in session; he pays in
  script and she puts it in the ledger at par because the bench is good
  for it. They argue about the law and the weather and neither yields.
- **Calder drove stock for Riddle once**, years ago, before Riddle walked
  out of the life. Calder will not discuss it. Riddle is grateful for the
  silence and reads it as judgment, which is also correct.
- **Pensinger assays at Sandoval's counter**; she fronts him provisions
  against the season's ore and keeps the assay numbers in a separate book
  no one else sees.
- **Carver's patrol waters at Sandoval's spring**; the standing order is
  that the Tenth's horses drink first, on the house, because the
  alternative is a patrol that hates her.
- **Dutch Miller's train stops at her platform**; he brings the
  newspapers, the mail, and the mood of the railroad, and he owes her for
  a winter she put him up when the engine threw a rod.
- **Voss has sentenced a man Riddle rode with**, and Riddle sat in the
  back of the saloon-court and watched and said nothing. Voss saw him and
  said nothing too. That is between them.
- **Carver has pulled settlers off a claim that Pensinger's company
  wanted**, on orders, and the two of them drink together anyway because
  neither blames the other for the work the country hands them.

This web is the resource the vignettes draw on. When a chapter opens, it
opens on one of these relationships, not on a stranger.

## How the vignettes work

Each chapter opens with one vignette featuring its anchor figure and at
least one crossover. The vignette's job is to plant the chapter's core
pressure in the reader's body before the mechanics begin. It is not
flavor text stapled to the rules. It is the rules' reason for existing.

A vignette is short — two to six paragraphs. It obeys every
non-negotiable rule in the western-writing skill: no mood without object,
no paragraph without a job, end on weight. It does not name a mechanic.
It shows a man or a woman under the pressure the mechanic is going to
model. The mechanic that follows is the explanation of what the reader
already felt.

Section-level vignettes are shorter still — a paragraph, sometimes a
line. They appear where a mechanic is easier to feel than to define: a
stampede, a verdict, a cave-in, a stamp of the throttle. The cast appears
in these too. They are not generic. _Calder_ watches the herd mill at the
river, not _a trail boss_. _Voss_ takes off the spectacles before the
sentence, not _the judge_.

The worked-fiction blockquotes that illustrate individual rules use the
same cast. When a rule says _roll ANIMAL HANDLIN'_, the example is Calder
or Carver or Riddle, in their voice, with their hands. When a rule says
_roll PRESENCE_, the example is Sandoval at the counter or Voss on the
bench. The outlaw chapter's Jeb and Mexican Joe do this for the gang
rules; this cast does it for everything in this book.

## The vignette plan (per chapter)

Each entry below names the anchor, the crossover, and the pressure the
vignette plants. The full vignettes are drafted with their chapters; this
is the plan they will be written to.

### Chapter 2 — Factions & Standing
**Anchor:** Adelina Sandoval. **Crossover:** Sam Riddle.
**Pressure:** the county's web of debts and favors, and what it costs to
call one in. Sandoval reads the ledger and chooses whose name to cross
out. The chapter that follows explains how a faction moves when the
player characters are not looking, and what Standing actually buys.

### Chapter 3 — Weather & the Trail's Teeth
**Anchor:** Adelina Sandoval. **Crossover:** Webb Calder (caught on the
plain).
**Pressure:** the country indifferent to the people in it. Calder comes
in out of a blue norther with two cattle short and a hand half-dead, and
Sandoval puts the kettle on and does not ask yet. The chapter that
follows is the weather generator and the disaster procedures — the
engine for the thing that just walked through the door.

### Chapter 4 — Justice, Trial & Incarceration
**Anchor:** Judge Harlan Voss. **Crossover:** Sam Riddle (in the back of
the room).
**Pressure:** the reach of the law and its short grip. Voss holds court
in the saloon, sentences a man, and knows the man Riddle used to ride
with is sitting ten feet away unindicted. The chapter that follows is
the trial as social conflict, the sentencing table, the jail, and the
wire that can summon Carver's troop when the bench cannot keep order.

### Chapter 5 — Train Robbery & the Iron Horse
**Anchor:** Dutch Miller. **Crossover:** Sam Riddle (who has done this
work and is trying not to again).
**Pressure:** the iron horse as a target and as a thing that does not
forgive a mistake. Miller sees the men at the obstruction and knows what
is coming. The chapter that follows is the train as an operation — stop
it, take it, run from it — and the railroad as a faction that remembers.

### Chapter 6 — Cattle Drives & the Open Range
**Anchor:** Webb Calder. **Crossover:** Sam Riddle (riding as an extra
hand).
**Pressure:** the herd as money on the hoof and as a thing that can kill
the men moving it. Calder reads the sky and the grass and the crew and
decides whether to push. The chapter that follows is the drive as
operation, the stampede, and the roundup as a seasonal enterprise.

### Chapter 7 — Mining Claims & the Ground's Promise
**Anchor:** Eli Pensinger. **Crossover:** Adelina Sandoval (at the
counter, with the assay).
**Pressure:** the ground's promise and the ground's cost. Pensinger sets
a round and comes up coughing and does not say what the cough means.
Sandoval fronts him the provisions against the season's ore. The chapter
that follows is prospecting, the claim, the strike as a seasonal roll,
and the dust as a Trouble that does not ask permission.

### Chapter 8 — Mass Combat & the Skirmish Line
**Anchor:** Sergeant Moses Carver. **Crossover:** Webb Calder (whose herd
is the cause of the fight); Sam Riddle (in the wrong place).
**Pressure:** the fight that is too big for the player characters'
pistols. Carver dismounts the troop on the ridgeline and reads the ground
and the enemy and the order that sent him there. The chapter that follows
is the unit, the skirmish turn, the attached player character, and the
siege.

## A note on the casting

Seven figures cannot stand for the whole West. They are not meant to.
They stand for the corner of country where this book's rules live, and
they are chosen so that every chapter has a human door in: the trader who
counts the debts, the hand who moves the stock, the judge who holds the
rope, the miner who sets the timber, the soldier who rides the column, the
engineer who holds the throttle, and the man who has been all the wrong
things and is trying not to be.

The West was Nuevomexicano, Anglo, Black, Cornish, German, Apache,
Lakota, Diné, Chinese, and more. This cast is deliberately not all Anglo,
because the country was not. It is also not a checklist. Each figure is
specific first — a widow with a ledger, a half-deaf miner, a sergeant
writing to Leavenworth — and a member of a people second, the way actual
people are. The Apache and the Lakota are present in this book as the
people the country belongs to and the people the army campaigns against;
they appear in the mass combat, faction, and weather chapters as
themselves, not as a face on a statblock. The honest rendering of the
Native nations is the business of `knowledge/native-material-culture.md`
in the writing skill, and that file governs every scene in which they
appear.
