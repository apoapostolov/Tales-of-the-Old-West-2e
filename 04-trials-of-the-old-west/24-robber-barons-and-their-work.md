## Chapter 24 - Robber Barons and the Long Reach

> _THE MAN WITH THE LEDGER IN NEW YORK DOES NOT RIDE A HORSE. HE RIDES THE WIRE AND THE RAIL AND THE LAW, AND THE WORK HE SENDS A MAN WEST TO DO IS THE WORK THAT BUILT THE FRONTIER OR UNBUILT IT, DEPENDING ON WHO IS LEFT HOLDING THE DEED WHEN HE IS DONE._
>
> — FROM AN EDITORIAL, UNSIGNED, IN THE SANTA FE NEW MEXICAN, 1874

### The Men Behind the Wire

The corebook's New Mexico campaign puts Clarence King and the Santa Fe Ring at the center of the county's politics, but the wider West was run by a handful of men whose names were on the deeds, the railheads, the mines, the legislatures, and the laws. The robber baron is the figure whose interest in a party is the work he has for them and the trouble that work brings, and whose reach — Federal, moneyed, legal, and long — makes him the most consequential faction leader a party will ever owe.

A baron is a faction of one with Federal reach, immense means, a decades-long clock, and a memory longer than the county's. Bring him in when local stakes are being set by distant money: a railroad right-of-way, a land grant suit, a mine consolidation, a star-route contract, a bank squeeze, or a political appointment. The party's work becomes the baron's work, the party's enemies become useful or inconvenient to him, and choices made at a kitchen table may be read in a boardroom a thousand miles east.

> _The man from the railroad sat in Sandoval's kitchen with his hat on his knee and his gloves on the table and he did not drink the coffee she had poured because men from the railroad did not drink coffee in kitchens, they drank it in offices, and the kitchen was where he had been forced to meet her because she would not come to the office._
>
> "Mr. Huntington sends his regards," he said.
>
> "Mr. Huntington can keep his regards," Sandoval said. "He can also keep his right-of-way. The answer is the same as it was in May."
>
> _The man from the railroad folded his gloves. He had not expected a different answer, but the wire had told him to ask again, because the wire was sent from New York and New York did not understand that the woman at the way-station was not a party to be negotiated with but a wall to be gone around, and going around her would cost the railroad twice what going through her would have._
>
> "The price has improved," he said.
>
> "The price is not the question. The question is whose name goes on the deed when the road is built, and Mr. Huntington's name is not the name I want on the spring my grandfather dug. Tell him I said so. Tell him in the wire."
>
> _The man from the railroad went out to send the wire. Sandoval poured the coffee back in the pot and sat down at the table and put her head in her hands for a count of ten, because the baron's work had come to her kitchen, and the baron's work was the kind that did not stop at the door when it was refused. It only found another door._

### The Baron as Faction

A robber baron is a faction (the faction chapter), with the following specifics that set him apart from the county's smaller interests.

- **Reach.** Federal. A baron's arm does not stop at the county line, or the territory line, or often the national line. His wires go east; his lawyers go everywhere; his money buys the legislature that buys the bench that swears the warrant that summons the troop. A party cannot outrun a baron's reach; it can only earn his regard or his enmity, and both are expensive.
- **Means.** Cash, the wire, the rail, the lawyers, the legislated favor, the detectives (the train robbery chapter's `Railroad or Express Detectives`, elevated), and the ability to buy any of the county's factions for a season. A baron's means are the source of his patience — he can afford to wait, and to push, and to ruin, and the table should let him do all three.
- **Purpose.** Singular and long. _Build the road. Take the grant. Break the union. Elect the judge. Corner the market. Ruin the rival._ A baron's purpose is the campaign's spine when the baron is in play, and everything the party does in the county is read by the baron as a move for or against that purpose.
- **Standing.** On the standard `-3` to `+3` ladder, but a baron's Standing shifts slowly and weighs heavily. A single season's work for a baron moves his Standing by `1`; a season's refusal moves it the other way; and a baron at `Hunted` (`-3`) is the most dangerous enemy a party can have, because he will spend years and fortunes to ruin them.

A baron's Faction Roll each season (the faction chapter) is his pressure on the county. A `Pressure` result means the baron has closed a town, bought a judge, or sent detectives; a `Bold Move` result means the baron has committed — a rail built, a grant taken, a union broken, a rival ruined. A party that has the baron's regard rides these moves; a party that has his enmity is in their path.

### The Work and the Opportunities

A baron offers the party work, and the work is the hook. The work is always well-paid and always costly — the baron's money buys the party's name, the party's Standing with the county's other factions, and often the party's conscience. Let the cost of the work weigh as heavily as the pay.

Roll or choose from the table for the work a baron offers, scaled to the baron's reach and the price of failure.

The work is always an operation (the outlaw chapter's frame), scaled to its size. The `dirty job` and the `war job` are `Big` operations (`8` Progress, `5` Trouble); the `escort` and `collection` are `Medium`; the `survey` and `salvage` are often `Small`. The Trouble is the cost of the work, and it lands on the party's `Standing`, `Wanted`, `Fame`, and `Reputation` according to what the job exposed.

### The Price of the Work

The baron pays well and owns the man who takes the work. A party that works for a baron finds its `Standing` with the baron rising and its `Standing` with the baron's rivals falling, and the county reads the party's allegiance in the party's deeds. A party that works for the Ring cannot work for the railroad that opposes it; a party that breaks a union cannot count on the union's help; a party that clears squatters is marked in the county's memory as the baron's hand, and the mark does not wash out when the season's pay is spent.

A baron's regard spends well. His wire can call off a marshal, his lawyers can quash a warrant, his money can buy the judge at the trial (the justice chapter). Regard is also debt. The baron collects at the worst moment, with a job the party cannot refuse because he owns too much of the party's name to let it go. Obligation accumulates slowly, until the party is his instrument whether it meant to be or not.

#### Example: The Right-of-Way Job

The Central Pacific offers the party `D6 × $100` per man to move a holdout off a spring needed for a right-of-way. The GM treats the job as a `Medium` operation: Progress is the signed deed or empty ground; Trouble is the county's outrage, the holdout's resistance, the law's attention, and the party's reputation as the railroad's hand.

The party refuses violence and tries paper first. They win the first exchange with a legal pressure play, but Trouble rises when the holdout's niece takes the story to the newspaper. The railroad's `Standing` with the party rises for doing the job, while the reformers' and nesters' `Standing` fall. If the party finishes, they get paid and owe the railroad. If they walk away, the railroad's `Standing` drops and the baron's next season's Faction Roll may turn against them.

The baron's work is never only the job. It is the obligation the job leaves behind.

Mark the town tracker when a baron's work becomes visible public fact. A rail extension, water project, payroll, or steady contract may add `1` `Mercantile`, `Civic`, or `Natural Riches` point, or make `Railroad Station`, `Telegraph`, `Trading Exchange`, `County Hall`, or `Town Expansion` available at `3` SP. A stripped line, bought judge, broken strike, poisoned water, or land job that drives people off may remove `1` or `2` points from the aspects the baron damaged. A baron's gift should improve something real and put his hand on it.

### Example Historical Robber Barons

Brief, accurate profiles the GM can drop into a campaign or use as templates. Each is a faction sheet and a portrait, grounded in the period. Render the barons as they were, not as their press releases described them, and name their fortunes for what they cost the country as much as for what they paid the baron.

**Leland Stanford and Collis P. Huntington (the Central Pacific).** These men built the western half of the transcontinental rail and broke the Chinese labor they built it with: hiring thousands, working them to death in the passes, paying them half the white wage, then campaigning to exclude them from the country when the road was done. Stanford was the governor, the senator, the university founder, and the man whose fortune was made on the difference between the wage he paid and the wage he should have paid. Huntington was the lobbyist and briber, the man who called congressmen by the price of their votes and kept the receipts. With the Central Pacific in play, the railroad becomes the county's ceiling: the interest that owns the railhead, schedule, wage, and labor law, and the interest that will ruin any party that crosses it. Name the Chinese workers honestly. The baron's prosperity was built on their backs.

**Jay Gould.** The railroad speculator and cornerer bought railroads to strip them and left the ruins in his wake. Gould's method was paper: stock, bond, corner, and the lie told often enough to move a market. His victims were the railroads he bought, the workers he fired, and the towns he passed by after the road was stripped to its rails. With Gould in play, the financier is distant pressure. He can buy the party's local railroad out from under it, strip the line, and leave the county stranded from an office in New York the party will never see. Gould's work is a swindle dressed as a deal, and the ruin lands far from his own door.

**Thomas B. Catron and the Santa Fe Ring.** Catron and the Ring are the land-grant operators of New Mexico, and the barons the corebook's campaign is built around. Catron was lawyer, sheriff, territorial delegate, and the man whose name was on more of the Maxwell Grant's title than any man living. His method was the law itself: quiet-title suit, tax sale, forged deed, intimidated witness, owned judge, fixed jury. The Ring held the territory's land, courts, and legislature for a generation, and its wealth was the wealth of a country legally stolen. With Catron in play, the Ring becomes the county's government, the faction that owns the bench (the justice chapter), the land (the family and trade chapters), and the wire to the troop (the mass combat chapter). The party must decide whether to serve it, oppose it, or survive beneath it. Name the people the Ring harmed: Nuevomexicano communities defrauded of their grants, settlers run off their claims, Apache and Navajo nations whose land the schemes encompassed.

**Henry Plant and the Express Companies.** These men moved the country's money and mail. Their interest in the county was the safe transit of specie, payroll, and bond. The express company is the baron-as-utility, less political figure than commercial power, but with the Federal reach of the mail contract and the detectives (the train robbery chapter) to protect it. With the express company in play, the payroll and strongbox are the county's liquidity. A party that robs the express (the train robbery chapter) has made an enemy of a baron whose reach is Federal and whose memory is the mail's memory: long, exact, and unforgiving.

**The Cattle Syndicates and Foreign Capital.** British or Scottish money often stood behind the open range, invested through a New York or Denver bank and invisible to the cowhands who worked the herds and the nesters who fought the fence. With the syndicate in play, the cattle chapter's ranch becomes the local face of distant investment, and the party's work is paid by a cheque drawn a continent away. The syndicate's baron is cold because he does not know the county's names and does not care to. He will sell the ranch, fire the hands, and move the capital the moment the return dips.

The baron is the ceiling over the county. He sets the price of land, rail, law, and labor. The player characters must decide whether to serve him, oppose him, survive beneath him, or bring him down, knowing the last has rarely been done and never cheaply.
