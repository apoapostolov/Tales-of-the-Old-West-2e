## Chapter 7 - Cattle Drives and the Open Range

> _THE HERD IS MONEY ON THE HOOF. IT IS ALSO FIFTEEN HUNDRED ANIMALS THAT CAN KILL EVERY MAN WHO WORKS THEM IF THE MEN GET IT WRONG. THE TRAIL BOSS WHO FORGETS THE SECOND THING TO REMEMBER THE FIRST IS NOT A TRAIL BOSS FOR LONG._
>
> — WEBB CALDER, TO A WRANGLER WHO WANTED TO PUSH

### The Open Range and Its Trade

The cattle industry is half the economy of the West, and the corebook's setting chapters render it well: the chuckwagon, the hierarchy, the pay, the daily grind. What the corebook does not give you is the drive and the roundup as procedures — the herd as a thing to be moved across country, the stampede as a thing that can kill the men moving it, the ranch as a business to be rolled at the Turn of the Season. This chapter is the missing half.

It is an optional framework for groups who want the herd on the table. It builds the drive as an **operation**, using the outlaw chapter's Progress-versus-Trouble track, with the herd's miles as the Progress and the trail's teeth as the Trouble. It builds the **stampede** as a discrete Trouble event with its own resolution. It builds the **roundup** and the ranch as a **seasonal enterprise**, parallel to the corebook's business-outfit rules. And it folds in the cross-pressures of the open range: the cattleman's association as a faction, the nesters and the wire, the rustler, the brand.

You do not need these rules for a trail drive that is a montage. Use them when the drive is the campaign — when the party is the trail crew, the herd is the stake, and the country between the home range and the railhead is the dungeon.

> _Calder read the sky to the west and the grass ahead and the herd's temper behind and the crew's condition around the fire, and he did not like what any of the four told him. The sky said rain by midnight. The grass said two more days of good feed and then the dry stretch. The herd said it had been holding too close for too long and was ready to run at any excuse. The crew said the wrangler from the wash was green and the night hawk had been in the bottle._
>
> _He did not say any of this to the crew. He said, "We push at first light," and he went back to his bedroll, and he did not sleep, because the choice to push was the choice to risk the stampede, and the stampede was the thing that had killed the last trail boss who had pushed this crew through this country in a year this dry._

### The Drive as Operation

A cattle drive is the archetypal operation: a long, linked sequence of situations, each of which can go well or badly, with a fixed destination and a hundred miles of country between. The outlaw chapter's operation rules model it almost perfectly, and this chapter uses them directly.

| Drive Scale | Progress Needed | Trouble Limit | The Drive |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `Local Move` | `4` | `3` | A shift of herds between ranges, or a drive to a local shipping point; a few days, a short country |
| `Trail Drive` | `8` | `5` | The long drive to the railhead; weeks, hard country, every trouble the trail can breed |
| `Roundup` | `6` | `4` | The spring or fall work: gathering, branding, cutting; not a move but a campaign of its own |

A `Trail Drive` is the only operation in this book that uses the `Big` scale of `8` Progress. That is deliberate. A long drive is the cattleman's equivalent of a campaign, and it should feel like one: three to six checks at minimum, with the expectation that Trouble will matter before the railhead is in sight.

**Progress** is miles toward the destination (or, on a roundup, beeves worked). **Trouble** is the trail's teeth: stampede, storms, river crossings gone wrong, rustlers, stampedes triggered by rustlers, bad water, a crew turning sick, a boss's judgment questioned. Each stage is tagged Safe / Risky / Desperate as the outlaw chapter tags a score.

| Stage of the Drive | The Problem | Likely Ability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reading the country | Choose the route, the water, the grass, the pace | `NATURE`, `HAWKEYE` |
| Holding the herd | Keep the beeves steady, point and drag and swing; keep the pace | `ANIMAL HANDLIN'`, `LABOR` |
| The water and the grass | Find the next water, hold the herd on good feed, decide to push or to camp | `NATURE`, `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` |
| The river crossing | The swim, the milling, the bogging; the loss of stock in the current | `ANIMAL HANDLIN'`, `LABOR`, `RESILIENCE` |
| The crew | Keep the hands fed, mounted, sober, and civil; settle the wrangler's bad judgment | `PRESENCE`, `DOCTORIN'` |
| The country's people | Pass through a settler's wire, a faction's range, a reservation line, a town | `PRESENCE`, `INSIGHT` |

The abilities will not surprise anyone who has read the corebook. The point is that the drive gives every cowboy ability a job, the way the outlaw score gives every band ability a job.

#### Drive Modifiers

Apply the modifiers that plainly matter, to the ±3 cap, on the drive's checks. The trail boss's ability is the acting pool, modified by the crew and the country.

| Condition | Modifier |
| --- | --- |
| The trail boss is a player character with `Bronc Buster` or `Companion` (a good horse) | `+1 die` |
| The crew includes hands the boss has driven with before | `+1 die` |
| The herd is mixed or wild, fresh off the range | `-1 die` |
| The crew is short-handed or green | `-1 die` |
| The country is in drought | `-1 die` to `-2 die`, by the drought's severity |
| The drive crosses a faction's range with `Standing -2` or worse | `-1 die` |
| The drive has a faction's blessing (`Standing +2` or better) | `+1 die` |
| The weather is a disaster operation | the disaster runs inside the drive |

#### Drive Trouble and the Herd

The herd is the drive's stake, and the Trouble that the operation accrues is paid for out of the herd. Each step of Trouble on a drive costs beeves: a stampede's worth, a river's worth, a rustler's worth, a drought's worth. As a rule of thumb, each Trouble step costs the drive `D6 × 10` beeves, by death, straying, or theft, and the GM should narrate where they went. A `Trail Drive` that hits its Trouble limit before the railhead may arrive with half the herd it started with, which is the kind of season that breaks an outfit.

The money in the corebook's `prices-and-anchors.md` sets what each beef is worth at the railhead. A drive that arrives with its herd intact is a drive that pays out; a drive that arrives light is a drive that explains itself to the owners, the way an outlaw gang explains a score gone wrong.

### The Stampede

The stampede is the drive's signature disaster, and it deserves its own resolution rather than a single Trouble step. A stampede is triggered by any of the following: a storm at night, a predator among the herd, a rustler's signal shot, a green hand's mistake, or a Trouble step that the GM decides has set the herd off.

When a stampede triggers, the trail boss (or the acting hand among the crew) makes an `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` roll to turn the herd — not to stop it, which is rarely possible, but to point the mass of beeves at ground that will not kill them and to ride the flank until the run burns itself out. The stampede is resolved as a short operation nested inside the drive, scaled to the herd's size and the country.

| Stampede Scale | Progress Needed | Trouble Limit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `A Milling` | `2` | `2` |
| `A Run` | `4` | `3` |
| `A General Stampede` | `6` | `4` |

**Progress** is turning and holding the herd until it tires. **Trouble** is what the stampede costs: stock lost in the dark or over a bank, a hand thrown or trampled, the herd split into pieces that take days to gather, the beeves that will not settle for the rest of the drive. A stampede at night, in a storm, on broken ground, is `Desperate` on every check; a daylight milling on flat ground is `Risky` at worst.

A hand down in front of a stampede is a `RESILIENCE` roll against the trampling rules, run as the stampede's Trouble rather than a separate scene. A horse down in a stampede is a dead horse, and the hand on it is the next Trouble step. The corebook's `Spooked Horse Table` fires for every mount the crew rides into the mass; the stampede is the table's worst-case scenario, and it should feel like it.

> _Calder had been in three general stampedes before the one on the Cimarron and he remembered each of them by which hand had not come back. The first had taken the wrangler's boy, sixteen, who had ridden the flank instead of the lead and whose horse had gone down at the bank of the draw. The second had taken two beeves in a hundred and a horse and had taken Calder's nerve for night work for a season. The third had been the good one, the one he had turned by himself in the lightning, and he did not remember it with pride because the men who remembered stampedes with pride were the men who had not been in enough of them._

### The Roundup and the Ranch as Business

The drive moves the herd. The roundup makes the herd. In the spring and again in the fall, the range is worked: the cattle gathered out of the country, the calves branded, the mavericks cut, the culls separated for the drive or the sale. A ranch that does not work its herd loses it — to the country, to the rustler, and to the brand of the next man who runs the iron.

The roundup is a `Roundup`-scale operation, as above, and it is also the ranch's season of business. The ranch itself is run as a **seasonal enterprise** parallel to the corebook's business-outfit rules, using the same pool and the same ladder, with a ranch-specific key ability and a ranch-specific Bonus/Penalty table.

#### The Ranch as Outfit

A ranch follows the corebook's business rules with the following specifics.

- **Key ability:** `ANIMAL HANDLIN'`, the rancher's (or the trail boss's, on a ranch run by its crew).
- **Prerequisites:** rangeland with water and grass (a property with a ranch location, per Chapter 6 of the corebook), a brand registered at the county seat (a `BOOKLEARNIN'` or `Lawyer` matter, and a faction's blessing if the cattleman's association is involved), and stock.
- **Town aspect modifiers:** the ranch reads the town's `Natural Riches` (the range's quality) and `Law` (the brand's protection), in addition to the universal `Law` modifier. A ranch in a `Law 1` county is a ranch the rustler works as surely as the rancher.
- **The Season Ranch Roll** is made at the Turn of the Season, like any business roll, by the rancher or the trail boss. It cannot be pushed, and it does not generate Trouble. Read it on the standard business ladder: `0` a Penalty roll, `1` steady, `2+` a Bonus roll with `+1` to the Tens die per success beyond the first.

#### Ranch Bonus and Penalty Table

| D66 | Ranch Bonus | Ranch Penalty |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `11-13` | A good calf crop. `D6 × $50` in added stock value. | Winter kill. Lose `D6 × $50` in stock to a hard season. |
| `14-16` | The brand held. `+1` to the ranch's effective `Natural Riches` modifier next season. | Rustlers. Lose `D6 × $75` in stock; the brand's reputation suffers. |
| `21-23` | A strong drive paid off. `D6 × $100` in cash from the railhead. | The drought. `Natural Riches` reduced by `1` rank; stock losses. |
| `24-26` | A maverick windfall. `+1` Capital that must be liquidated into stock. | Disease in the herd. Test the corebook's `Disease` rule; lose `D6 × $50` in stock. |
| `31-33` | Good hands, well led. `+1` to next season's Ranch Roll. | A stampede's cost. Lose `D6 × $100` in stock and `1` good horse. |
| `34-36` | A fence-law victory or a faction's backing. `+1` to the ranch's effective `Law` modifier next season. | The wire war. A `Law` or `Civic` loss in the town, and `Standing` with the cattleman's association shifts. |
| `41-43` | A buyer at the railhead. `D6 × $150` in cash at a good price. | A trail drive gone bad. No cash from the drive; lose `2D6 × $50` in stock. |
| `44-46` | The association's nod. `+1` Capital in stock or land, immediately realized. | The association's enmity. `Standing` with the cattleman's faction drops two steps. |
| `51-56` | A bonanza calf crop and a clean brand. `+2` Capital in stock. | A bad winter and a worse spring. `2D6 × $75` in stock; the ranch's future in question. |
| `61-66` | The year the ranch became an outfit. `+1D3` Capital. | The year the ranch nearly broke. Lose `1` Capital; the owners must cover the loss or sell. |

A ranch that rolls Penalty season after season is a ranch going broke, and the engine allows it. A ranch that rolls Bonus season after season is a ranch the faction system will notice — the cattleman's association, the nesters, the rustlers, the railroad at the railhead all want a piece of a profitable outfit, and the faction engine is how they come for it.

### The Open Range and Its Factions

A cattle drive and a ranch do not happen in an empty country. The open range is a contested thing in the 1870s, and the factions that contest it are the campaign.

- **The Cattleman's Association** is a faction whose purpose is to hold the open range for stockmen against the nesters, the wire, and the rustler. Its means are the association's detectives (the corebook's `Ranch Outfit or Range Detectives` pursuer), the social pressure of the county, and occasionally the rope. A drive backed by the association rides easier; a drive the association opposes meets wire across the trail and a detective at every waterhole.
- **The Nesters** are a faction whose purpose is to fence and farm the range the stockmen hold open. They are poor, numerous, and increasingly backed by the homestead laws and, sometimes, the bench. A drive through a nester's fence is a `Standing` shift with the nester faction and, often, a warrant sworn at the county seat.
- **The Rustlers** are a faction (or an outlaw band, run by the corebook's outlaw rules) whose purpose is the stock the rancher has not yet branded or cannot guard. Rustling is the corebook's `Rustling` score type; the ranch's Season Roll and the drive's Trouble are where its cost is felt.
- **The Railroad at the Railhead** is a faction whose purpose is to ship the beeves east and take a cut. The railhead is where the drive ends and the railroad's money begins, and the shipping rates and the buyers are the railroad's leverage over every rancher who has driven a herd a thousand miles to find one buyer.

### The Drive's Crew and the Cast

A drive is a crew, and the crew is the cast this book is built around. Calder is the trail boss; Riddle, in this book, rides as an extra hand, which is the kind of work a former outlaw finds when the gang is broken and the bench is watching. The drive is where the cast's histories surface: Calder's silence about his dead family, Riddle's careful avoidance of the county seats, the wrangler's greenness, the cook's patience. The GM should let the crew be people, not statblocks, and let the drive's Trouble be the door the people walk through.

The cattle drive is also the natural site of the campaign's weather, its mass combat (when the drive meets the raiders), and its faction pressures (the association, the nesters, the rustlers). A drive run with this book's full rules is a campaign in miniature: a destination, a stake, a crew, a country, and every pressure the West can bring to bear on a thousand beeves and the men moving them.

A campaign that uses these rules will find that the herd is a character — nervous, valuable, and capable of killing the people who work it. The trail drive is the cowboy's outlaw score, run across weeks instead of hours, paid in beeves instead of cash, and judged by the country it crosses rather than the fence it leaves behind.
