## Chapter 20 - Weather and the Trail's Teeth

> _THE COUNTRY DOES NOT HATE YOU. HATING YOU WOULD REQUIRE IT TO NOTICE YOU. THE COUNTRY IS BUSY WITH ITS OWN BUSINESS, AND ITS BUSINESS IS THE WEATHER._
>
> — ADELINA SANDOVAL, TO A HAND WHO WONDERED WHY GOD HAD SENT THE HAIL

### Weather and Its Trade

The West of the 1870s is a country of weather, and the weather is a character in every tale that crosses it. The blue norther comes down the plains in a wall and kills cattle and men who were sweating at noon. The prairie fire runs before a wind and takes the horses and the wagons and the grass for fifty miles. The cloudburst turns a dry wash into a river in ten minutes and the river takes the camp. The drought comes on slow and does not leave and the town withers around its dry spring. A man who travels the country without reading the sky is a man the country will write off.

This chapter is an optional rules set for groups who want the weather on the table instead of in the GM's narration. It does three things. It gives the GM a **weather generator** keyed to the regions and seasons of the West. It builds the country's worst weather — the blizzard, the fire, the flood — as **operations**, using the same Progress-versus-Trouble track the outlaw chapter uses for scores. And it folds the slow pressure of drought into the town and outlaw downtime systems already in the corebook.

It does not invent new conditions. The corebook already has `Starving`, `Dehydrated`, `Exhausted`, `Freezing`, and `Heatstroke`, each with a damage schedule and a death-roll clause. The weather in this chapter produces those conditions. The new work is the *generators* that decide when the conditions come, and the *operation framing* that turns a bad storm into a scene instead of a single roll.

> _Calder came in off the plain at dusk with two beeves short and the wrangler across the saddle like a sack of grain. The norther had come down on them at noon and the boy had not been dressed for it and the horses had been leg-weary from the morning's push and Calder had made the choice to ride for the way-station instead of making camp, because making camp in a blue norther with a half-frozen hand and no wood was the kind of choice that ended in a line shack with four men and three horses come spring._
>
> _Sandoval had the door bar up before the horse was at the rail. She took one look at the boy's hands and pointed at the bunk by the stove and did not point at Calder._
>
> "How many," she said.
>
> "Two," Calder said. "Maybe three. I didn't count the strays in the dark."
>
> "You rode for here."
>
> "I rode for here."
>
> _She put the kettle on and did not ask the next question, which was why, because the why was the boy's hands and the why was already answered. She asked about the cattle later, when the kettle had done its work and the boy had stopped shaking long enough to lie about how he felt._

### The Daily Weather

When the party is in the country — traveling, driving, campaigning, or on the trail — the GM sets the day's weather. Most days, the GM simply decides, the way the GM decides any other fact of the world. When the table wants the country to speak for itself, or when the GM wants the pressure of not knowing, roll on the regional table.

The West is not one country. A spring day on the Llano Estacado is a different animal from a spring day in the Sangres or the Mojave. The table below is keyed to the regions the game's setting material covers. Pick the region the party is in. Roll `D66` and read the result for the current season.

The seasons here are the four seasons of the corebook's Turn of the Season: Spring (March–May), Summer (June–August), Fall (September–November), Winter (December–February). The result names the day's weather and any modifier the weather applies to travel, ranged shots, or rolls where the weather plainly bears.

This is a base table for the high desert and plains country the game's setting is built on. The GM should adjust for the specific region: a summer day in the Mojave Desert shifts every `Heatstroke` risk toward the severe; a winter day in the high Rockies treats any `clear and cold` as `Freezing` risk; the Pacific Northwest's wet is a near-constant `-25%` travel modifier through the winter. Use `regional-landscapes.md` in the writing skill's knowledge base as the guide for what each region does to the table. The numbers are the same; the names and the risks shift.

#### Reading the Result

The result's travel modifier applies to the Traveling Table's miles-per-Shift distances. A `-50%` day halves the day's travel; a `-25%` day cuts it by a quarter. The `rushed` distances from Chapter 6 stack with these modifiers, and rushing on a bad-weather day is the open door to the conditions below.

When a result says **test** a condition (`Freezing`, `Heatstroke`), apply the corebook's rule for that condition directly. The condition is the engine; the weather table only decides when it fires. There is no new damage track here. A character caught in a `Full blizzard` rolls `RESILIENCE` against `Freezing` exactly as Chapter 5 describes, and dies of it exactly as Chapter 5 describes, if the dice and the exposure say so.

#### Spooked-Stock Risk

Several results carry a **spooked-stock risk**: hail, thunder, a hard squall, a fire. When the party is mounted or driving stock, the GM may call for an `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` roll from the rider or wrangler in the lead. On a failure, the stock spooks and the GM rolls on the corebook's **Spooked Horse Table** for mounts, or treats driven stock as beginning to stampede (see the chapter on cattle drives). This cross-references, not duplicates, the corebook's table.

### Disasters

The worst weather is not a single roll. It is a scene. A blizzard that traps the party on the high plains, a prairie fire that runs before a wind, a flash flood that takes the dry wash the party camped in — these are the weather at its worst, and they deserve the same weight the outlaw chapter gives a score. Frame them as **operations**, using the Progress-versus-Trouble track.

A weather disaster uses the operation rules in full. The GM sets the scale by the disaster's ferocity and the party's exposure, tags each attempt Safe / Risky / Desperate, and reads Trouble on the standard ladder. The abilities that matter shift with the disaster and the party's choices — `NATURE` to read the country, `HAWKEYE` to find the way, `MOVE` or `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` to outrun it, `LABOR` and `MAKIN'` to build what shelter can be built, `RESILIENCE` to hold together when holding together is all that is left.

#### Scale of a Disaster

| Disaster Scale | Progress Needed | Trouble Limit | When to Use It |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `Squall` | `4` | `3` | The party is exposed but close to shelter or a way out. A few bad hours. |
| `Storm` | `6` | `4` | The party is caught in the open a hard ride from anything. A day and a night. |
| `Catastrophe` | `8` | `5` | The party is deep in the country in the worst the season can do, with nowhere to go but through. Days. |

Calibrate so a disaster takes about three to six checks, like any operation. A `Squall` is a tense afternoon; a `Catastrophe` is the centerpiece of a session or two. If the disaster clearly needs more, split it — the first day's flight, then the second day's survival — rather than running one track to ten.

#### The Blue Norther (Blizzard)

The blizzard is the cold made into a wall. It comes down the plains with a roll of cloud and a drop of temperature that takes the sweat off a horse and freezes it. Once it is on the party, the only Progress that matters is reaching shelter — a line cabin, a timbered draw, a town, a fold in the ground out of the wind.

| Stage | The Problem | Likely Ability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reading the sign | Spot the norther coming, gauge the time left, choose a course | `HAWKEYE` or `NATURE` |
| The ride for shelter | Cover ground before the cold closes in; keep the horses and the hands together | `ANIMAL HANDLIN'`, `MOVE` |
| Holding together | Keep a frozen hand in the saddle, keep the stock from milling, keep the direction in a whiteout | `RESILIENCE`, `PRESENCE` |
| Making what shelter there is | Build a fire in a draw, bank the horses, dig in if there is nothing else | `LABOR`, `MAKIN'`, `NATURE` |

**Progress** is distance toward shelter, measured in the GM's judgment of the country. Each successful check moves the party closer. **Trouble** is exposure: a frozen hand, a horse blown, a hand separated in the whiteout, a fire that will not light. Each Trouble step brings a `Freezing` test closer for everyone exposed, and at `3+` Trouble the cold begins to take its toll in `Hurts` regardless of the condition rules — the GM may call for `RESILIENCE` rolls from anyone exposed, failure meaning `1` point of `Hurts` as the cold finds the gaps in the clothes.

When the party reaches shelter, the operation ends; the cold becomes a matter of the corebook's `Freezing` condition and ordinary recovery. If Trouble hits the limit before shelter is reached, the plan is dead and the party is in the storm for the night — which is where men die. The GM should not soften this. The country does not.

> _Calder's hand came off the print of the boy's ribs where the cold had found the gap between the vest and the britches. The skin was the color of the candle. Calder had seen the color before, in a man who had kept the hand and in a man who had not, and the difference had not been the cold but the speed of the getting-to-shelter. That was the operation: read the sign, ride for the timber, hold the boy in the saddle, make the fire. Four checks. The trouble was already on the page in the color of the boy's hand. Sandoval did not ask about the cattle because the cattle were not the operation. The boy was._

#### The Prairie Fire

The prairie fire runs before a wind across grass that has not had rain since spring. It moves faster than a horse can gallop in a straight line, and the only Progress that matters is reaching ground that will not burn — a plowed field, a river, a bare stretch, a wide enough firebreak — or making one.

| Stage | The Problem | Likely Ability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reading the fire | Gauge the wind, the speed, the front; choose to run or to stand and break | `HAWKEYE`, `NATURE` |
| The run for a break | Outrace the front to a river or bare ground; drive the stock ahead of it | `ANIMAL HANDLIN'`, `MOVE` |
| Making a break | Burn or plow a line ahead of the front, fast, with what is at hand | `LABOR`, `MAKIN'`, `NATURE` |
| Holding the line | Beat out the spot fires, hold the stock, keep the smoke from taking the crew | `RESILIENCE`, `LABOR` |

**Progress** is reaching or making safe ground. **Trouble** is the fire's work: stock panicked and scattered, a hand cut off, smoke inhalation, the wind shifting and taking the break, gear left behind in the run. The `Spooked Horse Table` fires on any Trouble step that touches the stock; at `3+` Trouble the fire is running around or through the party's position and the operation becomes a fighting retreat through burning grass.

A fire operation differs from a blizzard in one way: a successful backburn or a reached riverbank can end it in a single check, because the party has done the thing that stops the fire. The GM should reward a clever firebreak with Progress equal to the ingenuity of the move. A failed backburn in a shifting wind is Trouble, not Progress, and a badly judged one can add `1` to `2` Trouble as the party's own fire turns on them.

#### The Flash Flood

The flash flood is the dry country's trick. A cloudburst miles away sends a wall of water down a wash that has been bone-dry for months, and the camp in the bottom is in the water before anyone can name what is happening. The only Progress that matters is high ground.

| Stage | The Problem | Likely Ability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reading the sign | Hear the roar, see the debris wall, gauge the minutes left | `HAWKEYE`, `NATURE` |
| The scramble | Get the people, the stock, and the gear to high ground, in that order | `MOVE`, `ANIMAL HANDLIN'`, `LABOR` |
| What gets left behind | Choose what to leave to the water — gear, a pack animal, a wagon | player choice, paid for in gear or stock |
| The aftermath | Find the camp scattered downstream; rescue what can be rescued | `LABOR`, `HAWKEYE` |

**Progress** is everyone who can be saved reaching high ground. **Trouble** is what the water takes: a pack animal, a bedroll, the ammunition, the grub, a hand too slow to climb. A flood operation is usually short — a `Squall` scale, four checks — because the wall passes in minutes. But the Trouble it leaves is the kind that kills later: a camp with no food and no powder, two days from anywhere, is its own operation.

A hand caught in the water is a `RESILIENCE` roll against the drowning rules in Chapter 5 of the corebook, run as the operation's Trouble rather than a separate scene.

### Drought and the Long Dry

Not every weather pressure is a single storm. The drought comes on slow and does not leave, and it is the engine that withers a town and breaks an outfit. The drought is not a single operation. It is a pressure on the downtime systems already in the game.

When the GM declares a drought — by the season's weather rolls, by the campaign's fiction, or by a Fortune roll result that names it — apply its pressure at the Turn of the Season.

| Effect of a Drought Season | Where It Lands |
| --- | --- |
| `Farming` aspect tally halved (round down) for the season's yields | Town aspects, Chapter 8 |
| `Natural Riches` aspect reduced by `1` rank if the drought runs two seasons or more | Town aspects, Chapter 8 |
| Stock losses: reduce `Horsestock` by `1`, and any ranch outfit's yield by half | Outlaw/gang and business rolls |
| `Provisions` die (if used) steps down by one, as forage and game fail | Optional Consumables rule |
| `Welfare` aspect pressure: roll the Town Fortune with an additional `-1` to the Tens die | Town Fortune, Chapter 8 |
| Water and grass scarcity: travel through the district is `-25%` and `Dehydrated` risk applies if the party cannot carry water | Traveling, Chapter 6 |

A drought that runs three seasons breaks the country it is in. The town may fail, the outfit may go bust, the gang may have to move. This is the engine working as intended. The drought is the weather at campaign scale, and it should be allowed to do its work.

### Weather and the Other Rules

The weather in this chapter touches almost every other system in the game, which is why it is built from existing pieces rather than new ones.

- **Travel (Chapter 6).** The travel modifiers apply directly to the Traveling Table. Rushing on a bad-weather day stacks the rush modifier with the weather modifier and opens the door to the conditions.
- **Conditions (Chapter 5).** `Freezing`, `Heatstroke`, `Exhausted`, and `Dehydrated` are the engine the weather fires into. Nothing here duplicates them.
- **Chases (Chapter 6).** A chase in mud, snow, or a dust storm takes the cross-terrain pursuit modifiers and adds the weather's ranged and visibility penalties. A `High wind` day is `-1 die` to any long shot, for the pursued and the pursuer both.
- **Conflict (Chapter 5).** Ranged conflict is the system the weather touches most. Apply the ranged modifiers from the weather table as penalty dice where they plainly matter. A gunfight in a dust storm is a different fight from one on a clear day, and the table should feel it.
- **The drive and the campaign (this book).** A cattle drive crosses weather every day; a campaign in the field lives or dies by it. The weather generator is the engine both chapters draw on for the country's pressure.
- **The gang and the town (corebook).** A drought or a hard winter hits the outlaw's `Horsestock` and `Provisions` and the town's aspects and Fortune rolls, as above. The weather is not a side system; it is the country doing what the country does, felt wherever the party is.

A campaign that uses these rules will find that the party begins to read the sky. That is the point. The traveler who checks the weather table before he saddles and the trail boss who reads the barometer of his bad knee before he pushes the herd are doing what the men and women of the actual West did every day of their lives. The rules should make that reading matter, and let the country do its work when the reading is wrong.
