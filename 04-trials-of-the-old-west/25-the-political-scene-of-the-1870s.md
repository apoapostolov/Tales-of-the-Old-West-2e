## Chapter 25 - The Political Scene of the 1870s

> _THE COUNTY'S FACTIONS — THE RING, THE RAILROAD, THE POST, THE COMMITTEE — ARE THE MEN WHO RUN THE COUNTY'S DAY. THE PARTIES ARE THE MEN WHO RUN THE COUNTRY'S DECADE, AND THE COUNTRY'S DECADE IS THE WEATHER THE COUNTY'S DAY HAPPENS IN. THE PARTY IN THE ASCENDANT MAKES THE ACTS, AND THE ACTS MAKE THE COUNTRY THE COUNTY SITS IN, AND THE COUNTY SITS IN IT WHETHER IT NOTICES OR NOT._
>
> — JUDGE HARLAN VOSS, TO A YOUNG LAWYER WHO ASKED WHY THE BENCH READ THE DISPATCHES

### The Weather Made in Washington

The faction rules track the county's organized interests: companies, rings, troops, committees. The robber baron rules track great operators with territorial or federal reach. Above both sit the **national parties** and the political weather of the 1870s: the men and acts that shaped the nation around the campaign's county, year by year.

A faction is a company, an institution, or an interest. A party is a coalition of factions, regions, and ideas, organized to win elections (the press and election chapter) and to make the acts the country lives under. The Republican party of 1870 gathers the railroad, veterans, the freedmen's vote, Protestant reformers, and the industrial North. The Democratic party of 1870 gathers the Solid South, immigrant cities, the hard-money West, and many of the people the Republican coalition left out. The two parties are the weather the factions operate in, and the acts they pass — the Enforcement Acts, the Specie Resumption Act, the Desert Land Act, the Chinese Exclusion debate — are the climate the campaign's county sits in whether the county notices or not.

A campaign set in 1870 should not feel politically identical to one set after the Panic, after Custer's defeat, or after Reconstruction ends. Use the parties above the factions, the acts as national modifiers, and the year-by-year timeline to set the pressure of the chosen year. The county may notice only when the troop leaves the post, the marshal changes, the deed law shifts, or silver rises in Congress, but the weather was made before it arrived.

> _Voss read the dispatch from Washington in the county seat's paper, three weeks late and two columns in, and he read it the way he read the weather — as the thing that would set the conditions for everything the county did under it. The dispatch said the compromise was made and the troops were leaving the South and the Democrat was in the White House and the Republican coalition of the last twenty years was broken, and Voss read it twice and folded the paper and put it down._
>
> "The troops are leaving the South."
>
> "I can read, counsel."
>
> "What does it mean for the county?"
>
> "It means the country the county sits in just changed, and the county will find out how in the next two years. The party that held the South has let the South go. The party that takes the South will take the country in a decade. The act that ends Reconstruction ends the country we've been living in, and starts the one we'll be living in. The county doesn't notice. The county notices when the troop leaves the post, or the marshal's office changes hands, or the deed law changes, or the silver bill passes. The county notices the weather. The weather is what the dispatch is."
>
> _Voss picked the paper up again and read the dispatch a third time, because the weather was the thing a man planned around, and the party was the thing that made the weather, and the act was the thing the party left on the county's doorstep like a kitten in a sack. That was the political scene's work — the slow, distant making of the country the county woke up in._

### The Parties of the 1870s

The 1870s are a two-party decade, with two significant third-party eruptions (the Liberal Republicans in 1872, the Greenbackers from 1874) and the constant presence of factional operators (the robber barons, the machines, the reformers) working inside and across the parties. Each party is a coalition, and the coalition's internal tensions are as important as the party's opposition to the other.

#### The Republican Party

The party of Lincoln, of Reconstruction, of the railroad and the industrial North. In 1870 the Republicans are the ascendant party — they hold the White House (Grant), the Congress, the enforcement of the Fifteenth Amendment, and the patronage that flows from all three. The Republican coalition is the veterans and the Grand Army of the Republic, the freedmen's vote in the South, the Protestant reformers, the railroad and the industrial interest, the Nuevomexicano and Californio opportunists who read which way the wind blew, and the temperance and the church. The Republicans are the party that passed the Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments, the Enforcement Acts, and the land grant railroads, and the party that will fight, in the 1870s, to hold what it won in the 1860s.

**The Republican internal tension:** between the reformers (the Liberal Republicans, the civil-service reformers, the men who hate Grant's scandals) and the Stalwarts (the machine, the patronage, the men who hold the coalition by its spoils). The tension breaks the party in 1872, reforms partly, and defines the Republican presidential politics of the decade.

#### The Democratic Party

The party of the Solid South, of the immigrant cities, of the hard-money West and the agrarian discontent. In 1870 the Democrats are the party out of power at the federal level, holding the South by terror and fraud where they cannot hold it by vote, and building the northern urban machines (New York's Tammany, and the Catholic immigrant vote) that will make them competitive again. The Democratic coalition is the former Confederacy, the Irish and German immigrants, the hard-money and small-government West, the Catholic church, and everyone the Republicans' coalition excluded or offended. The Democrats are the party that opposed Reconstruction, the land grants, and the high tariff, and the party that will spend the 1870s clawing back the country the Republicans took in the 1860s.

**The Democratic internal tension:** between the Redeemers (the white-supremacist southern faction that takes the South back by violence and fraud) and the northern moderates (the reform Democrats, the hard-money men, the urban machines that need to win northern votes and cannot run openly on the Redeemer program). The tension is the Democratic party's central deceit, and the source of its return to power.

#### The Third Parties

The **Liberal Republicans** (1872) are the reform bolt from Grant's Republican party, led by Horace Greeley, who will be the Democrats' nominee as well, in the most awkward fusion the decade produced. The Liberal Republicans dissolve after 1872, but their reform program is absorbed by both parties.

The **Greenback Party** (from 1874) is the agrarian and labor response to the Panic of 1873 and the hard-money orthodoxy that followed it — the party of inflation, of the farmer's debt relief, of the labor union. The Greenbackers are the West's and the workingman's third party, and they will cost both major parties votes in the late 1870s and seed the Populist movement of the next decade.

### The Acts and the Modifiers

The parties make the acts, and the acts make the country's modifiers. Each act is a national event that shifts the pressures on the campaign's county — the `Standing` of factions, the law's reach, the economy's weather, the army's presence. The GM applies an act's modifiers in the year the act passes, and lets them run until the next act changes them.

The acts below are the 1870s' significant national legislation and executive action, with the modifier each produces. Treat them as national weather: distant in origin, local in consequence.

| The Act | The Year | The Modifier |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **The Enforcement Acts** (the Ku Klux Klan Act, the Force Acts) | 1870-71 | Federal `Law` reach into the South; the army and the marshals suppress the Klan. `+1` federal faction reach; the Committee's and the Redeemer factions' `Standing` shifts hostile to the federal government. |
| **The Treaty of Washington** (US-UK settlement) | 1871 | The Alabama Claims settled; Anglo-American tension eases. `+1` to the foreign-trade factions' confidence; the diplomatic weather clears. |
| **The Amnesty Act** | 1872 | Most former Confederates re-enfranchised. The Democratic coalition's southern base widens; the Republican southern coalition weakens. `Standing` shifts across the South. |
| **The Credit Mobilier scandal; the Salary Grab** | 1872-73 | Republican corruption exposed. The reform faction's `Standing` rises; the Republican Stalwarts' falls; the Liberal Republican bolt is fueled. |
| **The Specie Resumption Act** | 1875 (effective 1879) | The government will resume specie (gold) payment. Hard-money factions strengthened; debtor and agrarian factions (the Greenbackers' base) weakened and radicalized. The West's debt pressure climbs. |
| **The Desert Land Act** | 1877 | Arid land sold cheap, with irrigation conditions. The desert Southwest's land office business expands; the cattle and the land-speculation factions' opportunities widen. |
| **The Desert Land Act's complement, the Timber and Stone Act** | 1878 | More land cheap, for timber and stone. The logging chapter's and the land-speculation factions' opportunity; the fraud's invitation. |
| **The Bland-Allison Act** (free silver, vetoed then compromised) | 1878 | Silver coinage, in limited amount. The silver-mining faction's boom; the hard-money East's objection; the West's debtor relief. |
| **The Posse Comitatus Act** | 1878 | The army may not be used for domestic law enforcement. The army's role in Reconstruction and labor suppression ends; the marshal's and the posse's role widens; the troop's presence in the county shifts from law to war. |
| **The Chinese Exclusion Act** (the debate and the treaties) | 1879-82 | The anti-Chinese movement rises in California and the West; the Burlingame Treaty is rewritten. | **The Compromise of 1877** (the end of Reconstruction) | 1877 | The troops leave the South; the Democrat (Tilden, or in the compromise, Hayes) takes the White House; the Republican coalition of the 1860s breaks. ### The Year-by-Year Timeline

Each year's entry is the political and world weather the county sits in, the modifier shifts the year produces, and the hooks a campaign set in that year might use.

#### 1870 — The Ascendant Republic

The Republicans hold everything. The Fifteenth Amendment is ratified; the Enforcement Acts are passing; the Ku Klux Klan Act is coming. The South is under military Reconstruction and the freedmen's vote. The transcontinental railroad is a year old; the Indian Wars are ongoing; the frontier is the open question. The world: the Franco-Prussian War reshapes Europe; the Paris Commune is the radical specter the American press invokes.

**Modifiers:** Republican faction reach `+2` nationwide. Federal `Law` strong in the South. The freedmen's faction `+2` `Standing` with the federal government. The Klan and the Redeemer factions `-2` and hunted.

**Campaign hooks:** a campaign in the South is a Reconstruction campaign — the Freedmen's Bureau, the Klan, the troop. A campaign in the West is a railroad-and-Indian-Wars campaign.

#### 1871 — The Treaty and the Fire

The Treaty of Washington settles the Alabama Claims with Britain; the diplomatic weather clears. Chicago burns in October; the national mood darkens and the charity flows. The Klan Act suppresses the first Klan in South Carolina's upcountry. Stanley finds Livingstone; the press of the West prints it late and large.

**Modifiers:** Anglo-American trade factions `+1`. The Klan's suppression continues; the South's Republican state governments are at their height.

#### 1872 — The Liberal Bolt

The Liberal Republicans bolt, nominate Greeley, and the Democrats endorse him — the strange fusion. Grant wins re-election in a landslide. The Amnesty Act re-enfranchises most Confederates. The Credit Mobilier scandal breaks after the election. Yellowstone is the world's first national park.

**Modifiers:** The Republican coalition is at its peak and beginning to crack. The reform faction's `Standing` rises. The southern Republicans' base narrows. The Yellowstone designation is the conservation faction's birth — a new faction for the Western campaigns.

#### 1873 — The Panic

The Panic of 1873 — the great financial collapse, the railroad's overreach, the bank failures, the Long Depression that runs the decade. The Coinage Act of 1873 (the "Crime of '73") ends free silver, which the West and the miners will not forgive. The Modoc War. The Liberal Republicans are over; the Greenbackers are coming.

**Modifiers:** Economic factions (banks, railroads, the barons) `-2` to `-3` `Standing` and capital. Hard-money orthodoxy strengthens; debtor and silver factions radicalize. The Greenback movement begins. A campaign set in 1873-77 is a depression campaign — the unemployed, the tramp, the strike, the debt.

#### 1874-75 — The Depression and the Red River War

The Long Depression deepens. The Democrats take the House in 1874 — the first Democratic Congress since the war. The Red River War (1874-75) breaks the Comanche and the Kiowa on the Southern Plains. The Civil Rights Act of 1875 passes (the last Reconstruction act) and will be gutted by the Court in 1883.

**Modifiers:** The Democrats' national `Standing` rises sharply. The Comanche and Kiowa factions' `Standing` and power collapse (the regional-encounter table's plains entries shift). The reform and civil-rights factions peak and begin their decline. The Great Sioux War is coming.

#### 1876 — The Centennial, the War, the Stolen Election

The Centennial Exposition in Philadelphia. Custer and the 7th Cavalry die at the Little Bighorn (June) — the Great Sioux War's great Native victory, and a set-piece for the mass-combat chapter. The campaign ends with the Sioux's surrender in 1877. The presidential election (Hayes vs. Tilden) is the closest and most contested in American history, resolved by the Electoral Commission and the Compromise of 1877.

**Modifiers:** The Lakota and Cheyenne factions' `Standing` peaks at the Little Bighorn, then collapses with the war's end. The national political weather is unstable — the election is unresolved for months. A campaign in 1876-77 plays under the war and the contested election.

#### 1877 — The Compromise and the Strike

Hayes takes the White House; the troops leave the South; Reconstruction ends. The Great Railroad Strike of 1877 — the first great nationwide strike, the militia and the federal troops called out, the dead in the streets of Martinsburg, Baltimore, Pittsburgh, Chicago. The Nez Perce War (the flight, the fighting retreat, the surrender forty miles from Canada). The Desert Land Act.

**Modifiers:** The Redeemer faction takes the South entirely; the freedmen's faction loses federal protection (a `-3` shift, the largest single `Standing` shift of the decade). The labor faction's `Standing` and visibility surge with the strike and the suppression. The Nez Perce faction's war is the year's Western tragedy.

#### 1878 — The Silver and the Posse

The Bland-Allison Act (silver coinage, over Hayes's veto). The Posse Comitatus Act (the army out of domestic law enforcement). The Timber and Stone Act. The Timber Culture Act. The Lincoln County War in New Mexico (the faction chapter's and the families chapter's great Western example — the Tunstall-McSween against the Murphy-Dolan, and Billy the Kid in the middle).

**Modifiers:** Silver-mining factions `+2`. The army's domestic role ends; the marshal's widens. The land-office business (the inheritance and speculation chapter) expands across the West. The Lincoln County War is the New Mexico campaign's open wound.

#### 1879 — The Exclusion and the Specie

The Chinese Exclusion movement peaks in California; the new state constitution strips the Chinese of rights; the Burlingame Treaty is headed for renegotiation. Specie Resumption takes effect (January 1) — the dollar is redeemable in gold again; the hard-money victory is complete. Edison's incandescent lamp (the future, but not yet the West's). The Ute War (the Meeker Massacre and the White River campaign).

**Modifiers:** Chinese factions `-2` to `-3` `Standing` and legal position in the West. The hard-money victory holds; the Greenback faction fades. The Ute War shifts the Colorado factions.

#### 1880 — The Election and the Boom

Garfield wins the presidency; the Republicans return, narrowly. The census declares the frontier's closing (the "no more frontier" line, premature but resonant). The cattle boom peaks; the silver boom is on; the railroad reaches deeper into the West.

**Modifiers:** Republican faction reach `+1`, restored. The boom factions (cattle, silver, railroad) `+1`. The decade ends on a boom that will bust in the late 1880s, but the campaign at the decade's end does not know that yet.

### The Metaplot and the Campaign

The GM chooses the campaign's year, or years if play crosses the decade, and applies that year's modifiers to the factions, acts, and county pressures. The history is not a script. The county is the table's own, and the party's choices matter. But those choices happen under national weather, and a county that ignores it pretends it is not part of the country.

Across the decade, the modifiers shift under the party's feet. A Republican-collaborationist party in 1870 finds its federal patronage gone by 1877. A freedmen's-community campaign in 1870 finds its federal protection gone by 1877. A cattle boom campaign in 1873 meets the depression; a silver campaign meets the Crime of '73 and spends the decade fighting for the Bland-Allison Act. Labor in 1877 meets strike and troops. A Chinese-community campaign in 1879 meets exclusion. Those shifts make the decade specific, not a generic West that could be any year.

#### Example: Starting in 1877

The GM sets the campaign's opening year in 1877. The Compromise has ended Reconstruction, the Great Railroad Strike is in the papers, and the Desert Land Act has opened new opportunities in arid country. Before play begins, the GM applies the year's weather: Redeemer factions rise in the South, freedmen's protection falls sharply, labor visibility surges, and land-office pressure increases across the dry West.

In the county, this means the railroad can call on troops less easily for domestic labor trouble, but the marshal and local law matter more. The land office sees new filings. A labor faction that was background in 1873 becomes loud in 1877. A party trying to protect a Chinese community in 1879 will feel a different national weather again.

The timeline does not script the campaign. It sets the pressure the county wakes up under.

### The Parties and the Other Rules

The parties are the layer above the factions, and the acts are the layer above the county.

- **Factions:** County and territorial interests belong to national coalitions. A faction's `Standing` with a party is its access to the acts.
- **Robber barons:** Great operators buy into parties. Stanford is a Republican; Gould plays both. A baron's party affiliation is access to legislatures, committees, and acts.
- **Press and elections:** The county's election sits inside a national election. The party's national ticket shapes the county's down-ballot.
- **The justice, land-office, and labor chapters** are where the acts land — the Enforcement Act in the justice chapter, the land acts in the land-office chapter, the Posse Comitatus in the army's role.
- **The town tracker** records national politics only when the act reaches local life. Federal enforcement may add `1` `Law`; withdrawal of troops may remove `1` `Law` or `Welfare`; silver, railroad, land, or tariff policy may move `Mercantile` or `Natural Riches`; election reform may move `Civic`. Most national events should be a modifier, a faction move, or a Town Fortune seed before they become tally points.

The parties run the country's decade, and the acts become the weather the county sits in. The county notices late: the troop leaves the post, the marshal's office changes hands, the deed law shifts, the silver bill passes, or the troop comes South no more. By then the weather has already arrived.
