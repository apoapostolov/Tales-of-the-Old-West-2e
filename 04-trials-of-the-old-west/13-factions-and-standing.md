## Chapter 13 - Factions and Standing

> _THE COUNTY IS NOT RUN FROM THE COURTHOUSE. IT IS RUN FROM THE COUNTERS WHERE THE DEBTS ARE KEPT, AND THE LEDGER DOES NOT CARE WHO HOLDS THE PEN._
>
> — ADELINA SANDOVAL, TO A RIDER WHO ASKED WHO REALLY OWNED THE SPRING

### The County's Long Memory

Every tale of the Old West worth the telling is set inside a web of interests that were there before the player characters arrived and will be there after they are buried. The Santa Fe Ring. The cattleman's association. The railroad and its detectives. The mining company and the union hall. The cavalry post and the nations it campaigns against. The circuit court and the men who appoint it. The vigilance committee that meets in the back of the hardware store. None of these are dungeons to be cleared. They are neighbors, creditors, employers, enemies, and kin, and they remember everything.

A single rivalry can live in ordinary scenes. Two organized interests pushing on the same people and the same ground need a ledger the whole table can see. Track the factions too large, distant, or political for a town's `Law`, `Mercantile`, `Civic`, and `Welfare` scores: how they stand toward the player characters, how their relationships pull on one another, and what they do during the seasons when the player characters are somewhere else.

> _Sandoval turned the ledger so the rider could read it. Three columns: the name, the favor, the date. The favor column was the only one that mattered and they both knew it. She put the pencil behind her ear._
>
> "The railroad wants the right of way," she said. "The Ring wants the title. The post wants the grass. You want to ride through here tonight without any of them the wiser. Which of those would you like to put on the page."
>
> _Riddle did not answer at once. He was counting the names above his._
>
> "What does the page cost," he said.
>
> "It costs the favor," she said. "And it costs the date. A favor on the page is a favor the county can read, and the county reads everything sooner or later. You know that."
>
> "I know it."
>
> "Then you know what you're buying," she said. "Put your name down for the railroad and the Ring reads it by Thursday. Put it down for the Ring and the railroad reads it by Friday. Put it down for neither and you ride out of here tonight with the posse that's coming up the southern road and no one the wiser except me."
>
> _He put his name down for neither. The pencil waited until he was done, and then it crossed out the line above his, which was a man who had owed Sandoval a favor for three years and had just paid it._

### Factions and Standing

A faction is any organized interest with a name, a purpose, and the means to act on it. A single rancher is not a faction; the Cattlemen's Association is. A lone sheriff is not a faction; the territorial bench and its marshals are. A band of raiders is not a faction until it has a leader, a refuge, and a reason anyone outside the band has heard of it.

A campaign run with these rules should track between three and five factions. More than that is clutter. Fewer than three is not a web; it is a feud. The factions worth tracking are the ones whose choices change what the player characters can do in the country, and whose attention the player characters can earn or lose by what they do.

#### What a Faction Is

A faction is defined by five things. Write them down on an index card or a sheet of the campaign record.

- **Name.** What the table and the country call it.
- **Purpose.** What it wants, in one sentence. _Hold the open range for stockmen. Take the land grant whole. Keep the wire off the county. Break the union before it strikes._
- **Means.** How it acts. _Cash and the bench. Detectives and the wire. Riders and rifles. Stock and branding irons._
- **Reach.** How far its arm stretches. **Local** (one town, one county), **Regional** (several counties, a territory), or **Federal** (marshals, the army, the capital). Reach sets the ceiling on what a faction can bring to bear and how far it will pursue a grievance.
- **Standing.** How the faction currently regards the player characters, on the ladder below.

Reach mirrors the outlaw chapter's `Wanted` tiers for a reason. A faction that can call on Federal reach is a different animal from one whose arm stops at the county line, and the table should feel that difference.

### Standing

`Standing` measures how a single faction regards the player characters. It is tracked on a scale from `-3` to `+3`. A faction the player characters have never dealt with starts at `0`.

| Standing | Label | What It Means at the Table |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `+3` | Sworn | The faction treats the party as its own. Shelter, credit, gear, and warning come without asking. The faction will send riders or spend money on the party's behalf. |
| `+2` | Allied | The faction backs the party when asked, for fair cause. `+1 die` on rolls to call on the faction for help, shelter, or a word in the right ear. |
| `+1` | Friendly | The faction looks kindly on the party. Will trade, talk, pass a warning, or look the other way once. |
| `0` | Neutral | No debt either way. The faction watches the party the way it watches anyone. |
| `-1` | Wary | The faction does not trust the party. Trade is grudging, information costs, and the party is watched. |
| `-2` | Opposed | The faction works against the party where it can. `-1 die` on rolls to do business in the faction's country, and the faction will warn its enemies or pass the party's name to the law. |
| `-3` | Hunted | The faction wants the party gone, jailed, or dead. No safe ground in its reach; its agents pursue, and friends of the faction are no friends of the party. |

Standing is per faction. The party may be `Sworn` to the miners' union and `Hunted` by the company that employs them in the same week, and that tension is the campaign.

#### When Standing Moves

Standing moves when the player characters do something the faction notices and has reason to care about. The GM moves Standing directly when the event plainly justifies it, the way the outlaw chapter moves `Cohesion`. Do not move it for every kindness or every slight. Move it for the choices that change a relationship.

| Event | Standing Shift |
| --- | --- |
| The party does the faction a real service, at cost or risk to itself | `+1` |
| The party delivers a major victory or saves a named leader of the faction | `+1`, or `+2` if the service was extraordinary |
| The party refuses profitable work for the faction's rival when offered | `+1` |
| The party fails the faction badly, or leaves a debt unpaid | `-1` |
| The party is caught working for a rival, or breaks a promise to the faction | `-1`, or `-2` if the betrayal was public |
| The party kills a member, leader, or agent of the faction | `-2`, or straight to `-3` (Hunted) if the dead man was kin, named, or well-loved |
| The party robs, ruins, or humiliates the faction | `-2` to `-3` |
| A whole season passes with the party in the faction's good graces and active help | `+1` (a held favor ripens) |
| A whole season passes with the party ignoring a faction that once helped it | `-1` (a held favor sours) |

A faction's Standing should not swing every session. Save changes for moments that tell on the relationship. A favor done in passing is the weather; a debt paid in blood is the climate.

#### Standing and the Outlaw Gauges

`Standing` does not replace the outlaw chapter's `Refuge` or the town's aspects. It sharpens them.

- `Refuge` is a single settlement's regard for the gang. `Standing` is an interest's regard for the party, and an interest may sit inside many settlements or none. When a faction controls a town — a company town, a Ring county seat — its Standing sets the tone for the `Refuge` the gang can build there. Hostile Standing caps Refuge at `0` until the faction is dealt with.
- `Fame` and `Reputation` are the face the whole country sees. `Standing` is what one interest thinks of that face. Dangerous `Fame` earned through crime can sour a faction's Standing by one step the first time it hears of it, and warm an outlaw or radical faction's Standing by the same step. The bench and the Ring do not love a famous killer; the miners' union and the rival gang might.

### The Faction Web — Bonds, Debts, and Who Notices

The `Standing` ladder above tracks how each faction regards the _party_. But the county's factions also regard _each other_, and those relationships are the web's missing dimension. The railroad and the Ring are allies (the Ring's title work clears the railroad's right-of-way); the cattleman's association and the nesters are enemies (the fence war); the union and the company are sworn foes. When the party helps the railroad, the railroad's allies warm to the party and the railroad's enemies cool — and the flat `Standing` ladder, which models each faction as a silo, cannot show that.

Track the **bonds between factions** on a small grid: factions on both axes, and each pair scored on the same `-3` to `+3` ladder. Use the coarse marks to control bookkeeping: `+3` sworn allies, `0` indifferent, `-3` sworn enemies. The bond between the Ring and the railroad is `+2`; the bond between the association and the nesters is `-3`. Bonds are set when the factions enter the campaign and shift by the Relationship Event table and by the campaign's events.

#### When Allies and Enemies Notice

When the party's `Standing` with faction A changes by `1` or more, check the bonds faction A has with the other tracked factions.

- Every faction bonded to A at `+2` or better shifts its `Standing` with the party by `+1`.
- Every faction bonded to A at `-2` or worse shifts its `Standing` with the party by `-1`.
- Bonds of `+1`, `0`, or `-1` are too weak to carry the change.
- This check never cascades. Only A's direct allies and enemies shift.

Helping the railroad is never only helping the railroad. Allies notice, enemies notice, and the party's name moves through the county by road, wire, ledger, and mouth. In a polarized county, neutrality has a cost because everyone is keeping score.

> _Sandoval put the name on the page for the railroad, and she knew what the page would do. The railroad's `Standing` with the rider went up a step, and the Ring's `Standing` — the Ring that was bonded to the railroad at_ `+2` _— went up a step too, because the Ring read the rider's help to the railroad as help to the Ring's own title work. The Committee's `Standing`, bonded to neither, did not move. But the nesters' `Standing`, who were the railroad's enemies at_ `-2` _, went down a step, because the right-of-way the railroad was surveying was the nesters' fenced ground, and the rider who helped the railroad helped survey the nesters off their claims. One favor, three shifts. That was the web._

#### Debts Between Factions

Factions also owe each other **debts** — directional (A owes B is not B owes A), tracked on the same grid as the bonds, on a `-3` to `+3` scale (a positive value means B owes A; a negative value means A owes B). Debts arise from the Relationship Event table and from the campaign's events (the railroad covers the Ring's surveying cost; the Ring owes the railroad). A faction owed a debt by another may **call it** (see the debt-call procedure, below), which is the web's pressure valve — past favors turned into current demands. Debts are the currency the county's factions settle in, and the party that holds a debt over a faction holds a lever the faction cannot ignore.

#### The Relationship Event Table

At each Turn of the Season, after the Faction Roll, roll `D66` once for the **faction web** (not per faction — one event that shifts the graph). The event is the county's politics moving under the party, the way the Faction Roll is the county's actions moving against the party.

| D66 | Event | Effect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `11-13` | **Friction.** Two allied factions bicker over a minor issue. | Their bond drops by `1`. |
| `14-16` | **Old wound.** A historical grievance resurfaces between two factions. | Their bond drops to `-2` (if higher). |
| `21-23` | **Trade or cooperation.** Two factions share a profitable exchange or a mutual threat. | Their bond rises by `1`. |
| `24-26` | **Marriage or merger.** Two factions formalize a tie (a business partnership, a family alliance). | Bond rises by `1`; one assumes a debt of `1` to the other. |
| `31-33` | **Betrayal.** One faction breaks a pact with another. | Bond drops to `-3`; the debt between them inverts. |
| `34-36` | **Raid or aggression.** One faction moves against another's territory or interest. | Bond drops by `2`; the aggressor owes a debt of `1` (reparations, unenforced). |
| `41-43` | **Shared threat.** A third-party threat aligns two enemies temporarily. | Bond rises by `2` (reverts next season if the threat passes). |
| `44-46` | **Scandal.** A faction's leader is disgraced. | That faction's bond with all others drops by `1` for a season. |
| `51-52` | **Debt called.** A faction calls a debt owed by another (or by the party). | Apply the debt-call procedure. |
| `53-55` | **Summit or mediation.** Multiple factions meet; the party may mediate. | Success: two factions' bond rises by `1` and the party's `Standing` with both rises by `1`. Failure: both drop by `1`. |
| `61-63` | **War declared.** Two factions enter open conflict. | Bond drops to `-3`; both may call debts on the party. |
| `64-66` | **Shift in the wind.** A faction's leadership or fortune changes. | Roll `D6`: `1-3` the faction turns hostile to its previous allies (bond drops by `2` with one ally); `4-6` continuity, but the faction's `Standing` with the party shifts by `±1` (the GM's call, by whether the party backed the right side). |

The Relationship Event table keeps the county from sitting still. Together, the Faction Roll and the Relationship Event show what the county did while the player characters were elsewhere. The party is inside that motion, not above it.

#### The Debt-Call Procedure

A faction owed a debt (by another faction or by the party) may **call it** — a GM action, not a roll. The creditor names what it wants (a service, a vote, a non-aggression pact, a resource, access), proportionate to the debt's size (`1` = a small favor; `3` = a major ask). The debtor has three options:

- **Pay.** Perform the demand. The debt steps down by `1` (or to `0` if the demand was major). `Standing` with the creditor rises by `1` (good faith).
- **Refuse.** The debt is voided, but the creditor's `Standing` drops to `-3` (Hunted). Then check the creditor's allies and enemies as above. The debtor has made an enemy.
- **Renegotiate.** A `PRESENCE`/`INSIGHT` social conflict roll. Success converts the debt to a smaller or different form; failure doubles it.

A faction may call at most one debt per season, which prevents debt-spam. The debt-call is the web's primary pressure valve — it turns past favors into current traps, and it is the mechanic that makes the county's factions _dangerous_ in a way the flat ladder never was. A party that owes the railroad a debt is a party that will be asked to pay, and the asking will come at the worst moment, which is the nature of debts and the nature of the county.

### Reading a Faction

Before a faction moves against or for the party, the table may want to know where it stands. Sometimes the player characters know; sometimes they need to find out.

To read a faction's mood or intentions, a player character makes an `INSIGHT` roll. The GM sets the difficulty by how opaque the faction is. A small faction run by a man who drinks at the same bar is easier to read than a Ring with its business in sealed letters.

| Difficulty | Faction Posture |
| --- | --- |
| No roll | The faction has openly declared itself for or against the party. |
| `+1` bonus die | The faction is a small, local interest with a leader the party knows personally. |
| Base pool | The faction is regional, or its council is divided but talkative. |
| `-1` penalty die | The faction keeps its business in letters, wires, and closed rooms. |
| `-2` penalty die | The faction is Federal, foreign, or run by people the party cannot get near. |

Read the roll on the standard ladder. `0` successes, the read is wrong or stale and the GM may feed the party a half-truth. `1`, the party learns the faction's current Standing and its immediate temper. `2`, the party learns that and the faction's next likely move. `3+`, the party learns the Standing, the move, and a name inside the faction who can be reached, bribed, or warned.

The `Judge of Character` talent helps here, as does time spent in the faction's country, a friend on the inside, or a document taken from the faction's desk. The GM should not refuse a sensible source of help dice.

### Calling on a Faction

When the party wants something from a faction — riders, money, a word with the judge, a blind eye, a name kept off a warrant — the character with the strongest relationship to that faction makes a `PRESENCE` roll, or `PERFORMIN'` if the ask is charm, lies, speechifying, or the promise of easy money. Add dice for positive Standing; remove dice for negative.

| Standing | Modifier |
| --- | --- |
| `+3` Sworn | `+2 dice` |
| `+2` Allied | `+1 die` |
| `+1` Friendly | `+1 die` |
| `0` Neutral | no modifier |
| `-1` Wary | `-1 die` |
| `-2` Opposed | `-1 die` |
| `-3` Hunted | `-2 dice`, and the ask itself may be dangerous |

Apply other modifiers only where they truly matter, to the practical maximum of `+3` or `-3`. A favor done last session, a threat held over the faction's head, a shared enemy, a bag of cash on the table — each is one die, and never stack them all.

Read the roll on the standard ladder.

**0 successes:** The faction refuses, and the refusal has a cost. If the faction's Standing was `0` or better, reduce it by `1` (the party has asked and come up short, which is its own kind of insult). If the faction was already hostile, the ask itself may be reported up the chain — the GM may raise the faction's readiness to move against the party.

**1 success (limited):** The faction gives what was asked, but at a price. A favor owed, a job attached, a delay, a worse rate, or a condition the party must meet. Standing holds but the debt is felt.

**2 successes (full):** The faction gives what was asked, cleanly and without strings beyond the ordinary cost of the thing.

**3 successes or more:** The faction goes further than asked. A warning the party did not know it needed, an introduction, an extra man or a better price, or word of a rival's move. Consider raising Standing by `1` if the relationship has been well tended.

> _Riddle made the ask. Sandoval did not roll the dice for him; she watched the railroad's agent watch Riddle, and she counted the seconds before the man answered. The agent said yes, and named the price: a name on a wire, by Thursday, or the spring's title search would go hard for the way-station. That was a limited success. The faction gave what was asked and attached a job. Standing held at_ `0`_, but the debt was on the page now, and the ledger does not forget._

#### Influence — Standing Made Spendable

`Standing` is the relationship's _temperature_; **Influence** is the political capital that relationship generates. It is spendable, and it fades when neglected. A party that hoards it watches it evaporate. A party that spends it must rebuild it.

Each player character (or the party as a whole, by the table's preference) has an **Influence pool** per faction they are tracking, capped at `5` (use the lower cap to control bookkeeping). The pool starts at `0` for a faction the party has no relationship with, and it is **earned by public, witnessed acts** that build the party's name with that faction — the service rendered, the enemy defeated, the favor done in front of witnesses. Each such act rolls on the standard ladder: `1` success earns `1` Influence; `2+` earns `2`; `3+` earns `3` and the county talks. Private acts earn nothing — Influence is the _county's_ regard, not the party's self-regard, and the county only counts what it sees.

| Influence Spend | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| `1` | Gain an audience with a figure who would otherwise refuse; compel a minor ask (a letter, a passage, a small lie). |
| `2` | Compel a moderate ask (a vote, a timely appearance, a concealment); shift a faction decision by one step. |
| `3` | Put a major ask on the table (a pardon, a contract, a public endorsement) with `+3 dice`, or absorb one social failure's consequence if the faction can plausibly cover it. |

Influence does not replace the "Calling on a Faction" roll — it feeds it. A party may spend Influence to add `+1 die` per point spent to a Calling roll, to the normal `+3` cap, or to claim an ordinary minor favor from that faction as already settled. A minor favor can be claimed this way only once per faction per season, and it cannot buy murder, erase a capital charge, create free Capital, or force a faction to act against its purpose. The spend is visible (the party threw its weight around), which is the cost of the shortcut.

#### When Influence Fades

Influence **decays**. At each Turn of the Season, each Influence pool the party has not actively cultivated that season steps down by `1` (to a minimum of `0`). Cultivation is a public, faction-appropriate act — a service, a show of support, a visible presence at the faction's event. A party that earned the railroad's Influence in the spring and then rode north for the summer comes back to find the Influence has cooled by `1`, because the railroad's regard is a current, not a reservoir, and the county's memory is shorter than the party hopes.

`Standing` is the long relationship. Influence is the current favor the party can spend. A political campaign lives on Influence — earned, spent, rebuilt — and a party that treats it like savings will find the account empty when it needs it most.

> _Sandoval read the ledger and saw the Influence the way she saw the flour — a thing that spoiled if it sat. The railroad's Influence was at_ `3` _, earned in the spring when the rider had run the right-of-way survey past the nesters' fence, and it had not been touched since, and the season had turned, and the decay would take it to_ `2` _by the fall unless the rider did the railroad another visible service. The Ring's Influence was at_ `0` _, which was honest, because the rider had done the Ring nothing it could see. The post's Influence was at_ `1` _, fading, because the last patrol Carver had watered at the spring was two months ago and the post's memory was a season long._
>
> _She put the pencil behind her ear. Influence was a current. The party that forgot that lost the current and kept the ledger, which was the same as having nothing, in a county that only counted what it could see._

#### Example: One Favor, Three Consequences

Riddle helps the railroad survey a right-of-way past a nester fence. The railroad's `Standing` with the party rises from `0` to `+1`, and the public nature of the help earns `2` railroad Influence. Because the railroad is bonded to the Ring at `+2`, the Ring's `Standing` also rises by `1`. Because the nesters are bonded to the railroad at `-2`, the nesters' `Standing` drops by `1`.

At the Turn of the Season, the party spends `1` railroad Influence to ask the operator to delay a hostile wire. The ask succeeds, but the Influence is spent and visible. The nesters hear who helped the railroad, and their next Faction Roll comes up `Pressure`: a fence goes up across Sandoval's road. The favor did not create a bonus and vanish. It moved the county.

### The Faction Roll — When the World Acts

Factions do not wait for the player characters. The railroad builds. The Ring lobbies. The union organizes. The army rides. The vigilance committee meets on Tuesday whether the party is in town or a hundred miles up the trail.

At the end of each Turn of the Season, after the season's ordinary business is resolved, the GM makes a **Faction Roll** for each tracked faction.

Resolve the faction turn in this order:

1. Apply any `Standing` changes earned by the player characters during the season.
2. Check which allies and enemies notice those changes.
3. Step down any Influence pools the party did not cultivate this season.
4. Resolve one Relationship Event for the whole faction web.
5. Make one Faction Roll for each tracked faction.
6. Advance, erase, or reveal Faction Clocks.
7. Put at least one visible result in front of the table next session: a rumor, a wire, a rider, a newspaper notice, a lien, a warrant, a job offer, or a body in the road.

One roll per faction. Roll `D6` and modify by the faction's current Standing toward the party.

| Standing | Modifier |
| --- | --- |
| `+3` Sworn | `+2` |
| `+2` Allied | `+1` |
| `+1` Friendly | `+1` |
| `0` Neutral | `0` |
| `-1` Wary | `-1` |
| `-2` Opposed | `-1` |
| `-3` Hunted | `-2` |

Apply a further `+1` if the faction is Federal reach and well-funded (the army in winter quarters, the Ring in a legislative year), or `-1` if the faction is broke, divided, or under pressure itself this season. Do not stack these; pick the one that matters.

Read the modified roll on the table below. The result tells the GM what the faction did this season with its time and money. The player characters hear about it through rumor, the newspaper, a wire, a rider, or the damage it does to a place they know. The result is never silent.

| D6 | Faction Move |
| --- | --- |
| `0 or less` | **Setback.** The faction lost ground this season, to its rivals, the law, the weather, or its own blunder. The GM narrates the loss. If the party has been working against it, this is a reward felt at a distance. |
| `1` | **Quiet.** The faction tended its own and made no move worth the county's talk. Standing with the party holds. |
| `2` | **A small step.** The faction tightens its grip on something it already holds — a claim, a town, a contract, a trail. The party may feel this as a price gone up or a door gone shut. |
| `3` | **A hand extended.** The faction reaches toward the party or the party's country. An offer of work, a warning, a gift with a hook in it, or a request the faction expects answered. The GM puts the offer or the request in front of the player characters next session. |
| `4` | **A rival moved.** The faction acts against one of its own enemies — which may or may not align with the party's interests. The party may be offered a part, or warned away, or simply hear the shots in the distance. |
| `5` | **Pressure.** The faction brings its weight to bear on the party's country, allies, or interests directly. A tax, a lien, a warrant, a fence, a patrol, a boycotted merchant. The party must decide whether to absorb it, answer it, or move. |
| `6+` | **A bold move.** The faction commits to something large and visible — a land grab, a raid, a strike, an arrest, a war. The shape of the county changes. The player characters are in the path of it, or close enough to choose a side. |

The GM should not resolve a `5` or `6+` result with a single sentence. Those results are the seeds of the next stretch of campaign. Frame them as scenes, offers, threats, or news that demands a response, and let the table decide what to do about them.

A faction reduced to a `Setback` result while already weak may be removed from play, the way a town can fail. A faction that rolls bold moves season after season is winning the county, and that too is a campaign.

#### How Many Factions Roll

Roll for every tracked faction every season. That is the point. The county does not pause. If the GM finds that more than four factions are rolling and the table cannot track the results, retire the one the table cares about least. Three factions, each making one move a season, is enough pressure for any campaign.

> _At the end of the season Sandoval closed the ledger and read the county back to herself. The railroad had run the right-of-way survey past the way-station while she was arguing about the title — a_ `Pressure` _result, felt as a line of stakes in her pasture. The Ring had lost a man in Santa Fe to a rival's letter — a_ `Setback` _for them, which she marked as a small gain for everyone the Ring had ever robbed. The post had made a_ `Bold Move` _north, a column out after the horse raiders, which meant Carver was gone and the county was quieter and meaner by the same measure. She put the pencil behind her ear and went to put the kettle on. Three rolls. Three moves. None of them had waited for her._

### Factions and Towns

A faction is not a town, but a faction often sits inside one, and the town's aspects bear on what the faction can do there.

When a faction acts through a settlement — running its court, working its mine, holding its contract — use the town's aspects to read the faction's strength in that place. A faction backed by a town's `Law 5` and `Mercantile 4` is a power in that county; the same faction's local agent in a `Law 1` mining camp is a man with a title and not much else.

| Town Condition | What It Means for a Faction There |
| --- | --- |
| `Law 4-5` and `Civic 4+` | The faction's writ runs. Calling on the faction here is `+1 die`; calling against it is `-1 die`. |
| `Mercantile 5-6` | The faction has money and supply here. Cash asks are easier; boycotts are harder. |
| `Law 1-2` and `Welfare 1-2` | The faction holds on by force or not at all. Its agents are armed and nervous. |
| `Civic 1-2` | There is no institution to lean on. The faction acts through personalities, not offices. |

When a Faction Roll changes public life for a season, mark the town tracker. A faction that builds, funds, or protects something may add `1` tally point to the aspect it strengthened. A faction that closes a road, buys a court, breaks a strike, burns a rival, or drives people off land may remove `1` point from the aspect it damaged. Do not move the town for every faction move; use the town tracker only when ordinary people can see the change in work, safety, trade, institutions, or health.

Root a faction in a place or it floats above the county. The Ring is a different animal in the county seat where it owns the judge than it is in the mining camp where its only man is a foreman with a handgun.

### Faction Clocks and Operations

When a faction sets out to do something large — drive a people off a grant, break a union, take a herd, run a rail line past a holdout — the GM may frame it as a **Faction Clock**: a simple countdown the player characters can see, speed, or slow.

A Faction Clock is a track of `4`, `6`, or `8` segments, set by the scale of the faction's ambition. Each season the faction works the project, fill one segment (more, if a bold Faction Roll warrants it). When the clock fills, the project completes: the right-of-way is surveyed, the lien is filed, the warrant is sworn, the strike is broken, the raid is launched.

The player characters interact with a Faction Clock the way they interact with any operation. They can investigate it (`INSIGHT`, `HAWKEYE`), interfere with it (sabotage, argument, a counter-filing, a bribe to the right clerk), or help it along. A successful interference erases one or more segments and may raise the faction's readiness; a critical interference may turn the clock into a visible confrontation. The `Hidden Bad Luck` rule applies when the party keeps poking a faction's project without covering its tracks.

A campaign should not run more than two Faction Clocks at once. They are for the things the party can see coming and choose to do something about, not for background the GM is running for its own sake.

### Sample Factions

Three examples, in the county this book is set in. They are written in the statblock voice the rest of the book uses, so a GM can see how a faction looks on the page. The named figures inside each are drawn from the cast this book is built around, and from the corebook's New Mexico campaign where the histories overlap.

#### The Land Grant Operators

| Name | The Santa Fe Ring (the Quinlivan faction) |
| --- | --- |
| Purpose | Take the Maxwell Grant country whole, title, range, and mineral rights, before the rivals and the squatters can stop them. |
| Means | Cash, the bench, surveyors, hired pistols, the wire. |
| Reach | Regional, pressing Federal through the territorial delegate. |
| Standing | `0` to a party unknown to them; `-2` to a party that has crossed them. |

The Ring's agent in the county is a man named Rockcliffe, who keeps an office in the county seat and a copy of every filing. Its muscle is hired and deniable. Its patience is long because its money is longer. The Ring works through Faction Clocks: the survey, the quiet-title suit, the lien on the holdout's water. It does not draw iron in the street. It draws liens in the courthouse, which is slower and worse.

**Inside the faction:** a clerk who can be reached through Sandoval's counter (he owes her for a winter's board); a surveyor with a conscience and a drinking problem; the territorial delegate, unreachable except through Santa Fe.

#### The Tenth Cavalry, Company D

| Name | The Tenth, Company D (the Buffalo Soldier troop) |
| --- | --- |
| Purpose | Hold the military road, keep the raiding off the settlers, answer the bench's warrants when called. |
| Means | Carbines, discipline, horses, the column. |
| Reach | Regional, with Federal weight behind the warrants. |
| Standing | `0` to strangers; `+1` to settlers the troop has pulled out of trouble; `-2` to a party the bench has sworn a warrant on. |

The troop is commanded by a lieutenant the regiment does not trust with anything important and run, in practice, by its sergeants — among them Moses Carver, whose word is the troop's word in the county. The Tenth is not a political faction; it is the long arm of a court that cannot keep order by itself. When Judge Voss swears a warrant the county cannot serve, the wire goes to the post, and the Tenth rides. This makes the troop a tool of every faction that can get a warrant sworn, which is the colony's oldest problem.

**Inside the faction:** Carver, who reads everything he can steal off the post and can be reached at Sandoval's spring; the lieutenant, who can be flattered, fooled, or bypassed; the first sergeant, who can neither.

#### The Vigilance Committee

| Name | The Committee (no official name; meets in the back of the hardware store) |
| --- | --- |
| Purpose | Keep the county the way the men who founded it want it kept. Hang the horse thieves, run the Mexican families off the water, break the union, and call it all justice. |
| Means | Rope, torches, the silence of the men who know and say nothing. |
| Reach | Local, with one eye cast toward the Ring that winks back. |
| Standing | `+1` to a party of white Anglo men who do not ask questions; `-2` to `-3` to anyone the Committee has marked. |

The Committee is the violence the bench cannot or will not reach. It is small, local, and vicious, and its strength is the silence of the town around it. It does not work through Faction Clocks. It works through a single bad night, and the player characters hear about it at dawn. The Committee is a faction the table can fight, join, flee, or try to bring under the law — and the law, in the person of Judge Voss, is not sure it can reach them either.

**Inside the faction:** a storekeeper who chairs the meetings; two ranchers who supply the rope; the silence of a dozen more who attend and say nothing and are, in the committee's accounting, as guilty and as useful as the men who pull.

> _Voss sat on the hotel porch and watched the Committee's men cross the street and did not call them by name. He knew them. The court knew them. The county knew them. The rope had been up in the cottonwood since sundown and the body had been cut down by first light and there was no witness and no deposition and no man in the county who would swear he had seen a thing. The bench's reach was the bench's reach. He took off the spectacles and rubbed the bridge of his nose and put them back on and went in to write the letter to the marshal that he knew would go unanswered. That is what a faction that works through silence looks like, from the inside of the law that cannot reach it._

### Designing Your Own Factions

Steal the three above, or build your own. Answer the five questions, set the Standing, and put the faction on an index card. The best factions are the ones whose purpose puts them naturally in the party's path — not villains, but interests. A cattleman's association that hires the party to drive off nesters is not an enemy; it is an employer with a purpose, and the party's choice to take the work or refuse it is what moves Standing.

A faction needs a name, a purpose, a means, a reach, and a Standing. Then give it a person: a man with a desk, a woman with a ledger, a sergeant with a letter in his pocket. The town it holds and the clock it runs matter because someone in the county is doing the work.

At the table, the country should feel as if it has its own business and the player characters are inside it, not at the center of it.
