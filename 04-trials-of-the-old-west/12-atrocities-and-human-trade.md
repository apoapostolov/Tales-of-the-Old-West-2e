## Chapter 12 - Atrocities and Human Trade

> _THE WEST WAS BUILT ON LAND TAKEN AND PEOPLE SOLD AND PEOPLE KILLED WHO WERE IN THE WAY. A RULEBOOK THAT HIDES THAT IS LYING ABOUT THE COUNTRY. A RULEBOOK THAT CELEBRATES IT IS WORSE THAN A LIE, AND A RULEBOOK THAT MAKES A GAME OF IT WITHOUT ITS WEIGHT IS THE WORST THING OF ALL._
>
> — A NOTE FROM THE AUTHOR, AT THE FRONT OF THE CHAPTER

### Before the Rules

The West ran on atrocities. Native nations were removed, massacred, and confined by a campaign that lasted through the century and beyond. The trade in captive Native women and children was a border economy with a price list and a market. Chattel slavery survived the Thirteenth Amendment as debt peonage in New Mexico, convict leasing in railroads and mines, and the practical re-enslavement of Black life across the former Confederacy and into the territories. The sex slavery of the Chinese trade moved women across the Pacific and through the railheads in a ledger as cold as any plantation's. Texas Rangers and vigilance committees practiced racial terror that, at times, was less law enforcement than ethnic cleansing under a badge.

Handle this material only if the table has chosen it soberly. Careless use turns other people's suffering into spectacle; refusal to name it leaves the country cleaner than it was. The procedures below are for campaigns that need the worst parts of the period to carry mechanical weight: `Standing`, `Wanted`, `Fame`, `Reputation`, `Cohesion`, `Doubts`, and `Vexes` all move when characters commit, resist, profit from, or expose atrocities.

Read the whole section before use, agree what is and is not in play, and keep the focus on consequence rather than display. If the table leaves this material out, the rest of the supplement still works. If it comes in, no character who orders, carries out, excuses, or profits from the act remains clean.

> _Carver sat by the fire with the order in his hand and did not read it again because he had read it twice and the words had not changed between the readings. The order was to move the people off the creek and burn the wickiups and hold the ground until the surveyors came, and the people on the creek were a band of thirty-seven who had been there longer than the territory and longer than the treaty that said they were not._
>
> _The lieutenant was already asleep. Carver was not. The first sergeant was not._
>
> "You've read it," the first sergeant said.
>
> "I've read it."
>
> "And."
>
> "And we move them off the creek. The order is the order, Sam. You know that as well as I do."
>
> "I know what we did at the Washita," the first sergeant said. "I know what we carried out of there. I am asking what we are going to carry out of here."
>
> _Carver folded the order and put it in his blouse and did not answer, because the answer was the order and the order was the country and the country did not care what Moses Carver carried out of it, only that he carried what he was told. He sat by the fire until it burned down, and in the morning he gave the order to move, and the people on the creek moved, and the wickiups burned, and the sergeant carried it, the way the army carried everything it was told to carry, which is in the body and not on the page._

### The Atrocity as Operation

A massacre, a raid on a homestead, a punitive expedition against a camp, the clearance of a people off a creek — these are operations, run with the outlaw chapter's operation rules. Use them only when the table has chosen to put this material in play and the scene needs the pressure of procedure. Progress is the coercive aim accomplished; Trouble is the cost, human and otherwise. These rules do not reward this work the way they reward a clean score. They make the weight of it tell on the characters who do it, in their own damage tracks, their `Standing`, and the county's memory.

| Atrocity Scale | Progress Needed | Trouble Limit | The Act |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `A Raid` | `4` | `3` | A single homestead, a camp, a family; the work of a night |
| `A Clearance` | `6` | `4` | A settlement, a band, a community; the work of days |
| `A Campaign` | `8` | `5` | A people, a region, a season; the work the army or the militia was built for |

Progress is the atrocity accomplished — the people moved, the camp burned, the captives taken, the dead counted. Trouble is what the act costs the people who do it, and the cost is never only material.

#### The Moral Weight on the Doer

The damage tracks are the chapter's moral instrument. A character who participates in an atrocity takes `Doubts` and `Vexes` as the Trouble climbs, regardless of the character's will or the order that sent them. This is not a choice the player makes; it is the cost the body and the mind pay for the work, the way the body pays for the powder smoke and the mind pays for the face of the dead.

| Trouble Step | What the Doer Takes |
| --- | --- |
| `1` | `1` `Doubts`. The act is in the character's head and will not leave. Steps the **Corrosion Track** (below) up by `1`. |
| `2` | `1` `Doubts` and `1` `Vexes`. The character is short with his company, drinks later, does not sleep. Steps the Corrosion Track up by `1`. |
| `3` | `1` `Doubts` and `1` `Vexes`, and the character's `Reputation` begins to carry the act — the county talks. Steps the Corrosion Track up by `1`. |
| `4+` | `1` `Doubts` and `1` `Vexes`, a permanent `Reputation` shift toward the violent and the feared, and a `Cohesion` test for any unit or band that did the work together. Steps the Corrosion Track up by `1`. |

A character pushed to `Broken` on `Docity` by these `Doubts` is a character the act has broken. Let that play: the drink, the desertion, the sermon, the long quiet life of a man who cannot forget a face. The chapter does not offer absolution. It offers an accounting: the men who did these things in the actual West carried them, and many of them were broken by the carrying, and the broken ones were not less real than the unrepentant ones.

#### The Moral Corrosion Track

The `Doubts` and `Vexes` the table above assigns are the immediate cost — the act's weight in the doer's damage tracks, which heal and push and pay the way damage always does. But the moral weight of atrocity does not reset the way a damage track does. It **accumulates**, across atrocities and across the campaign, in a character's `Reputation`, his `Standing` with the county's decent factions, and the kind of man the county reads him as. Track this accumulation as a **Corrosion gauge** (`0`-`5`). The Corrosion gauge is the chapter's long-term instrument, and it is the thing that makes a man who has done three atrocities a different man from one who has done one, even if his damage tracks have healed between them.

| The Corrosion Gauge | What the Character Has Become |
| --- | --- |
| `0–2` | The character carries what he has done, but the county does not know him by it. The damage tracks tell the private cost; the public man is whole, for now. |
| `3` | **The Reputation shifts.** The character's `Reputation` takes a permanent lean toward the violent and the feared. The county's decent factions (the church, the reformers, the law-abiding) drop `Standing` by one step; the county's hard men (the Committee, the rustlers, the corrupt) rise by one. The character is read as a man who has done a thing, whether he admits it or not. |
| `4` | **The name travels.** The character's `Fame` shifts toward the feared; the decent factions drop another step; strangers have heard the name and the story. The character is the man who did the thing, and the thing is the first thing the county says about him. |
| `5` | **Corroded.** The character is the thing he did. The decent factions are at `Hunted` or worse; the county's worst factions court him; the character's `Docity` is capped (he cannot fully heal the `Doubts` the work left). This is a slow moral death the campaign tracks — the man the atrocity made, carrying it until he is undone by it or redeems it at a cost the ledger will name. |

The Corrosion gauge climbs with every atrocity the character participates in (one step per Operation, plus the Trouble steps above). It **does not fade on its own** — a season of peace does not wash the work off the man, the way a season of peace does not wash a rusted nail. The only way to step the Corrosion gauge down is **atonement**: costly, risky acts of repair or refusal — freeing captives taken by one's own side, testifying against the faction that gave the order, standing between the Committee and its target. Each atonement act is a `RESILIENCE`/`DOCITY` roll made under real danger (the faction's enmity, the county's ridicule, the physical risk), and success steps the Corrosion gauge down by one. Atonement is slow, dangerous, and never complete — a man who reaches `Corrosion 5` and works his way back to `2` has done heroic work, and the `2` he carries is still the record of what he did. The chapter does not offer absolution.

#### The Refusal

A character who refuses the order — who will not move the people, will not fire the camp, will not take the captives — has made the chapter's central moral choice, and the rules make the choice costly on both sides. Refusal steps the Corrosion gauge _down_ by one (the character chose not to do the thing, and the choosing is a kind of repair), but it shifts the character's `Standing` with the ordering faction sharply toward `Hunted` and risks the court-martial, the rope, or the bullet in the back for the refusal itself.

| The Refusal's Cost | The Effect |
| --- | --- |
| **With the ordering faction** | `Standing` drops to `Hunted` (or worse, if the faction is the army and the refusal is in the field — the court-martial, the firing squad). |
| **With the county's decent factions** | `Standing` rises by one step — the church, the reformers, the people who were spared. The refusal is the only act in this chapter that _improves_ a character's regard with the decent, which is why it is rare and why it hurts. |
| **On the Corrosion gauge** | Steps _down_ by one. The refusal is an atonement act made under fire, and it counts as one. |
| **On the character's safety** | The refusal risks `Wanted` (if the faction frames it as a crime), the Committee's attention (if the faction is the county's hard men), or the physical danger of being the man who said no in front of men with guns who said yes. The GM should frame the refusal's consequence as a real and present danger, not a footnote. |

The refusal is the chapter's central moral choice because it is genuinely _both-sided_: costly on the side of the faction whose order was refused, and redeeming on the side of the Corrosion gauge and the decent factions. A character who refuses is a character who has bet his life, his `Standing`, and his career against the weight of the act he would not do. Let that bet be as dangerous and as redeeming as it was in the actual West, where the men who refused were few, and the country did not thank most of them, and the ones it did thank were thanked long after the cost was paid.

### The Trade in People

The trade in people is the chapter's second subject, and it is run as an economy with a buyer, a seller, a price, and a faction behind it. The chapter models the trade's existence and its weight; it does not provide a price list for human beings that a table could use as commerce. If the GM names prices, name them only when the fiction demands it, and let the naming be the horror it is.

The trades of the period, each a faction or an outlaw operation:

- **Captive-taking (the border trade).** The Comanche and the border economies that traded in captives taken from both sides of the line — the Comanche taking Anglo, Mexican, and Tejano; the Texans and the rurales taking Comanche and Kiowa. The trade was old, organized, and human in both directions. A captive trade is an outlaw operation (the raid, the taking, the sale) with the captive's fate as the Trouble that does not wash off the taker, the buyer, or the faction behind them.
- **Debt peonage.** The Nuevomexicano grant system's survival of the Thirteenth Amendment, in which a worker's debt to the patron was heritable, enforceable, and indistinguishable from slavery for the peon held by it. The Santa Fe Ring and the local dons ran it; the courts upheld it. Treat the patron as a faction with a workforce that is not free, and treat the party's choice to use peon labor, oppose it, or free a peon as a `Standing` move with the patron and the law.
- **Convict leasing.** The system by which the Black freedman was returned to bondage by the law — arrested on a trumped charge, fined beyond his means, leased to the railroad, the mine, or the plantation at a profit to the state and a cost to the convict that exceeded the slave's cost, because the convict was cheaper to work to death than the slave had been. Treat the convict lease as a faction's resource and the party's encounter with it as a hard choice: a party that breaks a convict out of a lease camp has made a Federal enemy.
- **The Chinese trade.** The movement of women across the Pacific and through the railheads for the brothels of the West, run by the tongs, underwritten by the exclusion that made the women worth more for their scarcity. Treat the trade as the saloon chapter's hardest edge: the rooms that are not voluntary, the women who are not free to leave, the keeper who may or may not know, and the party's choice to disrupt the trade at the cost of the tong's enmity and the law's indifference.
- **The re-enslavement and the terror.** The Black codes, the Klan, the lynching (the justice chapter's hardest result), and the practical unfreedom of Black life in the former Confederacy and the territories. Treat the terror as a faction (the Klan, the Committee, the Rangers) operating outside or beside the law, and the party's relation to it as the chapter's central moral test: to oppose, to endure, to flee, or to be complicit.

#### The Captive as a Tracked Person

The person targeted by the trade is a person, and the rules treat that person as a person, never as a commodity. A captive is a **named NPC** — with a name, attributes, the corebook's condition ladder, and a `Standing` with the captor (a small gauge, `-3` to `+3`, that shifts with treatment). The captive's condition tracks are **live**: a captive who is starved takes the `Starving` condition; a captive who is beaten takes real harm on the damage tracks; a captive who is `Broken` on an attribute is a captive the captor has harmed in a way the player must _see_, not a Trouble step the dice abstract away.

The captive's `Standing` with the captor is the gauge the captor's treatment moves. A captive treated as a human being (fed, sheltered, not beaten) sees `Standing` climb toward `0` or above — which means the captive is more able to speak, remember, resist, testify, flee, or ask for help. A captive treated as property (starved, beaten, broken) sees `Standing` drop below `0` — which means the captive is nearer to death, to the abuse that kills, or to disappearance into the trade's machinery. The rules do not reward cruelty: cruelty steps the Corrosion gauge up, regardless of the captive's `Standing`. The captive's `Standing` is the face of the trade's central horror: the captive's survival depends on the captor's choices, and those choices are tracked where the table can see them.

A party that encounters a captive — by taking one, by paying a ransom to free one, by finding one in a raid — must decide what to do with the person, and the decision is tracked. Freeing a captive is an atonement act (steps the Corrosion gauge down); holding one is the trade; selling one is the trade's economy; and a captive who travels with the party is a named NPC whose condition and `Standing` the party tracks, the way it tracks any compadre. The captive is a person. The rules treat her as one. The player cannot look away, which is the chapter's whole purpose.

#### Example: Refusal at the Creek

Carver is ordered to clear a creek camp before surveyors arrive. The GM has already checked with the table that this chapter is in play. Carver refuses the order and stands between the troop and the camp. His `Standing` with the ordering faction drops to `Hunted`, and the GM frames the immediate danger: arrest in the field, a court-martial, or a lieutenant willing to make an example of him.

The refusal steps Carver's Corrosion gauge down by `1`, because refusing is an atonement act under real danger. His `Standing` with the county's decent factions rises by `1`, but that help is distant; the officer with the order is in front of him now. If Carver also helps the camp's captive boy escape the column afterward, that rescue is a second atonement act, but it carries new risk: the army calls it desertion or theft, and the faction's enmity follows.

The example is not a way to make the act clean. It is a way to make the refusal playable: a moral choice with a cost in career, safety, faction standing, and the campaign's future.

### The Army and the Ranger at Their Worst

The massacre is the order the sergeant is given and the order he may or may not refuse. The Indian Wars, the border wars, and the labor wars are the contexts in which the order comes, and the mass combat and faction chapters are the tools for running them. This chapter is the place where the honest work of naming what those orders were gets done — the Washita, the Marias, Camp Grant, Sand Creek — and the GM who uses this chapter should name them, and let the names carry the weight the names carry, rather than reducing them to a generic "clearance."

The Texas Rangers are rendered as the paramilitary instrument they were — a state-sanctioned force with a documented record of ethnic cleansing along the Rio Grande, the massacre of Mexican and Mexican-American communities, and the terrorizing of the border population under the authority of the State of Texas. The chapter does not soften this. A campaign with the Rangers in play has them as a faction whose methods are atrocity, and the party's relation to them as a hard choice.

The vigilance committee is the atrocity the county does to itself — the lynching (the justice chapter), the racial pogrom, the expulsion of the Chinese community, the terror visited on the Black family that prospered too visibly. The Committee is a faction (the factions chapter) whose purpose is the county's "purity" and whose means are the rope and the torch. Let it be what it was: the violence the law could not or would not reach, done by the county's respectable men in the dark, and remembered by the county's survivors in the light.

### Carrying the Weight

Let the consequences land on the characters who earned them. The actual West often failed to make that accounting; the table does not have to repeat the failure. Name the act, name the people harmed, move the gauges, and let the campaign change around the damage.

The country was built on these things. Saying so matters only if play changes afterward: a faction loses legitimacy, a soldier cannot sleep, a survivor's testimony shifts the county, a patron's money becomes poisonous, and a future opportunity closes because the past finally has a cost.
