## Chapter 8 - Logging, Fur Trade, and Hunting

> _THE WILD LAND'S TRADE IS THE TRADE OF TAKING WHAT GROWS AND TURNING IT INTO WHAT SELLS — THE TREE INTO THE BOARD, THE BEAVER INTO THE HAT, THE BUFFALO INTO THE ROBE. THE MEN WHO DID THIS WORK LIVED WHERE THE WORK WAS, AND WHERE THE WORK WAS WAS THE COUNTRY THE COUNTIES HAD NOT REACHED YET._
>
> — WEBB CALDER, TO A DUDE WHO ASKED WHAT MEN DID PAST THE RANGE

### Work Taken From Wild Country

Travel takes a party into the country past the trail; work keeps men there. The logging camp takes the timber, the trapline takes the fur, and the market hunt takes the hide and the meat. All three are seasonal enterprises built on wild resources, hard operations to bring the take out, and trades that emptied the country they worked as surely as rail and plow did.

Lifepaths tell you who a trapper, lumberjack, or market hunter is. A season in the wild trade tells you what the work takes. Run the logging camp as a business, the river drive as work timed by thaw, the trapline as a seasonal enterprise with a rendezvous at the far end, and the market hunt as a take-based trade whose success hastens the buffalo's disappearance from the country that paid for the hides.

> _The logger stood at the river's edge and watched the last of the winter's drive come through the boom, which was the logs caught in the pocket and waiting for the mill, and the mill was the year's wages and the camp's keep and the company's dividend, and the river had brought it all down from the timber in the high country. The drive had cost two men — one drowned at the jam at the narrows, one crushed by the swing of a sawlog at the take-out — and the logger counted the cost the way the company did not, because the company counted the board feet and the logger counted the names._
>
> "How much made the boom?"
>
> "The better part of the cut. We'll make the winter's wages."
>
> "And the two?"
>
> "The two don't make the boom. The two make the count at the camp and the letter to the widows. The drive's the drive, and the river takes what it takes." The logger did not look away from the water. "Next winter we go back up and we cut it again, and the river brings it down again, and the county builds its houses out of what the high country grew and the river carried and the men died for. That's the trade. That's all the trade is."

### Logging and the Lumber Camp

Logging is the trade that built the West's houses, its mines' timber, and its railroads' ties, and the lumber camp is the trade's home — a seasonal camp in the timber, a crew of axemen and teamsters and rivermen, and a company that sells the cut and pays the wages. The logging camp runs on the same season order as the other trades, but its central measure is the **Stand die** (the timber tract that depletes as it is cut) and its operating event — the river drive — is **timed by the spring thaw**, a clock that runs regardless of the drive's Progress and ends the drive when the water drops, whether the logs made the boom or not.

Run a logging season in this order.

1. **Assess the stand and water.** Read the timber, the snowpack, the river, and the market. Make a `NATURE`, `HAWKEYE`, or `BOOKLEARNIN'` check. On a success, learn whether the stand is rich, the thaw will be thin, the market is hungry, or a rival is cutting nearby. On a failure, the GM starts the river drive with `+1` Trouble or a shortened thaw-clock.
2. **Invest in the camp.** Choose one investment: **Sharp Tools and Fresh Team** (`+1` die to the Season Log Roll), **River Men** (`+1` die to jam and boom stages), **Camp Safety** (first injury Penalty becomes lost time), or **New Timber Rights** (`+1` die to finding the next stand).
3. **Cut the winter stand.** Step the stand die down once if the camp works the tract this season.
4. **Drive the river.** Run the River Drive as an operation, using the thaw-clock below.
5. **Reckon the season.** Make the Season Log Roll and apply the result.
6. **Move, reinvest, or take profit.** If the stand is cut out, prospect for new timber or close the camp. Otherwise, repair gear, pay widows, hire hands, secure rights, or take the season's money.

#### The Lumber Camp as Business

| The Camp | What It Is |
| --- | --- |
| Key ability | `LABOR` (the axeman's and the teamster's trade) or `MAKIN'` (the sawyer's and the millwright's) |
| Prerequisites | A timber tract (the land office chapter's claim or a company lease), a crew, the tools (axe, crosscut saw, the skidding gear), and a mill or a market |
| Town aspect modifiers | `Natural Riches` (the timber's quality and access) and `Mercantile` (the market for the cut) |
| The Season Log Roll | `0` a Penalty, `1` steady (wages and keep paid), `2+` a Bonus with `+1` to the Tens die per success beyond the first |

The logging Bonus and Penalty table follows the cattle and mining chapters' shape — the good winter's cut, the lost drive, the fire in the camp, the jam at the narrows, the market's price, the company's hand. The logging Trouble is the wild land's: the weather (the weather chapter and the winter chapter), the river drive's drownings, the falling tree's kill, the fire that takes the camp and the cut, and the simple attrition of men working in timber with sharp tools and heavy loads.

#### The Stand Ledger

The logger's distinct asset is the **stand** — the tract of timber the camp works, tracked as a **stand die** (the stepped die, `D6` → `D12`, representing the tract's board-feet and access). Each winter's cut **steps the stand die down** by one size — the timber that went to the mill is the timber that is gone, and a stand is a finite thing the way a vein is finite and a grass die is finite.

| The Stand Die | The Tract |
| --- | --- |
| `D6` | A small or worked-out stand — the last of the timber, or a thin tract far from the water |
| `D8` | A working stand — enough for the camp's winter, with decent access to the river |
| `D10` | A rich stand — the timber is thick and close to the drive |
| `D12` | A virgin stand — the high country's uncut timber, the kind that draws the company and the land-office man both |

When the stand die steps to `D6` and would step down again, the tract is **cut out**, and the camp must move — find a new stand (a `HAWKEYE`/`NATURE` prospecting run into uncut timber) or go under. The logger's life is a series of stand die step-downs punctuated by the move to new timber, and the logging company that lasts is the company whose stands keep finding timber. That is the timber frontier's restless push into the high country and the ultimate exhaustion of the western forests.

#### The Drive's Thaw-Clock

The river drive is logging's signature operation, and its distinct mechanic is that it is **timed by the spring thaw** — a clock that runs alongside the drive's Progress and ends the drive when the water drops. The drive's Progress is the logs delivered to the boom; the thaw-clock is the water that carries them, and the water does not wait for the logs.

Track the thaw-clock as a simple countdown: the drive begins at the spring break-up with a number of thaw-points set by the season (typically `6` for a good thaw, `4` for a thin one, `3` for a dry year). Each stage of the drive consumes **one thaw-point** whether the stage succeeded or not — the water drops as the days pass, and the logs that did not make the boom when the water runs out stay in the river until next year, or rot on the bank. When the thaw-clock hits zero, **the drive is over**, and the Progress made is the cut delivered. A drive that pushed hard and ran stages in parallel might beat the clock; a drive that hit a jam and spent three stages breaking it might watch the water drop with half the cut still upstream.

The thaw-clock is the logging chapter's distinct pressure. The season's window is the one thing the logger cannot extend, and the drive is the race between the logs and the water that no amount of skill can win if the thaw is thin or the jam is bad.

#### The River Drive as Operation

The river drive — floating the winter's cut from the timber to the mill — is logging's signature operation, and it is the logging chapter's centerpiece the way the stampede is the cattle chapter's. The drive is a `Medium` operation (`6` Progress, `4` Trouble): the Progress is the logs delivered to the boom, and the Trouble is the river — the jam (the logs' pile-up, broken by the riverman with a pike and a prayer), the drown (the man who went in and did not come up), the high water and the low, the weather, and the mill's competitor who cut the boom and sent the cut downstream.

| Stage of the Drive | The Problem | Likely Ability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The break-up | Start the logs moving with the spring thaw | `LABOR`, `NATURE` (the read of the water) |
| The run | Ride the logs down the river, pike in hand | `OPERATE`, `LABOR`, `RESILIENCE` |
| The jam | Break the pile-up before it floods the banks | `LABOR`, `MAKIN'` (the dynamite, if the jam is bad), `MOVE` (the quick work on the rolling logs) |
| The boom | Catch the cut at the mill's pocket | `LABOR`, `OPERATE` |

A river drive is one of the West's most dangerous operations. Use the drowning rules and the crush of the sawlog when Trouble turns against the crew. A bad drive costs the camp the men the logger counted in the vignette. The drive is also the logging chapter's great set-piece, and a campaign in the timber country will find the drive is the season's climax the way the round-up is the cattle chapter's.

### The Fur Trade and the Trapline

The fur trade is the oldest of the West's trades, and by the 1870s it is the trade in decline — the beaver hat is going out, the buffalo robe is still in, and the mountain man's era is ending in the market hunter's and the reservation's. The trade is still a livelihood, however, for the trapper who runs his line in the high country and sells his take at the rendezvous or the post, and the trade is still the reason men go into the wild land and stay there.

#### The Trapline as Seasonal Enterprise

| The Trapline | What It Is |
| --- | --- |
| Key ability | `NATURE` (the read of the country and the fur-bearer) or `HAWKEYE` (the sign and the set) |
| Prerequisites | A line (the deep-wild-land chapter's country), the traps, the pack animals, and a post or a rendezvous to sell the take |
| Town aspect modifiers | `Natural Riches` (the fur-bearer's country) and `Law` (the line's protection from other trappers and the nation whose country it is) |
| The Season Trap Roll | `0` a Penalty (a poor take, a lost line, a trapped man), `1` steady (the wages of a hard season), `2+` a Bonus with `+1` to the Tens die per success beyond the first |

The trapping Bonus and Penalty table follows the cattle and mining shape — the good winter's take, the lost trapline (the rival, the nation, the weather), the spoiled pelt, the market's price, the company post's hand. The trapping Trouble is the wild land's, plus the trade's own: the trap that takes the man (the corebook's traps and the injury table), the nation whose country the line is in (the `Standing` and the encounter), and the fur-bearer's decline.

Run a trapline season in this order.

1. **Assess the line.** Read sign, weather, rival tracks, and the country the line crosses. On a `NATURE` or `HAWKEYE` success, learn where the fur is good, where a rival has crossed, or which camp is dangerous to enter.
2. **Invest in the line.** Buy traps, repair gear, secure pack animals, pay for permission or gifts where the line crosses another people's country.
3. **Operate the line.** Play three to five Shifts using the Trapline Menu below, or one representative Shift for a background season.
4. **Reckon the take.** Make the Season Trap Roll and apply the take, spoiled pelts, injuries, or faction trouble.
5. **Rendezvous.** Sell the take, hear the news, buy next season's goods, and settle `Standing`.
6. **Return, move, or quit.** Keep the same line, move camp to fresher ground, or sell out before the line is empty.

#### The Trapline Menu

The trapline runs on the Investment Cycle, but its operate beat is the **Trapline Menu** — the work the trapper chooses Shift by Shift on the line, the way the miner chooses a shift underground. Each Shift on the trapline, the trapper picks **one** action:

| The Trapline Action | The Pool | The Effect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Run the line** | `HAWKEYE`, `NATURE` | Collect the catch from the sets. Success steps the season's take up; failure means spoiled pelts (a set not visited in time) and lost fur. The productive option, but it keeps the trapper on the known line and out of new country. |
| **Move camp** | `NATURE`, `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` | Pull camp and find new ground. Success opens fresh sets and a richer take; failure means crossing into a nation's territory or a rival's line, with the `Standing` and encounter Trouble that follows. The risky, expansionist option — the trapline's version of prospecting. |
| **Rest and repair** | `MAKIN'`, `LABOR` | Mend the traps, dry the pelts, doctor the animals, rest the body. No take this Shift, but the gear and the health hold; a trapper who never rests loses gear and condition to the wild land's attrition. The maintenance option. |

The Trapline Menu is the fur trade's distinct loop, and it is the thing that makes a trapline feel like a trapline and not a mine or a ranch. Every Shift is a choice: take the catch on the known line, or move for new ground and risk the nation; rest and repair, or push the gear and the body for one more day's take. A trapper who always runs the line depletes it; a trapper who always moves camp runs out of country and into trouble; the trapper who balances the three is the trapper who lasts, and the balancing is the mountain man's craft.

#### The Rendezvous

The rendezvous is the fur trade's crossroads — the annual gathering of the trappers, the traders, the nations, and the company at a fixed place and time, where the winter's take is traded for the next season's supplies and the year's news and gossip are exchanged. The rendezvous is a **scene** (the saloon chapter's Trade-Night, scaled up to the wild land and turned annual): a multi-faction trade fair where the trapper's take meets the company's goods, the nations meet the traders, and the `Standing` shifts of a season are settled in trade, drink, and the occasional fight.

At the table, the rendezvous is the fur trade's **reckon and reinvest** beat compressed into a single scene. The trapper sells the season's take (the Season Trap Roll's yield, realized as cash or trade goods), buys next season's supplies, and shifts `Standing` with the wild land's factions — the company post, the rival trappers, and the nations whose country the line runs through. A successful rendezvous (`PRESENCE`, `INSIGHT`, or `PERFORMIN'` by the trade's nature) shifts `Standing` favorably and supplies the next season; a failed rendezvous leaves the trapper oversupplied or underpaid, and the `Standing` shifts the other way. The GM may use the rendezvous as the campaign's annual anchor the way the families chapter uses the rites — the fixed point the wild land's year turns on.

### The Market Hunt

The market hunt is the trade that took the West's game at industrial scale — the buffalo-hide hunter who took fifty beeves a day for the robe, the market hunter who took the antelope and the elk for the railhead's table, the wolfer who took the wolf for the bounty and left the strychnine-baited carcass for whatever else found it. The market hunt is the trade the 1870s supplement renders in the most detail, and the trade that should be allowed to empty the country it works.

#### The Market Hunt as Take-Based Trade

The market hunt is not a seasonal enterprise in the cattle and mining shape; it is a **take-based trade** whose payoff is the take multiplied by the market price, and whose Trouble is the country's decline. The market hunt's distinct resource is the **take die**, and its distinct mechanic is the **Decline** — the cumulative cost the trade's success exacts on the country it works, tracked across seasons and regions.

| The Take | The Price (the trade chapter's spatial economy applies) | The Trouble |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The buffalo robe | `$1-3` at the rail, more finished | The herd's decline; the nation's anger (the encounter, the `Standing`); the weather; the skinning crew's labor |
| The market meat (antelope, elk, deer) | `5-10¢` a pound at the railhead | The game's decline; the spoilage; the railhead's demand |
| The wolf scalp (the bounty) | `$1-5` a scalp, by the county's bounty | The strychnine's collateral (the dead dog, the dead eagle, the dead man's stock); the wolfer's reputation |
| The hide and the fur (the smaller trade) | the fur trade's prices, above | The trapline's Trouble |

Track the season's take as a **take die** — the stepped die (`D6` → `D12`), representing the volume of the season's kill. A season of market hunting is resolved as an operation (the deep-wild-land chapter's expedition frame): the Progress is the take (the die's final size), the Trouble is the country's resistance. A market hunt that succeeds wildly steps the take die up (`D12` — a hundred robes, a thousand pounds of meat); a market hunt that struggles steps it down (`D6` — a thin season, a poor stand). The take die times the market price is the season's cash, and the market hunter's living is made or broken on the die's size and the railhead's rate.

#### The Decline

But the take die's size is also the country's loss, and the market hunt's distinct rule — the **Decline** — makes that loss playable rather than merely narrated. Track each region's game as a **game die** per region, shared by all the hunters working the region: `D6` (depleted) → `D8` → `D10` → `D12` (abundant).

| The Game Die | The Region's Game |
| --- | --- |
| `D6` | Depleted. The herds are thin or gone; the encounters are rare; the nation's anger is at its height. |
| `D8` | Worked. The game is still there but pressured; a few more seasons of heavy hunting will deplete it. |
| `D10` | Plentiful. The region supports the hunt and the nation both. |
| `D12` | Abundant. The game is thick — the country before the market hunt, the plains before the robe trade. |

Each season of market hunting in a region tests **Decline** by the hunters' take die. The larger the take, the harder the country is hit.

| The Take Die | Decline Result |
| --- | --- |
| `D6` | Thin take. The game die holds unless this is the second consecutive hunted season; if it is, step the game die down once. |
| `D8` | Working take. Roll the take die; on `1-3`, step the game die down once. |
| `D10` | Heavy take. Step the game die down once. |
| `D12` | Slaughter take. Step the game die down once, and shift the relevant nations' `Standing` one step toward hostile even if the game die remains above `D6`. |

The game die also sets the region's game entries on the encounter table (Ch.21): at `D12`, the buffalo-herd encounters are common; at `D6`, they are rare or gone, and the plains nations whose economy and subsistence the herds were shift toward hostility, for the obvious and historical reason. Each step the game die drops shifts the `Standing` of the plains nations (the Lakota, the Cheyenne, the Comanche) one step toward the hostile.

The Decline is the market hunt's honest accounting, and it is the book's strongest rule for the buffalo's end. The hunter's prosperity is the country's impoverishment, tracked season by season, until the game die hits `D6` and the herds are gone and the nation's `Standing` is at war — which is the history the robe trade made. A campaign that uses this rule will find the market hunter is the book's most consequential trade, not because of the cash it pays, but because of the country it leaves behind.

#### Example: The Jam at the Narrows

The logging camp begins spring with a rich `D10` stand and a thin thaw-clock of `4`. The river drive needs `6` Progress before the water drops. The crew invested in **River Men**, gaining `+1 die` on jam and boom stages.

The first stage succeeds and moves a good cut downstream, but it spends one thaw-point. The second stage hits a jam at the narrows. The riverman's `LABOR` roll succeeds, breaking the jam, but the roll also adds Trouble: a man goes into the water and comes out coughing river. The crew spends another thaw-point getting him out and resetting the drive. Now the thaw-clock is `2`, Progress is only halfway home, and the choice is sharp: push hard and risk another drowning, or accept that part of the cut will stay upriver until next year.

The logging season turns on that clock. The logs do not wait for the crew to be ready; the river does not wait for the logs.

### The Wild Trades and the Other Rules

The wild trades are the extractive industries of the wild land, and they tie the book's wilderness and economy threads together. Each runs on the season order — assess, invest, operate, reckon, reinvest — but each has a distinct resource or clock that makes it feel different from the settled-country trades.

- **The deep-wild-land chapter (Ch.20)** is the country the trades work in — the modes of travel, the stages, the Trouble the wild land imposes.
- **The regional-encounter chapter (Ch.21)** is the country's life the trades take from — the game entries the market hunt thins and the fur trade traps. The Decline track (above) is the bridge: the game die sets the encounter table's entries, and the market hunt steps it down.
- **The cattle, mining, and trade chapters** are the trades' settled-country cousins — the ranch, the mine, the freight outfit are the enterprises these trades are the wild versions of, and the Investment Cycle is shared across all of them.
- **The faction chapter** (Ch.13) holds the company, the nation, and the rival trapper as the trade's factions; the robber baron chapter (Ch.24) holds the company and the market's distant hand. The Decline's `Standing` shift on the plains nations is where the market hunt's cost becomes the campaign's war.
- **The town tracker** feels the cut and the take. A strong logging season may add `1` `Natural Riches` or `Mercantile` tally point; a lumber shortage or river disaster may remove `1` `Civic` or `Welfare` point. Fur trapping that feeds trade may add `1` `Mercantile`; market hunting that steps the game die down may remove `1` `Natural Riches` or `Welfare` if the town depends on the herds. Public investment may make `Timber Mill`, `Fur Trapping`, `Foragers`, `Animal Culls`, `Workers' Cooperatives`, or `Maintained Tracks` available at `3` SP.

The wild land's trade takes what grows and turns it into what sells. The log has its drive, the fur its trapline, and the robe its hunt; together they give the campaign its extractive edge, the work that lived where the counties had not reached and emptied the country as surely as the rail and the plow did. The men who did this work lived where the work was. Let the work pay what it paid and cost what it cost, in the country that did not care about the trade except as another thing the market could take.
