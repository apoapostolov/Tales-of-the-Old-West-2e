## Chapter 18 - The Press and the Election

> _THE NEWSPAPER AND THE BALLOT ARE THE COUNTY'S TWO PEACEFUL WEAPONS, AND THE MEN WHO WIELD THEM ARE THE MEN WHO CHANGE THE COUNTY WITHOUT FIRING A SHOT. THE EDITOR CAN RUIN A NAME THE ROPE CANNOT REACH, AND THE POLITICIAN CAN RUIN A FACTION THE GUN CANNOT. THE WEST WAS BUILT BY THE BULLET AND THE ROPE, BUT IT WAS GOVERNED BY THE PRESS AND THE POLL._
>
> — JUDGE HARLAN VOSS, TO A YOUNG LAWYER WHO ASKED WHY THE BENCH READ THE PAPER

### The County's Public Voice

The newspaper is the county's memory and its weapon. A story printed in the county seat's paper is read by every literate man in the county within a week and repeated to every illiterate one within two, and the story — true, half-true, or a bald lie paid for by a faction — moves `Fame`, `Reputation`, and `Standing` in a way the saloon's gossip cannot match. The press reaches farther than talk, it lasts longer, and it carries the editor's opinion disguised as the county's news. The election is the press's harvest — the vote that the editor's campaign and the faction's money shaped, the office that the paper and the poll delivered, the county's peaceful revolution once a cycle.

Give the press weight when a printed story can move `Fame`, `Reputation`, or `Standing` farther than gossip can. Give the election weight when the office changes who controls the road, the jail, the court, the tax, or the deputy's badge. The press is a faction's instrument; the election is either a quick operation or a full campaign whose currency is votes and whose tools are ink, money, speech, pressure, and fraud.

> _The editor set the type for the morning edition with his own hands, because the boy who usually set it was drunk and the story could not wait for the boy's sobriety. The story was the Davidson candidate for the commission, and the way the editor was setting the type the candidate was a thief and a liar and a man whose election would be the county's ruin, and the editor was setting the type this way because the Sandoval faction paid the paper's subscription list and the Davidson faction did not._
>
> "You're going to print that."
>
> "I'm going to print what the county will read, and the county will read what I set." The editor did not look up from the type. "The Davidson can answer in next week's edition, if he buys the column. The press is fair, counsel. It is fair to whoever pays for the ink."
>
> _Voss set his hat on the counter and did not argue, because the editor was right and the editor knew he was right and the paper was the county's memory and the county's memory was for sale, like everything else in the county, to the faction that paid for it. That was the press's work, and the election in November was the press's harvest, and the harvest would go to whoever's ink the county had been reading all year._

### The Press as Faction Instrument

The newspaper is a faction's loudspeaker, and the editor is the man who points it. A press is a thing the faction owns (outright, by subscription, or by the editor's allegiance), and a story in the press is a `Fame`, `Reputation`, or `Standing` move that reaches the whole county at once. The corebook's `Fame` and `Reputation` rules track the face the county sees; the press shapes that face, for a price.

#### Owning the Press

| The Press's Allegiance | What It Means |
| --- | --- |
| Owned (the faction bought the press) | The press prints what the faction says; the editor is the faction's hireling |
| Subscription (the faction's money keeps the press alive) | The press prints what the faction favors, with room for the editor's conscience, which the faction tests |
| Allied (the editor's opinion aligns with the faction) | The press prints the faction's line by conviction, not contract; harder to shift, and more credible for it |
| Independent (the editor's own opinion) | The press prints what the editor believes, which may favor or oppose any faction by the week |
| Opposition (the editor's enmity) | The press prints what the faction's enemies want to hear, and the faction cannot buy it without a siege |

A press is a faction asset (the factions chapter), tracked on the `-3` to `+3` ladder the way a settlement's `Refuge` is tracked. A press at `+2` or better is the faction's voice; a press at `-2` or worse is the faction's enemy, and the faction must either buy it, suppress it, or found a rival paper to shout it down.

#### Going to Press

A story printed in the press is a **single check** (not an operation) that moves `Fame`, `Reputation`, or `Standing` at county scale. The editor (or the party member acting as editor) rolls `PERFORMIN'` (the prose) or `BOOKLEARNIN'` (the facts and the law), with the press's allegiance as the modifier.

| The Story | The Effect on Success |
| --- | --- |
| The puff piece | `+1` `Fame` or `Reputation` to the subject; the county reads the subject's name in a good light |
| The smear | `-1` `Fame` or `Reputation` to the target, and a `Standing` drop with the readers who believe it |
| The revelation (a true fact, exposed) | `-1` to `-2` `Reputation` to the target; the beginning of a scandal, a trial, or a feud |
| The suppression (a story killed) | No effect — the story does not run, which is the press's quietest and most powerful tool |
| The endorsement (the press picks a side) | `+1` to the endorsed faction's `Standing` in the county; the press's weight thrown |

A story that fails (the editor rolls `0` successes) is a story the county disbelieves, which is a `Reputation` hit to the press and a `Standing` drop with the faction that paid for it. A story that succeeds wildly (`3+`) is a story the county repeats for months, which is the press's power at its height — the editor who broke the scandal, the editor who made the candidate, the editor who broke the faction.

#### The Press Campaign

A single story moves `Fame` or `Standing` by a step. But the press's true power is not the single story — it is the **campaign**, the sustained shifting of the county's regard over weeks and months, edition by edition, until the subject's name is what the paper made it. A press campaign uses the factions chapter's Influence rules at county scale, shifting a target's county-wide Influence over time rather than in a single check.

Each edition the press prints about a subject shifts that subject's **county Influence** — a tracked pool (cap `5`, like the faction Influence pool, but measuring the _county's_ aggregate regard rather than one faction's). A puff campaign (three or four favorable editions across a season) steps the subject's county Influence up; a smear campaign steps it down. At the end of the season, read the pool into the subject's `Standing` with every faction that cares about county opinion: a high pool helps with churchmen, merchants, reformers, and ordinary voters; a low pool gives rivals permission to distance themselves. The press's reach is scale. It changes what the county thinks all at once, which is why factions fight to own it.

| The Press Campaign | The Effect on the Target's County Influence |
| --- | --- |
| **The puff campaign** (3+ favorable editions in a season) | `+1` county Influence per edition that succeeds; the subject's name is read well. |
| **The smear campaign** (3+ unfavorable editions) | `-1` county Influence per edition that succeeds; the subject's name is read badly. |
| **The revelation** (a true scandal, exposed and repeated) | `-2` county Influence, and the beginning of a cascade (the factions propagate the drop; the justice chapter may open a case). |
| **The suppression campaign** (killing the subject's story, edition after edition) | No shift — but the subject's own attempts to build Influence through the press are blocked, which is the suppression's quiet power. |

The press campaign makes the paper a thing the factions fight to own, not a single roll that solves the county's opinion. A faction that owns the press and runs a season of favorable editions builds its county Influence methodically; a faction that owns the press and runs a smear against a rival tears the rival's Influence down. The press's power is its _reach_ — it shifts Influence at scale — and its risk is the libel suit, the suppression raid, the rival paper, and the county's eventual realization that it has been reading the paper's opinion dressed as the county's news.

#### Suppression and the Counter-Press

A story can be suppressed — killed before it runs, by the faction that would be harmed. A suppression is an operation (the outlaw chapter's frame) whose Progress is the story's death and whose Trouble is the editor's conscience, the rival press, and the leak. The robber baron chapter's "suppression job" is this operation, and a faction that cannot suppress a story must counter it — with a rival story, a rival paper, or the simpler tools of the county's other peaceful weapon, the election.

### The Election

An election — for sheriff, county commissioner, mayor, territorial delegate, judge — is the county's peaceful revolution. Run it in one of two ways. Use the **quick election** when the office matters but the campaign is background: it is a standard operation with Progress and Trouble. Use the **full election campaign** when the campaign itself is the story: it uses the electorate pool below, scene by scene, until election day.

#### The Quick Election

| Election Scale | Progress Needed | Trouble Limit | The Office |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `The Local Office` | `4` | `3` | Sheriff, justice of the peace, alderman — the county's face |
| `The County Office` | `6` | `4` | County commissioner, mayor, probate judge — the county's power |
| `The Territorial Office` | `8` | `5` | Territorial delegate, territorial council — the territory's reach |

The Progress is the party's vote share. The Trouble is the opposition's work and the election's friction — the rival's campaign, the smear, the bought vote, the challenged ballot, the faction that turned, the fraud that was caught or the fraud that was not. When Progress fills before Trouble hits the limit, the party wins the office. If Trouble hits the limit first, the rival wins, or the count goes to a contested election if the table wants the fight to continue in court and print.

#### The Electorate Pool — the Election as a Cumulative Contest

When an election deserves more than one operation roll, use the **electorate pool**. Debate, rally, back-room bargain, press exchange, canvass, and fraud attempt each shift a tracked pool of votes. The count climbs and falls across scenes, so momentum becomes something the players can win, squander, or steal.

Set the electorate pool by the office's scale: `20` votes for a local office, `50` for a county office, `100+` for a territorial office. The pool starts split — half leaning to the party, half to the rival, with a margin of undecided the campaign fights over. Each campaign scene (the debate, the stump, the press edition, the canvass) is a social conflict — a phased Quarrel (the justice chapter's frame, Ch.11) or a single `PERFORMIN'`/`PRESENCE` roll — whose winner shifts the electorate pool by the scene's weight. The campaign's tactics (the canvass, the money, the machine, the fraud, below) are the leverage that feeds the scenes, the way evidence feeds a trial.

| The Campaign Scene | The Pool Shift (winner) |
| --- | --- |
| **The debate** (a phased Quarrel, both candidates) | `±3` to `5` votes — the debate is the campaign's set-piece, and a strong showing swings the undecided. |
| **The rally / the stump speech** (a `PERFORMIN'` check) | `±1` to `3` votes — the candidate's reach, by the crowd's size and the speech's landing. |
| **The press endorsement / the smear** (the press chapter's move) | `±2` to `4` votes — the press's reach applied to the electorate; a smear campaign across the season swings the pool cumulatively. |
| **The canvass** (the door-to-door, `PRESENCE`) | `±1` vote per session of canvassing — the slow, honest work of turnout. |
| **The machine** (the faction's turnout operation) | `±2` to `5` votes — the ward heelers and the party organization, by the faction's `Standing`. |
| **The fraud** (the stuffed box, the bought count) | `±5` to `10` votes — the high-risk leverage. A fraud that succeeds swings the pool sharply; a fraud that is caught is a scandal (the press) and a crime (the justice chapter), and the pool swings back twice as hard. |

The election resolves on the electorate pool's **final count** on election day. The party with the higher count takes the office; a count within `5` votes for a local or county office, or within `10` for a territorial office, is a **contested election** — a paper war (the inheritance chapter) fought in the courthouse and the press. This shape gives the election momentum: a ground game that turns out votes, a press that shifts opinion, and a fraud that risks everything for the sharp swing.

#### The Campaign

Each stage of the campaign is a check, tagged Safe / Risky / Desperate by the tactic and the county's temper.

| Stage of the Campaign | The Tactic | Likely Ability |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The canvass | The door-to-door, the handshake, the promise | `PRESENCE`, `PERFORMIN'` |
| The speech and the debate | The stump, the hall, the county's ear | `PERFORMIN'`, `PRESENCE` |
| The press campaign | The endorsement, the puff, the smear (above) | `BOOKLEARNIN'`, `PERFORMIN'` |
| The money | The donation, the subscription, the bribe | `PRESENCE` (the ask), the faction's Capital |
| The machine | The party's organization, the ward heelers, the turnout operation | `INSIGHT`, the faction's `Standing` |
| The fraud | The stuffed ballot, the challenged voter, the bought count | `LIGHT-FINGERED`, `PRESENCE`, the sheriff's `Standing` |

A clean campaign runs on the canvass, the speech, and the press, and the county votes as the county was persuaded. A dirty campaign adds the money, the machine, and the fraud, and the county votes as the faction could buy or steal. Let both work, and let both cost: a clean campaign lost is a clean campaign the county did not buy; a dirty campaign won is a dirty campaign in which the faction owns the office and the county's suspicion forever after.

#### Electoral Tactics Table

| D6 | The Tactic | The Effect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `1` | **The canvass** | `PRESENCE` — Progress by the honest vote |
| `2` | **The stump speech** | `PERFORMIN'` — Progress and `Fame`, with the risk of a gaffe (Trouble) |
| `3` | **The press endorsement** | The press chapter's move — Progress and `Standing`, by the press's reach |
| `4` | **The donation / the bribe** | Capital for Progress — the faction's money, the voter's price |
| `5` | **The machine** | The faction's turnout — Progress by the ward and the heelers |
| `6` | **The fraud** | `LIGHT-FINGERED` — Progress by the stuffed box, with the Trouble of the marshal's eye |

A fraud that is caught is Trouble and a scandal (the press chapter's move) and possibly a crime (the justice chapter). A fraud that succeeds is Progress or votes the county cannot prove, which is the hard truth of many elections the West actually ran.

#### The Count and the Contest

If using the quick election, Progress filled means the office goes to the party, the faction's `Standing` rises in the county, and the county's peaceful revolution is complete. If Progress falls short or Trouble hits the limit, the office goes to the rival unless the result is close enough to contest. If using the electorate pool, compare the final count on election day. A close election becomes a **contested election**, and the contest is a paper war (the inheritance chapter) fought in the courthouse and the press: the challenged ballots, the recounted boxes, the territorial commission's ruling. A contested election means the county did not decide cleanly, and the lawyers and the editors will.

#### Example: Five Votes in November

The party backs a reform sheriff in a county election. The GM uses the full electorate pool: `50` votes, with `22` leaning reform, `23` leaning Ring, and `5` undecided. The reformers win a debate by `3` votes and spend a press endorsement for `+2`, moving their count to `27`. The Ring answers with the machine, shifting `4` votes back.

On election week, the Ring tries fraud for `+5` votes. The roll fails. The fraud is caught, becoming a press scandal and a justice problem, and the pool swings back twice as hard. The reformers win by `6`, outside the contested range. They take the office, but the Ring's `Standing` drops to hostility and the next season's faction roll will not be quiet.

An election is not one speech. It is momentum, turnout, press, money, and risk, counted until the day the county opens the boxes.

### The Press, the Election, and the Other Rules

The press and the election are the county's peaceful weapons, and they tie the book's faction and political threads together.

- **The faction chapter** holds the press as a faction asset and the election as a faction contest; this chapter gives both their procedures.
- **The robber baron chapter** holds the baron as the press's owner and the election's funder; this chapter gives the baron's media and political work table weight.
- **The justice chapter** holds the criminal trial; this chapter holds the civil election and the contested count, the county's other courtroom.
- **The families chapter** holds the family's political trait ("political," "commands"); this chapter is where that trait is spent.
- **The political-scene chapter (the next chapter)** holds the national party layer above the factions; this chapter holds the county election that the party's politics descend into.

The newspaper and the ballot are the county's two peaceful weapons, and a chapter that gives the press a reach and the election a vote gives the campaign its civil layer — the war that changed the county without firing a shot, fought in type and at the ballot box, and won by the men who could pay for the ink and turn out the vote. The West was built by the bullet and the rope, but it was governed by the press and the poll, and both should matter at the table.
