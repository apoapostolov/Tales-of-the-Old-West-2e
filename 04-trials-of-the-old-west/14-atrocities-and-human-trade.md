## Chapter 14 - Atrocities and Human Trade

> _THE WEST WAS BUILT ON LAND TAKEN AND PEOPLE SOLD AND PEOPLE KILLED WHO WERE IN THE WAY. A RULEBOOK THAT HIDES THAT IS LYING ABOUT THE COUNTRY. A RULEBOOK THAT CELEBRATES IT IS WORSE THAN A LIE, AND A RULEBOOK THAT MAKES A GAME OF IT WITHOUT ITS WEIGHT IS THE WORST THING OF ALL._
>
> — A NOTE FROM THE AUTHOR, AT THE FRONT OF THE CHAPTER

### A Word Before the Rules

This is the hardest chapter in the book, and it is the one most in need of being done right or not at all. The West ran on atrocities. The Native nations were removed, massacred, and confined by a campaign that lasted the whole of the century and beyond. The trade in captive Native women and children was a border economy with a price list and a market. Chattel slavery survived the Thirteenth Amendment as debt peonage in New Mexico, as convict leasing in the railroads and the mines, and as the practical re-enslavement of Black life across the former Confederacy and into the territories. The sex slavery of the Chinese trade moved women across the Pacific and through the railheads in a ledger as cold as any plantation's. The Texas Rangers and the vigilance committees practiced a racial terror that was, at times, less law enforcement than ethnic cleansing under a badge.

A game that refuses to engage with any of this is lying about the country the game is set in. A game that engages with it carelessly, or for spectacle, is making pornography of other people's suffering. This chapter exists because the engine should be able to model the country honestly, including its worst, for tables that want to grapple with it — the way the outlaw chapter models crime, the way the justice chapter models the noose, the way the sickness chapter models the cholera that emptied the wagon trains. The engine does its accounting without celebration and without flinching.

If a table does not want this material at the table, the chapter is optional, and the rest of the book does not depend on it. If a table does want it, read the chapter whole before you use it, agree at the table what is and is not in play, and hold the material to the standard the writing skill's `dark-frontier.md` and `uncomfortable-truths.md` set. The rules that follow are built so that the engine makes the weight of these acts tell on the characters who do them, the characters who resist them, and the characters who profit from them, in the engine's own currency: `Standing`, `Wanted`, `Fame`, `Reputation`, `Cohesion`, and the corebook's `Doubts` and `Vexes`. There is no way to use this chapter that leaves the player characters clean, and that is the point.

> _Carver sat by the fire with the order in his hand and did not read it again because he had read it twice and the words had not changed between the readings. The order was to move the people off the creek and burn the wickiups and hold the ground until the surveyors came, and the people on the creek were a band of thirty-seven who had been there longer than the territory and longer than the treaty that said they were not._
>

He sat by the fire until it burned down, and in the morning he gave the order to move, and the people on the creek moved, and the wickiups burned, and the sergeant carried it, the way the army carried everything it was told to carry, which is in the body and not on the page._

### The Atrocity as Operation

A massacre, a raid on a homestead, a punitive expedition against a camp, the clearance of a people off a creek — these are operations, run on the outlaw chapter's framework, and the engine treats them as it treats any score: a Progress (the atrocity accomplished) and a Trouble (the cost, human and otherwise). The engine does not reward this work the way it rewards a clean score. The engine makes the weight of it tell on the characters who do it, in their own damage tracks, their `Standing`, and the county's memory.

| Atrocity Scale | Progress Needed | Trouble Limit | The Act |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `A Raid` | `4` | `3` | A single homestead, a camp, a family; the work of a night |
| `A Clearance` | `6` | `4` | A settlement, a band, a community; the work of days |
| `A Campaign` | `8` | `5` | A people, a region, a season; the work the army or the militia was built for |

The Progress is the atrocity accomplished — the people moved, the camp burned, the captives taken, the dead counted. The Trouble is what the act costs the people who do it, which the engine refuses to let be only the act's material cost.

#### The Moral Weight on the Doer

The engine's damage tracks are the chapter's moral instrument. A character who participates in an atrocity takes `Doubts` and `Vexes` as the Trouble climbs, regardless of the character's will or the order that sent them. This is not a choice the player makes; it is the cost the body and the mind pay for the work, the way the body pays for the powder smoke and the mind pays for the face of the dead.

| Trouble Step | What the Doer Takes |
| --- | --- |
| `1` | `1` `Doubts`. The act is in the character's head and will not leave. |
| `2` | `1` `Doubts` and `1` `Vexes`. The character is short with his company, drinks later, does not sleep. |
| `3` | `1` `Doubts` and `1` `Vexes`, and the character's `Reputation` begins to carry the act — the county talks. |
| `4+` | `1` `Doubts` and `1` `Vexes`, a permanent `Reputation` shift toward the violent and the feared, and a `Cohesion` test for any unit or band that did the work together. |

A character pushed to `Broken` on `Docity` by these `Doubts` is a character the act has broken, and the engine should let that play — the drink, the desertion, the sermon, the suicide, the long quiet life of a man who cannot forget a face. The chapter does not offer absolution. It offers the engine's honest accounting, which is that the men who did these things in the actual West carried them, and many of them were broken by the carrying, and the broken ones were not less real than the unrepentant ones.

A character who refuses the order — who will not move the people, will not fire the camp, will not take the captives — makes an enemy of the faction that gave the order and risks `Wanted`, court-martial, or the rope. The engine makes the refusal costly too, because it was, in the actual West, costly and rare, and a rule that made it easy would be lying about the country. A refusal is a `Standing` drop with the ordering faction (often to `Hunted`), a `Fame` shift toward the principled (which cuts both ways), and the beginning of a different campaign — the campaign of the man who said no, and what the country did to him for it.

### The Trade in People

The trade in people is the chapter's second engine, and it is run as an economy with a buyer, a seller, a price, and a faction behind it. The chapter models the trade's existence and its weight; it does not provide a price list for human beings that a table could use without its stomach turning, and the GM who runs the trade should name the prices only when the fiction demands it, and should let the naming of them be the horror it is.

The trades of the period, each a faction or an outlaw operation:

- **Captive-taking (the border trade).** The Comanche and the border economies that traded in captives taken from both sides of the line — the Comanche taking Anglo, Mexican, and Tejano; the Texans and the rurales taking Comanche and Kiowa. The trade was old, organized, and human in both directions, and the chapter renders it as the faction and the economy it was, through the writing skill's `borderlands.md`. A captive trade is an outlaw operation (the raid, the taking, the sale) with the captive's fate as the Trouble that does not wash off the taker, the buyer, or the faction behind them.
- **Debt peonage.** The Nuevomexicano grant system's survival of the Thirteenth Amendment, in which a worker's debt to the patron was heritable, enforceable, and indistinguishable from slavery for the peon held by it. The Santa Fe Ring and the local dons ran it; the courts upheld it; the engine treats it as a faction (the patron) with a workforce that is not free, and the party's choice to use peon labor, to oppose it, or to free a peon as a `Standing` move with the patron and the law.
- **Convict leasing.** The system by which the Black freedman was returned to bondage by the law — arrested on a trumped charge, fined beyond his means, leased to the railroad, the mine, or the plantation at a profit to the state and a cost to the convict that exceeded the slave's cost, because the convict was cheaper to work to death than the slave had been. The engine treats the convict lease as a faction's resource and the party's encounter with it (a party that breaks a convict out of a lease camp has made a Federal enemy) as the engine's hard choice.
- **The Chinese trade.** The movement of women across the Pacific and through the railheads for the brothels of the West, run by the tongs, underwritten by the exclusion that made the women worth more for their scarcity. The engine treats the trade as the saloon chapter's hardest edge — the rooms that are not voluntary, the women who are not free to leave, the keeper who may or may not know, and the party's choice to disrupt the trade at the cost of the tong's enmity and the law's indifference.
- **The re-enslavement and the terror.** The Black codes, the Klan, the lynching (the justice chapter's hardest result), and the practical unfreedom of Black life in the former Confederacy and the territories. The engine treats the terror as a faction (the Klan, the Committee, the Rangers) operating outside or beside the law, and the party's relation to it as the chapter's central moral test — to oppose, to endure, to flee, or to be complicit.

### The Army and the Ranger at Their Worst

The massacre is the order the sergeant is given and the order he may or may not refuse. The Indian Wars, the border wars, and the labor wars are the contexts in which the order comes, and the engine's mass combat and faction chapters are the tools for running them. This chapter is the place where the honest work of naming what those orders were gets done — the Washita, the Marias, Camp Grant, Sand Creek — and the GM who uses this chapter should name them, and let the names carry the weight the names carry, rather than abstracting them into a generic "clearance."

The Texas Rangers are rendered as the paramilitary instrument they were — a state-sanctioned force with a documented record of ethnic cleansing along the Rio Grande, the massacre of Mexican and Mexican-American communities, and the terrorizing of the border population under the authority of the State of Texas. The chapter does not soften this. A campaign with the Rangers in play has them as a faction whose methods are atrocity, and the party's relation to them as the engine's hard choice. The writing skill's `borderlands.md` and `uncomfortable-truths.md` govern every scene in which they appear.

The vigilance committee is the atrocity the county does to itself — the lynching (the justice chapter), the racial pogrom, the expulsion of the Chinese community, the terror visited on the Black family that prospered too visibly. The Committee is a faction (the factions chapter) whose purpose is the county's "purity" and whose means are the rope and the torch, and the engine should let it be what it was: the violence the law could not or would not reach, done by the county's respectable men in the dark, and remembered by the county's survivors in the light.

### The Cast in the Hard Country

This chapter puts the cast at its hardest tests. Carver's vignette is the engine's central scene for this material — the sergeant with the order, the first sergeant's question, and the carrying of the act in the body rather than on the page. His chapter is the door in for the army's atrocities, and his exhaustion, his bitterness, and his letters to Leavenworth are the weight the engine puts on the man who gave the order to move. Riddle's outlaw past is the door in for the marauder's atrocities — the raid, the taking, the work he walked away from and cannot walk away from in his own head. Sandoval's Nuevomexicano ground is the door in for the debt peon and the grant theft; her grandfather's spring is the water the Ring's schemes would take, and the people her family lost to the grant's operators are the chapter's peonage. The Native nations — the Apache, the Lakota, the Diné, the Pueblo — are rendered through the writing skill's `native-material-culture.md` as the peoples the country was taken from, and their captivity, their removal, and their resistance are the chapter's first subjects, not its backdrop.

### A Closing Note on the Use of This Chapter

This chapter is offered to tables that want to grapple with the West as it was, not as the dime novels painted it. It is built to be honest and to be heavy, and to make the engine's currency — the damage tracks, the `Standing`, the `Wanted`, the `Reputation` — do the work of moral accounting that the actual West did not do for itself. If a table uses it, use it with the care the material demands, and let the consequences land on the characters who earned them. If a table does not use it, the book is complete without it, and the other chapters will give the table a West that is hard and true and playable.

The country was built on these things. A rulebook that can say so, and make the saying matter at the table, is a rulebook that respects the country and the people who lived in it more than one that looks away. That is the chapter's only justification, and the standard it must meet.
