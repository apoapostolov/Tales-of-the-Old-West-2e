# Chapter 19 — Army Life

> _"THE HORSES CAME FIRST. THE HORSES CAME FIRST EVERY MORNING OF THE WORLD."_

The frontier army of the 1870s held twenty-five thousand men scattered across hundreds of posts. The men were few. The pay was thin. They fought in small units against adversaries who moved. The cavalry charge was not their work. Their work was garrison duty, escort, scouting, campaign that might end in battle or not. This chapter covers the post and the field column: layout, routine, pay, discharge, ration, cavalry on campaign, Buffalo Soldier regiments, the officer's wife, discipline, the army's voice, campaign's failure.

---

## 1. The Frontier Post

### Layout

The standard frontier post was an open square — the parade ground — surrounded by buildings.

- **Officers' row** ran along one side of the square: wood-frame or adobe houses for the officers and their families, the post commander's house the largest. Officers' row held the post's social center.
- **Enlisted barracks** stood opposite officers' row — long single-story buildings (wood, adobe, or stone), each holding a company (about fifty men in the 1870s), a single open bay with bunks along both walls, small attached kitchen and mess, the first sergeant's room at one end.
- **Headquarters** or the adjutant's office was a small building, often near the flagpole at the parade ground's center. The **quartermaster** and **commissary storehouses** held the post's supplies, guarded.
- **Stables** stood at cavalry posts — long open barns, with the saddler's shop and farrier's shop nearby. The **hospital** was small (one or two wards), staffed by a contract surgeon. The **guardhouse** held the post's jail. The **sutler's store** or post trader's was a civilian business licensed to operate at the post, selling goods (tobacco, canned food, whisky, clothing) to officers and enlisted at posted prices — the post's only commercial space.
- **Laundresses' quarters** (Soapsuds Row) were often a separate row of shacks where the post's laundresses, frequently soldiers' wives, lived and worked.

Most posts had no walls. The stockade fort of film was largely gone by the 1870s; the open plan held the standard. Some exceptions — Fort Apache, some older posts — had walls.

### Flagpole and hierarchy

The flagpole stood at the center of the parade ground, the garrison's formal heart. Reveille and retreat sounded at the flagpole by the bugler. The flag went up at reveille and down at retreat, the garrison at attention.

The post commander was a senior officer (lieutenant colonel or colonel), responsible for all things. The adjutant was a junior officer handling administration — the post's day-to-day manager. The quartermaster held charge of supply. The surgeon was a contract or regular medical officer. Officers commanded companies — captains, with lieutenants under them; the company was the basic unit, and a post held two to six companies. The first sergeants were the senior enlisted of each company — the practical authority over the enlisted men. The first sergeant ran the company. Other NCOs (sergeants, corporals) and the privates filled out the rest.

The relationship between the company commander and the first sergeant is one of the great relationships in army work. The captain holds the formal authority; the first sergeant holds the practical authority. A good pair worked together; a bad pair tore the company apart.

## 2. The Daily Routine

The army day was made by bugle calls. The bugler was a figure who mattered; he announced every shift of the day to the entire post.

- **Reveille,** about sunrise: bugle call, formation in front of barracks, roll call.
- **Stable call** (cavalry): men go to the stables to feed, water, groom, and clean stalls — cavalry was forty percent horse-care.
- **Mess call** (breakfast): coffee, hardtack or bread, salt pork or beef, occasional eggs.
- **Sick call:** men reporting illness present at the hospital.
- **Fatigue call:** work details — wood-cutting, hay-cutting, repairing buildings, carrying supplies, digging latrines. The soldier's daily work was manual labor, not drill.
- **Drill** when held: formation drill, weapons drill, mounted drill (cavalry); frequency varied — some posts drilled daily, some rarely.
- **Mess call** (dinner, midday): the main meal — beef stew, beans, bread, coffee; vegetables were rare.
- **Fatigue continues.** **Stable call:** care of horses in the evening.
- **Mess call** (supper): light meal, often leftovers.
- **Retreat** (sunset): formation, lowering of the flag. **Tattoo**, about 9 p.m.: final formation, roll call.
- **Taps:** lights out.

Guard mount came about 9 a.m., when the new guard detail relieved the old. Guard duty was twenty-four hours and rotated through the company.

The frontier soldier's occupations, in order of time spent: manual labor (wood-cutting, hay-cutting, road work, building repair, latrine digging); horse care (cavalry; two formal stable calls plus time); guard duty (rotation); drill (variable); escort duty (escorting paymasters, supply trains, surveyors, civilians); scouting (patrols); combat (the rarest thing — years could pass between actions for many soldiers). The cavalry trooper was a manual laborer who rode and fought. He rode and fought rare.

## First Call

> The bugle sounded First Call at five fifty-five and the barracks lit up where the sergeants began going through them. Reilly sat up in his bunk and put his feet on the floor and reached for his pants in the dim light. The man in the next bunk, Karpinski, was already standing and pulling on his shirt.
>
> "Cold this morning," Karpinski said.
>
> "It's August."
>
> "Cold for August. I tell you this country is not made for men."
>
> "It's made for somebody. I have not figured out who."
>
> The bugle sounded Reveille at six and they fell in on the parade ground in the gray light with their carbines at order arms and their mounts standing in the picket line behind the barracks. The lieutenant came out from the orderly room with his hat under his arm and read the morning report. Three men sick. One man on extra duty for drunkenness on guard. The detail to escort the quartermaster wagon to the agency would leave at seven sharp. Sergeant Voss would command. Reilly heard his own name in the detail list.
>
> "Karpinski," Reilly said as they went back toward the barracks. "I drew the agency run. You want my mess for the day?"
>
> "Bacon and beans?"
>
> "Bacon and beans and whatever else they are giving out."
>
> "All right. I will save you a coffee for tonight."

## 3. Pay, Enlistment, Desertion

A private earned thirteen dollars a month in the 1870s (reduced from sixteen after 1871). Sergeants earned about twenty. Officers earned more — a lieutenant about $120, a captain about $150. The paymaster (a special officer) made his rounds every two months at most posts. The paymaster carried the pay in cash, with an escort.

The thirteen dollars went to the sutler (where the soldier had run a tab), to the laundresses (about a dollar a month), to gambling, to drink. The soldier had no money long after payday.

Enlistment was five years (cavalry) or three years (some periods, infantry). Re-enlistment bonuses were small. Many soldiers re-enlisted because they had nowhere else to go; the army held them. Honorable discharge produced a paper that showed service. Dishonorable discharge brought disgrace and a record. Desertion was the army's problem; about one in three enlistees deserted at some point. Penalties ran from forfeiture of pay to imprisonment to (rarely) execution.

The deserter is a figure in western work — the man who slipped away from the army, often headed for gold camps or for Mexico. The army hunted deserters through provost marshals when worth the trouble. Many deserters made new lives under new names.
