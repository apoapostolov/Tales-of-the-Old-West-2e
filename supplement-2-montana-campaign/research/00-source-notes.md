## Montana 1873 Design Anchors

- Southwestern Montana remains the oldest dense mining belt: Bannack, Virginia City, Helena, and newer satellite camps.
- Fort Benton matters because the Missouri still makes it the head of practical river navigation and a freight choke point.
- The Gallatin and Deer Lodge valleys matter because agriculture, hay, horses, road traffic, and cattle support the mining belt.
- Western valleys matter because water, timber, mission legacy, and passes make them different from the open plains.
- Northern and eastern Montana are not empty; they are militarized, Indigenous, under-governed, and dangerous in ways unlike the mining belt.
- Any settlement statblock must begin with terrain and supply, not saloon color.

## High-Value Historical Corrections

- Montana in 1873 is not yet the aftermath of the Great Sioux War. Little Bighorn is still three years ahead, on June 25-26, 1876.
- Railroad pressure is real in 1873, but it is survey, finance, and land-grant pressure rather than finished railroad power inside Montana. The Northern Pacific reached Bismarck in 1873, not Montana.
- Virginia City is still the territorial capital in 1873. Helena does not become the capital until 1875.
- Cattle are important by 1873, but eastern-plains open-range dominance is still emerging. The stronger 1873 cattle anchors are valleys, winter range, and supply relationships to mining districts.
- A Montana 1873 sandbox should therefore lean on mining decline and consolidation, steamboat freight, fort-centered military reach, Native territorial power, and pre-rail speculative pressure.

## Working Source Pointers

- Montana brief history: territory creation, mining, Native nations, cattle expansion
- Montana Historical Society capital timeline: Bannack 1864, Virginia City 1865, Helena 1875
- Montana Historical Society governors page: by 1870 non-Indian population was 20,595 and Potts begins a stabilizing governorship in 1870
- Montana Historical Society livestock brands guide: Montana starts recording brands in 1873
- NPS Bozeman Trail: route opened for access to Virginia City goldfields and remained a violent failed frontier road
- NPS Yellowstone expeditions: Fort Ellis escorts the 1870 Washburn expedition; Yellowstone becomes a national park in 1872
- NPS 7th Cavalry: Yellowstone Expedition of 1873 fought Lakota warriors; the Black Hills Expedition of 1874 led directly to the Great Sioux War of 1876-77
- Library of Congress blog on Northern Pacific: in 1873 construction reached Bismarck and the Yellowstone Expedition surveyed a railroad route along the Yellowstone River
