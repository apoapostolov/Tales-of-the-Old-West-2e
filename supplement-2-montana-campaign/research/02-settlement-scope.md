# Montana 1873 - Settlement Scope Proposal

This file answers the scope question in game-design terms rather than pretending the territory had one perfectly stable settlement count in 1873.

## First Principle

There is no single clean number for "villages in Montana in 1873."

Why not:

- camps rose and died quickly
- some places were post offices, ferries, crossings, or ranch clusters rather than true towns
- the same place might be described as a camp, settlement, town, landing, or district depending on the source
- population moved seasonally with freight, mining, military work, and stock

So the right output for the supplement is a tiered settlement model.

## Recommended Territory Model

### Tier 1 - Flagship settlements

Target: `7 to 9`

These are full chapter settlements with the New Mexico structure plus expanded Montana statblocks.

Current recommended list:

- Virginia City
- Helena
- Fort Benton
- Deer Lodge
- Bozeman
- Missoula
- Bannack

Strong promotion candidates:

- Diamond City
- Sun River / Fort Shaw corridor

### Tier 2 - Moderate settlements

Target: `12 to 18`

These should get compressed but still table-usable entries with statblock, factions, people, and hooks.

Current first-pass list:

- Diamond City
- Philipsburg
- Radersburg
- Stevensville
- Sun River Crossing
- Blackfoot City
- Argenta

Likely additions after deeper pass:

- Gallatin City or Three Forks orbit
- Junction nodes near Fort Ellis supply lines
- Mullan Road / Hell Gate route nodes
- one or two Missouri corridor landings
- one or two Judith Basin or Musselshell edge settlements

### Tier 3 - Minor camps, crossings, ranch clusters, and agency-adjacent nodes

Target: `25 to 40`

These do not need full writeups. They need:

- map placement
- one-line function
- one or two hooks
- nearby faction control
- nearest law and nearest fort

This is where designer leeway is not only acceptable but necessary.

### Tier 4 - Isolated ranches, stage stops, ferries, missions, and line camps

Target: `dozens`

These should mostly appear as:

- travel nodes
- encounter anchors
- rumor sources
- safe houses or unsafe houses
- outlaw fences
- Army or scout contact points

## Recommended Practical Count for the Product

For the supplement, build as if Montana in 1873 contains:

- `7` flagship settlements
- `14` moderate settlements
- `30` named minor nodes
- `40+` unnamed or lightly named ranch, ferry, roadhouse, and camp sites

That gives enough density for a true territory sandbox without forcing fake precision.

## Integrated Village Planning Notes

This section absorbs the useful planning content from `supplement-2-montana-campaign/villages/` so the scope file becomes the authoritative content map, not just a count sheet.

### Structural rules absorbed from village planning

- Every settlement begins with an overview rooted in real terrain, economy, and route logic.
- Every primary and secondary settlement has a statblock before hooks.
- Every settlement carries faction presence notes.
- Every settlement has notable characters with usable motives.
- Primary settlements use the full New Mexico rhythm.
- Secondary settlements use a compressed but still playable version.
- Farmsteads and other tertiary sites use prose plus owner and amenity tables, not the old generic field-value block.

### Template payload to preserve

For primary settlements, preserve this chapter rhythm:

- settlement overview with historical setup
- statblock
- Montana notes on district, roads, winter, Native-political pressure, federal reach, material base, and imports
- `King's People` or local power insertion
- `3` campaign adventures
- `3` standalone tales
- notable people roster

For tertiary holdings, preserve this compact payload:

- practical prose description
- owner table
- amenity history table

## Regional Breakdown

### 1. Southwestern mining belt

Highest density of named settlements.

Anchor places:

- Bannack
- Virginia City
- Helena
- Diamond City
- Argenta
- Radersburg
- Philipsburg
- Blackfoot City

This is the most urbanized and politically tangled part of the map.

### 2. Deer Lodge and western valleys

Lower density, better subsistence base, stronger ranch and mission legacy.

Anchor places:

- Deer Lodge
- Missoula
- Stevensville

This region should have more ranches, mills, road stations, and valley farms than mining camps.

### 3. Gallatin and Bozeman frontier

Moderate density, high military pressure, route importance.

Anchor places:

- Bozeman
- Fort Ellis orbit nodes
- valley farms and stock outfits

This region should feel exposed rather than crowded.

### 4. Missouri and north-country line

Sparse settlement, huge strategic importance.

Anchor places:

- Fort Benton
- Sun River / Fort Shaw corridor
- landings, trading points, wagon camps

This region should not feel settled. It should feel like trade and military movement cutting through a powerful Indigenous landscape.

### 5. Eastern and southeastern approaches

Very thin Euro-American settlement footprint in 1873.

Use mostly:

- trails
- temporary camps
- scout routes
- military movement
- Native-controlled or contested space

This is where the supplement should resist the urge to overbuild towns.

## Design Rule for Invented Settlements

Invent only where one of these is true:

- a real route needs an intermediate service node
- a faction needs a believable logistics base
- a geography gap would otherwise make travel unrealistically empty
- a local economy needs a grain elevator, ferry, hay camp, sawmill, roadhouse, or assay camp to function

Do not invent a town just to add content. Invent a node only if the map economy requires it.

## Immediate Recommendation

Lock the supplement to this working scope:

- `7` major settlement chapters
- `14` moderate settlement entries
- `30` minor named nodes in the atlas / appendix

That is large enough to feel epic and still small enough to finish coherently.
