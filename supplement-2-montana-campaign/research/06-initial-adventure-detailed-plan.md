# Initial Adventure Detailed Plan

## Working Title

`The Yellowstone Line`

## Design Brief

This is the opening adventure for the Montana supplement.

It should:

- run for `6 to 7` sessions
- assume `4` hours per session
- contain about `18 to 20` meaningful events or scenes
- include about `1` major combat encounter per session
- remain broadly linear at the macro level
- contain several large dilemmas with `3 to 4` valid options
- let consequences shape the outcome, future faction posture, and the party's place in Montana

## System Fit: Tales of the Old West 2e

The adventure should be built for the game's actual strengths:

- social pressure
- moral compromise
- concrete travel hardship
- tracking and fieldcraft
- sudden violence rather than tactical set-piece dungeon crawling
- towns, factions, and long-term campaign consequences

That means each session should usually include:

- 1 opening pressure scene
- 1 investigation / travel / negotiation scene
- 1 major turn or dilemma
- 1 major combat or violent confrontation
- 1 consequence or fallout scene

Not every event needs combat. Combat should be costly, risky, and decisive enough that one major fight per session is plenty.

## Historical Spine

The historical background remains the 1873 Yellowstone Expedition and the survey pressure associated with Northern Pacific ambitions.

The adventure is not trying to reproduce the expedition in documentary fashion. It is using the historical pressure system as the engine for a playable campaign opening:

- Army escort and prestige
- surveyors and route men
- speculators and boosters
- Crow scouts and intermediaries
- Lakota resistance in contested country
- freight, payroll, maps, and rumors

## Core Adventure Premise

The player characters join or are attached to a military-backed survey column out of Fort Ellis.

Officially, the expedition is about route-finding and protection.

Unofficially, multiple groups are trying to use it for their own ends:

- an Army officer wants a clean success to advance his reputation
- a railroad-linked survey man wants route information and private leverage
- a Montana town booster wants the projected line bent toward his interests
- a freight contractor wants Army money and future contracts
- a corrupt clerk is already copying and selling survey data
- hostile riders and local resistance mean to stop or bloody the effort

The party begins low in the hierarchy and finishes with information valuable enough to alter careers, towns, contracts, and future violence.

## Campaign Arc

### Act I - Join the line

Sessions `1-2`

- the party is hired, pressured, or attached
- they learn the personalities and hidden agendas in the column
- they see the first signs that the survey is not a neutral act

### Act II - The line cuts the ground

Sessions `3-5`

- the survey becomes politically and militarily dangerous
- the party learns maps, payroll, and route choice are all weapons
- several factions start trying to recruit, use, or kill them

### Act III - Break, retreat, and leverage

Sessions `6-7`

- the column fractures
- the party must choose what to save and whom to betray
- they emerge into free-roam Montana with enemies, allies, and proof

## Event Count Plan

Target: `20` events

### Session 1 - Fort Ellis

#### Event 1. Muster and employment

The player characters are recruited or ordered onto the expedition:

- hired teamsters
- guards
- scouts
- interpreters
- pack hands
- civilian specialists

Purpose:

- party formation
- expose class divisions
- establish stakes in pay, debt, and obligation

#### Event 2. Supper with liars

A mixed social scene between Army officers, civilian survey staff, local boosters, and camp followers.

Purpose:

- establish key NPCs
- let PCs test INSIGHT, PRESENCE, BOOKLEARNIN', PERFORMIN', or plain judgment
- seed the first suspicion that some men are here on private business

#### Event 3. The picket-line killing

A mule handler or camp laborer is found dead near the horse lines, throat cut or skull broken. Was it drunken violence, theft, or a message?

Purpose:

- first investigation pressure
- early evidence of expedition tension

#### Event 4. Major combat 1: night raid on the remuda

Unknown riders hit the horse lines or picket detail in the dark.

Purpose:

- teach chaotic frontier combat
- threaten mounts and expedition mobility
- make violence immediate from session one

Possible outcomes:

- horses scattered
- a prisoner taken
- valuable tack or dispatch case stolen

### Session 2 - Leaving settlement space

#### Event 5. Sorting the column

The expedition departs. Order of march, wagons, scouts, and rear guard matter.

Purpose:

- set logistics tone
- let party choose roles

#### Event 6. Dilemma 1: the deserter, the witness, or the thief

The PCs find or intercept one frightened civilian who may be:

- a frightened survey assistant trying to flee
- a thief who stole copied notes
- a witness to corruption
- all three at once

Options:

1. turn him over to the Army
2. hide him and hear him out
3. sell him to the civilian survey chief
4. let him run and keep whatever he carries

Consequences:

- changes trust with Army, survey faction, and booster faction
- may alter who later has the duplicate map packet

#### Event 7. The ranch stop

At a supply ranch or waystation the PCs hear direct civilian fear about what the survey line may bring.

Purpose:

- give voice to local Montana interests
- show that “development” means dispossession for some and profit for others

### Session 3 - The first markers

#### Event 8. Setting the stakes

The survey crew begins placing markers and taking measurements in a visible way.

Purpose:

- show that this work changes the world
- make the act of measurement political

#### Event 9. Dilemma 2: the route fork

Evidence appears that one proposed line serves one town interest and another serves a different route, freight, or military logic.

Options:

1. support the official line
2. quietly alter or misreport a marker
3. expose the booster trying to interfere
4. keep silent and sell the knowledge later

Consequences:

- determines who profits from the draft route
- affects later allies in Helena, Bozeman, and the freight world

#### Event 10. Major combat 2: attack at the marker line

Riders strike while the line is being surveyed.

Purpose:

- battle in open ground under bad visibility
- protect survey gear, mules, instruments, or wounded men

Possible outcomes:

- the instruments survive
- a survey notebook is lost
- a recognizable enemy dead or captive changes the politics

### Session 4 - The country closes in

#### Event 11. Tracks and countermoves

The PCs scout signs of shadowing riders, abandoned fires, false tracks, or decoy movement.

Purpose:

- use HAWKEYE, NATURE, INSIGHT, and tracking logic
- teach that being watched is not the same as understanding by whom

#### Event 12. A Crow offer

A Crow intermediary or scout offers information or a warning, but asks for something in return.

Options:

1. trust the Crow guidance and alter the route
2. report the offer directly to command
3. bargain privately for separate information
4. refuse and continue blind

Consequences:

- changes whether the party later walks into an ambush, skirmish, or avoidable crisis
- changes Crow view of the party after the opener

#### Event 13. Major combat 3: the ford fight

At a river crossing, ravine, or broken stretch of ground, the column is attacked in a place where wagons and horses cannot maneuver well.

Purpose:

- emphasize terrain
- force triage under fire

Possible secondary objectives:

- save the lead wagon
- stop the loss of survey poles and instruments
- recover a wounded officer

### Session 5 - Corruption inside the line

#### Event 14. The dead clerk

A clerk, note-copyist, or pay ledger man is found dead. The body, papers, or missing satchel indicate internal betrayal.

Purpose:

- turn attention inward
- make clear that not all danger is outside the camp

#### Event 15. Dilemma 3: the copied packet

The PCs obtain or locate a copied set of survey notes, falsified invoices, and private letters.

Options:

1. give the packet to the commanding officer
2. give it to the honest survey man
3. blackmail the corrupt faction quietly
4. keep the packet and tell no one

Consequences:

- determines who later tries to kill, bribe, or buy the party
- may decide whether the expedition collapses from mistrust before the final push

#### Event 16. Major combat 4: camp mutiny, payroll robbery, or both

This session's major violence should come from within:

- civilian guards try to seize the payroll
- contract men and enlisted men clash
- an attempted arrest turns into a gunfight

Purpose:

- prove the expedition is breaking apart
- make the party choose sides

### Session 6 - Break and retreat

#### Event 17. The order to push on

Despite losses, command or private interests demand one more forward move.

Purpose:

- sharpen conflict between military pride, private greed, and survival

#### Event 18. Dilemma 4: what do you save

When the column breaks, the party must choose the mission priority.

Options:

1. save the payroll
2. save the survey notes and instruments
3. save civilians and wounded
4. save the remuda and transport

Consequences:

- determines future enemies
- determines what resources the party carries into the open-world phase
- determines which newspaper story becomes dominant

#### Event 19. Major combat 5: rear-guard action

The broken expedition must fight while retreating from contested country.

Purpose:

- running fight
- hard sacrifice choices
- chance for heroism, desertion, or cold practicality

### Session 7 - Return and reckoning

#### Event 20. What the maps are worth

Back in or near settlement space, the party faces competing claims on what they know.

This is the final pressure event, not necessarily a pure talking scene.

Possible attached pressures:

- a legal hearing
- a private midnight buyout
- a threatened arrest
- a newspaper editor wanting the truth
- a bounty or robbery attempt

#### Major combat 6 or 7: final confrontation

This final major combat should only happen if the players' choices have created it. Good forms:

- a courthouse or hotel-room ambush
- attack by hired riders on the return road
- armed showdown over packet, payroll, or witness custody

If the players avert open violence through prior choices, this slot can instead become a drawn standoff or threatened showdown with no shooting. The important part is that the possibility of final violence must be real.

## Recommended Session Cadence

To hit `18 to 20` events over `6 to 7` sessions:

- Session 1: 4 events
- Session 2: 3 events
- Session 3: 3 events
- Session 4: 3 events
- Session 5: 3 events
- Session 6: 3 events
- Session 7: 1 to 2 large resolving events

That yields `20` events total while keeping room for roleplay and combat pacing.

## Key Dilemmas Summary

These are the non-negotiable branching pillars.

### Dilemma A - The runaway clerk or thief

Who do the PCs protect, if anyone?

Options:

- Army
- survey faction
- private bargain
- escape

### Dilemma B - Which route gets marked

Do the PCs keep the line honest, bend it, expose the bend, or store the knowledge for later use?

### Dilemma C - Whether to trust Crow guidance

Do the PCs treat Crow intelligence as truth, threat, bargaining chip, or trap?

### Dilemma D - What to do with the copied packet

Do the PCs expose, sell, blackmail, or bury the evidence?

### Dilemma E - What to save in the collapse

Do they save:

- people
- payroll
- notes
- horses and wagons

This final choice should have the largest downstream effect.

## Consequence Grid

The detailed draft should track at least these outputs.

### Army reputation

- loyal auxiliaries
- unreliable opportunists
- dangerous meddlers

### Survey and railroad faction view

- useful assets
- blackmail risk
- targets for elimination

### Crow relations

- trusted
- cautious
- hostile

### Montana local interests

- seen as defenders of local settlements
- seen as bought men
- seen as disruptive drifters carrying dangerous papers

### Open-world launch resources

The party exits the adventure with some mix of:

- money
- maps
- evidence
- faction letters
- a witness
- a safe house
- a dead man's enemies

## Combat Guidance

Because this is _Tales of the Old West 2e_, the major combat per session should be:

- short enough to fit the session
- dangerous enough to matter
- tied to terrain and objectives
- rarely about annihilating the enemy

Every combat should have at least one objective other than “kill them all”:

- hold the line
- get the wounded out
- keep the horses from scattering
- protect the notebook case
- stop the payroll theft
- escape with the witness

## Scenario End States

The opener should not end with one fixed success state.

Good end states include:

### 1. Bloody success

The party gets the evidence and survives, but makes powerful enemies.

### 2. Quiet corruption

The party profits by compromise and enters Montana already morally compromised.

### 3. Public exposure

The party exposes fraud and becomes known territory-wide.

### 4. Ruined column, hidden truth

The expedition collapses and the party alone knows why.

### 5. Divided party legacy

The party saves lives but loses money and documents, or saves documents and leaves people to die. Either outcome should stain the next phase of play.
