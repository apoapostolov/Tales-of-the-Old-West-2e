# New Mexico Campaign Structure and Montana Book Plan

This file does three jobs:

1. document the structure of the New Mexico campaign chapter
2. explain how that structure generates a sandbox
3. expand the model into a full Montana campaign book of 200+ illustrated A4 hardcover pages

## 1. New Mexico Chapter Structure

Reference text: [corebook/09-the-new-mexico-campaign.md](/mnt/c/git-public/Tales-of-the-Old-West-2e/corebook/09-the-new-mexico-campaign.md)

Measured word count:

- `16,567` words

### Chapter skeleton

The New Mexico chapter is not just setting lore. It is a nested campaign delivery system.

#### A. Framing and premise

- title and mood quote
- one-paragraph statement of what the chapter contains
- explicit statement that the chapter is both setting and campaign framework

#### B. Territory primer

- brief history
- explicit note on why the campaign is sandbox-style
- landscape and wildlife summary

This gives the GM enough macro context to understand why the place is unstable before any local play begins.

#### C. Campaign proposition

- a named campaign framework: `The King of Santa Fe`
- three possible home towns
- `Session Zero` instructions
- `What’s in the Campaign` explanation

This is the crucial hinge. The chapter tells the GM what kind of campaign this is, how characters enter it, and how much agency they have from the start.

#### D. Macro-antagonists and power blocs

- Clarence King
- the Quinlivan faction
- Santa Fe Ring politics
- King and Quinlivan personal profiles
- their objectives and methods

This creates a territory-wide engine that can touch every local town without erasing local specificity.

#### E. Three flagship local sandboxes

Each fictional town gets the same operational structure:

1. town overview: geography, economy, routes, local resources
2. power insertion: `King’s People in [Town]`
3. `Campaign Adventures`: three escalating local scenarios
4. `Standalone Tales`: three optional or insertable hooks
5. `The People of [Town]`: a roster of notable NPCs

The NPC format is especially important. Each person is written as:

- role and social position
- what they want
- what they need
- what they fear
- sometimes the secret they are concealing

That makes the town playable even if the GM never uses the named adventures.

#### F. Territory-level endgame

- `Until the End`
- adventures 4, 5, and 6
- travel to Santa Fe
- confrontation, bargain, murder, or collapse
- epilogue states

This is not a linear ending. It is a late-game escalation package that only activates once the players push high enough into the macro-conflict.

#### G. Real-town gazetteer

- Santa Fe
- Albuquerque
- Lincoln
- Silver City
- Las Vegas

This section widens the map beyond the three flagship towns and makes the territory feel real, larger, and traversable.

## 2. How the New Mexico Chapter Generates a Sandbox

The chapter generates sandbox play through layered containment.

### Layer 1: one big territory problem

There is one strong macro engine: Clarence King and his expansion plans.

This gives coherence. Without it, the chapter would be a bag of town notes.

### Layer 2: three distinct local theaters

The chapter avoids the railroad-track problem by giving players three valid home bases. Each one has:

- different geography
- different economy
- different faction expression
- different moral pressure

So the same macro plot feels different in each location.

### Layer 3: unresolved human motives

The towns are not generated by plot first. They are generated by people with clashing incentives. The wants / needs / fears structure means:

- the town keeps moving even if players do nothing
- every alliance is conditional
- rumors naturally produce side play
- the GM can improvise without losing coherence

### Layer 4: multiple scenario scales

Each flagship town contains:

- campaign adventures that move the main conflict
- standalone tales that widen or twist play
- NPCs whose motives generate emergent incidents

That produces the correct sandbox effect:

- there is always something to do
- players choose which pressure to answer
- the world keeps implying more depth than the page count literally contains

### Layer 5: delayed finale

The chapter does not force the PCs into Santa Fe immediately. The climax is held back until the players:

- gain information
- survive locally
- make enemies
- decide to escalate

That is why it works as a sandbox and not as a disguised linear adventure path.

## 3. What Must Be Expanded for Montana

Montana cannot just copy the New Mexico chapter at larger scale. It needs structural expansion because the intended product is a whole book, not one chapter.

The New Mexico chapter has:

- `1` macro conflict spine
- `3` flagship fictional towns
- `5` short real-town entries
- `16,567` words

The Montana book needs:

- `1` strong central territorial premise
- `7` flagship settlement chapters
- `14` moderate settlement entries
- `30` minor nodes
- a real faction dossier
- a real outlaw ecology
- a travel and region framework
- a starter campaign that opens the world

## 4. Word Budget for a 200+ Page A4 Hardcover

This must be estimated from layout assumptions because final typography and illustration count are not fixed yet.

### Working physical assumptions

- format: A4 hardcover
- layout: single-column or hybrid RPG layout with sidebars, maps, and character boxes
- illustrations: `25% to 35%` of page area across the whole book
- front matter, contents, credits, and index: `12 to 20` pages

### Practical text-density assumptions

For an illustrated A4 RPG book, the true average across the whole book is not the same as a pure prose page.

Use these working ranges:

- dense full-text page: `500 to 650` words
- typical illustrated RPG content page average across book: `350 to 450` words

### Calculated range

If the book is `224` pages total:

- subtract `16` pages of front matter and end matter
- leaves `208` content pages
- at `350 to 450` words per page average
- total usable range: about `72,800 to 93,600` words

### Recommended target

For the Montana sandbox, the right target is:

- `80,000 to 95,000` words

That is large enough to feel massive and detailed without becoming an unreadable brick.

### Scale comparison to the New Mexico chapter

- New Mexico chapter: `16,567` words
- Montana recommended target: `80,000 to 95,000` words

So the Montana book should be roughly:

- `4.8x` to `5.7x` the size of the New Mexico chapter by word count

That is the right order of magnitude for an expanded whole-book sandbox.

## 6. Structural Rules for the Montana Book

To preserve what works in New Mexico while scaling up:

### Rule 1

The book must still have one clear macro proposition.

Recommended version:

`Montana in 1873 is a pre-consolidation territory where capital, forts, Native power, river freight, and survey speculation overlap without any one system becoming supreme.`

### Rule 2

Every flagship settlement must be locally complete.

A GM should be able to run six sessions from one chapter alone.

### Rule 3

Every chapter must point outward.

Each settlement must connect materially to:

- at least two other settlements
- one region-level faction struggle
- one outlaw network
- one travel corridor

### Rule 4

The finale cannot be mandatory.

Montana is too big for a single forced conclusion. The book needs territory-wide escalation tools, not a compulsory end boss.

### Rule 5

The starter campaign must teach the entire book.

It should introduce:

- Army limitations
- Native power
- mining and freight interests
- survey / speculation politics
- the danger of distance
- the fact that no one authority can solve the players' problems
