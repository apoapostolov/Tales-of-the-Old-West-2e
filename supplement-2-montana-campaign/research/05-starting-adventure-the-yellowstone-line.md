# Starting Adventure Plan: The Yellowstone Line

This is the recommended opening campaign for Montana Territory, 1873.

It is built around the real 1873 Yellowstone Expedition and the wider struggle over whether military-backed survey and transport corridors can be forced through contested country.

## Why This Is the Right Opening

It is a strong opener because it introduces the whole book through one historical moment:

- Army presence
- Northern Pacific survey pressure
- Crow alliance possibilities
- Lakota resistance
- local Montana boosters and speculators
- scouts, guides, freighters, contractors, and opportunists
- the fact that maps and claims are political weapons

It also avoids the later-war problem. This is not Little Bighorn after the fact. This is 1873, when the territory still feels open, unfinished, and dangerously unresolved.

## Campaign Purpose

The opening campaign should do three jobs.

### 1. Introduce the world

Players must see the real structure of Montana:

- forts are not omnipotent
- speculators are not abstract
- Native power is strategic and immediate
- survey lines lead to land claims, town booms, and war

### 2. Give the party a reason to know the territory

By the end of the opening, the PCs should have:

- travel contacts
- faction enemies
- one or two safe settlements
- reasons to go to Helena, Virginia City, Fort Benton, Bozeman, or Deer Lodge

### 3. Release into free roam

At the end of the opening, the players should not be assigned to the next chapter. They should be holding information, debts, enemies, and opportunities that point in several directions.

## Recommended Starting Base

Start around:

- Bozeman
- Fort Ellis
- the Yellowstone approach corridor

This is the best launch point because it sits near:

- military movement
- Gallatin Valley civilians
- scouts and teamsters
- route politics
- the edge of contested country

## Campaign Premise

Spring and summer, `1873`.

A survey effort tied to Northern Pacific ambitions and military reconnaissance is assembling under Army protection. Civilians, contractors, scouts, teamsters, local boosters, and hard men gather around the expedition for their own reasons. The player characters become attached to this movement as:

- hired guides
- teamsters
- scouts
- translators
- hunters
- drovers
- guards
- drifters looking for pay
- locals dragged in by debt, law, or obligation

What begins as escort, logistics, and survey work becomes a lesson in how Montana actually functions.

## Seven-Session Structure

### Session 1 - Muster at Fort Ellis

Purpose:

- assemble the party
- show Army hierarchy and civilian contempt
- establish local contacts and initial factions

Core events:

- contract offers and recruitment
- disputes over pay, equipment, or authority
- a Crow scout delegation arrives
- one civilian survey man is clearly working for more than science
- one freight contractor is already fixing prices

End state:

- the party is attached to the expedition in some role
- they identify at least three power blocs: Army, survey capital, local Montana interests

### Session 2 - The Road East

Purpose:

- teach travel danger and logistics
- show that distance is the territory's first law

Core events:

- wagon trouble, horse trouble, or river trouble
- disputes between officers and civilians
- local ranch or supply stop reveals fear of what the survey line will bring
- evidence the expedition is being watched

End state:

- the party learns that no one on the expedition agrees on its true purpose

### Session 3 - Marks on the Ground

Purpose:

- make surveying political
- make maps feel dangerous

Core events:

- the survey party places markers
- scouts report shadowing riders
- the party discovers duplicate notes, altered measurements, or secret side-maps
- a local booster wants the route bent toward his preferred town site

End state:

- the party understands that survey data will create winners and losers far away from the current camp

### Session 4 - The First Blow

Purpose:

- bring contested country into direct play

Core events:

- an attack, ambush, or near-encirclement tied to resistance against the expedition
- confusion over who fired first
- Army officers press for retaliation
- Crow allies or intermediaries offer intelligence the Army does not fully trust

End state:

- the party must decide whether to harden into military auxiliaries, try to mediate, or pursue their own survival logic

### Session 5 - Camp of Liars

Purpose:

- turn inward and show corruption inside the expedition

Core events:

- missing dispatches
- stolen or forged survey sheets
- a dead survey clerk, scout, or teamster
- evidence that one or more civilians are using the expedition to prepare fraudulent claims, future town speculation, or private contracts

End state:

- the party possesses dangerous information

### Session 6 - Break and Retreat

Purpose:

- force decisive action under pressure

Core events:

- the expedition breaks, retreats, or splinters after escalating attacks and internal distrust
- the party must choose what to save:
  - people
  - maps
  - payroll
  - horses
  - proof of corruption
- this choice determines future allies and enemies

End state:

- survivors limp back toward Montana settlement space
- the party has become politically significant whether they wanted to or not

### Session 7 - What the Maps Are Worth

Purpose:

- convert the intro campaign into open-world play

Core events:

- return to Bozeman, Fort Ellis, Helena, or a chosen safe node
- rival claimants, Army officers, speculators, editors, lawyers, and outlaws all want something
- the party can sell, hide, expose, alter, or destroy what they know

End state:

The players now choose their future direction.

## Open-World Exit Routes

The intro should end by opening several equally valid campaign lanes.

### Route A - Bozeman and the Gallatin

For players drawn to:

- frontier farming
- stock
- Fort Ellis
- scouts
- south-central conflict

### Route B - Helena and political money

For players drawn to:

- banks
- claims
- law
- corruption
- newspapers

### Route C - Virginia City and old power

For players drawn to:

- capital politics
- vigilante residue
- old money
- Alder Gulch intrigue

### Route D - Fort Benton and freight

For players drawn to:

- river commerce
- smuggling
- northern-line danger
- Blackfeet-country pressure

### Route E - Deer Lodge and valley order

For players drawn to:

- courts
- prison and deputies
- road power
- ranching support economies

## Starter Cast Requirements

The opening campaign needs:

- one Army officer who believes in the mission
- one Army officer who cares only about career protection
- one Northern Pacific survey or land man with hidden aims
- one Montana booster who wants the route to favor his town
- one Crow scout or intermediary with independent priorities
- one freight contractor who turns every event into profit
- one editor or letter-writer who can spread consequences across the territory
- one outlaw or fence figure already sniffing around expedition payrolls and maps

## Why This Starter Produces Sandbox Play

It works for the same reason the New Mexico structure works:

- it has one coherent pressure system
- it exposes multiple factions at once
- it gives the party knowledge rather than a fixed destination
- it ends with leverage, not with orders

The party should finish the opening with:

- one settlement they trust
- one settlement they fear
- one faction they owe
- one faction hunting them
- one piece of information worth money or blood

That is the correct launch state for a huge Montana sandbox.

## Practical Size

Recommended final size for this starting adventure chapter:

- `9,000 to 11,000` words
- roughly `22 to 26` A4 pages in an illustrated RPG layout

That is enough room for:

- session-by-session guidance
- key NPCs
- travel pressures
- faction consequences
- branching outcomes
