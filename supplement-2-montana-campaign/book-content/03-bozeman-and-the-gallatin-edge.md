# Bozeman and the Gallatin Edge

> _The valley opens wide after the broken country east of it. Hay lies in clean windrows. Ditches catch light. Smoke lifts off cookfires. A man could mistake it for peace if he rode in at the right hour. Then he reaches the street and finds a deputy short of cells, a lawyer buying creek-bottom land from frightened widows, Army wagons coming hard from Fort Ellis, and three men in different saloons offering cash for the same missing survey paper._

Bozeman is where a Montana campaign can turn from one hard ride into a whole territory's worth of trouble.

The Gallatin Valley feeds people, horses, and draft teams. Fort Ellis throws soldiers, contracts, and military fear into town life. The old Bozeman Trail left a memory that still sits in men's bones. By `1873`, the town is not rich enough to feel secure and not small enough to keep strangers from changing it. A party coming out of `The Yellowstone Line` can hide here, testify here, sell truth here, get bought here, or make enemies who follow them into the snow.

## Bozeman

Bozeman stands because the valley around it can grow hay, grain, vegetables, and horseflesh in real quantity. That makes it stronger than a camp built on one exhausted gulch. It also makes it worth stealing from. Good creek water, fenced bottomland, ditch work, and meadow hay draw merchants, stockmen, lawyers, and men who mean to turn route rumors into land money before their neighbors understand what happened.

Fort Ellis sits close enough to matter every week. Officers buy in town, soldiers drink in town, contractors lean on town merchants, and every argument about escort duty, road safety, scout work, or missing Army property spills back into the street sooner or later. The Army keeps Bozeman alive and keeps it nervous.

The town itself still feels unfinished. There are stores, boarding houses, saloons, horse yards, a newspaper office, lawyers' rooms, and enough family houses to give the place a claim to permanence. After weather, the street goes to mud. After bad news from the roads, every porch becomes a court.

### What Keeps Bozeman Standing

- Valley grain, hay, beef, and horses
- Fort Ellis contracts and Army buying
- Wagon repair, blacksmith work, boarding, and horse trade
- Lawyers, editors, and claim men feeding on paper trouble
- Road traffic moving between the Gallatin, the Yellowstone approaches, and the older trail country

### What Bozeman Must Import

- Iron goods and machine parts
- Fine cloth and better liquor
- Much of its ready cash
- Medical supply beyond ordinary frontier stock
- Precision tools, legal paper, and survey instruments

### What Breaks First

- Winter feed
- Confidence in outlying roads
- Thin local law
- Freight regularity
- Any lie built on one frightened witness

### King's People in Bozeman

Bozeman has no single robber baron in the New Mexico style, but it has the same kind of pressure. Here the threat comes from a knot of respectable operators who all want the valley shaped to suit them before the rest of Montana catches up.

`Elias Pruitt`, attorney and distressed-claim buyer, feeds on bad titles, hurried signatures, and families one season short of cash. `Marta Ellison`, merchant widow and ditch investor, knows which farms are overextended and which officers can be made useful with credit. `Jonah Vick`, horse trader, launders honest stock and stolen stock with the same quiet face. `Tom Caldwell` at the `Avant Courier` can ruin a lie or print one if the evidence comes to him crooked. None of them rule Bozeman alone. Together they can tilt it.

Fort Ellis men add another layer. A captain may want order, a quartermaster may want a quiet theft buried, and a civilian contractor may want both. That gives Bozeman the right kind of frontier instability: too many powers, none complete.

## Campaign Adventures

### Adventure 1 - The Witness Between Two Doors

**SETUP** : A survivor from `The Yellowstone Line` agrees to testify about stolen survey copies, route fraud, or payroll theft. Before he can reach the deputy's room, he vanishes between a boarding house, a saloon back door, and a stable lane.

**STARTING THE ACTION** : The player characters can hunt the witness, guard the second witness nobody knows about yet, or decide which faction gets first access to the man's story. The search leads through Sarah Renshaw's boarding house, Caldwell's newspaper office, Vick's horse yard, and at least one lawyer's room where the ink is still drying.

**THE FINALE** : The witness is found dead, half-bought, or still alive behind some thin door while armed men close in. The party must decide whether the truth belongs in court, in print, in a saddlebag, or in the fire.

### Adventure 2 - Hay, Horses, and Hostages

**SETUP** : Army hay contracts, remount purchases, and private stock dealing have started crossing into theft. A valley ranch family loses horses, Fort Ellis reports missing property that officially was never lost, and one useful hostage disappears into the ranch country.

**STARTING THE ACTION** : The trail runs from Bozeman yards into Trail Creek and the Bridger approaches. The party can work for the fort, for the ranchers, or for themselves, but each choice changes who treats them as thieves and who treats them as deputies.

**THE FINALE** : The hostages, stock, and forged bills of sale converge at a winter feed cache or canyon hideout. The party can recover the animals, expose the contract ring, or cut a deal with men who already know too much about Fort Ellis and Bozeman both.

### Adventure 3 - The Gallatin Claims Club

**SETUP** : Three separate deeds surface for the same stretch of ditch-water land east of town. One claimant is a widow. One is backed by Pruitt. One traces to survey work connected to the line.

**STARTING THE ACTION** : The player characters are hired to escort documents, lean on a witness, inspect the ground, or keep the hearing from turning into murder. Every useful fact sits in the hands of somebody with reason to hide it.

**THE FINALE** : The case breaks open at a public hearing, a newspaper exposure, or a midnight attempt to burn the disputed papers. Whoever wins the land also wins the right to feed men and horses next winter. That is the real verdict.

## Standalone Tales

### Story 1 - A Scout Comes Home Late

A Crow or Army scout rides in hurt, carrying a warning about movement south or east of town. Half the men who hear him want to act. The other half want the warning buried until they can profit from the delay.

### Story 2 - Wolves in the Calving Ground

Something is killing stock beyond what wolves alone should manage. The truth may be rustlers, a neighbor feud, Army remount theft, or settlers using predators as cover.

### Story 3 - Missing Mail from the East

A mail pouch carrying route talk, legal notices, and one private letter never reaches town. Whoever holds it can make trouble cheap.

## The People of Bozeman

#### MARTA ELLISON

Merchant widow and ditch investor. She **wants** Bozeman to become too solid to bully. She **needs** good information before her rivals have it. She **fears** a rail decision or military scandal that makes her present holdings worthless.

#### TOM CALDWELL

Editor of the `Avant Courier`. He **wants** a story with names, paper, and blood enough to force Bozeman to look at itself. He **needs** witnesses who will stand after the first threat. He **fears** printing the truth one day too early and getting the wrong man killed.

#### DEPUTY HARVE LUTTRELL

Deputy with a thin jail and too much country. He **wants** enough order to keep the town from handling justice with ropes. He **needs** help he can trust for one night at a time. He **fears** being handed a truth he cannot keep alive until morning.

#### ELIAS PRUITT

Lawyer and claim predator. He **wants** creek-bottom land, distressed paper, and clients desperate enough to sign fast. He **needs** confusion and delay. He **fears** clean records and witnesses who know how to read them.

#### SARAH RENSHAW

Boarding-house keeper. She **wants** her house left outside faction vengeance. She **needs** to know who came in muddy, who burned a letter, and who asked for a back stair. She **fears** one killing under her roof that no amount of scrubbing will wash away.

#### JONAH VICK

Horse trader and quiet fence. He **wants** every disputed horse in the valley to pass near his yard. He **needs** brands altered before the right brother comes asking questions. He **fears** a public seizure tied to Army property.

#### REVEREND SILAS BROOKS

Churchman and mediator. He **wants** to keep families from being eaten by debt and spite. He **needs** respectable sinners to keep caring what he says. He **fears** the day the town learns it prefers profit to any language of mercy.

#### MARCUS HALE

Stockman and valley booster. He **wants** safe roads, cheap labor, and a Bozeman favored in every future transport plan. He **needs** the Army when violence rises. He **fears** that stronger men in Helena or farther east will use Bozeman and leave it thin.

#### LUCINDA VALE

Schoolteacher and message-reader. She **wants** the town's children to grow up into something other than drunks, claim sharks, and widows. She **needs** quiet access to letters, newspapers, and family gossip. She **fears** being right about which men are building Bozeman on theft.

#### FRANCOIS DURET

Freight foreman with Fort Benton ties. He **wants** control of information about delays, rates, and road losses. He **needs** Bozeman merchants to think they can trust him more than they should. He **fears** a winter of broken roads and unpaid promises.

## The Gallatin Edge

Bozeman does not stand alone. The valley breathes through a chain of nearby places, each one close enough to feed the town, shelter its enemies, or drag it into somebody else's quarrel.

### Fort Ellis

Fort Ellis is not a town, but it shapes Bozeman as much as any street in town does. It sits east of the settlement, planted there to cover the Gallatin and watch the roads running toward dangerous country. Pay chests, forage contracts, escort work, scouts, deserters, wounded men, and officers with private schemes all pass through it.

## Ten Good Bozeman Hooks

1. A witness from `The Yellowstone Line` disappears between Sarah Renshaw's boarding house and Luttrell's cell room.
2. A ranch widow produces a deed older than the one Elias Pruitt just filed against her.
3. Somebody is laundering rustled stock through Jonah Vick's yard with help from a man inside Fort Ellis.
4. Tom Caldwell has the truth, but not enough of it to print and live.
5. A survey page the party thought lost turns up in a lawyer's office copybook.
6. Bridger Canyon settlers beg for help after a road crew vanishes where no official patrol wants to go.
7. Marcus Hale offers private pay to keep one hearing from breaking the wrong way.
8. Fort Ellis wants a deniable recovery of property the quartermaster swears was never missing.
9. A Crow warning reaches the party first and then, impossibly, reaches Bozeman through another mouth.
10. A rustler band offers to return stolen horses for one paper the party should never surrender.

Bozeman works because it does not resolve anything. It spreads every unresolved matter into three more. The town can feed the party, employ them, betray them, or bury them, and the valley beyond it gives every decision room to grow teeth.
