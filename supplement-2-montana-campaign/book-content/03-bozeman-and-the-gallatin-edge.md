# Bozeman and the Gallatin Edge

> _The valley opens wide after the broken country east of it. Hay lies in clean windrows. Ditches catch light. Smoke lifts off cookfires. A man could mistake it for peace if he rode in at the right hour. Then he reaches the street and finds a deputy short of cells, a lawyer buying creek-bottom land from frightened widows, Army wagons coming hard from Fort Ellis, and three men in different saloons offering cash for the same missing survey paper._

Bozeman is where a Montana campaign can turn from one hard ride into a whole territory's worth of trouble.

The Gallatin Valley feeds people, horses, and draft teams. Fort Ellis throws soldiers, contracts, and military fear into town life. The old Bozeman Trail left a memory that still sits in men's bones. By `1873`, the town is not rich enough to feel secure and not small enough to keep strangers from changing it. A party coming out of `The Yellowstone Line` can hide here, testify here, sell truth here, get bought here, or make enemies who follow them into the snow.

## Bozeman

Bozeman stands because the valley around it can grow hay, grain, vegetables, and horseflesh in real quantity. That makes it stronger than a camp built on one exhausted gulch. It also makes it worth stealing from. Good creek water, fenced bottomland, ditch work, and meadow hay draw merchants, stockmen, lawyers, and men who mean to turn route rumors into land money before their neighbors understand what happened.

Fort Ellis sits close enough to matter every week. Officers buy in town, soldiers drink in town, contractors lean on town merchants, and every argument about escort duty, road safety, scout work, or missing Army property spills back into the street sooner or later. The Army keeps Bozeman alive and keeps it nervous.

The town itself still feels unfinished. There are stores, boarding houses, saloons, horse yards, a newspaper office, lawyers' rooms, and enough family houses to give the place a claim to permanence. After weather, the street goes to mud. After bad news from the roads, every porch becomes a court.

That half-made quality is part of the town's real history. Bozeman began as a trail settlement and valley claim point, then had to survive the collapse of the old road logic that first made its name. Men who came west dreaming of a through-road to the mines found instead a place that had to live by ditches, hay, grain, and stubborn local trade. The valley rewarded patience better than noise, but noise kept arriving anyway: survey talk, Army patrols, frightened travelers, disputed stock, and the constant question of who would control the country once the first wild chance at it was gone.

By `1873`, Bozeman is neither a raw camp nor a settled county town in the eastern sense. It is a district village with enough institutions to matter and not enough authority to feel safe. That makes it excellent campaign ground. A party can buy supplies, hear testimony, find a bed, trace a freight lie, ride out after rustlers, or get pulled into the valley's quiet wars over land, water, forage, and official favor. Every practical thing in town touches some larger tension: the Army, the road, winter, Native-country fear, Helena money, or a local man trying to turn public necessity into private advantage.

Bozeman is a village with a hard edge rather than a sleepy agricultural stop. The Gallatin makes it materially stronger than most Montana camps, but Fort Ellis, road violence, and winter exposure keep it from feeling secure.

Its statblock reads that way. `FARMING`, `MERCANTILE`, and `CIVIC` are all strong because Bozeman already serves as the valley's market, argument room, and message board. `LAW` exists, but only up to the point where distance, weather, or a powerful man's friends get involved. `WELFARE` is better than a mining camp's because families, churches, schools, and decent ground exist here, yet one bad winter, one broken ditch, or one stretch of road panic can still make the whole place feel thin.

The named offices matter because the town is trying to look settled before it has fully earned the right. The mayor gives boosters a face. The judge gives claim thieves and frightened widows a room in which to lie under color of process. The sheriff and deputy matter because everybody knows the county cannot keep up with the country around Bozeman unless local men choose to help it. That is the difference between a safe village and a dangerous one pretending to be orderly.

### What Keeps Bozeman Standing

- Valley grain, hay, beef, and horses
- Fort Ellis contracts and Army buying
- Wagon repair, blacksmith work, boarding, and horse trade
- Lawyers, editors, and claim men feeding on paper trouble
- Road traffic moving between the Gallatin, the Yellowstone approaches, and the older trail country

### What Bozeman Must Import

- Iron goods and machine parts
- Fine cloth and better liquor
- Much of its ready cash
- Medical supply beyond ordinary frontier stock
- Precision tools, legal paper, and survey instruments

### What Breaks First

- Winter feed
- Confidence in outlying roads
- Thin local law
- Freight regularity
- Any lie built on one frightened witness

### King's People in Bozeman

Bozeman has no single robber baron in the New Mexico style, but it has the same kind of pressure. Here the threat comes from a knot of respectable operators who all want the valley shaped to suit them before the rest of Montana catches up.

`Elias Pruitt`, attorney and distressed-claim buyer, feeds on bad titles, hurried signatures, and families one season short of cash. `Marta Ellison`, merchant widow and ditch investor, knows which farms are overextended and which officers can be made useful with credit. `Jonah Vick`, horse trader, launders honest stock and stolen stock with the same quiet face. `Tom Caldwell` at the `Avant Courier` can ruin a lie or print one if the evidence comes to him crooked. None of them rule Bozeman alone. Together they can tilt it.

Fort Ellis men add another layer. A captain may want order, a quartermaster may want a quiet theft buried, and a civilian contractor may want both. That gives Bozeman the right kind of frontier instability: too many powers, none complete.

That is the real power map of the place. Merchants want regular freight and regular law. Valley stockmen want the Army near enough to protect contracts and far enough away not to seize what they need. Church people want peace, schools, marriages, and the appearance of decency while the newspaper, the boarding houses, and the stables traffic in the facts nobody respectable admits in daylight. Men from Helena and Fort Benton reach in through credit, transport, and rumor. Fort Ellis reaches in through payroll, escort work, forage purchases, and missing government property.

No one controls all of it. That is why Bozeman is dangerous in the right way. A party can work for one pressure without realizing it has offended three others. A deputy trying to save a witness may be serving a newspaper man, a merchant widow, a frightened ranch family, and an honest officer at the fort all at once. The valley's respectable layer is real, but it sits over mud, debt, old fear, and an enormous amount of country that still resists being ruled from one street.

## Campaign Adventures

### Adventure 1 - The Witness Between Two Doors

**SETUP** : A survivor from `The Yellowstone Line` agrees to testify about stolen survey copies, route fraud, or payroll theft. He is no hero, only a tired man who has finally decided that being cheated is better than being buried. Deputy Luttrell arranges to move him quietly from Sarah Renshaw's boarding house to a back room near the jail, but too many people know a frightened witness means money. Before he can reach the deputy's room, he vanishes between a boarding house, a saloon back door, and a stable lane.

**STARTING THE ACTION** : The player characters can hunt the witness, guard the second witness nobody knows about yet, or decide which faction gets first access to the man's story. The search leads through Sarah Renshaw's boarding house, Caldwell's newspaper office, Vick's horse yard, and at least one lawyer's room where the ink is still drying. Along the way they find that several parties are moving at once: one wants the man dead, one wants him drunk and discredited, one wants him alive only long enough to sign a recantation, and one wants him hidden until the right hearing day. A cheap boot print, a stable ledger, and a bloodied collar are enough to prove the abduction was organized by someone who expects the law to arrive late.

**THE FINALE** : The witness is found dead, half-bought, or still alive behind some thin door while armed men close in. The party must decide whether the truth belongs in court, in print, in a saddlebag, or in the fire. If they save him, they still have to keep him alive through the night and decide which public forum can survive hearing what he knows. If they fail, the missing testimony becomes a rumor war that can poison Bozeman for weeks and make the wrong men look untouchable.

### Adventure 2 - Hay, Horses, and Hostages

**SETUP** : Army hay contracts, remount purchases, and private stock dealing have started crossing into theft. A valley ranch family loses horses, Fort Ellis reports missing property that officially was never lost, and one useful hostage disappears into the ranch country. On paper the cases are separate. In truth they touch the same ring of riders, forgers, and middlemen, some working out of Bozeman yards and some using the canyons and creek bottoms beyond town. The hostage may be a ranch daughter, a contract clerk, or a branded hand who saw the wrong transfer bill.

**STARTING THE ACTION** : The trail runs from Bozeman yards into Trail Creek and the Bridger approaches. The party can work for the fort, for the ranchers, or for themselves, but each choice changes who treats them as thieves and who treats them as deputies. They have to trace altered brands, false remount receipts, and feed purchases that do not match the hay actually stacked in the valley. The search passes through Vick's yard, one frightened ranch kitchen, and at least one outlying place where armed men have been waiting for someone to ask too many sensible questions.

**THE FINALE** : The hostages, stock, and forged bills of sale converge at a winter feed cache or canyon hideout. The party can recover the animals, expose the contract ring, or cut a deal with men who already know too much about Fort Ellis and Bozeman both. A clean victory is difficult because somebody respectable is profiting from the theft while pretending to demand order. If the party brings the truth home, they must decide whether to hand it to the fort, to the sheriff, or to a public witness who cannot be quietly bought.

### Adventure 3 - The Gallatin Claims Club

**SETUP** : Three separate deeds surface for the same stretch of ditch-water land east of town. One claimant is a widow. One is backed by Pruitt. One traces to survey work connected to the line. The ground itself is not glamorous, but the ditch water that runs through it will decide hay, grain, and winter feed for several households. Men who laugh at the size of the parcel turn grave as soon as they hear where the water cuts.

**STARTING THE ACTION** : The player characters are hired to escort documents, lean on a witness, inspect the ground, or keep the hearing from turning into murder. Every useful fact sits in the hands of somebody with reason to hide it. One map copy has been altered. One witness is ashamed of how cheaply he sold his name. One ditch hand knows exactly where the old marker stood before it was moved in the night. The party can work the legal angle, the field angle, or the coercive angle, but any path they choose drags them deeper into Bozeman's politics.

**THE FINALE** : The case breaks open at a public hearing, a newspaper exposure, or a midnight attempt to burn the disputed papers. Whoever wins the land also wins the right to feed men and horses next winter. That is the real verdict. If the party backs the wrong claimant, they may help make a thief unassailable behind court language. If they back the right one, they still have to keep the victory from being reversed by fear, arson, or a witness who vanishes before dawn.

## Standalone Tales

### Story 1 - A Scout Comes Home Late

A Crow or Army scout rides in hurt, carrying a warning about movement south or east of town. Half the men who hear him want to act. The other half want the warning buried until they can profit from the delay. The warning may concern a threatened road, a band moving through the country, or a military blunder that would embarrass Fort Ellis if it came out too quickly. The player characters can escort the scout, verify the report, or decide who gets to hear it first. Delay helps somebody. Action helps somebody else. By the time the truth is clear, one party in town has already started making money from the lie. If the scout dies before speaking plainly, the town divides around competing versions of his message, and the party has to ride out for proof while men at home buy hay, move stock, or place blame as if certainty already existed.

### Story 2 - Wolves in the Calving Ground

Something is killing stock beyond what wolves alone should manage. The truth may be rustlers, a neighbor feud, Army remount theft, or settlers using predators as cover. The carcasses are found too neatly opened, the tracks too carefully confused, and the panic too useful to one nearby claimant who suddenly wants to buy poor land cheap. The player characters can take the wolf story at face value, follow the spoor into broken ground, or begin asking which household benefits if the calving fields are declared unsafe. What starts as an animal problem can turn into a killing, a fraud, or a feud that has been waiting for an excuse. If they push too hard in the wrong direction, frightened families may arm against one another before the real culprit is found, which suits anybody hoping to settle a land or water quarrel under cover of livestock panic.

### Story 3 - Missing Mail from the East

A mail pouch carrying route talk, legal notices, and one private letter never reaches town. Whoever holds it can make trouble cheap. Inside are several things that matter to different people for different reasons: a legal notice tied to disputed land, transport talk that could move prices, and a letter whose contents would ruin a man trying to pass for respectable. The player characters may be hired by the stage line, by Caldwell, by a deputy, or by the writer of the missing letter. Finding the pouch is only the start. The harder question is who should read it, and who might kill to keep one sheet from reaching its owner. The pouch may have been taken by road agents, by a clerk in somebody's pay, or by a household desperate enough to open one stranger's mail in order to save its own land. However it happened, once the letters are missing the town begins acting on guesses, and those guesses can do almost as much damage as the truth.

## The People of Bozeman

#### MARTA ELLISON

Merchant widow and ditch investor. She keeps books tighter than most men keep revolvers and has quietly bought interests in storage, freight, and water. Marta **wants** Bozeman to become too solid to bully. She **needs** good information before her rivals have it, especially about contracts, ditch repair, and transport changes. She **fears** a rail decision or military scandal that makes her present holdings worthless. She is on speaking terms with officers' wives, storekeepers, and one or two ranch widows who trust her more than they should. Marta can fund a quiet search, suppress a rumor for a day, or tell the party exactly which respectable man is overextended and therefore dangerous.

#### TOM CALDWELL

Editor of the `Avant Courier`. Caldwell is not brave because he likes danger. He is brave because he has seen what happens when liars get whole valleys to themselves. He **wants** a story with names, paper, and blood enough to force Bozeman to look at itself. He **needs** witnesses who will stand after the first threat and documents he can print without handing a lawyer an easy victory. He **fears** printing the truth one day too early and getting the wrong man killed. Caldwell trades favors with Luttrell, distrusts Pruitt openly, and knows Sarah Renshaw hears things no court ever will.

#### DEPUTY HARVE LUTTRELL

Deputy with a thin jail and too much country. Luttrell is tired in the way frontier lawmen get tired: not from drink, but from counting how many miles of trouble sit outside one pair of boots. He **wants** enough order to keep the town from handling justice with ropes. He **needs** help he can trust for one night at a time, because formal law reaches only as far as a horse, a lantern, and the nerve of whoever rides beside him. He **fears** being handed a truth he cannot keep alive until morning. He works under Sheriff Guy, keeps a professional eye on Vick, and will use the party if they show they can hold a door under pressure.

#### ELIAS PRUITT

Lawyer and claim predator. Pruitt dresses like a man already living in the better town Bozeman hopes to become, which makes him persuasive to fools and hateful to people who know his trade. He **wants** creek-bottom land, distressed paper, and clients desperate enough to sign fast. He **needs** confusion and delay, because daylight and patience usually ruin his better schemes. He **fears** clean records and witnesses who know how to read them. He courts Judge Langhorne politely, feeds on survey mistakes, and keeps men on hand who can lose a ledger, pressure a witness, or buy a debt before a family understands what has been sold.

#### SARAH RENSHAW

Boarding-house keeper. Sarah's establishment sits in the useful middle ground between respectable and discreet, which means politicians, drovers, survey men, and liars all pass through it sooner or later. She **wants** her house left outside faction vengeance. She **needs** to know who came in muddy, who burned a letter, and who asked for a back stair. She **fears** one killing under her roof that no amount of scrubbing will wash away. Sarah owes Caldwell one old favor, distrusts Vick's yard hands, and can tell the player characters who paid cash, who carried Army mud on his boots, and who lied about riding alone.

#### JONAH VICK

Horse trader and quiet fence. Vick looks patient because patience is profitable when other men arrive angry, frightened, or in need of quick sale. He **wants** every disputed horse in the valley to pass near his yard. He **needs** brands altered before the right brother comes asking questions and before any quartermaster clerk starts comparing descriptions too closely. He **fears** a public seizure tied to Army property, because one official inspection could expose how many quiet transactions have passed through his hands. Vick smiles at Marcus Hale, pays attention to Fort Ellis gossip, and assumes everybody can be bought if the bargain is offered before pride stiffens.

#### REVEREND SILAS BROOKS

Churchman and mediator. Brooks is useful because he goes where more partisan men cannot: sickbeds, widows' kitchens, barns after arson, and the edges of hearings where people still want to think of themselves as decent. He **wants** to keep families from being eaten by debt and spite. He **needs** respectable sinners to keep caring what he says, because open wickedness is easier to name than polite theft. He **fears** the day the town learns it prefers profit to any language of mercy. Brooks speaks with Lucinda Vale often, tries to steer Marcus Hale toward restraint, and may ask the party to prevent a legal victory that would be morally rotten.

#### MARCUS HALE

Stockman and valley booster. Hale speaks as if valley prosperity and his own prosperity are the same thing, and he is not entirely wrong. He **wants** safe roads, cheap labor, and a Bozeman favored in every future transport plan. He **needs** the Army when violence rises, though he dislikes admitting how much his confidence depends on federal muscle. He **fears** that stronger men in Helena or farther east will use Bozeman and leave it thin. He buys from Vick when necessary, argues with Caldwell when printed truth threatens business, and may hire the party for work that sounds civic until they learn whose pasture benefits.

#### LUCINDA VALE

Schoolteacher and message-reader. Lucinda reads better than half the men trying to govern the valley and remembers more than most of them would like. She **wants** the town's children to grow up into something other than drunks, claim sharks, and widows. She **needs** quiet access to letters, newspapers, and family gossip, because the pattern of a town often reveals itself first in what women are asked not to repeat. She **fears** being right about which men are building Bozeman on theft. She trusts Reverend Brooks more than any official, pities Sarah Renshaw, and can put the party in touch with families too wary to speak to lawyers or deputies.

#### FRANCOIS DURET

Freight foreman with Fort Benton ties. Duret understands that in Montana transport is not a service but a power. He **wants** control of information about delays, rates, and road losses. He **needs** Bozeman merchants to think they can trust him more than they should, because half his advantage lies in knowing which wagon is late before anyone else does. He **fears** a winter of broken roads and unpaid promises, the kind that turns reputable freight into armed recovery work. Duret talks business with Marta Ellison, lies carefully to Marcus Hale, and may draw the player characters into escort work that becomes a fight over missing cargo, missing mail, or missing men.

### Fort Ellis

Fort Ellis is not a town, but it shapes Bozeman as much as any street in town does. It sits east of the settlement, planted there to cover the Gallatin and watch the roads running toward dangerous country. Pay chests, forage contracts, escort work, scouts, deserters, wounded men, and officers with private schemes all pass through it.

The post exists because the valley is useful and the surrounding country remains dangerous enough to justify federal muscle. That makes Fort Ellis both shield and irritant. It buys hay, grain, beef, horses, wagons, and labor out of Bozeman, then complains about prices like a man insulted to discover winter exists. Soldiers carry pay into town and trouble back out again. Civilians with contracts circle the fort like flies around a carcass, each one certain he is the respectable sort of thief.

Trouble here runs to escort work, scout politics, quartermaster theft, missing patrols, and all the business that begins in lawful hands before it turns criminal.

The internal pressure at Fort Ellis is simple to state and hard to resolve. Officers want order without scandal. Quartermaster men want inventories that balance, whether the goods are truly present or not. Enlisted men want warmth, whiskey, and an easier post. Scouts and guides want to be paid before they are forgotten. Civilian contractors want signatures. Every one of those wants collides with weather, distance, poor roads, and the fact that the Army's paper reality rarely matches its frontier reality for more than a week at a time.

The party can matter here even without rank. The fort needs deniable people. A captain cannot always send a formal detachment after stolen hay, a missing dispatch case, or one sergeant who knows too much about forged receipts. That gap between official power and practical need is where outsiders become useful and dangerous.

#### People Around Fort Ellis

##### CAPTAIN AMBROSE KEENE

Post officer with a decent war record and a poor frontier posting. Keene **wants** the fort to function without becoming a laughingstock in Helena or Washington. He **needs** thefts, patrol failures, and contractor fraud handled quietly before they become official scandal. He **fears** one bad winter report will freeze his advancement where he stands.

##### SERGEANT ORIN PEEL

Veteran quartermaster hand who knows exactly where inventory turns from fact into wishful handwriting. Peel **wants** to retire solvent and respectable. He **needs** certain shortages to stay unexamined. He **fears** some clerk, scout, or wagon boss has kept enough scraps of proof to ruin him.

##### DOCTOR ADA MERCER

Civilian doctor attached to the post more by necessity than by ceremony. Mercer **wants** decent medicine, cleaner water, and fewer men sent back to duty half-healed. She **needs** officers to admit that bad management kills almost as surely as bullets. She **fears** an outbreak or winter sickness wave that exposes how thin the fort's care really is.

#### Fort Ellis Hooks

##### Hook 1 - The Missing Forage Ledger

A ledger page tied to hay purchases and remount feed disappears just before inspection. The party can recover it for Captain Keene, sell it, or discover that more than one respectable Bozeman supplier is implicated in the false counts.

##### Hook 2 - Patrol at Snowline

A patrol comes back one horse short and one man light, with a story too neat to trust. The fort wants the matter settled before official inquiry begins. The truth may be desertion, ambush, smuggling, or an officer's order no one wants written down.

### Hamilton

Hamilton is older than Bozeman's present confidence. It sits west in the valley country as a farm village and local center that never became what its people once hoped. That failure gives the place a long memory and a short temper.

Its people remember when the valley's future still looked open and when older claims of location, seniority, and road advantage seemed enough to guarantee standing. Bozeman's rise cut across those hopes. Hamilton did not die, but it settled into the worse fate of surviving with memory. The fields still produce, the church bell still rings, and neighbors still gather at need, yet every small success is measured against what the place failed to become.

Trouble here runs to family land fights, church quarrels, old grudges against Bozeman merchants, and the kind of dispute where every witness is kin to one side or the other.

Hamilton's strength is its continuity. Families know the ground, the ditches, the burial plots, and the line where one old promise became another man's fence. Its weakness is the same thing. The place carries old memory like stored powder. A church slight from three winters ago may still govern who speaks to whom at planting time. A credit arrangement in Bozeman can be read here as a betrayal of the whole district. That makes law thin and social consequence thick.

Bozeman's merchants and lawyers reach west whenever Hamilton families are short of cash, short of seed, or one funeral deep into debt. Hamilton resents that dependence but cannot entirely refuse it. So the village becomes a place where outside paper meets inside memory. A party entering from Bozeman may be treated as buyers, spies, mediators, or thieves before they have finished dismounting.

### Gallatin City and the Three Forks Corridor

West of Bozeman, the confluence country matters because roads, storage, ferry logic, and river movement all begin to stack there. It does not shout the way Bozeman does. It waits and lets geography make its argument.

This corridor matters because the land itself compels movement. Wagons slow. Herds bunch. Freight changes hands. Men who do not trust one another still need the same crossing, the same yard, the same patch of higher ground. That makes Gallatin City and the Three Forks orbit less a single confident town than a practical knot of storage, ferries, road talk, and speculative ambition.

### Bridger Canyon Settlements

The Bridger country is no one clean town. It is a string of ranch places, hay fields, timber cuts, bad road stretches, and cabins where people hear danger before Bozeman does. Snow closes it hard. Ambush works there. So does loneliness.

The canyon settlements survive because road and pasture require them, not because anybody planned a tidy village there. Families hold rough ground, timber men cut what they can pull out, and riders use the corridor because there are only so many sensible ways through the country. In fair weather the place feels stubborn and useful. In bad weather it feels cut loose from law, commerce, and common mercy all at once.

Trouble here runs to ambushes, snowbound rescue, bad claims, hidden stock, and neighbor feuds that ripen into murder because formal law is two ridges too far away.

What makes the canyon playable is the delay between trouble beginning and help arriving. By the time Bozeman hears of a missing child, vanished drover, or blocked road crew, the people up here have already searched once, armed themselves twice, and guessed wrong three times. Every lie lasts longer in a place where the next witness may be a mile away and half-snowblind by the time he speaks.

The canyon is also where respectable men hide disrespectable business. Stolen horses can be held in side draws. Timber can be cut from the wrong claim. Armed riders can sit above a road bend and learn more in one afternoon than a county officer learns in a month. So the place is not only vulnerable. It is useful to predators who want distance without true wilderness.

## Farms and Outlying Clusters

### East Gallatin Grain Farms

Good bottomland, ditch work, grain, hay, and families who can survive a hard season but not many of them in a row.

The East Gallatin farms look safer than they are. One broken headgate or one bought-up lien can turn a competent farm into somebody else's acquisition before harvest is in.

Horseflesh, pasture, contract talk, and men who know exactly what a remount is worth to the Army.

## Ten Good Bozeman Hooks

1. A witness from `The Yellowstone Line` disappears between Sarah Renshaw's boarding house and the jail cell room.
2. A ranch widow produces a deed older than the one Elias Pruitt just filed against her.
3. Somebody is laundering rustled stock through Jonah Vick's yard with help from a man inside Fort Ellis.
4. Tom Caldwell has the truth, but not enough of it to print and live.
5. A survey page the party thought lost turns up in a lawyer's office copybook.
6. Bridger Canyon settlers beg for help after a road crew vanishes where no official patrol wants to go.
7. Marcus Hale offers private pay to keep one hearing from breaking the wrong way.
8. Fort Ellis wants a deniable recovery of property the quartermaster swears was never missing.
9. A Crow warning reaches the party first and then, impossibly, reaches Bozeman through another mouth.
10. A rustler band offers to return stolen horses for one paper the party should never surrender.

Bozeman works because it does not resolve anything. It spreads every unresolved matter into three more. The town can feed the party, employ them, betray them, or bury them, and the valley beyond it gives every decision room to grow teeth.
