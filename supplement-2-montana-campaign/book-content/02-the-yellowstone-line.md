# The Yellowstone Line

> The flies were thick on the horse lines. A mule stamped and rattled its chain. Farther off, by the parade ground, somebody was laughing too loud over a bottle. Fort Ellis looked steady in the late light. Whitewashed walls. Flag limp in the heat. Wagons in a row. Men who believed a post and a pay chest made a hard country obey.
>
> It took one dead teamster and a saddle cut loose in the dark to show what the country thought of that.

The Yellowstone country is the right way to open a Montana campaign in `1873`.

Nothing in it is settled. The Army wants prestige and safe movement. Survey men want measurements, notes, and a route other men can turn into stock, town lots, contracts, and profit. Gallatin merchants want the line bent toward their stores and wagon roads. Crow scouts want the Army to hear them when it suits Crow interests. Lakota riders mean to stop one more white road from being planted in good hunting country. The people who live along the margins want to know which kind of ruin is coming for them first. This chapter drops the player characters into that knot. They begin small. A hired guard, a teamster, a scout, a wagon hand, a hunter, a clerk, a discharged soldier, a gambler needing wages, a local guide, a printer's man sent to report on the expedition. All of those fit. By the end of the chapter they will have seen Army weakness, private greed, Native power, Montana distance, and the price of carrying paper men are willing to kill for.

The action is broadly linear. The column moves out from Fort Ellis and the player characters move with it. Inside that line, the players get repeated chances to decide who they are serving and what they are willing to sell, hide, save, or burn. Those choices shape the end of the chapter and the condition in which the sandbox opens under them.

## Historical Frame

This chapter uses the real pressure of the `1873` Yellowstone survey season without pretending to reenact one single historical expedition in documentary detail.

The Northern Pacific has survey interests pushing toward the Yellowstone. The Army is deeply involved because every chain laid over that country carries military meaning with it. News of fighting farther east and northeast has already reached Montana. By late summer every serious man in Bozeman and Fort Ellis understands the same thing: the first line on the map brings other things after it. Wagons. Titles. Settlers. Soldiers.

The expedition is a smaller supporting reconnaissance out of Fort Ellis. Its official task is to examine a practical route west of the Yellowstone basin, test crossings, mark grades, and report on forage, timber, and wagon movement. Its private uses are broader and dirtier.

> Historical note: In `1873`, railroad pressure in Montana was still more survey stake than iron rail. Men were fighting over routes, grades, and paper before a locomotive had crossed the territory.

## The Shape of It

The line of events is simple enough. The party joins the expedition at Fort Ellis. A killing and a night raid show that the trouble began before the column ever left the post. Out in the field the survey work turns political, then openly dangerous. A Crow warning offers one path through the country while private greed and Army pride push toward another. By the time the papers split and men begin shooting one another in camp, the expedition is no longer an expedition at all. It is a broken string of frightened men, wounded stock, stolen notes, and rival claims about who owns the truth. What survives the ride back to settlement becomes the first real leverage the player characters hold in Montana.

The shape is firm, but it should never feel locked. Parties will do what printed expeditions always pretend they will not. They will search wagons before they are told to, press a liar too early, ride after the wrong man in the dark, pocket papers that were meant to change hands later, threaten officers, bargain with enemies in plain sight, or decide that the whole white enterprise can go to hell after the first corpse turns over in the dust. Let them. The strength of this chapter is not that the players obey the line. The strength of it is that every attempt to seize control of the line changes who still trusts them, who fears them, and who thinks they can be used.

## Why a Character Rides with the Line

No decent person has to love this expedition. In fact, the best motives are mixed. A discharged trooper may sign on because Army bacon is still steadier than luck and because Captain Rowe remembers his name from some dirty business on another road. A scout or hunter may come because the survey wages will winter his horse, or because he wants to see what kind of fools are planning to lay a line through country that can barely carry a wagon in good weather. A gambler, drifter, or camp hand may only want cash and a place near the payroll. That is enough. Montana is full of men who started in a cause they did not believe in and stayed because too much blood, paper, or profit had already changed hands.

A better hook is often local. One character may have kin in the Gallatin and know that a route choice made in open country can lift one town and starve another. Another may have hauled freight for Pike and know from the look of his wagons that he is stealing somewhere. A clerk, draughtsman, photographer, or assistant surveyor may ride because paper is the nearest thing he has to power in a hard land, and because men with soft hands and neat writing are often the first to know where the real money lies. If the group wants stronger ties from the start, give each character one thread into the expedition: prior service with Rowe or Duffy, a spell of odd work under Reed, a bad debt in Bozeman, a night's shelter once taken at Calder Ranch, or a long acquaintance with LaRoche on the road between settlements. Those ties do more than bring the party together. They make the lies personal.

## Core Cast

These are the principal named figures of the chapter. Full statblocks appear in [02A-dramatis-personae-and-statblocks.md](/mnt/c/git-public/tales-of-the-old-west-2e/supplement-2-montana-campaign/book-content/02A-dramatis-personae-and-statblocks.md).

`Captain Nathaniel Rowe` is a white man in his early forties, Army to the bone, narrow through the waist and still hard in the saddle. The first impression he gives is of order held together by temper. He keeps his gloves clean longer than any field officer should and has the habit of looking past a man before that man has finished speaking, as if measuring whether the rest is worth his time. He speaks in short clipped sentences and wastes no ornament unless he is dressing down a fool in public. In his general view, the player characters are useful irregular tools: necessary because the country is too large, too mixed, and too mean to be handled by enlisted men alone, but still not people he will trust until they prove they can hold their heads.

`Lieutenant Ezra Harlan` is a white man of twenty-seven, handsome in the polished cavalry way and not yet old enough to hide that he enjoys being looked at in uniform. The first impression is energy straining to pass for command. He stands a touch too straight, smiles a touch too quickly in company, and reaches for his belt or cuffs whenever he feels himself being judged. He speaks well, speaks fast, and speaks half a rank above his actual station when he gets excited. In his general view, the player characters are either the kind of rough useful people he may someday boast of having led, or the kind of disreputable witnesses he must not let see him fail.

`Sergeant Owen Duffy` is an Irish-born white man near fifty, broad through the chest, weathered, and going gray under the brim. He looks like a man built from old leather, smoke, and regulations remembered better than prayers. The first impression is steadiness. He wastes little motion, checks straps and loads without seeming to look, and can hear trouble in a horse line the way other men hear it in gunfire. He speaks plainly, often dryly, and with the tone of a man who has long since stopped being impressed by officers. In his general view, the player characters are either hands worth having on a bad road or one more set of mouths likely to complicate his work.

`Professor Amos Reed` is a white man in his mid-thirties, spare, near-sighted, and permanently overtired. He gives the impression of a schoolman who has stayed too long in hard country and learned to hate incompetence more than discomfort. He squints at ground, instruments, and men alike, fingers ink stains without noticing, and grows visibly colder the more exact the matter under discussion becomes. His speech is educated, dry, and increasingly sharp whenever commerce starts meddling with measurement. In his general view, the player characters are potential protection, labor, or witnesses to the truth of the work, depending on whether they prove more loyal to money or to fact.

`Silas Vane` is a white man around forty, well-fed without looking soft, careful with his clothes, and too well groomed for any honest trail position. The first impression is smoothness. He never seems hurried even when events are hurrying around him. He lowers his voice when others would raise it, and he has the habit of stepping just close enough to make a private ally of whichever person he is speaking to. He talks in easy persuasive sentences, never quite saying who sent him or what authority he claims, only what sensible men ought to want. In his general view, the player characters are purchasable assets until proven otherwise, and if they cannot be bought they must at least be divided.

`Eli Mercer` is a white man not yet thirty, neat-handed, narrow-shouldered, and too fond of bottle courage. The first impression is that of a clerk trying to look less afraid than he is. He smiles too soon, laughs where no laughter is needed, and keeps touching pockets, papers, and straps to reassure himself that things remain where he left them. He speaks quickly, softly, and with the habit of trimming every sentence toward safety. In his general view, the player characters are dangerous because they live closer to the kind of force a liar cannot write his way out of.

`Thomas Keene` is a white man in his early twenties, thin from bad sleep, bad whiskey, and worse nerves. He looks like the kind of young fool the frontier manufactures by putting weak ambition too near strong men. The first impression is fret and guilt. He sweats in cool air, glances over both shoulders before answering, and grips his own sleeves or vest as if he expects to be searched at any moment. He speaks in bursts, with excuses outrunning sense. In his general view, the player characters are possible rescuers, possible buyers, possible executioners, and he will try to guess which from one breath to the next.

`Gideon Pike` is a white man in his forties, heavy in the shoulders and gut without ever losing the look of someone who has worked teams by hand. The first impression is practical force. He dresses half like a freight boss, half like a man who knows exactly how much profit can be pulled from military need. He checks loads, counts sacks, and studies harness with more visible feeling than he shows toward most people. He speaks in the idiom of teamsters and provision men, but numbers sit behind everything he says. In his general view, the player characters are labor, risk, or leverage, and he can live with any of the three if the account ends in black ink.

`Hannah Calder` is a white woman in her late thirties or early forties, sun-cut, strong-handed, and dressed for work first and judgment later. The first impression is self-possession. She stands like a person used to carrying her own water, speaking for her own place, and burying her own dead if she must. She wastes no gesture, keeps tools where others keep ornament, and watches groups of men the way a rancher watches weather. She speaks flat, direct, and without asking leave. In her general view, the player characters are one more column of strangers until they prove they know the difference between needing a place and taking it.

`Baptiste LaRoche` is a mixed-blood frontier man in his thirties, quiet-faced, well-seated on a horse, and harder to read than most officers like. The first impression is calm attention. He hears what is being said across camp without seeming to listen, notices shifts in tone before others notice the words themselves, and keeps his own counsel until it is useful to spend it. He speaks in measured sentences and wastes nothing in them. In his general view, the player characters are worth listening to before they are worth trusting, which already puts him ahead of half the expedition.

`Shows-the-Ford` is a Crow man in mature adulthood, neither young nor visibly old, lean in the saddle and hard to surprise. The first impression is control. He sits a horse as if horse and rider settled the matter years ago. He does not fidget, does not explain himself twice, and does not offer emotion where position will do. His manner of speech is spare, exact, and cutting when he chooses to cut. In his general view, the player characters are not yet important, but unlike the officers they may still be worth the trouble of measuring correctly.

`Iron Hail` is a Lakota war leader in his thirties, already old enough to have lived by judgment rather than speed alone, but still quick enough to kill a slower man before the slower man has named the threat. The first impression he leaves, even in rumor, is direction. Men do not talk about him as a stray raider. They talk about him as someone who chooses where the blow will fall. He is said to speak little and ride hard. In his general view, the player characters are part of the white line until they do something costly enough to prove otherwise.

## What Is Moving Here

On the surface, this is an escort-and-survey job. Under that surface, four fights are happening at once. The Army wants order, safety, and a report it can send upward. The survey interest wants route control and private leverage. Crow scouts and intermediaries want to shape who moves through the country and on what terms. Lakota resistance wants the line broken before it hardens into road, post, and rail. The player characters begin low enough in the hierarchy to hear truth from all directions. That is the real strength of the opening. Nobody yet thinks they are important enough to lie to carefully.

## Consequence Ledger

Track the party's standing with four groups: `Army Respect`, `Survey Leverage`, `Crow Trust`, and `Local Goodwill`. Start each at `0`. When the party makes a major choice, move one or more tracks by `+1` or `-1`. By the time they ride back into settlement space, the totals should already be shaping the next stage of the campaign. High `Army Respect` brings work, legal shelter, and future access through Fort Ellis. High `Survey Leverage` means the party carries paper Helena, Bozeman, and railroad interests can use. High `Crow Trust` changes who will talk to them in the southern country and who will warn them before danger arrives. High `Local Goodwill` means ranchers, freighters, and merchants treat them as useful Montana people rather than passing hands. Low scores close doors, sour credit, and put bad stories on the road ahead of them.

These tracks should not stay abstract. Let them throw visible consequences forward.

- If `Army Respect` rises early, Rowe gives the party more freedom of movement, asks for their private judgment, and may trust them with custody or escort work later.
- If `Army Respect` falls, Harlan or another officer begins watching them, orders come harder, and lawful shelter at the end grows uncertain.
- If `Survey Leverage` rises, more men try to buy, seduce, threaten, or flatter the party for copies and details.
- If `Survey Leverage` stays low, the party has cleaner hands but less power when the ride back begins.
- If `Crow Trust` rises, warnings come earlier, crossings get less deadly, and one or two later scenes can shift from ambush to hard negotiation.
- If `Crow Trust` falls, silence replaces warning and the party should feel that absence in the field.
- If `Local Goodwill` rises, ranchers, freighters, and town people become a living net under the party when official hands fail them.
- If `Local Goodwill` falls, small people stop helping and big men find it easier to bury the truth.

## What the Main Factions Want

Use this when players ask what is really going on.

- **Rowe and the Army:** Rowe wants to get the column out, bring it back, and not have his name tied to chaos, fraud, or needless death. He is not blind, but he is trying to keep his world inside a military shape. If the party helps him keep order, he rewards them with trust. If they prove the expedition was rotten from the start, he faces a choice between truth and his own career.

- **Reed and the survey men:** Reed wants good work preserved from bad politics. He is already failing. He needs the party because his own staff are divided, frightened, or bought.

- **Vane and the private line:** Vane wants route knowledge before it becomes public property. He does not need the whole expedition to succeed. He only needs enough paper to sell certainty to the next buyer.

- **Pike and the freight interest:** Pike wants delay he can bill for, urgency he can profit from, and a future route that still leaves room for men like him to make money before rail reaches the territory. He will talk practical sense while robbing everybody in sight.

- **Shows-the-Ford and Crow interests:** Shows-the-Ford wants the column slowed, redirected, or made to hear that not every stretch of ground is theirs to measure when they please. He can deal. He does not beg.

- **Iron Hail and Lakota resistance:** Iron Hail wants the survey bloodied, embarrassed, and sent home with less paper than it carried out. He does not need to wipe the column from the earth. He needs to prove the line cannot move without cost.

These pressures should answer later choices, not merely decorate them. If the party helps Rowe, the Army becomes stronger in the last act but the party may arrive at settlement under closer official control. If they help Vane or quietly keep their own packet, more of the ending belongs to private leverage and less to clean testimony. If they answer Shows-the-Ford honestly, later violence may narrow instead of spreading. If they answer him badly, the chapter should remember it in the ground itself.

## Clue Discipline

Do not hide the chapter behind one successful roll. The party should never fail the whole chapter because one player rolled badly at the horse line.

Spread information in layers.

- Bell's killing points toward internal corruption.
- Keene points toward copied notes.
- Mercer points toward fraud and divided packets.
- Vane points toward private route dealing.
- Pike points toward money siphoned through freight.
- Shows-the-Ford points toward the cost of pushing the line at all.

If the players miss one clue, move the same truth through a different mouth, pocket, or grave.

## Camp Scenes Between Major Events

Use these when a session needs breathing room or when the players want to live in the expedition rather than jump from ambush to ambush.

- **The bad coffee fire:** Two troopers and one survey hand are arguing over coffee boiled black as axle grease. The dispute starts over Army issue and ends with one man saying nobody rides into this country for the pay alone. If the players sit in, they can learn who among the rank and file thinks the expedition is honest and who already knows better.

- **Reed at the notebook:** Reed works by lantern after everyone else has wrapped in blankets. He scratches revisions into the margin while swatting gnats with the back of his wrist. A respectful player can get real technical information from him here. An observant one notices that Reed has begun keeping one personal note in a separate pocketbook.

- **Pike counting sacks:** Pike checks flour, bacon, oats, and harness leather himself when he thinks no one important is looking. He is not checking for discipline. He is checking where his profit has gone. If a player watches long enough, Pike's whole practical morality becomes plain.

- **LaRoche listening in the dark:** LaRoche sits where he can hear three fires at once. He does not seem to be doing anything. Then he answers a question nobody has asked yet because he heard it coming from the way voices shifted. This is a good scene to remind the players that the quietest man in the party may have the best idea of what is building around them.

- **Harlan with a bottle:** Harlan drinks more than Rowe would approve. Not enough to disgrace himself. Enough to confess the wrong ambition to the wrong listener. He wants glory before he knows what it costs. A player who handles him well can either stiffen his spine or steer him toward ruin.

- **Mercer trying to be liked:** Mercer offers tobacco, a story, a deck of cards, or help with letters. He is trying to build cover. The more pleasant he seems, the more useful it is when the truth comes out.

- **Horse line at midnight:** A good horse line is a society. Animals stamp, blow, pull, settle, and spook in a chain. If the players spend time there, reward them with early warning in later trouble. Men who know horses well should never feel useless in this chapter.

- **A distant fire in the wrong place:** One player on watch sees a fire where no camp should be. Then it goes out. No attack comes that night. The point of the scene is not immediate action. The point is to make sleep thin and judgment worse by morning.

- **A prayer over Bell:** One older trooper or civilian woman from the post says a few words over Bell that are not much of a prayer and not much of an accusation, but enough to remind the party that the dead do not stay abstract just because the expedition keeps moving. If a player stays for it, that player learns Bell had kin expecting money before winter.

- **The spoiled flour barrel:** One barrel in Pike's load has gone damp low in the staves and somebody has turned it so the leak will not show at first glance. A player who notices it can push Pike, protect the teamsters who will be blamed, or say nothing and remember the fraud for later use.

- **Cards with Keene:** If Keene is alive and not under hard guard, he tries to sit into a card game as if ordinary company might make him ordinary again. He loses attention faster than money. A patient player can get him talking sideways around the truth before fear drives him back into denial.

- **A horse gone lame for no clean reason:** One valuable horse comes up sore in a way that might be bad ground, bad luck, or quiet sabotage. This gives the party a chance to inspect tack, question order of march, and begin suspecting that not every attack in this chapter arrives on horseback with a rifle in hand.

- **LaRoche and the weather sign:** LaRoche studies cloud, wind, bird movement, and the smell in the air while everyone else is still talking about men. If a player joins him, he offers one dry remark that proves he is tracking more than weather. It is a good way to shift attention from intrigue back to country before the country punishes them again.

- **Hannah's plain ledger:** If the party stays long enough at Calder Ranch or comes back by it, Hannah can show them a few hard lines in a household account book: nail prices, flour prices, team hire, a debt called in too early. It is one of the cleanest ways in the chapter to show what men like Pike do to people who never see a railroad map.

## Side Events on the Line

Use these when the table needs one more turn of pressure, when travel should feel longer, or when the party's choices have made the expedition split into separate moving problems.

- **The wrong grave by the creek:** The party finds an older trail grave near water, shallow enough that weather has started uncovering it. The dead man is not from this expedition. He wore freight boots and carried a broken clasp knife, one spent pistol round, and a strip of paper too weathered to read cleanly. Pike recognizes the dead if pressed and lies first. The scene lets the party feel that this road was dirty before they ever set foot on it.

- **A rider under white cloth:** A lone rider approaches under a scrap of white cloth tied to a ramrod or carbine. He claims to be carrying a message, a warning, or a proposal from Vane's friends, Pike's contractors, or a local merchant with too much money at stake. The rider may be honest, stalling, or simply measuring the camp. The party can receive him, strip and search him, shadow him on the way out, or shoot the parley to pieces and live with the story that tells later.

- **The missing instrument screw:** One small but crucial piece from Reed's gear goes missing and suddenly the whole day turns around a thing no bigger than a thumbnail. This is a good side event when you want to show how easy it is to cripple a survey without killing a man. The party may discover sabotage, clumsy theft, or simple panic hidden under a lie.

- **A wounded horse and a faster choice:** One horse takes a bad shot or a bad break in a place where the column cannot linger. Somebody must shoot it, somebody must strip the tack, and somebody must decide whether the load it carried is worth redistributing. Give this scene to the player who has been most careful with animals so far. Let that care mean something.

- **A settler's claim marker:** The party passes one pathetic little claim marker on good ground: rough-cut wood, one name, one date, a promise written ahead of safety. It should be obvious how little such a thing weighs against survey stakes, Army need, or organized money. If the party remembers the place later, it becomes a moral landmark rather than a random signpost.

- **The wrong cartridges:** A handful of cartridges turn up where they do not belong: Army issue in a civilian pouch, civilian purchase in a saddlebag that should have held only survey gear, or mixed calibers hidden under oats. The scene gives suspicious players a chance to start building chains instead of isolated clues.

## If the Players Miss, Freeze, or Break the Plan

This opener should feel dangerous, but it should not crack because one fight went sideways or one NPC died early.

- **If Keene dies too soon:** Move his truth into Mercer, Bell's missing satchel, or a copy hidden in Reed's spare instrument case. The copied-paper problem must survive Keene.

- **If Mercer dies early:** Let the party find his partial cipher marks, account slips, or a letter draft to Vane. Mercer is useful alive, but the chapter does not require him breathing.

- **If Vane is killed in the field:** Good. Montana is full of dead men whose papers still move counties. Shift his role onto a lawyer or banker in Bozeman, one hired rider carrying instructions, or Pike, who was never as separate from Vane as he claimed.

- **If Rowe loses authority early:** Sergeant Duffy can keep the column moving in practical terms. Reed can force the issue by saying the notes must still go back. Disorder changes the tone; it does not end the tale.

- **If the party tries to side openly with Pike:** Let them. Pike can pay. He can also use them as labor until the moment silence becomes cheaper than loyalty. A Pike-aligned party gets a darker, dirtier ending, but still enters the sandbox with strong hooks.

- **If the party sides with Iron Hail or simply refuses the white mission:** Then the chapter becomes one of sabotage, guidance, flight, and political choice rather than escort duty. Keep the main dilemmas in place: what happens to the papers, what happens to the wounded, and whether Crow and Lakota interests collide or can be kept apart in this one matter.

- **If the party insists on court-proof evidence:** Reward that instinct. Give them marked pay slips, duplicate pages in different hands, the wrong man carrying the right packet, and one witness who heard a direct order and can be kept alive. The closing reckoning grows stronger when the players feel they built a case rather than listened to a confession.

## Arc I: Fort Ellis and the First Miles

This opening arc should take time. Do not rush the party out of the fort. They need to hear the different claims men make for the expedition before they see how little any of those claims hold in the field.

## Fort Ellis
### 1. Muster and employment

Read or paraphrase:

> Late summer hangs dry over Fort Ellis. The mountains south and west look blue in the distance. The parade ground is hard underfoot, churned at the edges by hooves and wagon wheels. Soldiers carry crates. Civilian laborers bicker over loads. A survey tripod stands near a stack of feed sacks as if it had been planted there by accident. Nobody looks relaxed. Too many kinds of men are drawing pay from the same table.

> Historical note: Fort Ellis was a real post east of Bozeman, founded in `1867` to watch the Gallatin Valley and the roads running toward the Bozeman, Bridger, and Flathead country.

Captain Rowe brings the player characters in, or has Sergeant Duffy do it. Each character is told what they are being paid for. Be plain about money. Army work is never enough pay, but it is cash, rations, and a horse line that may still be standing in the morning.

If Rowe speaks to them himself, keep it clipped and public:

> _"You are here to ride, watch, and keep your mouths shut until I ask for them. If you cannot do the first two, tell me now and spare us the waste."_

Give the players time to inspect the wagons and learn who belongs to which world. The Army wears rank and pretends to order. The civilians have no rank at all and still manage to throw weight about. Silas Vane gives directions without holding formal command. Reed hates that and endures it because he has no choice. Mercer has custody of the fair copies and camp accounts, which means he sits too near the heart of things for a man of his quality. If the party looks closely, reward it. `INSIGHT` reads the strain between Rowe, Reed, and Vane. `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` shows the horse line is crowded enough to go bad in the dark. `BOOKLEARNIN'` notices that survey copies and payroll papers are being stored too close together. `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` lets a player start choosing what kind of face they mean to show these people. The fort itself should do some of the work. It smells of horse sweat, old ash, and soap boiled too thin. One wagon carries fresh grease over an older freight mark. Pike's men gripe that they have been shorted on oats. Reed's assistants already hate Mercer.

Do not assume the party takes employment meekly. Some groups will haggle for more pay, insist on knowing who holds legal authority over civilians, or try to choose their own place in the order of march before the column even forms. Good. Let Rowe answer harshly if he is challenged in public and more fairly if he is pressed in private. Let Vane notice who wants terms instead of wages. Let Pike mark the practical ones and Reed mark the curious ones. If the party tries to search wagons or paper chests on the first day, they should not find the whole truth, but they should absolutely find enough to make somebody angry. That early anger is useful later.

Let the yard itself work on them before the chapter hurries into blood. Duffy is out by the horse line with his sleeves rolled, one boot deep in churned dust, one hand on a sour bay that has taken against the smell of the survey wagons. If a player goes to him there, he speaks more plainly than any officer will. The line is too mixed. The paper is packed wrong. The loads are stacked like some clerk thinks leather straps and canvas keep the country obedient. He does not complain for the pleasure of hearing himself. He is measuring who listens when the complaint is practical. A player who helps him with the line should earn more than a nod. Later trust ought to remember it.

Pike's side of the yard gives a different lesson. One flour barrel has gone damp low in the staves and been turned so the wet seam faces inward. His foreman is already in a low fight with a clerk about short oats, false weights, and promises made in town by men who will never touch a wagon tongue in mud. This is useful because the whole expedition is there in miniature. Reed cares what the ground can bear. Pike cares what a team can drag through it. Rowe cares what can still be defended after casualties. Vane moves between all three like argument itself is something he means to own.

If Mercer gets a private minute, use it. He should not begin with guilt. He begins with weak friendliness. Tobacco. Cards. An offer to write a letter fairer than the player can write it himself. He is sounding for loneliness, debt, vanity, and hunger. If a player lets him talk, Mercer may ask whether the player has ever worked a job where the official packet and the useful packet were not quite the same thing. He tries to make a joke of it. He has not the stomach.

### 2. Supper with liars

That evening, the principal figures take supper near the officers' quarters or at the sutler's room.

Read or paraphrase:

> The officers' lamps throw yellow light across tin plates, coffee cups, and one bottle already too low for the hour. Outside the circle of light the fort has gone to hoof-noise and shadow. Inside it, men are talking about grades, contracts, and the future as if the country has already agreed to be measured.

Put the important voices in one room or yard and let the party move between them. Rowe talks duty, escort limits, and responsibility in the plain clipped manner of a man already short of patience. Reed talks grades, crossings, wasted miles, and the practical damage done by fools who think a map is made from desire. Vane talks about the future of the territory, but never in a way that binds him to anything. Pike talks contracts, forage, delayed freight, and the cost of every delay as if he were the only man in Montana who understood arithmetic. If you want a sharper local edge, add a Bozeman merchant or messenger who keeps arguing that the Gallatin must not be bypassed. By the end of the meal the players should know that Rowe thinks the mission smaller than it is, Reed is being leaned on, Vane wants more than one set of notes made, and Pike has sold the same promise twice. They should also know how these men carry themselves. Rowe listens with his face shut hard against interruption. Reed gets colder the more exact the talk becomes. Vane speaks softly enough that men lean in. Pike sounds like a workingman and watches like a banker.

Good table lines here should be easy to hear and easy to remember:

> _"A line belongs where the ground permits it,"_ Reed says. _"Not where a merchant dreams it."_
>
> _"Dreams are what build towns, Professor,"_ Vane says. _"Your measurements only tell the rest of us where to spend the money."_

Some parties will try to break the room instead of reading it. They may accuse Vane openly, force Pike to answer for freight fraud before anyone has proof in hand, bait Harlan into bragging too much, or slip out to follow Mercer after the plates are cleared. Support all of that. A public accusation should not solve anything early, but it should sort the room fast. Rowe hates disorder more than suspicion. Reed dislikes scandal unless it protects the work. Vane is strongest when smiling and weakest when pressed to answer one clean question in front of witnesses. A shadowing player can catch Mercer checking paper custody, Keene moving like a man with a bad secret in his coat, or one of Pike's hands counting the remuda before the raid. Those discoveries should come with position, not certainty.

Give the supper some extra heat before it breaks. Harlan is just proud enough to talk too much if handled right. Reed is just tired enough to forget politeness when grade and route are spoken of by men who mean profit. Pike keeps sounding like the rough practical man in the room, which is precisely how he likes to enter a mark's memory. If the party leaves the light and follows Mercer, Vane, or one of Pike's hands, reward them with movement instead of revelation. Mercer checks one satchel, then checks it again after three minutes have passed and nothing has changed. Vane pauses with a man he should not know well enough to speak softly to. A Pike hand circles the remuda counting more carefully than feed tallies require. None of it proves enough. That is why it has value.

### 3. The picket-line killing

Near dark, a mule skinner named Joe Bell is found dead by the remuda.

Read or paraphrase:

> Bell lies near the picket line on his side with one arm under him and dust caked black at the edge of the wound. A cut rope trails over his boots. One of the horses keeps turning its head toward the body and rolling one eye white at it.

He has been struck from behind with something heavy and then cut once under the jaw to finish him. One hitch rope has been half-sawn through. A saddle blanket is missing. So is one dispatch satchel that should have held routine stores papers and wagon tallies.

This is the first hard sign that somebody is not waiting for the expedition to get into the field before taking a hand.

There should be enough on the ground to trouble a smart party. One set of tracks shows Army issue heels. Another comes from a civilian riding boot. A shaving of survey pencil sits in the dirt near Bell's hand. Bell had been drinking with Mercer, and earlier in the day he saw Thomas Keene rifling through a chest. Let the players question Mercer, Keene, Pike's men, and the troopers on remuda duty until the scene begins to tighten around them. If they press hard, reward them before the shooting starts. Mercer lied about when he last saw Bell. Keene has mud on his cuffs from crawling under a wagon. One trooper admits a civilian promised whiskey for a look at the dispatch chest. Pike says Bell owed him money, which is true and not the point.

This is an early place where parties often try to take the whole chapter by the throat. They may clap Keene in irons at once, beat Mercer for an answer, lock down the dispatch chest, demand Rowe suspend the expedition, or decide Bell's death proves the fort itself is compromised. Let them push. If Keene is seized early, Vane has less room and Pike moves sooner through hired hands. If Mercer is beaten or humiliated in public, he becomes sloppier, more frightened, and more willing to run. If Rowe is forced to choose between discipline and departure before midnight, make him choose departure with bad order still hanging over the column. The party should feel that strong action changes the line without ending it.

Let Bell's death sit in the camp for a moment before the raid breaks over it. One old trooper, laundress, or camp woman from the post can kneel by him and say a few hard words that are not much prayer and not much accusation, only enough to remind everyone that Bell belonged to somebody before he became evidence. If a player stays, that player can learn Bell had kin expecting money before winter and a sister farther east who still writes as if Montana were something a man can endure a few years and then leave. Small facts like that make later lies heavier.

If the party treats the yard like a murder scene and not a prelude, help them. Mercer should not answer like a melodrama villain. He trims every sentence, turns the dead man meaner than he was, and tries to reduce murder to drink, debt, and bad company. Pike's men close ranks fast in the way work crews do when they smell one of their own may be selected for punishment whether guilty or not. Rowe will tolerate aggressive inquiry only so long as it still looks like discipline. Once it starts looking like civilians running the post, he hardens. That is good pressure. It gives the party room without giving them ownership of the fort.

### 4. Raid on the remuda

Before the killing is settled, riders hit the horse line in the dark.

Read or paraphrase:

> The first shot comes from beyond the line and the second comes from inside it. Then the horses go mad. A bay gelding tears loose dragging stake rope. A mule screams like a man. Somebody in the dark is shouting for a satchel and somebody else is shouting for God.

Use confusion. Use animals breaking loose. Use muzzle flashes at bad angles. Make it hard to tell whether the raiders are after horses, panic, or one particular saddlebag.

Put `6-8` mounted raiders in the dark, one man already inside the line cutting pickets, and enough panic in the animals to make horseflesh more dangerous than a single pistol. Their purpose is plain even if their employer is not. They mean to loose the remuda, cover the escape of the inside man, and seize one leather dispatch wallet if they can. The fight matters because lost horses can lame the whole expedition before it starts, because a captured rider can point toward hired interference rather than a simple field raid, and because Rowe will remember who held their head when the dark broke open around them. Afterward, let one dying raider carry something wrong in his pocket: a Gallatin store chit, Army carbine cartridges he should not have, or a marked silver dollar from Pike's wagon chest. It should start suspicion, not settle it.

What happens here should travel forward. If the remuda is badly scattered, the whole first half of the chapter grows slower, meaner, and more brittle. If the party captures a living raider, Vane and Pike both become nervous sooner. If the inside man escapes cleanly, Mercer grows bolder and Keene more frightened. If the party protects the dispatch wallet, later fights become more openly about route and paper rather than simple theft.

Expect players to do more here than trade shots in the dark. They may give chase instead of securing the line, fire the hay cart or wagon stack to make light, loose part of the remuda on purpose to deny the raiders a target, or drag a living attacker back for questioning before the blood dries. None of those choices should be wrong. They should simply cost something else. A hard pursuit may identify whose men these are and still leave the expedition short horses. A lit yard gives clearer fire and tells half the fort where the weak point lay. Saving the dispatch wallet may mean letting the inside man vanish. Interrogating a captured raider before dawn may produce one broken phrase, one false name, or one item in his pockets that matters more than anything he says.

If a raider is taken alive, do not rush past him. A living attacker is not only a source of facts. He is a problem men start arranging themselves around. Rowe wants order and a chain of custody. Duffy wants to know where the cut came from and whether the line can be hit again before daylight. Pike may want the man dead before sunrise for reasons he will never state cleanly. Vane wants to know whether the prisoner's memory is dangerous without ever seeming too interested in his comfort. The party can hand him over, keep him under their own watch, trade him later, or use his body as proof this was paid work rather than random field theft. A silent prisoner is still a powerful thing in camp.

**Combat Encounter: Raid on the Remuda**

- **Enemies:** `1 REMUDA CUTTER` inside the line, `4-6 HIRED RIDERS`, and `1-2 HIRED WHITE SABOTEURS` riding cover through the dark.

- **Area:** The fight runs across the horse yard at Fort Ellis: picket ropes, hitch rails, feed crates, wagon tongues, scattered tack, a hay cart, and too many animals packed too close together for calm work.

- **Terrain and Effects:** Darkness keeps sightlines uncertain until lanterns are raised or something catches light. Loose horses can knock men down, foul lines of fire, or force sudden `MOVE` and `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` tests. Stakes, cut ropes, and dragged pickets turn the ground treacherous at foot level. If the party lights the yard, attacks become easier to place but everyone standing in the glow becomes easier to hit in return.

By the time the party beds down, they should know which leaders were useful under pressure and which looked mean, frightened, or slippery. Hand out private impressions if you like. The character who watched the officers sees Rowe beginning to distrust Vane. The one who guarded paper notices Mercer trying to count packets twice. The one who held horses hears a raider call to the inside man in English. The one who searched the dead sees Bell had dirt under his nails as if he clawed for something small and hard.

## Leaving Settlement Space
### 5. Sorting the column

At first light the expedition leaves Fort Ellis.

Read or paraphrase:

> Morning comes pale and mean over the valley. The fort shrinks behind them by degrees: flag, roofs, corrals, then only smoke hanging in the still air. Ahead the road is nothing but wheel tracks, cut banks, and the promise that every mile west will make turning back a harder thing to explain.

Make the order of march matter. Front scouts, flankers, wagon guard, remuda detail, survey close guard, rear guard. Each place in the column gives a different first look when trouble comes. The road still feels half-civilized at the start. The valley is broad. Hay stands cut in places. The river shows bright in bends. Behind them lies pay, whiskey, a telegraph, and a surgeon. Ahead lies distance. Use the march itself. Front scouts see good country narrowing. Wagon guards hear Pike and his foreman at odds over load weight. Rear guard notices one saddle horse not properly branded. Close guard hears Reed and Vane sparring over note copies.

### 6. Thomas Keene

By noon the party catches Thomas Keene trying to slip away or finds him hidden in brush near a water stop.

If they corner him cleanly, give him a proper frightened voice:

> _"I never meant to carry the whole thing off,"_ Keene says. _"I only copied what Mercer told me to copy. Ask him. Ask Vane. Ask any of them if they'll own it in daylight."_

He is a junior survey hand with copied notes sewn into his vest lining. He swears he is not a thief. Then he admits he is a thief, but not the only one. Then he says Mercer made him do the copying. Then he says Vane wanted a private set.

All of that can be true in part.

Keene knows enough to die over and not enough to control any of it. Mercer has been copying notes for sale. Vane wants a route packet clean enough to trade privately. Bell saw too much. A second bundle of papers exists, or did. The party can turn Keene over to Rowe at once, hide him and hear him out, sell him to Vane or Mercer, or strip him of the papers and let him run. Mark the consequences plainly. Turning him in strengthens `Army Respect`. Keeping his copies improves `Survey Leverage`. Selling him like freight stains `Local Goodwill`. `Crow Trust` does not shift yet, though the papers they keep will touch it later. Keene should feel wretched rather than lively. He sweats through his shirt, talks too fast, and keeps trying to guess what kind of men the player characters are. Show him kindness and he clings. Show greed and he bargains. Show violence and he breaks.

Push these branches farther ahead than the immediate choice.

- If Keene goes to Rowe, the Army gains the first clean hold on the copying scandal, but Vane starts working through deniable hands sooner.
- If the party hides Keene, they gain better paper and better truth, but Mercer may try to run or betray them before the ford.
- If they sell Keene, he may return later under guard, wounded, or half willing to swear against them if that keeps him alive.
- If they let him run, some later evidence becomes weaker, but the possibility of Keene reappearing in Bozeman or Helena becomes stronger.

Parties also like forcing triangles into straight lines. They may drag Keene directly to Mercer and demand they face one another, or carry both men to Rowe and try to force a public hearing in camp. Let that happen if the players earn it. Mercer denies first, then trims, then folds. Keene accuses too much and too fast. Rowe hates the disorder but cannot ignore it if the papers are in hand. Such a confrontation should never settle every question. It should only make later betrayals more desperate, because the guilty now know the party is willing to bring darkness into the lamp light.

If the party handles Keene gently, let him tell one thing in full instead of ten things in fragments. He was first asked for neat copies, then cleaner copies, then one copy not to be mentioned, then one line altered in the name of clarity, then one night kept too long at the lamp while a better-dressed man said almost nothing and Mercer watched the door. That sort of telling is stronger than a blunt confession because it shows how corruption grows in a camp like this: not as one great leap into villainy, but as a slope of little obediences.

### 7. Calder Ranch

Toward evening the column reaches a small ranch run by Hannah Calder near a fork of usable country.

Read or paraphrase:

> The place appears by degrees. First a line of cut hay. Then the corral. Then a low cabin with smoke rising thin from the stovepipe. A woman stands by the trough with one hand on a bucket and watches the whole column come on as if she had seen trouble from farther off than this before.

The ranch is small and hard-kept: a low cabin, a half-leaning stable, three hay stacks, a bean patch, and a corral with more repair in it than lumber. Hannah Calder is not impressed by the Army, the railroad, or men from town with promises in their pockets. She lets the column water stock because refusal would only get her trampled by authority anyway. This halt matters because it brings in the view from below. Hannah knows what survey stakes call after them. Tax men, speculators, thieves. She knows Pike's freighters have already overcharged valley people for flour and nails. She knows Crow scouts sometimes warn before danger comes and white men almost never do. She knows three riders watched the place yesterday from a rise to the east. If the party treats her fairly, she becomes one of their strongest local allies. Respect wins more than talk from her: a dry loft to hide a man, one fresh team for a short haul, a local cut that avoids bad ground, and the truth about which Bozeman merchants buy up distress land cheap.

Remember Calder later according to how the party treats her now.

- Help her and she becomes a witness with no polish and no reason to lie.
- Use her place without respect and the party may find her door shut in the last act.
- Involve her in the Keene matter and she may become endangered later by whichever faction learns she helped.

If she decides to speak plainly, let her do it plainly:

> _"You put stakes in the ground,"_ Hannah says, _"and the rest follows behind. Tax men. Claim men. Men saying your creek was theirs on paper before you ever drank from it. I have seen the breed."_

> Historical note: Montana began officially recording livestock brands in `1873`. Even on the edge of settlement, ownership was being nailed down in ink, hide, and county memory.

Do not keep Hannah outside the life of the place. If the party enters the house, let them see the patched chair, the common crockery, the rifle cleaned beside kitchen tools, the ledger folded into a jar, the hand or dependent who makes the place feel lived in instead of symbolically poor. If she trusts them enough, she can show one plain page of accounts: nails bought high because Pike's people controlled the timing, flour bought damp because there was no second seller, team hire doubled after survey talk first passed through the valley. It is one of the cleanest ways in the chapter to show what all the Fort Ellis talk becomes at the level of a household.

### 8. The hay-yard fight

After dusk the ranch is hit.

Read or paraphrase:

> Night at the ranch is close and full of dry smells. Hay. Horse sweat. Lamp smoke. Then something strikes the barn wall hard enough to thump through the boards and Hannah is already moving for her carbine before the second shot comes.

Choose the attackers according to what you want the party suspecting at this stage. They may be hired white riders come to recover Keene or the copied notes. They may be Lakota-aligned raiders testing the escort and trying to stampede the animals. Either way the fight runs through hay stacks, corrals, and spooked stock. Hannah's barn can catch. A draft mule can break loose trailing chain. Mercer may try to flee with a satchel. Keene, if present, may bolt for the dark. By the end of the fight the party should understand three facts without needing them spelled out: somebody wants the expedition blind, somebody inside it wants private advantage, and the country ahead will not wait for them to sort the difference.

Before the expedition climbs farther into the country, count the damage. How many animals are lost or blown. Which NPC now blames the party. Whether Keene is still with the column. Whether Mercer still has free access to the papers. Those answers should change the mood at once. A column with its horse line cut apart should not sound like one that merely scraped through.

Some groups will try to leave Calder Ranch with more than water and a shaken night's sleep. They may hide a witness there, cache a packet in her loft, borrow stock without asking, or ask Hannah to ride as far as the next cut. Support that freedom and make it costly in the right way. A hidden witness can survive the field and matter later, but Hannah's place becomes a marked place afterward. Cached papers may survive the column's collapse, but the party risks returning to find Vane's men, Pike's riders, or simple fire got there first. Borrowed stock bought honestly becomes goodwill. Borrowed stock taken under color of force becomes a story that outruns the party before they ever reach Bozeman again.

If the party asks Hannah for testimony later, make the agreement sharp. She may well say yes, but not to being coached by a town lawyer or used as one more respectable face in somebody else's clean story. If she helps, she helps in her own words. That is worth more than polish. If the party betrays that trust, `Local Goodwill` should fall harder than if they had never asked.

**Combat Encounter: The Hay-Yard Fight**

- **Enemies:** Either `4-6 HIRED RIDERS` with `1 HIRED WHITE SABOTEUR`, or `4-6 LAKOTA RIDERS` with `1 LAKOTA WAR SCOUT`, depending on what pressure you want the party to read first.

- **Area:** The fight crosses Calder Ranch at night: hay stacks, corral rails, trough, barn wall, yard gate, cabin front, stacked tools, and narrow lanes between outbuildings where men can fire from close cover and disappear into shadow.

- **Terrain and Effects:** Dry hay burns fast if lanterns or gunfire catch it. Corrals and rails provide quick cover but can trap horses and people together. Spooked stock may break weak fencing and force `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` or `MOVE` checks to avoid being thrown down or shoved into the open. If a chain-dragging mule or panicked horse breaks loose, anyone crossing its path risks losing footing or cover.

## Arc II: Markers, Forks, and the Closing Country

This middle arc is where the line stops feeling like escort work and becomes a struggle over who gets to name the ground.

## The First Markers

### 9. The work itself

Give the survey its due on the page. Men who measure country are changing it.

Read or paraphrase:

> Reed's men carry chain, level, staffs, and notebooks up a rise above the valley. The work is slow and exact. A man plants a marker and steps back from it. Nothing dramatic happens. No thunder. No speech. Still, everyone looking on knows that a stake driven into open ground has a way of calling more stakes after it. Road. Title. Fence. Tax. Sheriff. Graveyard.

Let the party help or watch, but make the work itself matter. A character can ride perimeter, carry gear, read weather or ground, spot ambush country, or keep one eye on Vane and Mercer while pretending to be about other business. If they lean into the craft, reward them. They should come away understanding what Reed's men are actually doing: measuring grade so wagons and later engines can climb it, marking creek approaches that can bear heavy movement, noting timber stands for ties and bridge work, and counting forage because hungry stock ends any grand plan quick.

### 10. The route fork

The column reaches a point where two workable lines diverge.

Read or paraphrase:

> The two routes do not look like history when a man first sees them. One drops easier through the country and follows ground a freight wagon might bless. The other rises harder but truer. Reed studies both through narrowed eyes. Vane studies the men studying Reed.

One route offers easier freight, better ties to Bozeman and Gallatin interests, and more immediate settlement pressure. The other offers the better grade over time, stronger military logic, and deeper intrusion into contested hunting country. Vane presses hard for one. Reed hates being pressed. Rowe wants whatever least resembles a massacre. Pike wants the line that keeps his present choke points profitable as long as possible. The party can back Reed's honest assessment, alter marker or note to tilt the outcome, expose Vane in front of Rowe, or say nothing and keep what they know for sale later. Mark the consequences in the ledger. Honest support for the safer course improves `Army Respect`. A hidden manipulation improves `Survey Leverage`. Choosing the route that buys small valley people a little more time helps `Local Goodwill`. Pushing farther without hearing warning first should cost `Crow Trust`. Keep it concrete. One line passes country where small ranches and hay claims may live another year or two. The other brings military speed and commercial force at once.

This choice should echo through the rest of the chapter.

- If the party backs Reed openly, Rowe trusts them more but Vane begins treating them as an obstacle.
- If they alter the route in secret, they gain leverage but risk later exposure if Reed catches the fraud.
- If they expose Vane too early, Pike may step forward as the smoother liar while Vane turns openly hostile.
- If they keep silent, they hold saleable knowledge later, but every later wound along the chosen line belongs partly to that silence.

Do not underestimate how destructive players can be at a fork. They may pull stakes, mismark a line, hide a notebook page, threaten the instrument crew into delaying the measure, or argue that the expedition should split and test both routes at once. Let them. A split survey creates a stronger sandbox inside the chapter for a session or two, but it also puts one weaker group out where hired riders or Iron Hail can hit it. A falsified marker may buy time for locals and create a future scandal when the ground and the paper no longer match. A refusal to choose at all should not freeze the scene. Reed makes his best technical call, Rowe makes his best military one, and the party has then chosen silence as surely as if it had backed a liar.

Make the ground itself argue the case. One line should feel like freight, speed, and appetite: easier pull, more immediate use, faster pressure on every hay claim and creek-bottom holding downstream of the decision. The other should feel harder, cleaner, and more openly military: worse temper in the short run, stronger grade in the long run, deeper insult to country already tense with Crow and Lakota attention. If the party is to split here, it needs more than labels. It needs two visible futures laid in front of it.

This is one of the best places for the party itself to crack by motive. One player may prefer the route that makes money faster because he sees the territory as already sliding that way. Another may back Reed because lying to the ground kills men later. Another may want the line that buys small ranchers another year. Another may decide no line should leave this fork at all. Support all of that. NPCs should notice the split immediately and begin courting it.

> Historical note: The old Bozeman Trail had already shown what happened when a road was treated as a mere line on a map. In the northern Plains, wagon routes had a way of becoming military roads whether the first surveyors admitted it or not.

### 11. Attack on the marker line

The strike comes while the crew is spread out.

This is the clean field fight of the chapter. Open ground. Survey men exposed. Horses not all saddled. Markers standing plain as the thing being fought over. Iron Hail's riders do not need to stay and die. They need to break instruments, kill confidence, and seize or burn notebooks. Keep the pressure moving. Riders shoot from partial cover. One pair circles for the wagon carrying spare stakes and paper. Another rushes close on Reed's position. The party's victory is measured in what survives: Reed alive, at least one notebook set intact, instruments not smashed or carried off. If they fail badly, the expedition keeps moving on panic and guesswork.

If Reed is wounded here, the party may need to protect his field books while he dictates corrections through fever or pain. That turns the middle of the chapter harder and gives paper-keeping characters something real to do.

If the players try to outthink the ambush rather than absorb it, reward that too. They may post false markers, send decoy riders with empty cases, keep Reed out of sight, or bait the attackers into charging the wrong wagon. Those plans should never nullify the attack, but they can change what the enemy reaches first. The point is not that Iron Hail's riders spring from nowhere because the text says they must. The point is that the country is already full of eyes, and good player caution can move the blow from one set of hands to another.

If the party captures one of Iron Hail's riders here, resist the lazy habit of turning the prisoner into a clue box that speaks perfect campaign logic. He may say little. He may say nothing. He may refuse the party completely and still change the chapter by existing. A live captive slows the column, sharpens Rowe's military instinct, angers frightened civilians, and forces the party to decide whether they mean to keep a prisoner, exchange one, release one, or kill one. Each of those choices should stain later talk in a different way.

**Combat Encounter: Attack on the Marker Line**

- **Enemies:** `1 IRON HAIL`, `1-2 LAKOTA WAR SCOUTS`, and `5-7 LAKOTA RIDERS`.

- **Area:** This is open survey ground broken by low rises, brush pockets, marker stakes, survey chains, instrument cases, and one wagon carrying spare stakes, paper, and fragile gear. The crew is too spread out for comfort when the attack lands.

- **Terrain and Effects:** The field favors movement and angle over static exchange. Low rises and brush provide partial cover, but exposure punishes anyone who stands too long in one place. Stakes, chains, and tripods can foul hurried movement. Protecting Reed, the notebooks, and the instruments at once should be physically difficult. If the party prepared decoys or false marker lines, let those redirect the first rush or spoil enemy accuracy rather than cancel the attack outright.

### 12. Camp after the attack

That night the camp divides.

Rowe wants tighter discipline.

Reed wants the work continued before delay kills the mission.

Vane says the attack proves the line is important and therefore worth pushing.

Pike says the outfit cannot move if animals and hired hands keep dying.

Let the players move among the fires because this is where private bargains begin in earnest. Vane offers money for copies of any notes they control. Reed asks them to watch Mercer. Rowe asks them to keep an unofficial eye on Vane. Pike hints that a man who helps the right route can do very well later. Keep the scene tired and ugly rather than theatrical. Men eat with dirty hands. Somebody is crying quietly beyond the firelight. One mule has to be put down. Reed keeps writing because stopping would mean admitting the work may already be lost.

If Vane presses a player in private, make the line easy to read:

> _"Every man here is drawing pay for one purpose and serving another,"_ Vane says. _"You need not look shocked. I only ask whether you mean to be poor at both."_

Use this scene to seed Vane's later betrayal offer. He should notice one player who counts faster than the others, one who takes insult poorly, or one who already dislikes taking clipped orders from Rowe. He does not yet have to lay out the whole bargain. Recognition is enough to begin with. He tells the chosen player that some men are built to obey the event and some to own the account after it. That sentence can do a great deal of damage if it lands on the right conscience.

If the table wants intrigue to breathe before the next field movement, run a night of separate fires here. The camp no longer feels like one body. Rowe and Duffy hold one fire together by habit and exhaustion. Reed works under a lantern at another, writing like motion of the pen might still keep the expedition from collapsing into rumor. Pike's men make a harder circle around a lower fire and talk in the language of losses, loads, and whether they are still being paid enough to die here. Vane moves between them, never sitting long. Let the party divide itself by curiosity or loyalty. It is a strong scene because mistrust can begin without a shot being fired.

## The Country Closes In

### 13. Tracks and shadowing

Morning brings signs that the expedition is being worked from outside.

Use `HAWKEYE`, `NATURE`, and `INSIGHT` and let the ground answer. There are decoy fire sites, one rider dragging a branch to blur numbers, pony sign showing the shadowing party split in the night, and evidence that somebody from camp walked out to meet them. If the party pushes this hard enough, Mercer begins to crack. He should not do it all at once. First he lies clean. Then he lies badly. Then he admits copying pages but denies murder. At last he names Vane or Pike in whatever order best saves his skin.

### 14. The Crow warning

Shows-the-Ford approaches under a flag of caution or by way of Baptiste LaRoche.

Read or paraphrase:

> He is already there when they notice him. Horse quiet. Seat easy. No fuss in the man at all. Only the fact that he has come and stopped where the line must either hear him or insult him.

He is not there to save white men from their own mistakes for nothing. He says the country ahead is wrong for a heavy line right now. He can guide them toward a safer crossing and a day of better ground, but he wants something in return.

Pick a price that fits your table and the temper of the chapter. He may want trade goods being held under Army claim released. He may want medicine or powder. He may want a stretch of country left unmarked. He may want papers returned that name Crow use-country in false or insulting terms. However it is framed, the party can trust him and alter the route, report the matter openly to Rowe, strike a private bargain and keep the officers half-blind, or refuse and ride on. Honest treatment strengthens `Crow Trust`. Handling the matter openly and well strengthens `Army Respect`. Leaving valuable ground unsurveyed for peace should cost `Survey Leverage`. A choice that reduces immediate bloodshed helps `Local Goodwill`. Shows-the-Ford is not there to hand out a task. He is weighing them. He watches who answers first, who lets an officer speak over everyone else, who understands that trade goods and insult both carry weight, and whether LaRoche is treated as a man or only as a mouth to speak through.

Carry the answer forward plainly.

- Trusting Shows-the-Ford may spare blood at the ford and open one later line of aid.
- Reporting him honestly may strengthen Rowe if Rowe handles it well, or poison the whole contact if he does not.
- Bargaining privately gives the party its own channel into the country, but if discovered it can damage both `Army Respect` and later trust.
- Refusal should not produce an immediate lecture. It should produce harder ground, less warning, and colder faces later on.

If he speaks at length, keep it spare:

> _"You mark too hard and too fast,"_ Shows-the-Ford says. _"Then you act surprised when men come to break the marks. That is white-man foolishness. I did not ride here to admire it."_

This is another place where players may try something uglier than the chapter's polite version. They may demand a hostage exchange, insist that only they speak and not Rowe, ask Shows-the-Ford for proof he is not guiding them into a kill pocket, or try to strike one private bargain while telling the Army another. Let every one of those choices breathe. A demand for proof may be met with proof. It may also be met with insult and departure. A double bargain may work for one day and poison three later scenes. If the party tries to seize Shows-the-Ford or use force at parley, the expedition should pay for that stupidity in silence, bad ground, and the closing of every easy path afterward.

Players may also bring their own offers instead of merely answering his. They may promise a delayed return of papers, future warning when survey men ride again, safe passage for a specific wounded man, or the destruction of one insulting page in exchange for help now. Those promises matter. If made lightly, they become some of the best later hooks in the whole Montana book. If made honestly, they should buy real help and real memory.

If you want the cleanest and most dangerous private bargain in the chapter, make it here. Through LaRoche or in a quieter second exchange, Shows-the-Ford can speak to the player most likely to understand consequence better than rank. He offers no money. He offers memory, safer ground, and a later hand extended in Crow country if one act is done now: one page returned or burned, one stretch left unmarked, one warning sent if survey men ride again. This should be able to split a party worse than Vane's money precisely because it is not dirty in the same way. One player can defend taking it as justice. Another can condemn it as betrayal. Both may speak honestly.

> Historical note: In southern Montana, Crow, Lakota, and other Native leaders were not scenery around white travel. They were strategic actors deciding where roads, surveys, and armed movement would be endured, diverted, or fought.

### 15. The ford fight

Whether by warning or by stubbornness, the expedition reaches a crossing under pressure.

Read or paraphrase:

> The river is not broad, only ugly. Brown current presses around the wheels. The near bank is churned to slick clay. Men are already wet to the thigh and angry about it. On the far side the cottonwoods stand still as witnesses.

This fight must feel different from the marker-line attack because here the ground fights too. Slick banks, half-loaded wagons, men in water to their knees, balking animals, powder and papers close to soaking ruin. Iron Hail strikes at the moment of worst order. The party may be trying to get the survey wagon across, cut it free and save only the paper chest, keep Hannah Calder's borrowed team alive, or decide whether to hold the far bank or retreat back in disorder. If Shows-the-Ford was trusted, give the party one clear edge: a concealed shallow line, warning of the ambush point, or time to prepare cover. If he was dismissed or betrayed, the crossing turns meaner and Crow aid is absent. Good complications here are physical, not clever. A chest bursts and papers slap downstream. A wounded trooper pins a mule under him. One frightened civilian clings to the wrong wagon and blocks the ford. Harlan tries something brave and stupid on horseback.

**Combat Encounter: The Ford Fight**

- **Enemies:** `1 IRON HAIL`, `1 LAKOTA WAR SCOUT`, and `4-6 LAKOTA RIDERS`. If the corruption thread is biting hard, add `1 HIRED WHITE SABOTEUR` on the near bank trying to seize packets during the confusion.

- **Area:** The fight centers on an ugly river crossing with slick mud on the near bank, current strong enough to foul footing, half-loaded wagons in the water, cottonwoods and brush on the far bank, and paper crates or survey chests at risk of soaking open.

- **Terrain and Effects:** Water is the main enemy. Movement in the current should feel slower, less certain, and easier to punish. Muddy banks can turn simple repositioning into a `MOVE` problem. Dropped gear risks being soaked, carried off, or trampled under frightened stock. Wagons can become cover, obstacles, or traps depending on where they stall. If Shows-the-Ford was trusted, one better crossing line, warning angle, or prepared cover point should visibly reduce the worst of the surprise.

### 16. What the dead man carried

After the ford fight, Mercer is found dead, wounded, or missing.

If dead, he has been shot with a weapon not used by the party.

If alive, he is half out of his head and sure that Vane meant to leave him to die.

On or near him the party finds enough to change the shape of the story: a partial duplicate of Reed's field notes, account slips showing Pike overbilling the Army, private notations on the route fork, and initials that finally link Bell's killing, Keene's fear, and Mercer's copying. This is the hinge that opens the last movement of the chapter.

By now the expedition should feel hollowed out. The wounded need carrying. The animals are failing. The officers no longer speak with one voice. Every surviving packet matters. Make camp quieter after the ford. A wet fire and tired men do more for the scene than one more speech.

## Arc III: Broken Column

Now the expedition stops being one thing. It becomes several frightened groups around a few packets of paper.

## The Split Papers

### 17. Camp of accusations

No one trusts anyone now.

Read or paraphrase:

> The camp has gone mean. No songs. No cards. Men sit with blankets over their shoulders and watch one another over tin cups. Every packet of paper looks heavy enough to sink a horse.

Rowe wants the papers brought under military guard.

Reed wants his working set left intact and away from meddlers.

Vane wants a private meeting and offers astonishing money for one duplicate.

Pike wants the overbilling slips gone forever.

Keene, if still alive, wants protection or a fast horse.

This is a full roleplay session opener. Let the party search trunks, confront liars, and decide who gets what packet.

This is where the party should begin asking itself harder questions. Which lie matters most: murder, fraud, or the route itself. Are they trying to leave Montana richer, cleaner, or merely alive. Which of these men becomes most dangerous if he comes out ahead.

Players rarely stop at conversation once packets are visible. They may try to disarm Vane, search Pike's wagons under force, place Mercer under their own watch, forge Rowe's authority in the night, or tell Reed only half of what they hold so they can measure which part he cares about most. Good. This scene should accept greed, panic, and overreach. An armed search may turn up enough fraud to justify itself and still push Pike into violence by midnight. A false order may buy an hour and cost the party forever with the Army if discovered. Telling only half the truth should sharpen later loyalties, because every surviving NPC begins to understand the party is now its own faction.

This is where Rowe should make his private move. He takes aside the player he has judged most steady, not necessarily the most lawful. He speaks like a man too tired to posture and too proud to call what he is doing desperation. He says the camp has crossed beyond ordinary command. He says he needs one person he can trust when rank is no longer enough. He offers legal shelter if the chapter ends ugly, future Army work, and the right to act unofficially under his word if the night breaks open. What he asks in return is not simple obedience. He wants truth. About Vane. About Mercer if Mercer yet lives. About what the rest of the party is hiding. The danger is that a player can accept all of this and still believe he is the only one acting honorably.

Vane's full offer belongs in the same stretch, but to a different temperament. He approaches the player most likely to prefer position over innocence, or survival over official cleanliness. He offers money now, protection later, introductions in Bozeman or Helena, and a simple arrangement: let one packet move out of official custody before dawn, or at least give warning before it does. He does not ask for theatrical treason. He asks for selection. One copy. One warning. One honest answer about which party member can be bought and which cannot. The best thing about this offer is that a player can defend taking it without ever thinking himself the villain of the chapter.

If Rowe snaps in front of them, give him one hard line:

> _"I will not carry private thieves back under Army guard,"_ Rowe says. _"If there is rot here, name it now."_

### 18. The copied packet

By now the party should control, or have access to, one important packet.

It may hold route measurements, corrected crossing notes, Pike's false charges, proof of Mercer's copying, proof that Vane tried to steer the line for private gain, or some mixed bundle of all of them. The party must decide what to do with it before the night ends. They can surrender everything to Rowe and force clean custody, give Reed only the survey matter and hold the corruption back, promise or sell one copy to Vane while keeping another, or burn the most dangerous pages and carry only what serves survival. Mark the consequences. Full surrender helps `Army Respect`. Keeping or duplicating key route paper greatly strengthens `Survey Leverage`. Preserving Pike's fraud for later exposure helps `Local Goodwill`. Withholding offensive or dangerous survey pages from the line helps `Crow Trust`. If the party tries to make extra copies, make the work ugly. The light is poor. Hands shake after the ford fight. Mercer, if alive, only helps at a price. Reed can spot a bad copy. Time is exactly what the camp no longer has.

Push the outcomes into the last act.

- Full surrender makes the final reckoning cleaner, but the party may lose practical control of its own best leverage.
- Splitting the packet between Rowe and Reed may preserve truth while keeping part of the party's hand hidden.
- Promising a copy to Vane buys short-term safety and long-term danger.
- Burning pages may save lives in the moment and still leave the party haunted by what it can no longer prove.

The party may also do the thing many adventures quietly fear. It may refuse every named claimant and create its own custody. If they sew the papers into saddles, divide them between riders, copy them by firelight into three imperfect sets, or hide one bundle under a dead man's blanket, let that ingenuity matter. Imperfect copies create later arguments over authenticity. Split custody makes betrayal harder and survival messier. Private custody turns the whole final movement into a contest over the players themselves rather than over whichever NPC happened to hold the packet last. That is not a broken chapter. That is often the best version of it.

Some parties will go further and try to weaponize uncertainty itself. They may tell Rowe one packet exists, tell Vane there are two, hint to Pike that the overbilling slips were burned, and let all three men move against shadows for half a night. This should work, to a point. Men under pressure make ugly guesses. The cost is that once the lies collapse, nobody with a long memory will again mistake the party for neutral hands.

Make the packet feel physical here. Wet fingers. Bad lamplight. One copied page neat enough to fool a man at a glance and false in the one line that matters. One blood mark where somebody grabbed the wrong paper before dying. If the papers stay abstract, the scene shrinks. If they feel like future lawsuits, graves, fortunes, and denials folded into damp leather, the whole chapter grows heavier in the hands.

### 19. Midnight in broken camp

Someone makes the first move after dark.

Choose the version that grows naturally out of the party's decisions. Vane's hired men may come for the packet. Pike's freight toughs may decide witnesses are too expensive to leave breathing. Iron Hail's riders may strike while the camp is already split against itself. Or all three pressures may collide in one bad dark hour. Run it close and ugly among tents, mule lines, lantern light, and men shooting too near canvas and powder while the wounded cry out and draw more fire. The stakes are simple: who rides away with the best copy, whether Rowe survives with authority intact, and whether Harlan finally proves his courage or his foolishness.

Read or paraphrase:

> The camp breaks open all at once. One lantern goes over. Then another. A mule jerks back against its tether. Somewhere close a man shouts _"Down!"_ and gets a bullet for the warning.

At the end of the fight the column cannot continue the mission as planned.

If Vane survives the midnight fight, decide whether he leaves as a hunted civilian or as a man still holding enough influence to strike bargains at the end. Either version works. What should not happen is for him to turn into a simple bandit chief. He is a paper man. Keep him one.

**Combat Encounter: Midnight in Broken Camp**

- **Enemies:** Build from the party's earlier choices, but keep the shape legible. Use `2-4 FREIGHT TOUGHS` and `2-4 HIRED RIDERS` if Pike and Vane move first, `4-6 LAKOTA RIDERS` if outside pressure lands during the split, or mix both with `1 HIRED WHITE SABOTEUR` if you want total camp collapse.

- **Area:** This fight erupts in a divided night camp full of tents, mule lines, bedrolls, wounded men, lanterns, paper packets, powder, and too many bodies trying to move through the same small spaces at once.

- **Terrain and Effects:** Lantern light creates islands of visibility with deep shadow between them. Canvas, bedrolls, and stacked gear conceal movement but do not stop bullets. Tent ropes, tethers, and camp clutter make charges and retreats hazardous. Panic among noncombatants can block lines, spoil shots, or force fast `PRESENCE`, `MOVE`, or `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` responses. If fire catches, smoke and light both worsen the fight fast.

### 20. Breaking point

Morning settles nothing.

Reed says the line is finished if the field books do not reach settlement.

Rowe says discipline has failed and the living come first.

Vane may be dead, fled, or still pressing for control depending on the last fight.

Pike openly talks of saving freight over men unless checked.

The party now has to choose the shape of the retreat. Fast and light with the most valuable papers. Slow and organized with the wounded. Divided into two groups, one decoy and one carrier. None of these plans should be clean. If they choose speed, someone heavy and slow pays for it. If they choose order, the pursuers close. If they divide, both halves grow vulnerable at once.

Expect bolder plans here than the obvious three. Players may bury a tin cylinder of copies and mark the spot in code, send the least suspicious NPC ahead alone, dress Keene or Mercer in another man's coat to draw fire, or put false papers where Pike, Vane, or the Lakota riders are most likely to seize them. Support stratagem. A false packet can buy distance. It can also create a later scandal when the wrong reader thinks he has won. A buried packet may become the seed of a later Montana job all by itself. A disguised courier may survive because nobody notices him, or die because the wrong man notices exactly enough.

Before the retreat actually starts, make room for one last ugly human choice. A wounded man may ask not to be left with morphine and one cartridge like some polite arrangement with the dark. A civilian may care more for his own valise than for the packet because the valise holds his whole previous life in shirts, letters, and papers no one else values. Another may swear he can still ride when every eye in camp says otherwise. These scenes are not delays. They are the retreat becoming personal enough to matter once the shooting stops.

## The Retreat

### 21. What do you save

Before the column withdraws, make the players choose plainly.

They do not have the strength to save everything.

Put four claims plainly in front of them: Reed's master field book, the corruption papers on Vane, Mercer, and Pike, wounded men who slow the retreat, and valuable instruments with a camera or plate box if such gear is in play. Whatever they choose, something else is left. This is the moral center of the chapter and it should stay hard. Saving the wounded helps `Army Respect` but weakens `Survey Leverage`. Saving the papers strengthens `Survey Leverage` but can stain `Local Goodwill` if abandoned men live to be remembered. Saving the instruments wins Reed's gratitude while soldiers curse the choice. Splitting up can work, but any success grows fragile and casualties rise easily.

If the group freezes here, put Captain Rowe's question on the table:

> _"I am done pretending we can save all of it. Name the load. Name the men. We move on your word or mine."_

### 22. Running the line backward

The retreat leg should feel hunted.

The retreat should feel hunted. Riders pace the column at long distance. One wagon breaks an axle at the worst hour. A messenger loses his horse under him. Clouds bank up and weather turns. Let the land choose some of the trouble too. Alkali water makes men sick. A ravine proves too steep for the loaded cart. Buffalo sign offers meat if the party dares the delay. Storm light hides movement until it is close. This is a good stretch for chained practical tests: `LABOR` to get a wagon moving, `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` to calm a crazed team, `NATURE` to choose a defensible night stop, `MOVE` to get scouts into place before dusk.

Do not be afraid to interrupt the retreat with small human decisions instead of one more running fight. A wounded man may beg not to be left with a morphine dose and one cartridge. A civilian may insist on carrying his own valise instead of the packet that matters. A trooper may ask whether the party means to hand the whole business to Rowe at the end or whether he should think now about where his own future lies. These are not delays. They are the retreat becoming personal enough to matter after the dust settles.

### 23. Rear-guard action

The enemy comes hard once, not in dribbles.

This fight should feel like a retreat being paid for in men and time.

Choose a battlefield that looks improvised and desperate: a low ridge with broken rock, a wagon-tongue barricade, a creek bend with cottonwoods, or a half-built breastwork made from flour sacks and survey crates. If you want a larger, uglier set-piece, mix the threats. Iron Hail's riders press from the flanks while one or two white gunmen cut in for the papers during the confusion. This is the right place for hard hero moments. Duffy holds a line long enough for the wagon to clear. Harlan volunteers for a foolish charge and must be saved or abandoned. Baptiste leads mules through a near-impossible cut if the party trusts him. Shows-the-Ford appears with warning fire or does not, depending on how the party treated him earlier.

End this movement with the column alive, shattered, and close enough to settlement that politics can kill what the field did not.

Before the final reckoning, settle the list. Who is dead. Who is wounded. Which papers survived. Which are false, altered, or incomplete. Who believes the party owes them silence. Bring those answers into town with the riders. Montana remembers who came back and who did not.

**Combat Encounter: Rear-Guard Action**

- **Enemies:** `1 LAKOTA WAR SCOUT`, `4-6 LAKOTA RIDERS`, and, if the corruption line still bites, `1-2 HIRED WHITE SABOTEURS` or `HIRED RIDERS` cutting in for the packet during the confusion.

- **Area:** Pick one desperate battlefield and make it concrete: a low broken ridge, creek bend under cottonwoods, wagon-tongue barricade, or rough breastwork built from flour sacks, survey crates, and smashed gear while the rest of the column struggles behind it.

- **Terrain and Effects:** The ground should feel improvised and defensive. Rock, creek banks, wagon wreckage, or rough barricades provide uneven cover and broken sightlines. The retreat behind the line matters; every turn bought should mean distance gained for wounded, stock, or papers. Loose freight, shattered crates, and uneven barriers can slow charges and spoil clear lanes of fire. If Shows-the-Ford aids the party, one warning angle, withdrawal line, or concealed path should visibly change the battle.

## Reckoning

The reckoning belongs in and around Fort Ellis or Bozeman. There should be boots, paper, witnesses, lies, and the feeling that men are now trying to do with offices what they failed to do with carbines.

### 24. Who receives the papers

When the party returns, everyone wants first possession. Rowe wants official custody. Reed wants technical control. Vane's allies, if he is gone, want quiet access. Pike wants the accounts destroyed. Bozeman interests want to know which route won and whether there is profit in it. The first reading matters more than one more fight in the grass because the first reader gets to decide which packet becomes official, which missing pages are called regrettable loss, which dead men are named honest and which are called thieves, and whether the route becomes fact, rumor, or commodity.

Read or paraphrase:

> The room smells of wet wool, stove heat, and paper drying too close to flame. Men who would not share a fire in the field now stand elbow to elbow waiting to see whose hand touches the packet first.

### 25. Local pressure

Hannah Calder, a Bozeman merchant, a newspaper man, or a territorial lawyer may appear depending on the party's earlier friends.

This is where `Local Goodwill` cashes in. High goodwill means a safe room, honest witnesses, food, horse care, and word running ahead of the party in their favor. Low goodwill means closed doors, bad rumors, and witnesses bought off before sundown. If the party treated Hannah Calder well, she can send one plainspoken letter or appear in person. A widow with no reason to lie can cut through a room full of polished men.

Do not assume the party brings the papers first to the proper desk. They may go to a newspaper office, a lawyer, a banker, a church safe, Calder's people, a telegraph operator, or one locked room above a saloon where witnesses can be sorted before names go public. That freedom is the whole point of the reckoning. The first room they choose should decide what kind of Montana story this becomes. Official room means chain of custody and uniforms. Editor's room means public scandal and libel risk. Bank room means leverage, delay, and bought silence. Private ranch or boarding room means one more night of knives before daylight makes the matter legal.

Take time over the first room. Barracks smell of wet wool, stove heat, and authority trying to reassemble itself. A newspaper office smells of lampblack, dust, and men who know scandal has a price by the column. A banker's back room smells of ledgers, cigar ash, and the kind of silence money learns to buy before courts ever hear a thing. A church office offers moral shape and very little real protection. A boarding room gives privacy at the price of fragility, since boarding-house doors were made for manners rather than defense. The place chosen should tell the reader what kind of ending the party believes in.

If the party splits the evidence across institutions, let the repercussions multiply rather than simplify. One packet in Army hands may create legal form. One packet in an editor's room may keep men from disappearing quietly. One sheet in a lawyer's office may ripen into a case months later. One copy in a bank vault may become blackmail, investment, or murder. The reckoning is strongest when three doors close in three different parts of town and three different men realize too late that none of them now controls the whole story.

> Historical note: Montana in the `1860s` had already built a reputation for vigilante hangings and rough justice. By `1873`, law existed on paper, but many people still trusted armed reputation, local alliances, and the first story into town.

### 26. Final standoff

The finale should fit the accumulated choices.

Choose the ending that fits what the party has made. Rowe may order Vane or Pike seized and force a fight in a yard, stable lane, or storehouse row. Evidence may spill into Bozeman and bring armed friends into the street. Vane or Pike may offer one last settlement of cash, silence, patronage, and buried facts. Or one enemy may try to settle everything with a quiet knife behind a warehouse rather than a public fight. The strongest ending usually forces a choice between lawful custody and practical control. One feels cleaner. The other is often safer. In Montana those are rarely the same thing.

Use the party's earlier choices to decide which ending is most honest.

- Strong `Army Respect` and full paper custody lean toward arrest, official seizure, and a more public reckoning.
- Strong `Survey Leverage` and hidden copies lean toward bargain, blackmail, or private violence.
- Strong `Local Goodwill` leans toward a room full of witnesses who will not all be bought.
- Broken trust and buried evidence lean toward knife work, vanished packets, and a fugitive ending into the sandbox.

Players may also refuse to let the chapter resolve in one place at all. They may split the evidence between institutions, publish one part, sell another, and keep the bloodiest truth for themselves. They may put Pike in a courtroom shape and Vane in a graveyard shape. They may spare a guilty man because his survival opens a larger Montana road later. Do not pull against that instinct. This ending is strongest when the party leaves it having made enemies on purpose.

One more option belongs here if the party has played a colder game: they may decide the best ending is not justice, profit, or testimony, but control. They may keep enough evidence to govern Vane, Pike, Reed, or Rowe in future chapters without destroying any of them today. That is a dark Montana ending and a strong one. It opens the sandbox under a burden of favors owed, threats remembered, and one quiet packet nobody can afford to forget.

If you want one last turn of the knife, add a final private approach after the matter appears settled. Vane, if alive, may send one line asking for a meeting no decent man should grant. Rowe may ask whether one player's unofficial loyalty still holds now that the field is behind them. Calder may send word that strangers have begun asking after what was left in her loft. Shows-the-Ford may simply have a message waiting with LaRoche: remembered. These are not epilogues. They are the first true roads out into the sandbox.

**Combat Encounter: Final Standoff in Settlement Space**

- **Enemies:** Match the ending the party built. A lawful seizure may mean `1 GIDEON PIKE` with `2-4 FREIGHT TOUGHS`, or `1 SILAS VANE` protected by `2-3 HIRED RIDERS` and `1 HIRED WHITE SABOTEUR`. A wider collapse may add armed townsmen or rattled soldiers as moving complications rather than clean enemy lines.

- **Area:** Run the final violence in a place that fits the reckoning: stable lane, storehouse row, freight yard, boarding-house stair, editor's office, or alley behind a banker or lawyer's rooms. Settlement space should feel tighter, more public, and more dangerous for what it means afterward than for the raw body count.

- **Terrain and Effects:** This is not open-country fighting. Fences, porches, stacked freight, muddy alleys, stairwells, hitch rails, and windows all matter. Witnesses complicate every shot. Property damage, public visibility, and the nearness of law should weigh on decisions. `PRESENCE`, `INSIGHT`, and intimidation may matter as much as `SHOOTIN'` if the party tries to force surrender before the whole street chooses a side.

### 27. End states

#### Army victory

The packet goes into official hands. Rowe survives with reputation mostly intact. The party becomes known to the Army as useful people who can be trusted with ugly work.

#### Railroad shadow victory

The party keeps or sells a copy. No one outside a small circle knows the whole truth. They leave richer, less clean, and more important than before.

#### Local Montana victory

The route corruption and freight fraud become known. Small local interests win a temporary breathing space. The party gains hard friends among ranchers, freighters, editors, and town men.

#### Crow understanding

The party preserved trust, limited intrusion, or returned offensive papers. That does not bring peace, but it changes who will talk to them later in the southern country.

#### Bad ending, good sandbox

The truth is buried, a liar prospers, and the party has to leave fast with only what proof they could hide under a coat. This is still a strong sandbox opening. Montana is full of unfinished business.

## Pacing the Expedition

The broad rhythm is straightforward. The opening should spend time on arrival, hiring, supper politics, Bell's killing, and the first night raid. The middle should breathe long enough for the party to know the people, the stock, the country, and the work before the route fight and the ford fight begin tearing the expedition apart. The last movement should feel faster and meaner: accusations in camp, broken packets, retreat hardship, one hard rear-guard action, then the ride back into boots, paper, witnesses, and lies. However long your table likes to take over a thing, do not let the opening pass without blood and suspicion, and do not let the ending arrive before the party has had to decide what is worth carrying out.

## Running the Major Combats

Each big fight here should feel different. If they all turn into men standing in open ground rolling SHOOTIN', the chapter flattens. The remuda raid should feel confused, all loose horses, darkness, crossed lines of fire, and men shouting names they cannot place. The hay-yard fight should feel cramped and civilian, with burnable hay, narrow lanes between outbuildings, and noncombatants who matter. The marker-line attack should be all exposure and distance, with the party forced to choose between men, instruments, and paper. The ford fight should belong as much to bad footing and current as to the riders attacking it. Midnight in broken camp should feel treacherous and too close, all lantern light, tent ropes, and gunfire near powder. The rear-guard action should feel like time being bought with blood. The last showdown in settlement space should feel dangerous in a different way because there are witnesses, law nearby, and one last chance to stop before the whole town chooses sides.

## Reward, Reputation, and What the Party Carries Out

Do not pay the party only in cash. Cash matters, and Rowe or Pike should pay what they can if the party lives long enough to collect, but the true rewards are rougher than coin. A party may leave this chapter with Rowe's recommendation, Duffy's respect, Reed's technical trust, a copied route packet worth money in Helena or Bozeman, Calder's word among small valley people, Crow warning or passage, or a list of names tied to fraud, bribery, and murder. They should also leave carrying damage. A lame horse. A winter cough from the river crossing. Bad press in Bozeman. One faction convinced they stole what was rightfully theirs. A dead friend whose kin now know the party's names. The best Montana rewards come with dirt on them.

## Carrying Chapter 02 into the Sandbox

When this chapter closes, the party should have at least three living leads into the wider book. One may come from the Army, with Rowe or Duffy sending for them when a court matter, escort job, or field problem turns ugly. One may come from paper, with Reed, Vane, or a Helena banker wanting the surviving notes, originals, or copies. One may come from local Montana, with Calder, Bozeman merchants, or Gallatin ranchers wanting help against land, freight, or stock pressure. One may come from Crow country, with Shows-the-Ford offering warning, work, or one hard chance to prove earlier trust was not wasted. One may come from an enemy who lived. That is the proper ending here. Nobody is clean. Nobody is done. The country is still there when the smoke thins.

## Running the Dilemmas

The dilemmas work best when they are not hidden behind one skill roll. Let rolls change position, timing, and leverage, but let the choice itself remain with the players. They should know enough to understand each road before choosing it. Every road should cost something. No branch should glow like the obvious moral answer written in brighter ink. Earlier choices should pay off later in ways the table can feel.

## Travel, Pace, and Weather

Use the country as pressure, not wallpaper. Dust should foul tempers and paper. Cold nights should turn wounded men worse by morning. Rivers should decide timing. Horseflesh should set the real pace. Smoke should be visible far enough off to betray the column. If the action needs slowing, do it with broken harness, bad water, lame stock, or an officer insisting on one more mile after dark.

## If the Party Goes Off the Line

The party may try to quit the expedition early. Do not forbid it. Make it costly and playable. Desert with nothing and they enter the sandbox poor and suspected. Desert with papers and the chapter turns into a flight story at once, with the appendix matters becoming immediately useful. Stay near Calder Ranch or vanish into Bozeman and Vane's men, Pike's contractors, and Army warrants should drive the next scenes. The point is not to force obedience. The point is to make the line matter enough that leaving it also carries weight.

They may also try stranger breaks than simple desertion. They may take Rowe's side and arrest Vane before the field work is half done. They may decide Reed is the only honest center and build the chapter around protecting one surveyor instead of one expedition. They may shift entirely toward Crow or Lakota interests and spend the second half sabotaging the line while still trying to keep some wounded fool alive. They may kill Pike, lose Rowe, and come limping back with only half a packet and one witness. All of those are valid versions of the story. Keep the four real questions in sight and the chapter will survive almost anything: who controls the papers, who gets brought home, which power the party has chosen to strengthen, and who now wants them dead.

## Sandbox Exits

The ending should throw the party into the wider Montana book with open hooks, not a ribbon tied around the matter. Bozeman and Fort Ellis are strong if the party wants Army follow-up, ranch pressure, Gallatin politics, and trail work. Helena fits if they kept papers worth money, blackmail, or investment. Virginia City suits a group that wants courts, politics, and public reckoning. Fort Benton matters if Pike or the freight interests survive and the party wants to pull at the river trade. Crow country and the Yellowstone edge fit best if trust or betrayal with Shows-the-Ford was the heart of play.

## GM Notes on Tone

Keep this chapter hard and specific.

Men should want cash, horses, contracts, land, paper, and authority. They should not speak like a theme statement. Nobody in the fort says he is advancing colonial expansion. He says he wants the crossing marked, the packet delivered, his contract honored, or the fools under him to stop getting killed.

That is enough. The larger truth will show itself.
