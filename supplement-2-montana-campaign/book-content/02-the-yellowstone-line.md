# The Yellowstone Line

> _"The flies were thick on the horse lines. A mule stamped and rattled its chain. Farther off, by the parade ground, somebody was laughing too loud over a bottle. Fort Ellis looked steady in the late light. Whitewashed walls. Flag limp in the heat. Wagons in a row. Men who believed a post and a pay chest made a hard country obey._
>
> _It took one dead teamster and a saddle cut loose in the dark to show what the country thought of that."_

The Yellowstone basin forces an `1873` Montana campaign open. 

Nothing in it sits quiet. The Army wants prestige and safe roads. Surveyors want a route other men will turn into stock, town lots, and contracts. Gallatin merchants want the rails bent toward their stores. Crow scouts want the Army deaf to everything but Crow interests. Lakota riders mean to bleed the white line before it crosses good hunting ground. The margins wait to see which ruin arrives first. The player characters drop into that violence. They start as contract hands: guards, teamsters, scouts, hunters, clerks, discharged soldiers, gamblers, or guides. By the end of the chapter, they see Army weakness, private greed, Lakota force, and the cost of carrying paper men kill for.

The column goes one direction. The party moves with it. Inside the march, players choose who they serve and what they sell, hide, save, or burn. Those choices build the reckoning. 

## Historical Frame

The Northern Pacific needs a route. The Army needs a road. The `1873` Yellowstone survey season pushed both across hunting ground.

News of fighting farther east already reached Montana. By late summer, every hard man in Bozeman and Fort Ellis understands the stake: the first map line brings wagons, titles, settlers, and soldiers.

The expedition runs as a small reconnaissance out of Fort Ellis. Officially, it measures crossings, grades, forage, and timber west of the basin. Privately, its uses turn dirty.

> Historical note: In `1873`, railroad pressure in Montana remained mostly survey stakes. Men fought over routes, grades, and paper before locomotives crossed the territory.

## The Shape of It

The party joins the column at Fort Ellis. A killing and a night raid prove the rot started before the wagons moved. Field work turns political. A Crow warning offers a clean path while greed and Army pride demand another. The papers split. Men shoot one another in camp. The expedition shatters into frightened men, wounded stock, and rival truths. What survives the ride back becomes the first leverage players hold in Montana.

The events are tight but not locked. Parties search wagons early, press liars, ride after the wrong man, pocket papers, threaten officers, bargain with enemies, or wash their hands of the white enterprise. Let them shift the balance. Controlling the line changes who fears them, who trusts them, and who uses them.

## Why a Character Rides with the Line

No decent person loves this expedition. Motives come mixed. A discharged trooper signs on because Army bacon beats luck, or because Captain Rowe knows his name from older roads. A hunter rides because survey wages feed a horse through winter. A drifter only wants a place near the payroll. Montana holds men who start without belief and stay because money and blood already changed hands.

Ties bind tighter. One character holds Gallatin kin and knows an open-country route choice lifts one town and starves the next. Another hauled freight for Pike and reads theft in his wagon harness. A clerk or draughtsman rides because paper means power, and ink shows where the real money settles first. Give each character one thread: prior service with Rowe, odd work under Reed, debt in Bozeman, shelter taken at Calder Ranch, or acquaintance with LaRoche. Those ties turn the lies personal.

## Core Cast

These are the principal named figures. Full statblocks appear in [02A-dramatis-personae-and-statblocks.md](/mnt/c/git-public/Tales-of-the-Old-West-2e/supplement-2-montana-campaign/book-content/02A-dramatis-personae-and-statblocks.md).

`Captain Nathaniel Rowe` is a white man in his early forties, Army to the bone, narrow through the waist, and hard in the saddle. He is order held up by temper. He keeps gloves clean in dirt and looks past men before they finish speaking. He talks in clipped sentences. He sees players as irregular tools: necessary for a mean country, but not people he trusts before they hold their nerve.

`Lieutenant Ezra Harlan` is a white man of twenty-seven, polished like cavalry brass and too proud of the uniform. He strains for command. He stands too straight, smiles too quickly, and touches his belt when judged. He talks fast and half a rank above his station. He sees players as useful roughs he might boast of leading, or disreputable witnesses he must not let see him break.

`Sergeant Owen Duffy` is an Irish-born white man near fifty, broad through the chest and gray under the brim. He is steady. He built himself from old leather, smoke, and regulations. He checks loads blind and hears trouble in a horse line before gunfire. He speaks dry and plain, done with officer posturing. He sees players as hands worth having on a bad road or mouths complicating his work.

`Professor Amos Reed` is a white man in his mid-thirties, spare, near-sighted, and tired. He hates incompetence more than discomfort. He squints at ground, tools, and men. He fingers ink stains. He grows cold when commerce touches measurement. His speech cuts sharp. He sees players as protection or labor, judging whether they follow money or fact.

`Silas Vane` is a white man around forty, well-fed, careful with clothes, and too groomed for a trail job. He is smooth. He never hurries. He lowers his voice and steps close to make an ally of whoever listens. He talks easy persuasion without naming his masters. He sees players as purchased assets or obstacles requiring division.

`Eli Mercer` is a white man not yet thirty, neat-handed, narrow-shouldered, and soaked in bottle courage. He smiles too soon. He touches pockets and straps to prove his papers remain. He trims sentences toward safety. He sees players as violent threats because they live near force a liar cannot write away.

`Thomas Keene` is a white man in his early twenties, hollowed by bad sleep and worse whiskey. He sweats in the cold. He grabs his sleeves and checks his shoulders. He throws excuses ahead of sense. He sees players as rescuers, buyers, or executioners passing judgment breath by breath.

`Gideon Pike` is a white man in his forties, heavy in the shoulders and gut, marked by hand-working teams. He is practical force. He dresses like a freight boss and looks for military profit. He studies harness tighter than he studies men. He speaks teamster idiom layered with arithmetic. He sees players as labor, risk, or leverage ending in black ink.

`Hannah Calder` is a white woman near forty, sun-cut and strong-handed. She holds her own ground. She buries her own dead. She keeps tools instead of ornament and watches men like she watches weather. She speaks flat and asks no leave. She sees players as a passing column until they learn the difference between needing shelter and taking territory.

`Baptiste LaRoche` is a mixed-blood frontier man in his thirties, quiet, well-seated, and hard to read. He gives calm attention. He hears voices across camp, tracks shifting tones, and holds counsel until spending it cuts deepest. He measures words. He sees players worth listening to before trusting them, placing him ahead of half the Army.

`Shows-the-Ford` is a Crow man in mature adulthood, lean and unsurprised. He controls the field. He sits his horse like an old agreement. He never fidgets and never explains himself twice. His speech comes spare and exact. He sees players as unmarked men who might yet justify the trouble of knowing them.

`Iron Hail` is a Lakota war leader in his thirties, riding on judgment and speed. He kills slower men before they name him. Rumor gives him direction. Men say he picks the blow and delivers it hard. He sees players as part of the white line until they prove their separation in blood.

## What Is Moving Here

The visible task is escort and survey. Four fights run beneath it. The Army wants safety and a clean report. Surveyors want route control and private paper. Crow scouts shape movement and terms. Lakota resistance bleeds the line before it hardens to rail. Players begin low enough to catch truth from all corners. Nobody thinks them important enough to lie to carefully yet.

## Consequence Ledger

Track party standing with four groups: `Army Respect`, `Survey Leverage`, `Crow Trust`, and `Local Goodwill`. Start each at `0`. When players make a hard choice, shift the tracks `+1` or `-1`. The ride back into settlement cashes the totals. High `Army Respect` opens Fort Ellis work and legal shelter. High `Survey Leverage` means players hold paper Bozeman interests crave. High `Crow Trust` opens warnings and southern country access. High `Local Goodwill` makes ranchers and merchants a net beneath the party. Low scores build walls, ruin credit, and run bad stories ahead of them.

Factions act on later choices:

- High `Army Respect` pushes Rowe to grant freedom, ask judgment, and offer escort work.
- Low `Army Respect` draws Harlan's watch, hardens orders, and strips lawful shelter.
- High `Survey Leverage` draws men offering cash, seduction, or threats for copies.
- Low `Survey Leverage` leaves clean hands but weak power at the end.
- High `Crow Trust` brings early warning, safer crossings, and negotiation in place of ambush.
- Low `Crow Trust` means silence and blood.
- High `Local Goodwill` makes small valley people help when official hands fail.
- Low `Local Goodwill` turns small doors tight and lets big men bury the truth.

## What the Main Factions Want

- **Rowe and the Army:** Rowe wants the column out and back cleanly. He refuses fraud or pointless death. He holds his world in military shape. Helping him earns trust. Tainting the expedition forces him between truth and career.

- **Reed and the survey men:** Reed fights bad politics. He is failing. His staff fractures, runs, or takes coin. He needs the party.

- **Vane and the private line:** Vane takes route knowledge before public release. He only needs paper to sell certainty to the next buyer.

- **Pike and the freight interest:** Pike wants billable delay, profitable urgency, and a route leaving him room to haul before the iron rail crushes his margin. He robs men while talking common sense.

- **Shows-the-Ford and Crow interests:** Shows-the-Ford slows the column and redirects the blind push. He deals. He never begs.

- **Iron Hail and Lakota resistance:** Iron Hail intends to bloody the survey and strip its paper. He refuses free movement.

## Clue Discipline

One failed roll does not end the chapter.

Spread facts across layers.

- Bell's killing points to corruption.
- Keene points to copied notes.
- Mercer points to divided packets.
- Vane points to route dealing.
- Pike points to freight theft.
- Shows-the-Ford points to the cost of the push.

If players miss a clue, move the truth into another mouth, pocket, or shallow grave.

## Camp Scenes Between Major Events

Use these scenes when players want to live in the column.

- **The bad coffee fire:** Troopers and a survey hand argue over axle-grease coffee. A man says nobody rides here for the pay. Players learn who trusts the work and who understands the rot.
- **Reed at the notebook:** Reed scratches margin notes by lantern light and swats gnats. Respect gets technical facts. Observation catches Reed putting one personal note into a hidden pocketbook. 
- **Pike counting sacks:** Pike checks flour, bacon, and harness when nobody watches. He checks his profit margin. Pikes's morality lies in the count.
- **LaRoche listening in the dark:** LaRoche catches three fires at once without moving. He answers a question before it forms. He tracks everything building in the dark.
- **Harlan with a bottle:** Harlan takes enough drink to confess bad ambition. He wants glory and misses the cost. Players either stiffen him or steer him to ruin.
- **Mercer trying to be liked:** Mercer pushes tobacco and cards to build cover. Friendliness insulates him for the upcoming theft.
- **Horse line at midnight:** The line lives. Animals stamp, blow, and spook. Players standing watch earn early warning when the raid breaks.
- **A distant fire in the wrong place:** One watcher catches a fire where no camp sits. It dies. The night ends without an attack, stripping sleep and rotting judgment before dawn.
- **A prayer over Bell:** A laundress says hard words over Bell's body. The dead stop abstracting. The group remembers Bell fed kin before he bled out.
- **The spoiled flour barrel:** One Pike barrel rots low where the leak hides. Players push Pike to protect blamed teamsters, or hold the fraud for leverage.
- **Cards with Keene:** Keene joins a game trying to look ordinary. He drops attention fast. Patience gets him talking sideways around the truth before fear chokes him.
- **A horse gone lame for no clean reason:** A good mount comes up sore from bad luck or sabotage. Players trace tack, march order, and begin reading violence deeper than rifles.
- **LaRoche and the weather sign:** LaRoche reads cloud and wind while others argue. He drops one dry remark proving he tracks worse storms than rain.
- **Hannah's plain ledger:** Hannah shows hard accounts: high nails, bought-damp flour, and heavy team hire. Pike crushes people absent from railroad maps.

## Side Events on the Line

Use these stops to drive pressure and divide the expedition.

- **The wrong grave by the creek:** Weather exposed a trail grave. The dead man wore freight boots and carried a broken clasp knife, a spent pistol round, and illegible paper. Pike lies about the face. The road was dirty before the survey found it.
- **A rider under white cloth:** A man rides in under white cloth claiming to carry Vane's friends or Pike's contractors. He might stall or measure the camp. Players receive him, strip him, shadow him, or shoot the parley dead and run the rumor later.
- **The missing instrument screw:** A piece from Reed's gear vanishes. The day stalls over a thumbnail of brass. Players uncover sabotage, clumsy theft, or panic wrapped in a lie.
- **A wounded horse and a faster choice:** A horse snaps a leg in bad ground. Somebody shoots it, strips tack, and redistributes the load. Give this to the most careful player. Care costs.
- **A settler's claim marker:** A pathetic claim marker sits on good ground. It holds one name and a date. Survey stakes, Army boots, and organized money crush it. The marker becomes a moral anchor.
- **The wrong cartridges:** Army issue turns up in civilian pockets, or civilian bullets hide in survey bags. Players build chains instead of finding isolated clues.

## If the Players Freeze or Break the Plan

The opener survives broken fights and dead men.

- **If Keene dies early:** Move his copy to Mercer, Bell's satchel, or Reed's spare case. The copied-paper rot survives Keene.
- **If Mercer dies early:** Players find his cipher marks, account slips, or letter drafts. Mercer breathes to be useful; the chapter does not need his lungs.
- **If Vane drops dead in the field:** Good. Dead men holding paper still warp counties. Shift his role to a Bozeman banker, a hired rider, or Pike.
- **If Rowe loses command:** Duffy holds the line. Reed forces the field books back. Disorder shifts the tone without snapping the arc.
- **If the party openly sides with Pike:** Let them. Pike pays them and works them until silence becomes cheaper than loyalty. The ending turns dirty but hooks the sandbox.
- **If the party sides with Iron Hail or refuses the white mission:** The chapter becomes sabotage, flight, and political rupture. Papers burn, wounded bleed, and native interests collide.
- **If the party brings court-proof evidence:** Give them marked pay slips, mismatched copies, and a living witness holding explicit orders. They build a case instead of hearing a confession.

## Arc I: Fort Ellis and the First Miles

Let the opening breathe. The party hears claims before field mud proves them worthless.

## Fort Ellis
### 1. Muster and employment

Read or paraphrase:

> _Late summer hangs dry over Fort Ellis. The mountains look blue in the south. The parade ground churns under hooves and wagon wheels. Soldiers haul crates. Laborers bicker. A survey tripod sits near stacked grain. Nobody rests. Too many men draw pay from the same table._

> Historical note: Fort Ellis sat east of Bozeman, planted in `1867` to cover the Gallatin Valley and watch the roads to the Bozeman, Bridger, and Flathead country.

Captain Rowe or Sergeant Duffy musters the players. Army pay lacks weight but offers cash, rations, and a standing horse line. 

Rowe cuts it public:

> _"You ride, watch, and keep your mouths shut. If you cannot do the first two, save us the waste and walk away now."_

Players inspect the wagons. The Army plays at order. Civilians hold no rank and throw weight anyway. Silas Vane dictates directions without authority. Reed stomachs it. Mercer guards the clean copies and accounts, sitting too near the machinery for a small clerk. `INSIGHT` tracks the strain between Rowe, Reed, and Vane. `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` spots a horse line packed tight enough to shatter at night. `BOOKLEARNIN'` spots survey notes lying dangerously close to the payroll. `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` sets the party's face. The fort reeks of horse sweat, old ash, and thin soap. Pike's hands fight over short oats. Reed's fixers hate Mercer.

If players haggle pay or challenge civilian authority, Rowe answers harsh in public and fair in private. Vane marks the men who want terms. Pike marks the workers. If players search paper chests, they find enough loose ends to trigger anger. Anger forces action. 

Duffy sinks into the horse-yard dust. He curses the mixed line, the badly packed paper, and the false security of canvas and leather. He speaks to measure listeners. A player helping the line earns his nod and later trust.

Pike's yard shows the rot early. Damp flour barrels turn their rot inward. Pike's foreman fights a clerk over short oats and false weights. Reed watches the ground. Pike watches load capacity. Rowe counts the guns. Vane claims the argument. 

Mercer tests for loneliness with tobacco and cards. He hints at a difference between the official packet and the useful packet, then laughs weakly and backs down. 

### 2. Supper with liars

The principals eat near the officers' quarters.

Read or paraphrase:

> _Lamps throw yellow light over tin plates and a bottle running low. Beyond the light, the fort sinks into hoof noise and dark. Inside the circle, men divide the future as if the territory already submitted to the chain._

Rowe talks duty and escort limits. Reed talks grade, crossings, and the damage done by map-dreamers. Vane talks territorial future without binding his word. Pike talks freight cost and forage lines like he holds the only arithmetic in Montana. Bozeman merchants argue the Gallatin needs the iron. Rowe shuts his face to interruption. Reed speaks precise coldness. Vane draws men close. Pike mimics rough labor and thinks like a bank.

> _"A line belongs where the ground permits it,"_ Reed says. _"Not where a merchant dreams it."_
>
> _"Dreams are what build towns, Professor,"_ Vane says. _"Your measurements only tell the rest of us where to spend the money."_

If players accuse Vane, bait Harlan, or shadow Mercer, let the room crack. Rowe despises disorder. Vane shrinks when cornered under light. Shadowing Mercer catches him checking paper custody twice. Shadowing Keene uncovers mud on his knees. Shadowing a Pike hand reveals a remuda count taken too closely. 

### 3. The picket-line killing

Near dark, mule skinner Joe Bell bleeds out by the remuda.

Read or paraphrase:

> _Bell lies dead in the dirt, one arm crushed under his weight. A cut rope trails over his boot. A horse watches the body and rolls one eye white._

A heavy blow struck him. A blade cut his jaw to finish it. One hitch rope snapped. A saddle blanket and a dispatch satchel vanished.

The ground tracks trouble. Army issue heels cross civilian boots. A survey pencil shaving rests near Bell's hand. Bell drank with Mercer. Earlier, Bell watched Thomas Keene dig through a chest. Players push Mercer, Keene, Pike's men, and the guards. Mercer lies about timings. Keene shows mud on his cuffs. A trooper names a civilian offering whiskey for a look at the papers. Pike claims Bell owed him money. 

Players escalate. If they clap Keene in irons, Vane squeezes hard elsewhere. If they beat Mercer, he panics and plots to run. If they force Rowe to freeze the column, he does it and carries the fury into the field. 

A laundress kneels by Bell. She speaks hard truth over him. Bell had a sister waiting on cash. 

Mercer trims his sentences and blames bad company. Pike's men form a wall of silence. Rowe hardens if civilians play marshal. 

### 4. Raid on the remuda

Riders tear into the horse line before the killing settles.

Read or paraphrase:

> _The first shot drops from outside. The second comes from the line. The horses break. A bay tears loose on a stake rope. A mule screams. Men shout for a satchel and pray to God in the dark._

Muzzle flashes cut bad angles. Raiders hunt panic, a loose remuda, an inside cutter's escape, and the leather wallet. Saving the line matters. A lost line cripples the march. A captured rider points to dirty money. 

A dead raider carries a Gallatin store chit, Army cartridges, or a marked silver dollar from Pike's chest.

If the remuda scatters, the march starts lame. If players capture a raider, Vane and Pike twitch. If the inside man vanishes, Mercer breathes deep and Keene shakes. Protecting the wallet pushes the next fights toward paper control.

Players act wildly. They chase riders, light the hay cart, or loose the horses to spoil the raid. A hard pursuit tags the employer but strands the column. A lit yard sharpens aim and exposed the targets. Saving the wallet lets the cutter vanish. 

A captured raider anchors the yard. Rowe demands chain of custody. Duffy hunts the picket line gap. Pike wants the man buried before dawn. Vane asks if the man remembers dangerous names. 

**Combat Encounter: Raid on the Remuda**
- **Enemies:** `1 REMUDA CUTTER` inside the line, `4-6 HIRED RIDERS`, and `1-2 HIRED WHITE SABOTEURS`.
- **Area:** The Fort Ellis horse yard: picket ropes, hitch rails, feed crates, wagons, and packed animals.
- **Terrain and Effects:** Darkness blurs sightlines. Loose horses knock men down, foul firing lines, and force `MOVE` and `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` tests. Stakes and dragged ropes foul footing. Lit yards make targets visible to everyone.

## Leaving Settlement Space
### 5. Sorting the column

The column breaks Fort Ellis at first light. 

Read or paraphrase:

> _Morning hits the valley pale and mean. The fort shrinks to a smudge of smoke. The road turns to wheel tracks, cut banks, and the knowledge that turning back demands an explanation._

March order matters. Front scouts see the narrowing valley. Wagon guards hear Pike curse his loads. The rear guard catches a strange brand. Close guard hears Reed and Vane fight over copied notes. The river bends bright. Distance opens ahead.

### 6. Thomas Keene

By noon, the party collars Thomas Keene trying to slip into the brush.

> _"I never meant to carry the whole thing off,"_ Keene says. _"I only copied what Mercer told me to copy. Ask him. Ask Vane. Ask any of them if they'll own it in daylight."_

He copied notes into his vest lining. He denies theft, then admits it, then blames Mercer, then fingers Vane. The truth splits. Mercer sells notes. Vane buys private routes. Bell bled for watching. 

Players surrender Keene to Rowe, hide him, sell him to Vane, or strip him and run him off. Army surrender spikes `Army Respect`. Holding copies builds `Survey Leverage`. Selling him kills `Local Goodwill`. Keene sweats, talks fast, and gauges the players. 

- Army delivery gives Rowe a grip on the rot. Vane shifts to deniable hands.
- Hiding Keene grants truth. Mercer plots betrayal before the ford.
- Selling Keene brings him back later, wounded or offering testimony for life.
- Letting him run dulls the evidence but plants him in Bozeman down the line.

Dragging Keene to Mercer or forcing a trial under Rowe rips the mask early. Mercer lies, trims, and folds. Keene throws wild accusations. Rowe acts on hard papers. Confrontations breed desperate betrayals as the guilty realize the party hunts light. 

A gentle hand pulls the full confession. Keene copied neat pages, then dirty pages, then altered lines while Mercer watched the door and a clean-dressed man sat in the shadows. The rot slid into the camp through small obediences. 

### 7. Calder Ranch

The column halts near a fork at Hannah Calder's ranch.

Read or paraphrase:

> _Cut hay borders the corral. Smoke lifts from the cabin. A woman waits by the trough, watching the column like she watched trouble cross this valley before._

Hannah Calder holds a low cabin, three hay stacks, and a patched corral. She gives no ground to Army brass, railroad ghosts, or town promises. She allows water because refusal invites trampling. She knows survey stakes pull tax collectors. She knows Pike overbills locals. She knows Crow scouts warn while white men shoot. Three riders scouted her ranch yesterday. Fair treatment wins her truth, a dry loft, fresh stock, a cut trail, and names of Bozeman men buying distressed land. 

- Helping her builds a witness lacking polish and pretense.
- Using her place badly shuts her door at the end.
- Dragging her into the Keene fight paints a target on her back.

> _"You put stakes in the ground,"_ Hannah says, _"and the rest follows behind. Tax men. Claim men. Men saying your creek was theirs on paper before you ever drank from it. I have seen the breed."_

> Historical note: Brand registration locked ranches onto paper in `1873`. Ink, hide, and county memory formalized ownership.

Her cabin holds patched chairs, clean rifles, and ledger books shoved into jars. She proves Pike squeezed the valley: high nails, damp flour, and doubled teamsters. 

### 8. The hay-yard fight

Riders hit the ranch after dusk.

Read or paraphrase:

> _Night closes the ranch in hay and horse sweat. Wood cracks under a heavy bullet. Hannah reaches her carbine before the second shot falls._

Choose the strike. White riders hunt Keene or the notes. Lakota raiders test the escort and spook the stock. Hay stacks, corrals, and spooked mules dominate the fight. The barn burns. A draft mule drags chain. Mercer tries a run. The party learns three hard truths: blinders are wanted, private advantage drives the camp, and the territory offers no shelter. 

Count the cost: blown stock, angry NPCs, missing clerks. A shattered horse line means a brutal morning. 

Players hide witnesses, cache packets, or borrow stock. A hidden witness survives. A cached packet risks Pike's fire. Borrowed stock builds goodwill if asked for, or paints them thieves if taken. 

Asking Hannah for testimony means trusting her words, not town polish. Betray her, and `Local Goodwill` breaks. 

**Combat Encounter: The Hay-Yard Fight**
- **Enemies:** `4-6 HIRED RIDERS` with `1 HIRED WHITE SABOTEUR`, or `4-6 LAKOTA RIDERS` with `1 LAKOTA WAR SCOUT`.
- **Area:** Calder Ranch: hay stacks, corrals, trough, barn wall, cabin, stacked tools, and narrow lanes.
- **Terrain and Effects:** Dry hay flashes in gunfire. Corrals offer cover but trap men and horses. Panicked stock force `MOVE` checks. Chain-dragging mules wipe feet and break cover. 

## Arc II: Markers, Forks, and the Closing Country

Escort work dies. The fight for the map begins.

## The First Markers

### 9. The work itself

Give the line its weight. Surveyors change dirt to property.

Read or paraphrase:

> _Reed's crew drives chain and level over the valley rise. A man hammers a stake and steps away. No thunder follows. But every driven stake drags roads, titles, taxes, and graveyards behind it._

Players ride perimeter, carry chain, read weather, or shadow Vane. Work teaches the truth. Reed measures grades for iron, marks solid crossings, counts bridge timber, and measures forage to feed the machine.

### 10. The route fork

The line hits a split. 

Read or paraphrase:

> _The routes show plain difference. One drops easy, letting freight wagons breathe. The other rises hard but holds true grade. Reed narrows his eyes at the dirt. Vane narrows his eyes at Reed._

The easy route feeds freight and Bozeman pockets. The hard route builds long-term grade, forces military logic, and crosses deeper Lakota hunting ground. Vane presses. Reed stonewalls. Rowe seeks survival. Pike demands choke points to bill against. Players back Reed, forge markers, expose Vane, or hold the truth for auction. `Army Respect` climbs on honest support. `Survey Leverage` climbs on forged markers. `Local Goodwill` increases if the decision spares ranchers. `Crow Trust` drops if the line drives deep without warning. 

- Backing Reed wins Rowe's trust and Vane's hostility.
- Altering the route builds leverage but risks exposure when Reed checks the math.
- Exposing Vane lets Pike slide forward to run the game smooth.
- Silence turns early choices into blood on the back end.

Players pull stakes, forge books, threaten crews, or push split routes. A split line begs Lakota or hired strikes. False markers buy time for valley ranchers. Refusal to choose hands the decision to Reed, Rowe, and silence. 

The ground forces facts. Freight routes promise speed, money, and pressure. Military routes promise temper, grade, and deep Lakota anger. 

> Historical note: The Bozeman Trail proved that wagon roads turn into military roads the instant the survey ink dries. 

### 11. Attack on the marker line

The hit drops while the crew spreads out.

Open ground. Surveyors exposed. Saddles empty. Iron Hail's riders want smashed glass, dead confidence, and burning notebooks. Gunfire cracks from low brush. Riders hit the spare stake wagon. Another wave locks onto Reed. Survival counts in intact books and unbroken tripod glass. 

Make paper-heavy characters dictate notes from a bleeding Reed. 

Decoys, empty cases, or false lines draw the initial fire. Native eyes track the land, but smart players dictate the killing ground. 

Taking an Iron Hail rider alive slows the march. A prisoner forces choices: exchange, release, summary execution, or permanent guard. Every choice bleeds into the camp's mood. 

**Combat Encounter: Attack on the Marker Line**
- **Enemies:** `1 IRON HAIL`, `1-2 LAKOTA WAR SCOUTS`, and `5-7 LAKOTA RIDERS`.
- **Area:** Open ground, brush pockets, markers, chains, tripods, and the paper wagon.
- **Terrain and Effects:** Rises and brush offer partial cover. Standing still invites execution. Stakes and gear foul movement. Protecting the records forces brutal positioning. Decoys spoil the first enemy wave.

### 12. Camp after the attack

Night fractures the camp. 

Rowe demands discipline. Reed demands measurements. Vane demands blood on the line to prove its worth. Pike demands safety for his wagons. 

Players move through the dark. Vane buys duplicates. Reed tasks players to shadow Mercer. Rowe asks for unofficial eyes on Vane. Pike hints at massive profit for the right map. Tents smell of dirty hands, wet powder, and bad coffee. Dead mules rot. 

> _"Every man here is drawing pay for one purpose and serving another,"_ Vane says. _"You need not look shocked. I only ask whether you mean to be poor at both."_

Vane tests one player. He offers money, Bozeman connections, and survival. He asks for one copy or warning. The offer cuts deep because the money comes clean.

The camp splits. Rowe and Duffy share a fire. Reed grinds his pen against the paper at another. Pike's teamsters count losses by load limits. Vane walks the gaps. 

## The Country Closes In

### 13. Tracks and shadowing

Morning bleeds proof of outside manipulation.

`HAWKEYE`, `NATURE`, and `INSIGHT` read the dirt. Decoy fires, dragged branches, split pony sign, and boot prints walking out from camp point to a sellout. Pushed hard, Mercer cracks. He lies clean, lies badly, admits the copying, denies the killing, and names Vane or Pike. 

### 14. The Crow warning

Shows-the-Ford rides in under white caution or calls Baptiste LaRoche.

Read or paraphrase:

> _He sits quiet on the horse. He waits until the column hits the line between hearing him or insulting him._

He offers no free salvation. He calls the heavy line an error. He trades safer crossings for trade goods, medicine, blank maps, or stripped pages. Trusting him modifies the ground. Refusing him hardens it. Strong `Crow Trust` or `Army Respect` hangs on the choice. `Survey Leverage` drops if ground vanishes. He judges the quick answers and the posturing. 

- Trusting him spares blood.
- Honest reports strengthen Rowe.
- Private bargains seal native alliances but damn Army standing if broken open.
- Refusal means a violent river crossing.

> _"You mark too hard and too fast,"_ Shows-the-Ford says. _"Then you act surprised when men come to break the marks. That is white-man foolishness. I did not ride here to admire it."_

Players demand hostages, require proof, or strike double bargains. Native memory holds the insult. Promising delayed returns or safe passage builds iron-clad hooks. Shows-the-Ford offers memory, safety, and alliances for one burned page or unmapped valley. 

> Historical note: Native leaders acted as strategic elements, directing or bleeding roads based on their own sovereign needs. 

### 15. The ford fight

The column hits the water.

Read or paraphrase:

> _Brown current pushes against the wheels. Slick clay lines the near bank. Men curse the cold. The far cottonwoods stand quiet and wait._

Slick mud, half-loaded wagons, and thigh-deep freezing water drag on the party. Iron Hail strikes the weakest point. Players defend the survey wagon, cut paper chests loose, drag Calder's borrowed team through the mud, or abandon the far bank. Shows-the-Ford's warning offers submerged paths, cover, or ambush sightlines. Refusal drops everyone into the slaughter. Chests crack open and wash away. Wounded men trap mules. 

**Combat Encounter: The Ford Fight**
- **Enemies:** `1 IRON HAIL`, `1 LAKOTA WAR SCOUT`, `4-6 LAKOTA RIDERS`. Corrupted lines add `1 HIRED WHITE SABOTEUR` stealing packets.
- **Area:** Deep current, muddy banks, stuck wagons, and heavy brush. 
- **Terrain and Effects:** Water slows tests and punishes movement. Mud forces `MOVE` survival. Chests drop and scatter. Wagons function as cover or traps. Shows-the-Ford's guidance mitigates the worst surprise.

### 16. What the dead man carried

Mercer drops after the ford. 

A foreign bullet kills him, or he survives bleeding and insane, babbling about Vane. 

His body yields copied field notes, Pike's false checks, route notations, and the initials tying Bell's throat to Keene's terror.

The camp hollows out. Wounded bleed in wet gear. Stock limits crack. Packets turn deadly. Wet fires end the action. 

## Arc III: Broken Column

The expedition shatters around three leather folders.

## The Split Papers

### 17. Camp of accusations

The truce snaps. 

Read or paraphrase:

> _The camp breathes mean terror. No songs. No cards. Men watch each other over tin cups. Every paper packet weighs like lead._

Rowe demands military guard. Reed demands technical possession. Vane offers gold for copies. Pike commands the destruction of his billings. Keene trades his life for a horse. 

Players tear into trunks, break liars, and hold the map. 

Decisions lock down here. Murder, fraud, or the map itself. Characters search Pike under gunpoint, lock Mercer away, forge Rowe's pass, or deal half-truths to Reed. Rowe hunts a trusted hand. He offers military immunity and later contracts for the truth about Vane and Mercer. 

> _"I will not carry private thieves back under Army guard,"_ Rowe says. _"If there is rot here, name it now."_

Vane steps to the player honoring survival. He buys warning, selects copies, and pushes the knife into the party's center. 

### 18. The copied packet

Players control the core packet: field notes, Pike's fraud, Vane's treason, or Mercer's copies. 

Delivering everything boosts `Army Respect`. Holding routes locks `Survey Leverage`. Holding Pike's fraud jacks `Local Goodwill`. Withholding Lakota maps builds `Crow Trust`. Copying books requires dark light, cold hands, and fast time. 

- Total surrender cleans the ledger and strips the party's hand.
- Splitting the packet covers truth while protecting secrets.
- Selling to Vane provides escape and a long shadow.
- Burning pages saves lives and leaves ghosts.

Players split custody, copy blind, hide books inside saddles, or ditch the notes in trail graves. Imperfect copies breed violent arguments. Shadow games force Rowe and Vane to kill for ghosts. The packet drives the violence—make it wet, blood-stained, and undeniable. 

### 19. Midnight in broken camp

The violence breaks inside the line.

The attackers match the consequences: Vane's hired men, Pike's freight muscle, or Lakota riders. Gunfire strikes tents and powder crates. 

Read or paraphrase:

> _The dark rips open. A lantern crashes. A mule jerks a tether. A man screams a warning and catches a bullet for the trouble._

The expedition dies in the crossfire. Vane flees or holds influence. He remains a paper-man, never a bandit. 

**Combat Encounter: Midnight in Broken Camp**
- **Enemies:** `2-4 FREIGHT TOUGHS` and `2-4 HIRED RIDERS` for Pike/Vane. `4-6 LAKOTA RIDERS` for outer pressure. `1 HIRED WHITE SABOTEUR` for total camp collapse. 
- **Area:** A split camp: tents, mules, wounded men, and volatile crates.
- **Terrain and Effects:** Lanterns light islands. Canvas offers zero protection. Tents and ropes break charges. Panic forces `PRESENCE` and `MOVE` responses. Fire turns smoke thick.

### 20. Breaking point

Morning forces the final choice. 

Reed wants the books. Rowe wants the wounded. Vane buys the map. Pike dumps the liability. 

Players organize the retreat. Fast carriers, slow guards, or split decoys. Speed kills the wounded. Decoys risk Lakota isolation. 

Players bury the copies, disguise couriers, or push true notes into enemy hands to blind them. Buried packets spawn new quests. False couriers draw death. A wounded man begs for morphine and a carbine. A civilian hugs his valise while the expedition burns. 

## The Retreat

### 21. What do you save

The column drops weight. 

Players choose between Reed's master book, Vane and Pike's corruption, wounded soldiers, and the heavy surveyor plates. Saving men jacks `Army Respect`. Saving paper jacks `Survey Leverage`. Abandonment kills `Local Goodwill`. Saving glass secures Reed's gratitude.

> _"I am done pretending we can save all of it. Name the load. Name the men. We move on your word or mine,"_ Rowe says.

### 22. Running the line backward

The run smells of blood and exhausted animals. 

Riders pace the dust. Axles snap. Water turns alkali. Ravines block the wheels. Players use `LABOR` for wagons, `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` for crazed teams, `NATURE` for camps, and `MOVE` for scouting. 

### 23. Rear-guard action

The hammer comes down hard. 

A broken ridge, a wagon barricade, or breastworks build the kill zone. Heavy flanks, white saboteurs, and desperate charges. Duffy holds the line. Harlan risks it all. Shows-the-Ford turns the tide if honored. 

**Combat Encounter: Rear-Guard Action**
- **Enemies:** `1 LAKOTA WAR SCOUT`, `4-6 LAKOTA RIDERS`, `1-2 HIRED WHITE SABOTEURS`, or `HIRED RIDERS`.
- **Area:** Broken rock, barricades, creek bends, and shattered flour sacks.
- **Terrain and Effects:** Debris breaks sightlines and movement. Fast withdrawals save men. Wreckage spoils clear fire. Shows-the-Ford provides alternative retreat lines.

## Reckoning

Fort Ellis or Bozeman hold the finale. Offices judge what carbines could not finish.

### 24. Who receives the papers

Every man wants the delivery room. Rowe demands the desk. Reed demands the office. Vane demands silence. Pike demands ash. The first reader decides the official casualties and the official map.

Read or paraphrase:

> _Wet wool, stove heat, and drying paper. Blood enemies stand shoulder to shoulder waiting to see whose fingers touch the packet first._

### 25. Local pressure

`Local Goodwill` lands here. High marks force open doors and honest witnesses. Low marks lock rooms and distribute bribes. Hannah Calder's raw letters shatter polished silence. 

Players pick the venue: Army barracks, a newspaper floor, a banked vault, a church holding, or a boarded room above a saloon. The room anchors the ending. Barracks mean chain of custody. Newspapers mean libel and blood. Banks mean blackmailed silence. Boarding rooms mean one more knife before morning. 

Splitting evidence guarantees scattered violence. 

> Historical note: By `1873`, paper law existed alongside vigilante steel and the fastest horse to town.

### 26. Final standoff

The conclusion drops the hammer. 

Rowe arrests Vane in a stable. Vane issues a buyout in a warehouse. Pike attacks in daylight. The choice boils between legal custody and living survival. In Montana, those lines rarely cross.

- `Army Respect` drives the public seizure.
- `Survey Leverage` builds private violence and blackmail.
- `Local Goodwill` relies on unbought witnesses.
- Broken paper runs into knife work and fugitive ends. 

Players split the paper, publish the routes, sell the fraud, and pocket the Lakota marks. Controlling the survivors works better than justice. 

Vane requests one dark meeting. Rowe tests player loyalty. Calder warns that hunters probe her barn. LaRoche delivers native memory. The sandbox tears open.

**Combat Encounter: Final Standoff in Settlement Space**
- **Enemies:** `1 GIDEON PIKE` with `2-4 FREIGHT TOUGHS`, or `1 SILAS VANE` with `2-3 HIRED RIDERS` and `1 HIRED WHITE SABOTEUR`. Adding townsmen twists the fight.
- **Area:** Alleys, stairwells, boarding rooms, offices, or freight yards.
- **Terrain and Effects:** Wood, mud, windows, and packed spaces. Witnesses penalize blind shooting. Law restricts the blood. `PRESENCE` and `INSIGHT` drive the end. 

### 27. End states

#### Army victory
The packet lands clean. Rowe holds reputation. The Army uses players for ugly jobs.

#### Railroad shadow victory
The players sell or hold copies. They stand rich, corrupted, and hunted. 

#### Local Montana victory
Fraud hits the light. Ranchers win a breath. The party gains local friends.

#### Crow understanding
Respected alliances adjust Crow hostility. Safe passage requires maintenance.

#### Bad ending, good sandbox
Liars prosper. The party flees carrying proof under a heavy coat. The territory offers no rest.

## Pacing the Expedition

The campaign needs time. Bell's killing opens the door. Marching, grading, and testing the water drags the middle. The rear-guard drops the hammer. Reckonings turn lead into paper.

## Running the Major Combats

Rotate the ground. Remudas use darkness and loose stock. Hay yards use fire and cabins. Marker lines use open exposure. Fords use deep mud and drowning. Broken camps use panic and tied lines. Rear-guards sell blood for minutes. Final standoffs test the law. 

## Reward, Reputation, and What the Party Carries Out

Cash answers the short game. Influence buys survival. Recommend Rowe, ally with Reed, sell to Vane, stand by Calder, honor Shows-the-Ford, or hold names linked to murder. Wounds outlast the payment. Weak lungs, dead horses, Bozeman targets, and waiting enemies. Good Montana rewards leave permanent scars. 

## Carrying Chapter 02 into the Sandbox

The end breaks three lanes open. Army court jobs. Paper chases in Helena. Local Gallatin resistance. Crow native tasks. One surviving enemy stalks the player trail. 

## Running the Dilemmas

Choices exist without rolls. Let dice shift timing and blood, but keep the moral hammer on the table. Every road costs. No route shines bright. 

## Travel, Pace, and Weather

Make the territory work. Dust fouls weapons. Frost kills the wounded. Rivers strip time. Weak horses drag the line. 

## If the Party Goes Off the Line

Desertion pays in starvation. Quitting with papers unlocks immediate hunting. Turning purely Native forces a sabotage angle. Let them kill Pike or blind Rowe. The book survives. Who holds the paper? Who breathes? Who hunts them now? 

## Sandbox Exits

Bozeman and Fort Ellis offer military runs. Helena demands paper and blackmail. Virginia City demands law. Fort Benton demands river freight. Southern boundaries demand Crow promises fulfilled. 

## GM Notes on Tone

Men demand cash, horses, contracts, and power. No one speaks their theme. They demand the payload. The rest bleeds out later.
