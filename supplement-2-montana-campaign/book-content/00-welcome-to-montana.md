# Montana Territory, 1873

The maps lie.

They do not lie in the way a drunk lies or a politician lies. They lie cleanly. They put a line where no line holds. They print a road where there is only a track through mud and buffalo grass, good for a wagon in dry weather and a graveyard in the thaw. They mark a fort and let a man in Boston or Philadelphia imagine that means the country around it is pacified, owned, understood. They mark a river and fail to mention the ice that will choke it, or the spring rise that will take a ferry, a team, and six men at one sweep. They print town names in neat type and do not say how many of those towns were built in a fever, or how many are hanging on by one mine, one landing, one storekeeper willing to extend credit another month.

Montana in 1873 is not a finished country. It is a hard country under argument.

Gold made the first argument. It pulled men into the gulches and creek bottoms with shovels, sluice boxes, whiskey, greed, and a great capacity for self-deception. Some found fortunes. More found cold water, bad partners, and gravel enough to break the back of a mule. Then the easy gold thinned and men who had come to dig turned to buying claims, lending money, hauling freight, fixing courts, and calling themselves respectable.

The river made another argument. Fort Benton, far up the Missouri, takes in the trade of a vast and violent country. Freight, whiskey, robes, tools, powder, cloth, flour, coffee, rumor, and disease all come upriver together. There is money there and there are men who will knife for it. There are also people who have lived in that country longer than any clerk or surveyor has put pen to paper over it, and they are not in the habit of surrendering it because some company has begun to dream.

The Army makes its own argument, though less convincingly than it imagines. A fort is a claim staked in timber and lime. It is also a promise that cannot be kept at every distance. A troop may ride hard and still arrive late. A commander may hold a valley and not the next ridge. Uniforms impress merchants, editors, drifters, and fools. They do not impress winter. They do not impress hunger. They do not impress a people defending its own ground.

The railroad has not yet come to Montana in iron fact, but it is already here in appetite. Surveyors, speculators, lobbyists, town builders, and men with rolled maps in leather tubes are its advance guard. They are measuring futures not yet built and selling them before the ground is settled. A line on a survey sheet can make one town rich and leave another to rot. It can turn a ranch into a depot or a grave.

And beneath all of it stands the oldest fact of the place. Montana is not empty. It never was. Crow, Blackfeet, Salish, Pend d'Oreille, Kootenai, Assiniboine, Gros Ventre, Lakota, Cheyenne, Shoshone, and others move through this book not as scenery but as nations, communities, scouts, enemies, allies, traders, raiders, families, and political actors with their own memory of every promise made and broken. If this book does its work, they will not appear at the edge of the page as flavor for someone else's story. They are part of the argument itself.

This supplement is about that argument.

It is about mining camps growing old and mean. It is about valley towns trying to look permanent. It is about cattle beginning to harden into property and brand law. It is about steamboat freight, winter roads, whiskey traders, crooked deputies, territorial officeholders, hired guns, scouts, missionaries, road agents, ranch wives, clerks, whores, editors, and men who mistake possession for right. It is about distance and how distance changes every bargain. It is about the fact that no one power in Montana is yet strong enough to own the whole board.

That is what makes it a proper sandbox.

There is no single throne to seize and no one villain whose death settles the country. There are only systems pressing against one another: money against law, law against custom, the Army against the land, survey against memory, one people against another, and hunger against whatever moral principles a man claimed to possess when his belly was full. Player characters in Montana are not stepping into a neat plot. They are stepping into motion. Their choices matter because nothing here is settled.

Welcome to Montana Territory.

Do not expect fairness. Do not expect safety. Expect weather, debt, rumor, false friends, long roads, and men who smile before they cheat you. Expect whole districts to turn on a missing dispatch, a bad assay, a murdered surveyor, a herd gone lame in an early snow, a wagon train that does not arrive, a court decision bought in Helena or Virginia City, or a line on a map no one out in the sage ever agreed to honor.

If your players want a country where the law is clear, the roads are marked, the enemy is obvious, and every town means the same thing, send them elsewhere.

If they want a place where a man can leave Fort Ellis with a survey chain, a Spencer carbine, a pack mule, and a contract in his pocket and come back three weeks later with blood on his coat, three factions at his throat, and enough information to start a range war, then they are in the right country.

## Vignette

By first light the frost had taken the mule grass white along the creek and the survey stakes stood out of the ground like grave markers. The camp was slow waking. A mule bawled somewhere in the dark and a teamster cursed at it without heat. The fire had fallen in on itself. A pot of coffee sat in the ash with a skin of black grease moving on it when the wind touched it.

The boy on watch had seen riders before dawn and said nothing because he had not been sure they were riders. In that country a man learned caution about naming shapes. Horse, stump, sage, dead horse, crouched man. The distance changed them and the light changed them again. When the sun came up thin behind the eastern haze there were no shapes left on the ridge at all.

Captain Bell came out of his tent pulling on his gloves. He was a narrow man with a red mustache and the look officers got when they had slept in their boots and meant to blame someone else for it. He stood by the fire and looked over the camp and asked where Greeley was.

No one answered him at once.

Greeley was the civilian surveyor with the field notes. He was not liked. He had the habit of speaking to teamsters as if they were deaf and to scouts as if they were children. He had spent the previous evening at the officers' fire explaining what the country would amount to once the rail came through. He had spoken that way in front of men who had buried friends on that same line of country and in front of a Crow scout named Two Cuts, who had listened without expression and then gone off into the dark.

Bell asked again.

A corporal came from the picket line carrying Greeley's hat.

It had been cut through front to back by something sharp. There was frost on the brim.

They found the mule first, still hobbled, standing in the willow break with the survey chain tangled round its forelegs. The field satchel was gone. One boot lay in the creek among the stones. Farther up the bank there was blood where a man had gone to his knees and pushed himself up again.

Bell ordered the camp turned out and the order ran through the men with the old familiar sound of work becoming danger. Cinches drew tight. Carbines came out of scabbards. The cook stamped dirt over the fire and looked north as if he could read the next hour there.

Two Cuts knelt by the blood and touched it with one finger.

What do you make of it, Bell said.

The scout stood up. Four men, he said. Maybe five. One wounded.

Whose?

Two Cuts looked at him a long moment. If I knew that now, he said, I would not be standing here.

Bell took the insult and let it pass. He looked to the ridge where the boy had seen riders and then down at the line of stakes running east through the sage and greasewood, each one placed yesterday with such care and each one meaning money to someone far from this frost and mud and fear.

The teamsters were already whispering. The clerk from Helena had gone pale. The drover with the broken nose spat into the creek and said they ought to turn back now if they had any sense left. One of the enlisted men said Greeley had likely run off drunk with the payroll. Another said if Lakota had taken him there would be no blood because they would have killed him cleanly. A third said nothing at all, only checked the loads in his revolver and looked west where the valley opened.

Bell folded Greeley's ruined hat and tucked it under his arm.

Saddle up, he said.

No one in camp had yet said aloud what all of them knew: if the notes were gone then this was no simple disappearance. The line they had come here to draw had already become worth stealing, worth killing for, perhaps worth lying about for years to come.

The sun came clear then and laid the whole country open. Frost in the grass. Blue shadow under the cutbank. A hawk circling over the creek. Beyond that the ridges one behind another to the south and east and no mark on them that promised help.

They saddled and rode anyway.
