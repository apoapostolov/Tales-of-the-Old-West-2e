# Book 04 Publication Rescue Analysis

This audit covers `04-trials-of-the-old-west` as a manuscript for release. It uses `02-the-1870s` as the prose benchmark and `01-corebook` plus `docs/design-bible.md` as the rules benchmark.

## Executive Diagnosis

Book 04 has a strong skeleton. The chapter list is useful, the subjects belong in the line, and many mechanics are close to the existing Old West rules: Progress vs Trouble operations, Turn of the Season business rolls, small gauges, resource dice, Standing, Wanted, Refuge, Capital, and town aspects.

The draft is not yet publishable. It often reads like a design memorandum wearing a western coat. Strong opening paragraphs give way to internal vocabulary, repeated statements about "the engine," and chapter endings that summarize the design intent instead of leaving the reader with a rule or consequence. Several chapters have enough pieces to imply a good subsystem but not enough ordered procedure to let a GM run it cold.

The rescue should not discard the book. It should convert the current draft into finished publication prose, replace exposed design language, and harden each subsystem until it produces meaningful choices, risks, rewards, and future hooks.

## Voice and Publication Structure

### Current Voice Defects

- The text often names design categories instead of rendering them for readers: engine, framework, dial, loop, primitive, metaplot, propagation, decay valve.
- Several chapters refer to internal files, skill names, or developer documents. That breaks the illusion of a finished book.
- The voice repeats "this chapter gives..." and "the engine should..." too often. Those phrases are useful in an outline, but they sound unfinished in a supplement.
- Some strong western paragraphs end in design explanation. A good section should end on a hard table fact, a consequence, a material image, or a decision.
- The text sometimes assumes the reader already understands why the subject matters. Book 02 succeeds because it teaches the historical pressure first.

### Target Voice

Book 04 should sound like a rules supplement written by the same hand that wrote the stronger Book 02 material. The voice should be:

- concrete before abstract;
- historically specific without becoming a lecture;
- plain in rules and hard in consequences;
- flavorful in examples, clean in procedures;
- self-contained as a published book.

The ideal paragraph teaches one thing: what the West was like, what the table should track, or what happens next.

### Required Publication Structure

Every chapter should follow the same hidden discipline:

- a historical or fictional hook that explains the pressure;
- a short "when to use this" paragraph;
- the exact things tracked at the table;
- a clear procedure with trigger, choices, roll, result, consequence, and next decision;
- one worked example using the rule as written;
- tables only where tables are the best lookup form;
- final prose that points the GM toward play, not toward internal design theory.

## Rules Quality Analysis

### General Strengths

- The draft largely respects the existing game spine.
- Progress vs Trouble is the correct chassis for drives, robberies, trials, holdouts, outbreaks, wilderness disasters, and military actions.
- The Turn of the Season is the correct chassis for businesses, families, factions, weather contraction, and politics.
- Most proposed gauges use the correct small ranges or resource dice.
- The best chapters understand that profit should create future attention and obligations.

### General Failures

- Many chapters name their special resource without making it do enough. A Route Ledger, Herd die, Vein die, Reserve gauge, Influence pool, or Weather Pattern must change decisions at the table, not merely decorate a roll.
- Some procedures skip steps. A chapter may explain what is tracked and what success means but omit who chooses risk, when costs are paid, how often the roll occurs, or what happens after a partial result.
- Some options are strategy labels instead of play procedures. They need costs, limits, and consequences.
- Several systems risk rewarding the same behavior every season: invest in output, take profit, repeat. Each enterprise needs pressure that makes caution, repair, reputation, reserves, and growth compete.
- Faction and influence mechanics need strict anti-farming rules. Public acts, seasonal decay, and one-debt-call limits are good starts, but the text must prevent easy Influence generation and repeated no-cost asks.
- Large-scale systems need a stronger GM burden check. If a chapter asks the GM to track too many moving parts without a seasonal order of operations, it will not survive play.

## Game-Theory and Balance Risks

### Enterprise Chapters

Enterprise play must not become "pick the highest profit option every season." Each trade needs a real triangle or square of competing goods.

- Freighting: margin, safety, schedule, route reputation.
- Ranching: herd size, herd quality, grass, labor peace, rustler pressure.
- Mining: ore, safety, dust, timber, labor politics.
- Banking: profit from loans, reserves, trust, robbery exposure.
- Saloon: revenue, Refuge, law attention, worker welfare, faction heat.

Each season should force a pre-roll commitment. If investment happens after the roll, the risk vanishes.

### Faction and Social Capital

Standing is stable relationship. Influence is spendable current favor. Debt is an obligation with a named creditor. These three must not blur.

Risks:

- Influence farming through repeated trivial public acts.
- Debt calls becoming GM punishment instead of proportionate pressure.
- Propagation causing runaway Standing cascades.
- Too many factions creating bookkeeping without better play.

Corrections:

- cap tracked factions at three to five;
- use no-cascade ally/enemy shifts;
- require public, costly, witnessed acts for Influence;
- call at most one debt per creditor per season;
- tie every faction move to a visible rumor, letter, rider, newspaper item, wire, or scene.

### Operation Chapters

Operations should finish in roughly three to six checks. If a procedure needs more, split it into phases. Trouble must threaten the plan before success is guaranteed.

Risks:

- stage lists becoming ability check shopping lists;
- every player picking the same best ability;
- Trouble limits too high for the scale;
- failure ending play instead of changing the scene into chase, fight, exposure, debt, or partial gain.

Corrections:

- each stage presents a different kind of problem;
- the GM tags Safe, Risky, or Desperate before the roll;
- the result must advance either Progress or Trouble;
- when Trouble hits the limit, the clean plan dies but play continues.

### Moral and Historical Material

The book covers disease, atrocity, human trade, labor exploitation, genocide, lynching, and political violence. These subjects need sober publication prose, not shock value or design cleverness.

Rules should:

- track consequences on perpetrators, communities, factions, law, and survivors;
- refuse to turn people into cargo;
- give the GM procedures for refusal, rescue, testimony, exposure, and aftermath;
- keep historical specificity without flattening peoples into symbols.

## Priority Order

1. `00-introduction.md` because it sets the book's promise and currently exposes internal scaffolding.
2. `13-factions-and-standing.md` because many chapters depend on it.
3. `05`, `06`, `07`, `09` because they define the enterprise model.
4. `14` and `22` because they define long-term household/county pressure.
5. `23` because it handles the largest conflict scale.
6. Scene and operation chapters (`01`, `02`, `10`, `11`, `19`, `20`) once the shared language is clean.
7. Historically sensitive chapters (`12`, `24`, `25`) with extra care and restraint.

## Acceptance Standard

A chapter is ready only when a GM can read it once and answer:

- when do I use this;
- what do I track;
- what choices do players make;
- what roll or procedure resolves the matter;
- what changes on the campaign sheet afterward;
- what happens on failure that remains playable;
- why this subject feels like the 1870s and not a generic western.
