# Chapter 18 — Mining Camps

> _“THE STEEL RANG AGAINST THE ROCK AND THE DUST CAME OFF THE FACE AND THE CARBIDE LAMP THREW THEIR SHADOWS LONG ON THE TIMBERED WALLS. THEY HAD BEEN AT IT SIX HOURS. THEY HAD TWO MORE TO GO.”_

The mine happens in the dark, in noise, in rock dust, in a space few outside the trade have stood in. What follows is the hardrock camp of the 1870s and 1880s — what kind of mine, what the underground looks like, the eight hours of a shift, the men in their order, the surface camp, the boarding house, the company store, the Cornish, the union and the Pinkertons, the hazards, and the vocabulary of the work.

---

## 1. What Kind of Mine

The 1870s West had two kinds of hard-rock mining and one kind of soft.

- **Hardrock gold and silver** is what the fiction almost always means: an underground mine following a vein of ore in consolidated rock — granite, quartzite, porphyry. Nevada (Comstock), Colorado (Leadville, Cripple Creek), Arizona (Tombstone, Globe), Idaho (Coeur d'Alene), Montana (Butte) are the canonical districts.
- **Placer gold** is worked at the surface — streams and gravels, the prospector's world (covered in Chapter 7).
- **Coal** is a different world — underground, shaft-based, but in softer sedimentary rock, often in company towns with a different ethnic and political character. Coal does not appear in western fiction nearly as often as silver and gold.

This chapter covers the hardrock mine.

---

## 2. The Underground

### The shaft

The mine enters the mountain by a **shaft**: a vertical opening, rectangular (six feet by eight feet in most operations), lined with square-set timber from the top down. The shaft drops level by level — the 500-foot level, the 700-foot level, the 1000-foot level. Each level is a horizontal floor in the mine. The men ride the **cage** — an iron platform on a wire rope — up and down the shaft. The hoist engineer at the surface controls the cage. At depth: the air is hot. Below 500 feet in many Comstock mines the temperature reached 120°F. The Comstock had to pipe ice water underground. In most 1870s mines the deep levels were simply hot, wet, and dark.

### Drifts and crosscuts

From each level, **drifts** run horizontally along the vein. A drift is a tunnel with a roof and two walls and a floor, square-set with timber. Seven feet high, six feet wide in a well-run operation. The floor has a pair of iron rails. The **mucking car** — a steel tub holding half a ton of broken ore — runs on those rails. A man pushes it to the ore chute at the shaft.

**Crosscuts** run perpendicular to the drift, cutting across rock to reach a new vein or to connect workings. They are driven blind — you do not know if the vein is there until the drill and powder tell you.

### Stopes, raises, winzes

A **stope** is the open working space left behind as ore is removed. Where the drift follows the vein horizontally, the stope opens vertically — the ore taken out above and below the drift line. A stope might be twenty feet high and sixty feet long. Square-set timbers hold it open. Without the timbers the stope collapses.

A **raise** is a vertical opening driven upward from a lower level toward an upper one. A **winze** is driven downward. They are connection shafts, ventilation paths, and ore-pass chutes. A raise is driven by a man lying on his back on a plank, drilling upward into the rock above his face. The dust falls into his eyes.

### Square-set timbering

Deidesheimer's square-set system (Comstock, 1860) was the engineering solution that made deep hardrock mining viable. The timbers are pre-cut to standard lengths and fit together at right angles like a three-dimensional grid — each set interlocking with its neighbors above, below, and to the sides. A stope can be held open at any width. The system travels west with every mine that opens after 1862. The smell of wet pine and fresh-cut Douglas fir is the smell of a working mine — the timberman's crew always one level behind the miners, setting new sets as the ore comes out.

## 3. The Shift — Eight Hours

### First call

The day shift comes on at six in the morning. The men change in the **change house** — a low building near the shaft collar where they swap their street clothes for their working clothes. The working clothes are wool in winter (wool dries from the inside out), duck canvas in summer. The boots are heavy leather, nailed with hobnails for traction on wet timber. Each man carries his own candle holder — a bent spike that can be jammed into a timber crack — and a candle, or in better-equipped mines by the late 1870s, a carbide lamp on his hat. The carbide lamp makes a white acetylene flame and a soft hissing sound and smells of a struck match.

### Going down

The men crowd the cage — six men, plus the shift boss who rides with them to the working level. The cage drops fast enough to lift the stomach. The shaft walls flicker past the candle's range. At depth the air changes: warmer, damper, carrying the smell of spent powder from last night's shift and the underlying rock smell — iron, wet clay, sometimes sulfur.

### The drill

Most of the shift is drilling. The miners work in pairs: one man holds the **drill steel** — a tapered iron rod — against the rock face and turns it a quarter revolution after each blow. The other man swings the **sledge** — a four-pound hammer in the single-jack, an eight-pounder in the double-jack. The rhythm is the mine's heartbeat: strike, turn, strike, turn. The steel goes in an inch at a time. A twelve-inch hole takes twenty minutes. The noise in the drift — the ringing of the steel, the bass thud of the sledge on the striker's end — carries through the rock.

- **Single-jack** work is a man alone: small hammer, lighter steel, slower. Used in tight spaces, in raises where a second man cannot stand.
- **Double-jack** work requires trust. If the striker misses the steel, the holder's hands take the blow. The men who have worked together for years do not look at each other during the strike. They listen for the rhythm.

By the 1870s, mechanical drills powered by compressed air are appearing in the larger mines. The men call them **widow makers** because the water injection that dampens the dust often ran dry and the dry-drill dust went into the lungs. A man on a widow maker in an unventilated stope was breathing his future silicosis with every breath.

### Loading and firing

When the holes are drilled — a face receives eight to twelve holes in a pattern the shift boss has marked — the **powder man** loads them. Black powder until the early 1870s; dynamite after 1867, increasingly common in the West through the 1870s. The powder man tamps the charge, cuts the fuse, and begins at the holes farthest from the exit. Each fuse is timed so the shots fire in sequence — the cut holes in the center first, the relievers next, the perimeter holes last. The sequence breaks the rock efficiently. A bad sequence wastes powder or leaves a wall standing.

The shift boss counts the men out of the drift. The powder man lights the fuses. He counts: _One fuse burning. Two fuses burning. Out. Run._ The rule is never to run before all fuses are lit. The rule is never to walk. Between the two is a quarter of the dead men in any mine.

The shots go off in sequence: a series of flat concussions felt in the chest before they are heard, the rock breaking in waves. Dust and smoke fill the drift. The men wait outside the entry on the level until the smoke clears — twenty minutes at minimum, longer in poor ventilation. Then the muckers go in.

### Mucking

The **muckers** are the bottom of the mine's hierarchy and the hardest-worked men in it. After a blast, the face is a pile of broken rock — **muck** — extending back ten or fifteen feet. The mucker loads the muck into the car by hand with a square-faced iron shovel. The car holds half a ton. He pushes it to the ore chute and tips it. He goes back and does it again. For eight hours. The mucker eats his lunch underground, sitting on a rock with the candle between his knees. He does not usually speak to the men around him. He has not enough to say.

### End of shift

The whistle at the shaft. The men ride the cage up. Daylight again. The change house. The walk to the boarding house or the saloon. The miner's day is either underground or sleeping off underground. There is not much middle.

## 4. The Hierarchy

A working mine of any size has a distinct chain.

The **superintendent** is the manager of the whole operation — often an engineer (the Comstock and later mines were managed by graduates of the Columbia School of Mines or the Freiberg Mining Academy in Saxony). He answers to the owners or the board.

The **shift boss** is the foreman underground. He assigns men to faces, decides which drifts to advance and which to hold, knows the mine's geology better than the superintendent. A good shift boss is irreplaceable. He is usually the highest-paid hourly worker in the mine.

The **miner** (first-class miner, or just "miner") is the skilled man who drills, fires, and reads rock. Paid $3–$4 a day in the 1870s. He is not a laborer; he is a tradesman. He knows the difference between good ground and bad, can read a vein's dip and strike, knows when the timbers are talking.

The **powder man** is sometimes a specialized role, sometimes the senior miner on a face. The man who handles the dynamite is the man the crew does not argue with.

The **timberman** sets the square-set. As skilled as the miner in his way — he reads the rock's pressure and knows where to put the timber so it does not move. The miner and the timberman work in sequence: the miner opens the ground, the timberman holds it open.

The **mucker** is the unskilled laborer at the face. Often a recent immigrant: Cornish, Irish, Italian, Croatian, Mexican. The job that kills the back before the lungs go. A mucker who lasts three years becomes a miner. Most do not last three years.

The **hoist man** operates the cage. Never seen underground by anyone who matters. He controls the lives of every man in the mine at the moment of the shift change and at every emergency. A good hoist man knows from the feel of the rope whether the cage is loaded or empty, whether it is stopping on a timber or a man.

The **blacksmith** is the mine's own smith, who resharpens the drill steels. He works at the surface, at a coal-fired forge, sharpening steel all shift. One blacksmith can keep four or five drilling teams supplied. A mine that runs out of sharp steels stops.
