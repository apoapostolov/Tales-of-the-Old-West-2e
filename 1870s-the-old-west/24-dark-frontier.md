# Chapter 24 — The Dark Frontier

> _"WALK INTO THE COUNTRY. SET THE CAMERA. RECORD WHAT IS THERE. WALK BACK OUT."_
>
> — A WORKING NOTE ON HARD CONTENT

This is the harder chapter. The non-negotiable rule for everything that follows: this material exists in fiction to render the country's truth. It does not exist for the reader's titillation. The scene that exists for the reader to enjoy the cruelty has failed. The scene that records what happened with the camera at the right distance and the prose stripped of editorial weight is the scene that earns its place. If a scene cannot be cut without removing a fact the rest of the book depends on, write it. If it can be cut without removing such a fact, cut it.

This chapter names what the country contained: sexual violence, lynching and pogrom, madness, torture, cannibalism, the drug economies, domestic killing, the abuse of children, slavery beyond the Civil War, the starving homestead, the institutional cruelty to animals. None of it is offered as spectacle. All of it is on the historical record.

---

## 1. The Four Exploitation Tells

Before the content sections: the patterns that mark dark material as exploitation rather than truth-telling.

**The camera lingers where it does not need to.** The rape scene that follows the rapist's hands. The torture scene that catalogs each cut. The lynching scene that holds on the kicking feet. The reader is being asked to consume the violence as spectacle. The corrective is to frame the scene on the witness or the aftermath and to refuse the long take on the act.

**The prose registers pleasure.** Adjectives go liquid. Sentences lengthen. The verbs become caressing (_traced, lingered, opened_). The narrator is enjoying the material. Cut to one-syllable verbs and short declarative sentences. The country's cruelty is recorded in the same prose that records the weather.

**The victim has no name and no interior.** A rape victim who exists for one chapter and is never mentioned again has been used by the author as the reader is being shown the rapist using her. If she enters the book, she stays in the book until her arc is done.

**The cruel act is the point.** The scene exists because the act exists. There is no consequence in the chapters that follow — no changed character, no buried debt, no town that has not forgotten. The reader feels the scene was a stunt. Cut it or rewrite it so the cruelty is folded into a story that takes it seriously.

## 2. Sexual Violence

The frontier was not safer for women than the city was. It was less safe in specific ways: fewer witnesses, no police, weaker law, isolation, the captivity economy on both sides of the Native-Anglo line.

**The categories the period contained.** _Marital and domestic rape_ — not named as a crime in any western jurisdiction in the period; a husband had legal access to his wife's body; a woman had no recourse beyond the husband himself. _Rape of women on the line_ — the cribs, the dance halls, the parlor houses; coercion was the economic floor; a woman who refused a customer the madam wanted served could be beaten or thrown out, which on the line meant the ditch. _Rape on the road and the trail_ — stage stations, freight camps, the open road between settlements; a woman traveling alone was understood to be at risk and was usually warned not to travel alone. _Rape in captivity, both directions_ — Anglo women taken by raiding parties; Native women taken by Anglo soldiers, hunters, and freighters; Mexican women in the borderlands taken by Anglo Texans and by Mexican bandits; the captivity narrative as a literary form has scrubbed most of this; the period reality did not. _Rape of children_ — the orphan, the bound-out girl, the child of the line woman, the Native child taken from the reservation school dormitory; predator clergy, predator schoolmasters, predator employers; the period had few words for this and almost no remedy. _Sex slavery_ — the Chinese sing-song houses of San Francisco, Portland, and the smaller Pacific cities ran on girls bought in Guangdong, shipped under the name of laundry workers, and sold into brothels for terms of contracted years from which most did not survive; the Mormon polygamous community contained genuine abuse cases alongside its legitimate marriages; New Mexico's debt peonage system held women as well as men.

The canon's hard rule on rape on the page: render through the witness or through the aftermath. The act itself is almost never the camera's subject. The reader knows what the camera is showing without needing to see.

A working passage in the canon's register:

> They found her at the spring at first light. She had crawled from where the men had left her to the water and she was still trying to pull her dress around her with the hand she could move. The boy who found her ran for his mother and did not come back to the spring for a year.

The scene contains everything: what happened, what was done, what the witness will carry. No description of the act. No image of the men. The boy's failure to return to the spring is the moral weight.

The aftermath is more important than the act in almost every case. Render it through the body's marks (the limp the woman keeps, the silence she keeps, the wound that does not close properly), through the marriage that does or does not survive, through the way the town remembers, through the daughter who hears the story when she is old enough.

**The hard cases.** _The rapist as a major character_ — the rapist is given his work and his interior insofar as the story requires it; he is not shown enjoying his work on the page; the author refuses the seduction. _The victim as the protagonist's lover later_ — the trope of "she fell in love with the man who saved her" plus earlier rape is a 1970s pulp move; if your character survives sexual violence and finds love later, the path is long, the scars are real, and the love itself is altered by what happened. _Captive narratives_ — Cynthia Ann Parker's actual life is the model: taken at nine, became Comanche, married, bore Quanah, was forcibly taken back at thirty-four, starved herself to death; the captivity that the captive did not want to be rescued from is the real story the white pulp invented its way around.

## 3. Lynching, Mob Violence, and the Pogrom

The vigilance committee, the lynch mob, and the racial pogrom were institutions of the West. They were not aberrations. In many western towns they were the operating mode of community self-defense and racial enforcement.

**The vigilance committee.** A formalized extralegal body. San Francisco's was the most famous (1851 and 1856), but they appeared in any western town where the citizens decided the law was inadequate. The membership was the town's commercial elite. The targets were the local criminal element, sometimes a corrupt official, often a man whose only crime was that he stood in the way of the committee's business interests. The committee held a trial. The trial was a formality. The hanging took place at the committee's chosen tree, often at night, often with the town watching. The bodies were left up overnight as a notice.

**The lynch mob.** Less organized than the committee. A man accused of horse theft, of murder, of cattle stealing, of an assault on a white woman — taken from the jail by the town's men, walked to the nearest tree, hanged. The sheriff sometimes resisted and was sometimes complicit. The men of the mob went home and ate supper and were not prosecuted.

The targets of the lynch mob in the West, by frequency:

- **Suspected horse thieves.** The largest single category. Every horse thief lynched was understood to be a deterrent to the next.
- **Black men accused of any crime against a white person.** The post-Reconstruction lynching pattern that defined the South was practiced in the West too. The numbers were smaller because the Black population was smaller. The ratio was comparable.
- **Mexicans.** The 1850s and 1860s in California saw a sustained anti-Mexican lynching campaign that historians estimate killed at least 350 men. The period's Anglo newspapers reported the killings with approval.
- **Chinese.** The Rock Springs massacre (Wyoming, 1885) killed at least twenty-eight Chinese miners. The Hells Canyon massacre (Oregon, 1887) killed at least thirty-four. Smaller pogroms against Chinese workers happened in dozens of western towns through the 1870s and 1880s.
- **Native Americans.** Often killed without trial in any context: the trader who killed a man who came to his post to barter, the rancher who killed a Native who crossed his land, the scouting party that found a small camp.
- **Italians, Irish, Slavs, anarchists.** Less frequent but documented. The 1891 New Orleans lynching of eleven Italian immigrants was the largest single mass lynching in U.S. history and was widely celebrated in the press.

**The pogrom.** When the mob acted against an entire community rather than an accused individual, the event was a pogrom. The Chinese quarters of dozens of western towns were burned and their inhabitants driven out: Tacoma (1885), Seattle (1886), Eureka (1885), and many smaller. Property destroyed. Residents killed or run out of town with what they could carry.

The canon's hard rule: the lynching is not redemptive. The crowd does not learn anything. The lynched man's death does not balance any account. The scene is rendered through the witness who tries to stop it and fails, or through the witness who does not try, or through the body in the morning.

> They had cut him down before sun. The marshal had not been there when the men came for the jail and was not there when they brought the body to the carpenter for the box. The marshal was at his sister's place outside of town and would say later that he had not known. Pruitt knew that he had known.

The pogrom is rendered in numbers and specifics: the count of the dead, the count of the burned buildings, the count of the displaced. The reader's horror is in the count.

## 4. Madness on the Frontier

The West produced madness at a rate the East did not. The combination of isolation, deprivation, weather, displacement, loss, and the absence of community structures broke many people. The period's word was _mania_ or _brain fever_ or _nostalgia._ The institutional response was the territorial asylum (Yuma in Arizona, Pueblo in Colorado, Stockton in California, the federal asylum at St. Elizabeths in Washington for federal employees including soldiers).

**Prairie madness.** The form of breakdown specific to the homestead wife on the high plains. The condition was real and was named in the period: women who had been transplanted from a town in Ohio or Iowa to a soddy in western Nebraska or Dakota Territory went strange under the combined weight of isolation, the ceaseless wind, the unbroken horizon, and the absence of other women. The husband worked the field or rode the line. The wife was alone in the soddy with the wind and the children for days at a time.

The progression: first a withdrawal from speech, then a fixation on a small object or small task, then a refusal to go outside or a compulsion to walk on the prairie at night, then, in the worst cases, the wife found by the husband seated by the cold stove with the children unfed and the fire out, or walking on the prairie in the snow without a shawl and not knowing why. The period writers who knew this from the inside: O. E. Rolvaag's _Giants in the Earth_ (the wife Beret), Mari Sandoz's _Old Jules_ (her own mother), Willa Cather's _My Antonia_ and _O Pioneers!_ (the suicides at the edge of the narrative).

> She had not spoken since the second week of March. The cow was unmilked and the children were eating cold biscuit and the wind was in the stove pipe. Halvor stood in the door with his hat in his hand and looked at her sitting where she had been sitting since first light.

**The line shack rider.** The cowhand sent to ride a line shack alone for the winter, checking fence and pushing strays back to the home pasture. The shack: one room, a stove, a bunk, a dirt floor, a window with the glass packed in newspaper against the cold. No other human within twenty miles for three months. The forms the breakdown took: talking to the horse, talking to the dog, talking to a person who was not there, the fixation on a single sound (a loose board, a wind-shake), the fear of the dark inside the shack, the long ride to a neighbor's place at the wrong time of day for no reason, the sudden refusal to ride out. Some men were suited to it. Others broke.

**The prospector alone.** The man who went into the high country with a burro and worked a placer claim by himself for years. The forms: the hoarding of small objects, the conversation with the burro that was the most important relationship in the man's life, the development of long elaborate theories about claim-jumpers who were not there, the periodic descent into a town where the man could not function around other people. Most died in their cabins and were found by the next prospector who came up the draw in spring.

**The religious mania.** The frontier produced and attracted men whose religious condition had moved past devotion into something stranger. The wandering preacher who had heard a voice and followed it into the wilderness. The visionary who could not stop preaching even when the camp had asked him to leave. The self-flagellant. The Penitente in the New Mexico borderlands whose practice the Anglo could not understand and could sometimes mistake for madness. The line between the religious mania and the regular religious intensity of the frontier (see Chapter 10) is a line the writer has to draw scene by scene. The genuine religious is rendered with respect; the mania is rendered as the breakdown it is. The character himself does not know which side of the line he is on.

**The Civil War legacy.** The veteran who came home from Shiloh or Vicksburg or the Wilderness was often broken in ways the period had no name for. Some of the worst cases ended up west: in cabins, in mining camps, on the road. The man who startles at the campfire crackling, who screams in his sleep, who cannot sit with his back to a door, who drinks until he can sleep and not before. McCarthy's _Blood Meridian_ contains some of these men. McMurtry's Captain Call carries his version.

## 5. Torture

The frontier contained torture as a practice. By all parties. The romantic western has scrubbed most of it. The honest western puts it on the page when the story requires it and not when it does not.

**Comanche and Apache practice.** Both the Comanche and the Apache practiced ritual torture of captured enemies. The practice was not aberrational; it was part of the warrior culture and was understood by both sides in the conflict. The methods included staking out, slow burning, mutilation, and the use of the captive's own family as audience when the family had also been captured. The Comanche burning of captured Texans is documented in many period sources. The Apache slow killing of captured Mexican peasants and Anglo soldiers is documented in others.

How to render it. The canon's approach: name what was done in plain words and at the length the scene requires. Do not catalog the methods. Do not give the torturer interior. Show the captive as a human person with a name and a family before the scene begins, and show the witness's face afterward.

The mistake: the long rendering of the methods that turns the scene into pornography. McCarthy's _Blood Meridian_ contains some of the most extreme violence in American literature and almost all of it is rendered in sentences shorter than the event would have taken. The brevity is the morality.

The other mistake: the implication that torture was practiced only by Native nations. Anglo and Mexican vigilantes and soldiers practiced their own forms freely. The Texas Rangers in the 1850s and 1860s were known for the slow killing of captured Mexicans and Comanches. The Sand Creek massacre (Colorado Volunteer Cavalry under Chivington, 1864) included the mutilation of women's bodies as trophies that were displayed afterward in Denver. The Wounded Knee dead were left on the field for days in a blizzard.

**Vigilante torture.** The lynch mob sometimes did more than hang. The whipping post, the dragging behind the horse, the burning of the victim alive (rare in the West, more common in the Reconstruction South). The Bisbee massacre (Arizona, 1883) saw five men hanged after a botched holdup and a sixth man lynched separately a few weeks later when the mob took him from the jail.

The cardinal rule: render the witness's face. The reader does not need the camera on the body's slow change. The reader needs the face of the man who is watching and who will have to live with what he saw. Render the aftermath. Render the smell that does not leave the witness's clothes.

## 6. Cannibalism

The West contained a small number of documented cases. The Donner-Reed party (Sierra Nevada, 1846–47) is the famous one. Alferd Packer (Colorado, 1874) killed and ate five prospecting companions and was tried twice and convicted. The smaller cases — prospectors and emigrants caught by snow, men shipwrecked off the coast, families starved on failed homesteads — left fewer records.

The historical truth: cannibalism in survival situations was not common but was not unknown, and the surviving participants almost always understood what they had done as the failure that ended their old lives. The Donner survivors included people who never spoke of it and people who became famous for having survived it. The shame did not pass.

How to render it. Two principles. **The fact is short.** The scene that describes the act is short. The bodies were already dead. The decision was made under the pressure of three weeks without food. The act was done. The prose moves on. **The afterward is long.** The character carries it. The family member who shared the act and the family member who arrived after the rescue can never speak to one another the same way again.

The Donner case is also the model for the moral framing: the party's leadership had made decisions that put the party in the position. The cannibalism was the consequence of bad decisions made in the autumn. The moral weight does not sit on the act in the snow; it sits on the route choice in August.

## 7. The Drug Economies

The West ran on alcohol and laudanum and ran into morphine and opium. The addictions were widespread and were not hidden from period readers. They have been hidden from the movie western.

**Laudanum.** A solution of opium in alcohol. Available without prescription in any drug store from the 1840s to the federal regulation of 1914. Used for everything: pain, cough, diarrhea, menstrual cramps, infant colic, the wife's nervous condition, the husband's bad back. The user did not know laudanum was opium-based in any meaningful sense. Many users became addicted without knowing they were taking the same drug as the Chinese opium den. The laudanum addict in fiction: the doctor's wife who carries a small bottle and takes a sip at intervals through the day, the line woman whose madam keeps the bottle and dispenses, the rancher's widow who started taking it for her grief and cannot now stop.

**Morphine.** Isolated 1804, manufactured commercially from the 1820s, used by injection from the 1850s after the hypodermic syringe. Civil War wounded who survived their injuries often returned home addicted; the condition was called _soldier's disease_ and it was widespread among veterans through the 1880s. The morphine user in fiction: the veteran who carries the syringe in a small case, the doctor who has access and who uses, the gambler who started with a wound and stayed with the medicine.

**Opium.** Smoked, not injected. The opium den was a real institution in the Chinese quarters of San Francisco, Portland, Denver, Tucson, and many smaller cities. The clientele was Chinese and Anglo. The Anglo customer was often a person of the local upper class slumming, sometimes a woman of the line, sometimes a working-class Anglo who had developed the habit. The den itself: a back room, low pallets, a small lamp on each pallet, the Chinese attendant who prepared each pipe. The smell was sweet and heavy and unmistakable. The Anglo backlash against the Chinese in the 1880s used the opium den as one of its rallying points. The hypocrisy was that the same Anglo establishment was prescribing morphine freely.

**Alcohol.** The medical course of frontier alcoholism. Stage one: the heavy drinker who functions. Stage two: the man whose work suffers but who still works. Stage three: the man who has lost his work and is on the slow slide. Stage four: the man on the street, the man under the porch, the man in the alley behind the saloon. Stage five: the body found in spring when the snow goes off. The canon writes the alcoholic as a character whose deterioration is the through-line: he is a man who was something once and who now is mostly the bottle.

How to render. Do not describe the drunkenness with detail. Describe the missed work. Describe the wife buying flour on credit she cannot repay. Describe the children's clothes. Describe the day the man does not come back from the saloon and the wife knows before she sees him that he is not coming back.

## 8. Domestic Violence and the Family Killing

The frontier homestead was a sealed environment. The husband was the law inside the cabin. The wife had no recourse beyond running, which usually meant running to her parents or her sister and which was often impossible because of distance. The neighbors did not interfere. The minister did not interfere unless the violence had become public.

The historical record: high rates of domestic violence, under-reported. The killings inside the cabin — the husband who killed the wife, the wife who killed the husband, the parent who killed the child, the child who killed the parent — appeared in the period coroner's reports at rates that would shock a modern reader if they were collected.

The category that the period's law could not handle: the wife who killed the husband after years of beating. The defense was almost never accepted. She hanged or she went to the territorial prison or, in the rare case, she was acquitted by a jury of men who themselves had wives.

How to render. The scene inside the cabin is rendered through the wife's perceptual range or through the child's. The husband is not given the camera. The bruise is named in plain words. The night the husband does not come home and the wife hopes he is dead is rendered in the wife's small private calculations: what flour she has, what she will tell the neighbors, what the children will say. The killing scene, when it comes, is short. The afterwards is long. The trial, the verdict, the children sent to relatives, the wife in the territorial prison at Yuma writing letters home until the letters stop.

## 9. Children

The frontier child was at risk in ways the period understood and did not understand.

**The mortality.** The graveyard of small markers behind every frontier church. Diphtheria, scarlet fever, measles, whooping cough, the intestinal infections that killed infants in summer, the accidents on the working homestead (the kick, the fall from the wagon, the scald, the burn). A frontier mother who buried three of her ten children was not unusual. A frontier mother who buried five was within the range. Name each child by name when possible. The grave marker reads the same words as the period markers read: _Aged 3 years 5 months._ The mother's grief is rendered through her work — the dress that does not get sewn, the meal that does not get cooked, the morning she does not get up.

**The bound-out child and the orphan train.** The orphan train (1854–1929) carried about 250,000 children from East Coast cities to placement on western farms. The placements were not adoptions. The children were taken into households as workers and were expected to earn their keep. The good placements were genuine families in the receiving sense; the bad placements were free labor under conditions ranging from harsh to abusive. The bound-out child was a similar case at the local level: the orphan whose parents had died and who was placed by the county with a farm family who took him in exchange for his labor until he reached majority. The contract was binding. The child had no recourse. The categories of abuse the bound-out child faced: beating, overwork to the point of disability, sexual abuse, withholding of food, withholding of clothes adequate for winter, denial of schooling. The child could run — and often did, ending up on the street in a frontier town or, worse, back into a similar placement at the next county over.

**The line woman's child.** The child born to a woman of the line was raised by the mother in the parlor house or by a wet nurse hired with the mother's earnings. The child of the line had no legal father and no inheritance. The boys often ran away to the open country at twelve or thirteen and found work as horse-holders, camp boys, then cowhands. The girls often went into the trade themselves at fifteen or sixteen.

**Abuse the period did not name.** The period had almost no concept of child sexual abuse as a prosecutable category. The cases that made the records were the most extreme — the prosecution of a man for what would now be called rape of a child usually required that the child had also been physically wounded. Most cases of abuse went unprosecuted and unspoken. The same rules as adult sexual violence above. The camera does not follow the act. The aftermath is rendered through the changed child: the silence, the wetting, the nightmares, the avoidance of a particular adult, the school performance that falls. The adult perpetrator is not given interior. The child is the protagonist of his or her own healing or not-healing through whatever follows.

## 10. Slavery Beyond the Civil War

Race-based chattel slavery in the U.S. was abolished in 1865. Slavery as an institution did not disappear from the West.

**Comanche and other Native slave economies.** The Comanche raided into Mexico and into the Anglo settlements through the 1840s, 1850s, and 1860s and took captives as slaves. The captives were used for labor, for sexual service, for adoption into the band, and for trade with other nations. The Comanche economy through the mid-19th century included slave-trading with the Pueblos, the Diné (Navajo), and the Anglo and Mexican traders at the Comanchero markets. The Apache, Ute, and other nations practiced similar slave economies at smaller scales.

**Diné (Navajo) and Pueblo slavery.** The Diné raided into the Pueblo and Spanish and Mexican communities and took captives. The Pueblo and Spanish raided back. The system of mutual captive-taking created a class of _genízaros_ — captives who were neither fully their birth nation nor fully their captor nation — that persisted in New Mexico into the 1880s. The federal government formally abolished the system in 1867. The system continued in practice for two more decades.

**Debt peonage.** The peón system in New Mexico, Texas, and Arizona held workers in legally enforceable debt to a _patrón._ The worker could not leave the patrón's land until the debt was paid. The debt was structured so that it could not be paid. The system was formally outlawed by federal law in 1867 and continued in practice well into the twentieth century in the Southwest. The cases the courts addressed: a worker who tried to leave was returned by the sheriff to the patrón. A worker's children inherited the debt. A worker's daughter could be required to work in the patrón's household as part of the debt service, with all the consequences such an arrangement implies.

**Chinese sex slavery.** The Chinese sex slavery trade ran from Guangdong province to San Francisco and Portland from the 1850s through the Exclusion period. Girls aged twelve to fifteen were bought from impoverished families, shipped under fraudulent papers, and sold in San Francisco for $300 to $1,000 each. They worked in the _sing-song_ houses of the Chinese quarter under contracts that were structured never to be paid off. The average working life was four to five years. Most died of disease, violence, or self-inflicted opium overdose. The Chinese-language reformer Donaldina Cameron and her mission rescued some of these women in the 1880s and after.

The character does not deliver a speech about the system. The character lives in it.

## 11. The Starving Homestead

The homestead failure that ended in death by starvation. The period had a name for it (_played out_). The historical record has many cases.

The progression: a homestead claim taken in spring, the first year's crop a partial failure, the savings exhausted to buy through the winter, the second year's planting on insufficient seed and worse soil, the second year's crop a worse failure. By the autumn of the second year the family is in a position where the husband has to choose between selling the team (without which the homestead is finished) and watching the children go hungry through the winter.

The deaths came in late winter and early spring. The family had eaten the seed corn. The cow had been killed and eaten by February. The husband had ridden out to look for work and not returned. The wife and the children stayed in the soddy. The oldest child died first, then the next, then the wife, then the youngest. The neighbor who came up the trace in April found the dugout and the bodies.

The scene is rendered through the wife's calculations. The flour barrel. The salt pork that has been gone since January. The youngest child's cough. The decision not to write to her sister in Indiana for help because she cannot bear to write the letter that would say what she has to say. The death itself is rendered briefly. The aftermath is the neighbor finding the family. The neighbor's report to the county. The graves dug in ground still half-frozen. The husband, when he is found later in a different county where he had walked in search of work, who has to be told what happened.

The canon: Hamlin Garland's _Main-Travelled Roads_ and _Son of the Middle Border._ O. E. Rolvaag's _Giants in the Earth._ Mari Sandoz's _Old Jules._

## 12. Animal Cruelty as Institution

This section names the institutional practices that were entertainment or industry rather than necessity.

**The pit fights.** Bear-and-bull fights in the California ranchos and the early Anglo California of the 1840s and 1850s. A grizzly bear chained by a hind leg to a stake; a fighting bull released into the pit; the bull gored the bear or the bear killed the bull. Sometimes bear-and-dogs. Sometimes bull-and-dogs. The audience paid admission. The fights continued in California through the 1860s and persisted in Mexican rural areas into the 1880s. Cockfighting was universal across the West. Dogfighting was common in the cities. The audience was male, working class, and gambled.

**The systematic killing of horses.** The cavalry pursuit of a Native band on the southern Plains was understood by both sides as a contest of horseflesh. The pursuit ran the horses to death, killed and ate the dead horses, and continued on whatever horses could be commandeered. The 1874 Red River War destroyed the horse herds of the Comanche, Kiowa, and Cheyenne by deliberate slaughter — at Palo Duro alone the Army shot over a thousand captured ponies. The break of the Plains nations was in part the break of their horses.

**The buffalo slaughter.** Covered also in Chapter 1. The relevant point for this chapter: the slaughter was not the by-product of settlement. It was a deliberate federal policy executed by commercial hunters on contract. The hide hunters of the 1870s killed an estimated fifteen million buffalo in a decade. The bones were collected afterward and shipped east for fertilizer. The plains were emptied as policy. The hunter who knows what he is doing is doing it for the money is the character. The hunter who has noticed that the shooting has lost its character as hunting and has become extermination is the character. The Native witness to the slaughter is the character. The white settler who sees the bones piled at the railhead and says nothing is the character.

## 13. The Author's Stance

The discipline is restraint at the local scale. A book that contains a rape, a torture, a child's abuse, a starvation death, and a lynching is not necessarily an exploitation book. A book that contains any one of these and lingers on it for the reader's enjoyment is. The test is the camera. The test is the prose. The test is the consequence.

The other discipline is the refusal of the redemption arc. The dark material in a serious western fiction does not get balanced by a happy ending. The lynching does not produce a town that learned. The rape does not produce a love story. The starving family does not get found in time. The cannibal does not become respectable. The country contained these things, and the country was not redeemed by containing them.

The serious western that includes the dark material includes it because the country contained it and because the story cannot be told without it. The reader is being shown what was. The author does not perform horror at what was. The author does not invite the reader to enjoy what was. The author records, at the right distance, in the right prose, and goes on.

---

## 14. The Rule the Others Serve

The dark frontier is a country the canon writes when the canon writes the country honestly. The writer who refuses to go there is writing a softer book than the country contained. The writer who lives there is writing a worse book than the country contained.

The country contained sustained love and casual cruelty in the same towns on the same days. It contained mothers who sang to their children and mothers who killed them. It contained men who built schools and men who hanged Chinese. It contained the silent woman at the cold stove and the silent man on the line shack porch and the silent child behind the barn. It contained more silence than a book can hold.

The writer chooses what silences to break. Each silence broken is an argument for breaking that silence and not another. The writer who can defend each scene is the writer whose dark material earns its place.

The dark is not the country's whole truth. It is part of the country's whole truth. The book that contains it truthfully is the book the canon recognizes. The book that performs it for effect is the book the canon walks away from.

## Vignette — The Cold Stove

> She had not spoken since the second week of March. The cow was unmilked and the children were eating cold biscuit and the wind was in the stove pipe. Halvor stood in the door with his hat in his hand and looked at her sitting where she had been sitting since first light.
>
> He said her name once. She did not turn her head.
>
> The oldest girl came up from the pen with a bucket of water and stopped in the door behind her father and looked past him at her mother. She set the bucket on the floor without a sound and went back out and stood by the corral and did not go in again until he called her at supper.
>
> Halvor crossed the room and put his hand on his wife's shoulder. The shoulder was very thin under the dress. He stood there a moment and then he went to the stove and built up the fire because the fire had gone out and the room was cold.
