# Chapter 17 — Horse Culture

> _“BOWMAN THREW THE BLANKET ON. HE SET THE SADDLE ON TOP AND SLID IT BACK ALONG THE BAY'S SPINE UNTIL IT SAT RIGHT. THE BAY HAD LEARNED TO PUFF AND HELD HIS BREATH AGAINST THE RIG. BOWMAN STOOD WITH HIS SHOULDER TO THE BAY'S SHOULDER AND WAITED HIM OUT.”_

The horse in the West was the principal means of transportation, the principal beast of burden, the principal instrument of war, the principal status object, the principal subject of pride and theft and ceremony. A western that does not get the horse right is a western that has not done its work. This chapter is the reference for the horse on the page in his full economic, cultural, and physical reality — beyond what Chapter 15 (saddling, riding, trail care) and Chapter 16 (the cowhand's string) already cover. Breeds and types, mustanging, the cavalry remount, racing, theft, the trade, the equipment, the ceremony, the vocabulary, and the relationship.

---

## 1. The Breeds and Types

The 1870s West contained a specific population of horse types. Not breed in the modern registered sense — most horses were unregistered grade animals — but type, with characteristic conformation, ancestry, and work.

### The mustang

The descendant of horses brought to the Americas by the Spanish from the sixteenth century onward and gone wild on the open ranges of Texas, the Southwest, and the Great Basin. By the 1870s, mustang herds numbered in the millions across the West.

The conformation was small (thirteen to fourteen hands typical, fourteen to fifteen at the larger end), tough, sure-footed, intelligent, often stubborn. Coat colors ran every color, with the strong Spanish lines (paint, dun, buckskin, grulla) common. When broken — a serious undertaking; see below — the mustang made a tough trail horse, cow horse, and Indian war horse. Plains Indians' horses were predominantly mustang stock crossed with whatever the herd had absorbed. The mustang was not large or strong enough for heavy farm work or fast enough for the formal race meet; the thoroughbred and the draft horse filled those roles.

### The quarter horse in formation

The American quarter horse as a recognized breed dates from the 1940s, but the type — short-distance racer with quarter-mile sprinting speed — was established in colonial Virginia and carried West with the cattle trade. By the 1870s the type was concentrated in Texas, where the working cow horse drew on this stock heavily. The Steeldust line (descended from a stallion named Steel Dust brought to Texas in the 1840s) and the Copperbottom line were the principal foundations. A "Steeldust" or "Copperbottom" cow horse meant a specific quality: compact, heavy-quartered, fast out of a stand, intelligent on a cow, easily trained. The 1870s cowhand on a top horse was on a Steeldust or a Steeldust cross.

### The Morgan

A New England type traced to the stallion Justin Morgan (foaled 1789). Compact, muscular, willing, versatile. Brought West in numbers from the 1840s onward and used as a saddle horse, light harness horse, cavalry horse, and family horse. The U.S. Cavalry remount service prized the Morgan and Morgan-cross horses.

### The thoroughbred

The English race horse line. In the West, used primarily for racing and as a bloodline improver — the thoroughbred stallion bred to grade mares to add height, speed, and refinement. Not common as a working horse — too leggy, too thin-skinned, too fragile for cow work or rough country. The Kentucky-bred thoroughbred coming West for the race tracks of San Francisco, Denver, and the mining camps was a recognizable type and a substantial trade.

### The draft horse

The heavy work horse. Percheron (French), Belgian, Clydesdale (Scots), and crosses thereof. Brought West for plowing, freight hauling, mine work, and heavy team work. The freight wagons of the period ran on heavy teams — six to twelve animals, horses or mules — pulling loaded wagons across country. The draft horse was not common on the open range but was substantial in the agricultural districts and on the freight roads.

### The mule

Not a horse but central to western transportation. The mule (horse-donkey hybrid, sterile) combined the horse's size and strength with the donkey's sure-footedness, hardiness, and intelligence. The mule was the Army's principal pack animal, the freighter's principal team animal in rough country, and the miner's principal mine animal. The Missouri mule was the famous American type — tall, strong, well-conformed; a Missouri mule team was the freight industry's standard.

### The criollo and the Spanish horse

In the Southwest, the Hispanic ranches ran horses of direct Spanish descent — the criollo type. Tough, small, well-suited to rough country and to the vaquero's working style. Many of the West's best cow horses had criollo blood through Texas stock that had absorbed Mexican animals over generations.

### The Indian pony

Not a separate breed but a type: the Plains Indian's horse, mustang in foundation, often well-cared-for within the limits of the band's resources, valued for specific qualities. The buffalo runner — the horse trained to ride into a buffalo herd — was a specialist animal of high value. The war horse was another specialist. The travois horse and the ordinary camp horse were less prestigious. A Comanche or Cheyenne war horse was as carefully chosen and trained as a thoroughbred race horse. The contempt with which white memoirists sometimes speak of "Indian ponies" misses the level of horsemanship and selection involved.

## 2. Mustanging

The capture and breaking of wild horses — a substantial industry in the West through the late nineteenth century.

### Capture

**The walk-down.** A team of mustangers (typically three to six men, multiple horses each) located a wild herd and pursued it on a relay basis. Each rider would push the herd until his horse tired, then drop back and a fresh rider took over. The wild herd, unable to rest, eventually exhausted itself and could be driven into a corral or caught individually. A walk-down might take days.

**The trap corral.** A corral built in a canyon or draw with wing fences leading into it. The herd was driven into the wings and on into the corral, the gate slammed shut behind. Required scouting to find herds that used predictable water or travel routes.

**The water trap.** A corral built around a water source the herd used. The mustangers waited until the herd entered to drink, then closed the gate. Effective in dry country where water was scarce.

**Roping individuals.** A mounted rider on a fast, tough horse cut individual animals from the herd and roped them. The roped horse was thrown, hobbled, and held until it could be led back to camp.

### Breaking

The captured mustang was broken to the saddle by a procedure varying by region and by breaker. The general sequence ran in three stages. First, **hold and handle**: the wild horse was held in a small corral and handled — touched, talked to, eventually halter-broken — over days or weeks. Some breakers skipped this and went straight to the next step. Second, **saddle and ride**: the horse was saddled (often with considerable bucking the first times), then mounted; the first rides were rough — the horse bucked until exhausted, then was ridden in a circle until it would respond to the reins. Third, **refine**: subsequent rides taught the horse to neck-rein, to stop, to back, to side-pass. A well-broken horse was the result of weeks or months of patient work.

The "buckaroo" tradition (Northwest, derived from California vaquero practice) emphasized slow, careful breaking with the hackamore (bosal) and spade bit, producing a highly responsive horse over years. The Texas tradition was rougher and faster. Both produced working horses; the buckaroo horse was more finished.

### Trade

A broken mustang sold for $10 to $25 in the 1870s, depending on quality and region. A top cow horse might bring $50 to $100. The mustanging trade supplied the cavalry remount service, the cattle ranches, and the general horse market.

## 3. The Cavalry Remount

The U.S. Cavalry needed a constant supply of horses. The 1870s remount service operated through contracts with regional suppliers who delivered horses meeting Army specifications: height fifteen to sixteen hands; weight 950 to 1,150 pounds; age four to eight years; sound — no major conformation faults, sound feet, legs, eyes, wind; color solid (bay, sorrel, brown, black, gray) — no paints, pintos, or appaloosas in the regular cavalry, though officer's privilege might allow exceptions; temperament trainable, not vicious or spoiled.

Contractors (often substantial ranchers with horse operations) bought from many smaller producers and delivered consolidated remounts to the Army at a contracted price (typically $100 to $150 per horse in the 1870s). A failed delivery — sick horses, horses that did not meet specifications — was rejected on Army inspection.

Each trooper was issued a horse on enlistment or arrival at his unit. The trooper was responsible for the horse's care: feeding, watering, grooming, shoeing (assisted by the company farrier), veterinary attention. A trooper who abused his horse was disciplined; a trooper whose horse died of neglect or injury might be fined the horse's value. The cavalry horse worked hard. A campaign might mean weeks of long marches, short rations, no shelter. Horse mortality on hard campaigns was substantial — twenty to forty percent of the cavalry's horses might be lost on a major expedition through disease, exhaustion, accident, or combat. (For the trooper's life around the horse, see Chapter 19.)

## 4. Horse Racing

A major western entertainment, especially in Texas, the Southwest, and California.

**The match race** was the classic western race. Two horses, one race, a short distance — a quarter-mile to a half-mile in Texas; longer distances in California. The horses ran from a standing start on a straight track: sometimes a graded race track at a town, sometimes a straight stretch of road, sometimes a measured course on the prairie. The match race was the occasion for substantial betting. The Texas "quarter race" (quarter-mile sprint) was a particular form, drawing on the short-distance racing tradition the Anglo-American settlers brought from the South. The Steeldust horses excelled at this distance.

**The Mexican carrera.** The Hispanic horse racing tradition. The carrera de caballos was a community event with substantial betting, often run on Sunday after Mass on a designated track at the village edge. The Mexican gambler at the carrera was a recognizable figure; the prize horse was the village's pride.

**The track meet.** In the larger western towns, formal race tracks operated with multiple races per day, organized betting (parimutuel and bookmaker), and thoroughbred racing on the Eastern model. San Francisco, Sacramento, and (later) Denver were the principal centers.

## 5. Horse Theft

Horse theft was a major western crime, distinct from cattle rustling though often practiced by the same people. The horse was more portable than cattle, more valuable per head, easier to steal in small numbers. Horse thieves were despised in a way cattle rustlers sometimes were not — the horse thief took a man's specific working partner, sometimes left him afoot in hard country.

### Methods

**The bunch theft.** A group of horses driven off a range and taken cross-country to a distant market. Required getting the horses to a place where they would not be recognized. Cross-border theft was common — Texas horses driven into Mexico (and vice versa), Wyoming horses driven into Colorado.

**The single-horse theft.** A specific horse taken from a corral, hitching rail, or pasture. Typically a riding horse the thief intended to use himself. The thief might leave his own worn-out horse in trade.

**The remuda theft.** The theft of a working ranch's or trail outfit's horse herd. Catastrophic for the victim — the outfit could not work without horses.

**The Indian raid.** Plains tribes practiced horse raiding as a major economic and prestige activity. Raids took horses from other tribes, from Hispanic ranches, and from white settlements. The cultural framework was different from white horse theft, but the economic effect on the victim was similar.

### Brand alteration and penalty

A stolen horse with a recognizable brand had to be re-branded to be salable. The methods were the same as for cattle (see Chapter 20): adding lines to convert one brand into another, hot iron over the old brand, killing and skinning if the brand could not be altered (uncommon for horses, as the meat had little value).

Horse thieves were among the most likely western criminals to be lynched. The horse thief who fell into the hands of a vigilance committee or a ranchers' posse was apt to be hanged from the nearest tree. The legal penalty for horse theft was typically a long prison term; the extra-legal penalty was death.

A horse-theft scene can carry substantial dramatic weight: the dawn discovery of an empty corral; the trail followed for two days; the thief overtaken at the bend of the river; the hanging tree. A scene of being left afoot is its own subject — the horse stolen out from under the rider in remote country could be a death sentence. The rider on foot in waterless country, walking out — a Cormac McCarthy scene, often.

## 6. The Horse Trade

The 1870s western horse trade was substantial and informal. A man might buy and sell horses at the local livery, at the stockyards, at horse fairs, or in private deals. The horse trader (a recognized character type — see Conrad Richter's _The Sea of Grass,_ where the horse trader appears) traveled from town to town buying and selling.

A buyer evaluating a horse looked at **age**, determined by the teeth (a process called _mouthing;_ the 1870s buyer expected to be told the horse's age and to verify by mouthing); **conformation** — build, balance, legs; **soundness** — no lameness, no broken wind, no blind eye, no tender feet; the buyer led the horse out, watched it walk and trot, picked up the feet, examined the eyes; **disposition** — the buyer wanted to see the horse saddled and ridden, preferred to ride it himself; and **brand and ownership** — the buyer wanted a bill of sale and a clear brand.

In the 1870s, horse prices varied enormously by type and quality:

| Animal                             | Price        |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------ |
| Plug or worn-out horse             | $5–$15       |
| Ordinary saddle horse              | $25–$50      |
| Good cow horse                     | $50–$100     |
| Top cow horse or matched team      | $100–$200    |
| Race horse or trotter              | $300–$1,000+ |
| Cavalry remount (contractor price) | $100–$150    |
| Mustang (newly broken)             | $10–$25      |

Rough; market and region varied.

The horse trader's pitch was the period's characteristic salesmanship. _A four-year-old, sound of wind and limb, no vice, gentle as a lamb to ride, will work in any harness, has been on the trail to Kansas twice. I'll let him go for sixty dollars, and I'm losing money._ The buyer's counter-pitch — finding the flaws to drive down the price — was the conversation's other half.

## 7. Equipment Beyond the Basics

Chapter 15 covers the saddle, the bit, and the basic gear. This section expands on equipment with cultural and regional dimensions.

### Bits

**The snaffle**, a jointed bit working on the corners of the mouth, is the mildest standard bit — used for green horses, for English riding, and by some western riders for trail work. **The curb**, with a port (the curved central section that fits over the tongue) and shanks that produce leverage when the reins are pulled, is the standard western working bit. **The spade bit**, a severe bit with a high port and long shanks, is used in the buckaroo tradition on highly trained horses; required a soft hand to use without injury. **The hackamore** — a bridle without a bit, working on the horse's nose with a heavy rawhide noseband (the bosal) — is used in the buckaroo tradition for starting a horse. **The jaquima** is the Spanish form of the hackamore, used in the vaquero tradition. **The war bridle** is a simple bit and bridle used by Plains Indians, sometimes only a thong tied around the lower jaw, permitting close control with minimal equipment.

### Saddles beyond the basic stock saddle

**The Mexican saddle (silla),** the original parent of the western stock saddle: high pommel, high cantle, big horn, ornately tooled, heavy and durable. **The Texas saddle,** a development of the Mexican saddle for Texas cattle work: lower than the Mexican, with the strong horn for roping. **The California saddle,** a development of the Mexican saddle for California vaquero work: more ornate than the Texas saddle, with specific details (the round skirt, the differing rigging). **The McClellan saddle,** the U.S. Cavalry's service saddle (adopted 1859, used through World War II): a spare, open-tree saddle designed to spare the horse's back on long rides — hard on the rider's seat. **The English saddle,** used for race horses and by some Eastern-trained riders, rare on the working range. **The pack saddle** — the sawbuck or aparejo: the sawbuck a wooden cross-frame strapped to the pack animal; the aparejo a Mexican-derived padded cushion saddle that distributed load better. The aparejo was preferred by serious packers.

### Boots, spurs, chaps

**Boots** were high-heeled, pointed-toed, underslung-heeled. The high heel kept the foot from slipping through the stirrup; the pointed toe slid in easily; the underslung heel caught on the stirrup if the horse fell, helping the rider pull free. The boots were typically pulled on (no laces) and reached well up the calf.

**Spurs** were worn on the boots. Various types: the Texas spur (small rowel, short shank), the California spur (large rowel, long shank, often with jingles), the Mexican spur (the largest, most ornate). Spurs were used to signal the horse more than to punish; the experienced rider used the spur lightly.

**Chaps** were leather leg coverings worn over the trousers to protect against brush, weather, rope burn, and accidental falls. Two main types: shotgun chaps (full-length tubes pulled on like trousers) and batwing chaps (open at the back, buckled around the leg). Plus the Mexican chaparreras (the parent type) and the woollies (winter chaps with the hair left on).

### The rope

The American cowhand typically used a braided rawhide reata (in California and the Spanish tradition) or a hemp or grass rope (in Texas and the south). The rope was thirty to sixty feet long. The roping technique varied by region — the Texas "hard and fast" style (rope tied to the horn, no slip) versus the California "dally" style (rope wrapped around the horn after the catch, can slip to absorb shock). The roping style was a source of regional pride and rivalry. The vaquero/buckaroo dallied; the Texas hand tied hard and fast. Both styles produced substantial accidents — the dally rider could lose fingers in a slipping wrap; the hard-and-fast rider could be jerked from the saddle by a heavy catch.

## 8. Horse, Ceremony, Relationship

The horse was not only labor and transport. It was status, gift, ceremony, partner.

### The Plains Indian horse culture

The horse transformed Plains Indian life from the seventeenth century onward. By the 1870s, mounted horse cultures (Comanche, Cheyenne, Lakota, Crow, Blackfeet, Ute, Shoshone) had developed sophisticated horse-handling traditions. Horses were **wealth** — a man's wealth was counted in horses; **bride price** — marriage involved horses given to the bride's family; **gift** — the giving of horses was a major diplomatic and social act; **war booty** — captured horses were a war's principal economic gain; and **ceremony** — horses figured in dances, in burials (a man's favorite horse might be killed at his grave), in regalia. A Comanche or Cheyenne man's relationship with his favorite horses was substantial and recognized within the culture. The buffalo runner, the war horse, were specific animals known by name and treated as partners.

### The cowhand's horse

The American cowhand was issued a string of horses (typically four to ten) on a working outfit. He did not own them; the ranch owned them. But he rode them daily and developed substantial relationships. A cowhand's "top horse" — his best animal in the string — was treated with care and pride. The relationship was working, not sentimental in the modern sense, but real. A cowhand whose top horse was killed or seriously injured felt the loss. The horse-and-rider partnership was the cow work's basic unit.

### The Hispanic vaquero's horse

The vaquero tradition placed substantial weight on the finished horse — the highly trained, beautifully responsive saddle horse developed over years of patient work. The vaquero's horse was an expression of the vaquero's skill and the hacienda's prestige. A famous bridle horse was a local celebrity.

### The army horse

The cavalry trooper's relationship with his issued horse varied. Some troopers became attached; others did not. The horse was the trooper's working partner under fire — a relationship that created strong bonds in some cases, indifference in others.

### Funerals and gifts

The ceremonial dimensions of horse culture appear in funeral practices (the killing of the favorite horse at the grave in some Plains traditions; the riderless horse with reversed boots in cavalry funerals), in gifts (the horse as the major gift in many cultures), and in races and contests (the horse as the medium of competition and prestige).

## 9. Voice — the Horseman's Vocabulary

Render the horseman through his vocabulary. The specific terms carry the period.

**Mouth** is a verb — to examine a horse's teeth to judge its age. _He mouthed the gelding and pronounced him eight years old._ A **mouthy** horse has a hard mouth that does not respond to the bit. **Wind** is the horse's breathing capacity — _sound of wind_ meant healthy lungs; _broken wind_ was heaves, a chronic respiratory condition. **Hand** is the unit of horse height, four inches; _fifteen and a half hands_ was about 5'2" at the withers. **Withers** is the high point at the base of the horse's neck where it joins the back. **Hocks** is the major joint of the horse's hind leg.

**Cinch** or **girth** is the strap holding the saddle on — _cinch_ in Western usage, _girth_ in English. **Latigo** is the leather strap by which the cinch was fastened. **Off side** and **near side** are the right side and the left side of the horse; the horse was traditionally mounted from the near (left) side. **Up** means mounted — _he was up by daylight._

**Bunch up** is a horse drawing his legs together and rounding his back, often as a prelude to bucking. **Crow-hop** is a mild buck — the horse hopping with all four legs, not violently. **Sun-fish** is a serious buck — the horse twisting the body in mid-air, presenting the belly to the sun; a sun-fishing horse threw most riders. **Rear** is the horse standing on his hind legs, dangerous; a rearing horse can fall over backwards on the rider. **Bog down** is a horse trapped in mud or a bog. **Founder** is laminitis, a severe inflammation of the horse's feet, often from overfeeding or overwork. **Galled** is a horse with sore patches under the saddle or harness from rubbing. **Pulled up lame** is a horse that became lame on the trail and could not continue at speed. **Saddle-sore** is either the rider after long riding, or the horse with sore patches. **Throw a shoe** is a horseshoe coming off in the field — a horse without a shoe might quickly become lame on stony ground.

These terms came to the horseman's mouth naturally and gave the period its weight.

## Vignette — The Horse Trade

> The trader had four head in the corral when Colter rode up. Colter looked them over from the saddle for a minute and then climbed down and tied his own horse to the rail.
>
> “What you got?”
>
> “Three saddle horses and a pack mule. The bay mare is six years old, sound as a dollar, broken to anything you want her broken to. The sorrel gelding's eight, pretty good cow horse, been on two trail drives. The little dun is green-broke, only five, but he'll make somebody a hand. The mule's old, fifteen years, but he'll pack two hundred pounds all day.”
>
> Colter went into the corral. He went to the bay first and ran his hand down her near foreleg and picked up the foot and looked at it. He set the foot down and walked around her and ran his hand down each leg in turn. He stood at her shoulder and pulled her lips back and looked at her teeth.
>
> “Six?” he said.
>
> “Six this spring.”
>
> “She's eight.”
>
> “Well. Maybe seven.”
>
> “Eight. The cups are gone out of these incisors.”
>
> The trader did not argue further. Colter went to the sorrel and mouthed him too and then put a halter on him and led him out into the open and jogged him in a circle. The sorrel was sound. The dun he handled briefly and passed by; he did not want a green horse. The mule he checked the feet and the back.
>
> “Forty for the bay,” he said. “Sixty-five for the sorrel. Twenty for the mule. I'll give you a hundred and twenty-five for the three.”
>
> “A hundred and fifty.”
>
> “A hundred and thirty.”
>
> “A hundred and forty.”
>
> Colter took out his wallet and counted out a hundred and forty dollars in greenbacks and laid them on the corral rail.
