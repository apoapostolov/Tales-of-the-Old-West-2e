# Chapter 20 — Outlaw Craft

> _“THE PISTOL HE HAD NOT YET DRAWN WAS STILL UNDER HIS COAT.”_

Tales of the Old West treats the outlaw as one of its central character types. The dime novels lied about the work. The outlaw was not a romantic figure on horseback — he was a working criminal in a specific economy with specific procedures, specific risks, and a specific average life expectancy that was not long. This chapter is the corrective: the practical mechanics of train robbery, bank robbery, the road agent's stage hold-up, horse theft, cattle rustling, the fence, the hideout, the posse, and the discipline of writing the outlaw without either glorifying him or flattening him.

For the band's internal procedure as a game system, see Chapter 11 of the corebook on outlaw bands. For the law's side, see Chapter 14 of this supplement.

---

## 1. The Economic Reality

Most outlaws made far less than the dime novels claimed and most died young or went to prison. The numbers matter to the writer.

A successful train robbery in the 1870s netted between $1,000 and $5,000 for the gang, occasionally as much as $30,000 — the great exception being the 1873 Adair, Iowa robbery by the James-Younger gang ($2,300 cash and $30,000 in bonds the gang could not negotiate without fences; the bonds were largely lost). A successful bank robbery netted similar amounts. The Northfield, Minnesota raid (September 1876) netted $26.70 in cash because the cashier locked the vault and was killed for it; the raid cost the gang four members shot dead in the street and three captured. A stage robbery netted $50 to $500 typically — passengers carried personal cash, the express box carried the real money, but most stages did not carry large express on most days. Horse theft of a single good horse fenced for $30–$50; a herd driven to Mexico might net $20–$30 per head wholesale. Cattle rustling: a stolen steer brought $10–$20 depending on the buyer's questions; a small operation rustling fifty head a season made $500–$1,000, less feed, men, and the fence's cut.

Gang shares were not equal. The leader took a larger cut. The man who went into the express car or the bank vault took more than the man holding horses. Inside men (railroad employees, bank clerks who tipped the gang) took ten to twenty percent off the top. A six-man gang clearing $3,000 might split $600 to the leader, $400 each to the inside men, $300 each to the hands-on robbers, $150 to the horse holder. Less than a year's wages for a cowhand in many cases — and the cowhand did not face hanging.

The Wild Bunch (Cassidy, Longabaugh, Logan, et al.) was an unusual gang in that several members lived into the 1900s. The James-Younger gang lost half its members at Northfield. The Daltons lost four out of five at Coffeyville (1892). Most named outlaws of the 1870s were dead, in prison, or in hiding by 1880. The career outlaw was a man who beat actuarial odds for as long as he beat them.

## 2. The Hideout

The outlaw camp was not a romantic canyon retreat. It was a cramped, cold, hostile space where a gang waited between jobs.

A hideout was usually a remote line shack, a played-out mine cabin, a brushy box canyon with one entrance, or an isolated ranch owned by a sympathizer. None were comfortable. Hideouts were chosen for remoteness, not shelter; wind cut through the walls, snow drifted in, and a fire was risky because smoke could be seen for miles. Six to ten men lived in a shack built for two, bedrolls touching, body smell, constant friction. Food was what men could carry in or shoot in the immediate vicinity — beans, bacon, coffee, and whatever game had not been hunted out. After a month at a hideout the men were sick of each other and sick of the food. A few hideouts had a woman cooking and doing washing — usually a sympathizer's wife or the leader's wife — but most did not. The lack of women is noted in every honest account. And then there was the boredom: cards, whisky if it could be brought in, cleaning weapons, repairing tack, talking the same talk for the third week. The boredom of the hideout was the worst part of the outlaw life.

- **Hole-in-the-Wall.** Powder River country, Wyoming. A box canyon with one approach defended by a single rifleman. Used by the Wild Bunch and others. **Brown's Hole** (later Brown's Park). Northwest Colorado at the corner of three states; the jurisdictional confusion was its defense.
- **Robbers Roost.** Southeastern Utah, slickrock country, almost impossible to track a man through.
- **The Indian Territory.** The whole region was a hideout for fugitives from the surrounding states until 1907 — federal jurisdiction only, few marshals, vast distances.

A working hideout had a lookout. The lookout sat on a high point with a glass and watched the approach for hours at a time. The lookout was rotated. The lookout who fell asleep was the man who got the gang killed. The lookout system is the writer's friend: a scene at the lookout post is a scene of dread without action.

## 3. The Train Robbery

The classic late-1870s western crime, with specific procedure.

- **Pre-robbery.** Inside information — a railroad employee or a station agent tells the gang which train, which date, which express car carried what; the inside man takes a cut. The site is a remote stretch of track, often a watering tank or a curve, far from the next town. The party is five to eight men: two on the locomotive, two at the express car, one or two guarding the passenger cars, one or two with the horses.
- **The stop.** The signal is a red lantern at night, a flag in daylight, a crosstie laid across the rails, a track obstruction. The engineer stops because the alternative is a derailment. Two men climb the locomotive with pistols on the engineer and fireman. The engineer is ordered to uncouple the express car from the passenger cars and pull it forward a quarter mile or so. This separates the messenger from any rescue and from the passengers.
- **The express car.** The express messenger (Wells Fargo or the railroad's own express) was an armed employee whose job was to defend the safe. Some fought; most did not. The famous fighters were exceptions. The messenger barricaded the door from inside. The gang demanded entry. The messenger refused. The gang threatened to kill the engineer. If the messenger held out, the gang blew the door with a charge — the charge often blew the door, the safe, the messenger, and a good part of the car's interior, and anything paper inside burned. Two safe types: the through safe, with combination known only to the destination clerk (the gang could not open it without explosives), and the local safe, which the messenger could open. The gang hoped for the local safe. They blew the through safe with care because the contents could be destroyed.
- **The split and scatter.** The take was split at the site or at a pre-arranged rendezvous a few miles off. Each man rode a different direction. The gang did not ride out together; a pursuit had to follow seven different trails. The leader often took the largest share for himself and a second share to deliver to the inside man later.
- **The pursuit.** The express messenger or the conductor cut for the next station. A telegram went up the line. By morning, every sheriff in three counties had the description. The Pinkerton Detective Agency or the railroad's own detectives took the case — they had the budget for long pursuit; the local sheriffs did not. A posse formed in the nearest town. Posses were effective for two to three days; after that they ran out of food, money, and patience and turned back.

## 4. The Bank Robbery

A bank robbery had a different set of constraints than a train robbery: the bank was in a town, the town had citizens who could shoot, and the timing was measured in minutes.

- **Pre-robbery.** Two to three men rode into town a day or two before the job, posing as cattle buyers or drifters. They mapped the bank's layout, the street, the route in and out, the location of the marshal's office, the saloon where the men gathered. The horses were rested and watered the morning of the job. Each man's horse was tied to a hitching rail in a specific spot on the street near the bank.
- **The job.** Five to seven men. Two or three went inside. One or two stood outside the door as guards. One or two stayed mounted as horse holders and street watchers. The lead man covered the cashier and any customers. The second man went over the counter to the cash drawer and the vault. The third man watched the door. The vault was the question — a small-town bank vault might be a brick room with an iron door; a city bank had a true safe. The cashier was ordered to open it. If he refused, he was beaten or shot. A locked vault that could not be opened ended the job. The whole inside job took four to seven minutes. Longer than that and the town was alerted and the citizens had rifles.
- **The street.** The town learned of the robbery within two minutes — usually because a citizen saw the guards at the bank door and shouted. Citizens with rifles took positions in second-floor windows and on rooftops. The gang's mounted men returned fire to keep the citizens' heads down while the men inside finished. The exit was the dangerous moment. **Northfield 1876** is the textbook disaster: the citizens of Northfield, Minnesota fired from second-floor windows and killed two of the gang in the street and wounded several others; the gang fled without the money, and three Younger brothers were captured a few days later.

The pursuit was the same as a train robbery, with the addition that the town's citizens often joined the posse — the posse was therefore larger and angrier than the typical professional posse.

## 5. The Stage Robbery

The road agent's classic crime. Smaller take, smaller risk than a train, smaller crew. Often one or two men. Three at most. The road agent worked alone or with a partner.

The site was a grade where the stage was slowed (an uphill or a sharp curve) or a known watering stop. The road agent knew the route and the timing. The agent stepped out from cover with a shotgun — the shotgun was the road agent's preferred weapon, short range, devastating, a threat the driver could not refuse. He stood twenty paces from the lead horses. _Throw down the box._ The express box was a wooden chest bolted to the front boot of the stage; the driver kicked it off or the messenger unbolted it and dropped it. Some stages carried an armed messenger riding shotgun. The messenger could fight back, and many famous gunfights were stage messenger vs. road agent encounters. The agent ordered the passengers out and lined them up beside the stage. He took watches, cash, jewelry. He did not generally take women's wedding rings (some did; the practice was considered low even among outlaws).

A variation, the box-off-the-road method, did not stop the stage. The agent arranged for the box to be thrown off the stage at a specific location by a confederate riding inside, and the agent picked it up later. This avoided the confrontation but required an inside man.

Stage robbery pursuit was less intense than train or bank — the take was smaller and the company's resources were less. Wells Fargo, however, hired detectives (notably James B. Hume, chief detective for Wells Fargo for decades) who pursued road agents relentlessly. A road agent who hit Wells Fargo boxes had Hume on his trail.

## 6. Horse Theft

The most common western crime by frequency. The most absolutely punished.

A horse thief came in the dark. He scouted the corral or the picket line in advance. He cut the picket rope or opened the corral gate without sound. He led the horses out by the lead rope, walking. He did not mount until well clear of the camp. Once mounted, he ran the horses fast for the first ten miles to put distance between himself and the discovery. The horses went to a known buyer who would not ask questions. Fences in border country drove the horses across the line into Mexico or up to the next territory.

The community enforced the death penalty for horse theft because a man without a horse on the frontier could die. Stealing a man's horse was attempting his life. The vigilance committee that hanged the horse thief acted on the explicit understanding that the thief had endangered the life of the owner. The cowhand caught with another man's horse received no trial in many cases. The rope was on hand. The nearest tree did the work.

## 7. Cattle Rustling

The crime built into the cattle trade — different mechanics from horse theft.

A small operator cut a few head out of a large herd's edge at night and drove them off. The cut was small enough to go unnoticed in a herd of several thousand. The stolen brand had to be altered. Skilled rustlers used a hot iron to add to the existing brand and turn it into something else: a Bar-S could become a Box-S; a Lazy-K could become a Lazy-KK. The art was to make the alteration look like the original. The rustled cattle were driven to a remote pasture and held until the brands healed. Then they were driven to a buyer who would not look too closely — a small slaughterhouse, a corrupt rancher, a packer who paid less and asked nothing. Or, in border country, a drive across the line.

Some cattlemen built their herds substantially through rustling. The Lincoln County and Tularosa cattle wars were partly disputes about who was rustling whom. The distinction between a rustler and a small rancher building a herd by appropriating mavericks (unbranded cattle) was sometimes a matter of who was making the accusation.

Cattlemen's associations hired range detectives. The detective's job was to ride the country, watch the brands, and report. He was paid by the association. He had wide latitude. Some detectives shot rustlers on sight and answered no questions later. The most famous was Tom Horn — a complicated case; convicted and hanged in 1903.

## 8. The Fence

Most outlaw operations depended on a fence — a man who bought stolen goods and moved them into legitimate markets.

- **The horse fence.** A horse trader or remount contractor who bought stolen stock and re-sold it through legitimate channels in distant markets.
- **The cattle fence.** A small slaughterhouse or packer who paid less and asked nothing.
- **The general fence.** A storekeeper, often in a border town, who bought watches, jewelry, paper money, cash; he paid ten to thirty cents on the dollar.
- **The bond fence.** A specialist who could move stolen bonds and securities — rare and well-paid; the reason the James-Younger Adair bonds (1873) were largely lost was that the gang did not have the connections to move them.

The fence and the gang were a working relationship of years. The fence trusted the gang not to bring trouble to his door. The gang trusted the fence to pay promised amounts and not turn informant. Either could betray the other; both knew it.

## 9. The Posse

How posses actually worked, vs. the dime novel version.

A posse was deputized by the sheriff (or, in the Indian Territory, by a U.S. marshal). The men were local citizens who could be spared from work. The sheriff promised them per-day pay (rarely fulfilled in practice). A posse was usually six to fifteen men — local cowboys, townsmen, sometimes a tracker (often a freedman or a Native scout — the work paid). The marshal or sheriff led. Each man brought his own horse and rifle.

A posse rode hard for two days, slower for the third. By the fourth day the men were tired, hungry, and out of grain for the horses. Most posses turned back after three days because they could not be supplied in the field. The Pinkerton Agency and the Wells Fargo detectives did the long pursuit work the local posse could not. A Pinkerton man might follow a fugitive for months, across multiple states, hiring local help as needed. The fugitive who beat a posse usually still had the Pinkertons to deal with.

A posse that caught its man sometimes did not bring him back for trial. The lynch posse hanged him on the spot. The justification: he was caught with the goods, the trial was a foregone conclusion, the trip back was expensive. This was illegal but widely practiced and almost never prosecuted. (See Chapter 14 of this supplement and Chapter 24 on the dark frontier.)

## Vignette — The Killing at the Card Table

> Ramsey came through the door fast and Bell was already standing. Bell shot him once and Ramsey kept coming and Bell shot him a second time and Ramsey took two more steps and sat down at the table where the hands of the dead man from the first round of cards were still face down on the green cloth. He sat there a moment looking at the table and the cards. Then he laid his head down on the table beside them. The pistol he had not yet drawn was still under his coat.
