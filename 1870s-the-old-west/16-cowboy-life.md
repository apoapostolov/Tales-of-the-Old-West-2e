# Chapter 16 — Cowboy Life

> _“THE HERD HAD BEEN FOUR DAYS ON THE CIMARRON WITH NO WATER AND THE LEAD STEER HAD BEGUN TO BAWL.”_

The open-range cattle business ran from roughly 1866, when the Texas-to-Kansas drives began, to about 1890, when barbed wire and the railroads ended it. What follows is the working hand of those years: who he was, what he was paid, the year of the drive, the chuck wagon and what came out of it, the gear on his back, the horses under him, the bunkhouse he came home to, the order of the outfit, the sound and smell and feel of the work.

---

## 1. Who He Was

He was twenty-four on average. Many were teenagers; few were over thirty-five. He was often a southerner, often a Confederate veteran or the son of one, and after about 1875 plenty of Black hands and Mexican vaqueros worked the same drives alongside him. He was usually unmarried — the work and the pay did not support a family — and he was as often illiterate as not. He owned his saddle, his bridle, his bedroll, his pistol or rifle (sometimes), his clothes, his hat, and his rope. He did not usually own his horse; the outfit provided the remuda. He worked an average of seven to ten years before he was used up: broken back, broken leg, kicked head, bad lungs, bad knees, alcoholism, dead.

---

## 2. Pay and Where It Went

| Position                               | Monthly pay (1870s–1880s)     |
| -------------------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Cook (cookie, coosie, biscuit-shooter) | $30–$50 (more than a hand)    |
| Trail boss                             | $100–$125                     |
| Top hand / segundo                     | $35–$45                       |
| Cowhand                                | $25–$40                       |
| Wrangler / horse jingler               | $20–$30 (often a teenage boy) |

Pay was held until the end of the drive and paid out in Abilene, Dodge, Ogallala, or whichever railhead they made. A drover with $100 in his pocket in Dodge in 1878 was a wealthy man for ten days. Most of it went to a hotel room and a bath (fifty cents to a dollar), a new shirt and hat and boots (five to twenty-five dollars all told), whiskey, the line of cribs and parlor houses (fifty cents to five dollars), faro and monte and poker (any amount), and the trip home or the next outfit. A drover who saved his pay was unusual. The ones who did became foremen and small ranchers. The ones who did not kept signing on for another season until they could no longer ride.

---

## 3. The Cattle Drive

The classic Texas drive ran from south Texas to a Kansas railhead — roughly a thousand miles, mid-March to mid-September.

### The gather (March–April)

The herd was gathered from the open range, ear-marked and branded if not already. A drive of 2,500 head — a typical size — required about ten to twelve hands plus a trail boss, a cook, and a horse wrangler. The remuda, the saddle herd, ran sixty to a hundred horses, six to eight per hand.

### Breaking the herd (April)

The first week of any drive was the worst. The cattle were not yet trail-broken; stampedes were common; hands slept in their boots. After two weeks the herd had a leader — an experienced steer called the _lead steer_; sometimes the same animal led drive after drive (Charles Goodnight's famous Old Blue led eight). After three weeks the herd could be moved with two night riders instead of four.

### The trail (May–August)

A drive moved ten to fifteen miles a day in good country. Less in rough country, less in wet weather, less when grass was poor. The day ran on a clock the country set:

The cook was up at 3:30 and the coffee was on the fire. The hands rolled out at four or four-thirty to coffee and biscuit and beans. By five the wrangler had the remuda in and each hand was roping his morning horse from the rope corral. By five-thirty the men were saddled and mounted and in position and the herd was moving. All morning they rode the herd: two point men in front, swing riders along the sides, flank riders behind them, two drag riders eating dust at the back. Drag was the worst job in the outfit. At eleven or noon the herd halted and was watered if water was to be had; the hands switched horses; the cook served beans and bacon and biscuit out of the chuck wagon. By one they moved again. By five or six they came on the bed-ground the cook and the wrangler had ridden ahead to choose, and supper was on. After supper the night-watch shifts began, two hours at a turn. Every man rode at least one shift, usually two on a hard drive. He slept in his bedroll on the ground with his hat over his eyes if the moon was full.

### River crossings

The major rivers — the Red, the Canadian, the Cimarron, the Arkansas, the Republican, the Platte — were the worst points of any drive. A herd would not cross a swift river willingly. The lead steer was driven in by riders on either side; the rest followed. A drowning was common. So was the loss of a hundred head if the herd milled in the current.

### Stampedes

Caused by lightning, by a dropped pan, by a coyote, by nothing. A stampede in the dark was the great terror of the drive. The herd ran for miles. Hands rode into the leading edge and tried to turn the lead, working it into a tighter and tighter circle until the herd milled and stopped. A man unhorsed in the middle of a stampede was usually a dead man.

### The railhead

Abilene from 1867 to 1871. Dodge City from 1875 to 1885. Ogallala on the Union Pacific. Cheyenne. The herds were sold to buyers who shipped east. The hands were paid off. Some signed on with northern ranches and stayed. Most rode south for another season.

## 4. The Chuck Wagon

Designed by Charles Goodnight in 1866 from a converted Studebaker army wagon. The chuck box at the back, with a hinged lid that folded down into a worktable. Drawers for coffee, sugar, salt, flour, baking soda, lard, beans, dried fruit. A water barrel strapped to the side. A _coonie_ — a stretched cowhide hung underneath — for firewood collected on the trail.

The cook (cookie, coosie, biscuit-shooter, dough-puncher, sourdough) was the second-highest-paid man in the outfit and ran the camp. His authority was absolute on his own ground. His chuck wagon was off-limits unless he invited you. The fire was his fire; you got coffee when he poured it. You did not ride past the wagon at speed — it raised dust into the food. You did not tie your horse to the wagon. The first plate went to the trail boss, not because of rank but to test the food. A bad cook ruined a drive. A good cook was guarded jealously.

What they ate ran on a short list. **Beans** — pintos, boiled all day in a Dutch oven with bacon fat and salt pork. **Sourdough biscuits** — made fresh twice a day from a starter the cook kept in a crock under the wagon; the starter was the most precious thing in camp. **Bacon and salt pork.** **Fresh beef** — but only from a stray or a crippled animal, rarely from the trail herd, which was money on the hoof. **Coffee** — boiled in a big enamel pot, no filter, grounds in the cup, black, no sugar usually, no milk ever after the second day out. **Dried apples or peaches**, stewed. **Molasses** in a tin. **Hardtack** when the flour ran low. **Antelope, deer, prairie chicken** when shot. **Wild greens** if the cook knew them. No green vegetables on a long drive. Scurvy was uncommon — citrus and fruit at the start helped — but vitamin deficiency was real. Constipation was the universal complaint.

What they drank: coffee, all day, every day. Water, often bad, often boiled into coffee for that reason. Whiskey at the end. Almost never on the drive — a drinking hand was a fired hand.

## 5. The Gear

(For full detail see Chapter 8.) The **hat** was a Stetson Boss of the Plains (1865) or a slouch hat — felt, wide brim against sun and rain, used for fanning a fire, watering a horse from a creek, signaling, and occasionally fighting a fire on the prairie. The **bandanna** was red or blue cotton: a filter for dust on drag, a wash cloth, a tourniquet, a sling, a hot-pad for a Dutch oven, a head-cover under the hat. The **shirt** was wool or cotton, button-front collarless, with a separate collar buttoned on for town. The **vest** carried tobacco, matches, a knife, a watch. The **pants** were wool, often denim by the 1870s — Levi's introduced the riveted denim trouser in 1873 — worn over the boot tops. **Chaparreras** (chaps) of leather went over the pants for working brush country (shotgun chaps, full leg) or for show (batwing, later).

The **boots** were high-heeled to keep the foot from sliding through the stirrup, pointed-toe to find the stirrup blind, tall-shafted to protect the calf — bad for walking, designed for the saddle. The **spurs** were low-rowel for working stock, longer-rowel for show or for a green horse. The **slicker** was a yellow oilcloth coat rolled behind the cantle. The **bedroll** was a canvas tarp wrapped around two wool blankets and a pillow that was actually a sack of clothes — tied behind the cantle or carried in the chuck wagon. The **saddle** was personal property and the cowhand's largest asset, $40–$60 for a working saddle in the 1870s; he'd sell his pistol before he sold his saddle. The **rope** was thirty to forty feet of grass rope (manila), later rawhide, coiled and tied to the saddle and used constantly. The **pistol** was a Colt revolver or similar; many drovers carried none on the trail — the boss often forbade it after the first drunken accidental shooting — but most carried it in town. The **rifle** rode in a saddle scabbard if at all, a Winchester '73 or '76 in .44-40 the common choice. The **knife** was a jackknife in the pocket and a sheath knife on the belt for serious work.

## 6. The Horse

A working cowhand had six to eight horses in the remuda assigned to him and rode each one a few times a week. The **morning horse** was the fresh, cold-backed one — often bucked at first light. The **cutting horse** was the trained one for working cattle out of the herd, worth twice what a green horse was. The **night horse** was sure-footed and calm, picketed near the bedroll for emergencies — the hand caught it without a saddle if the herd ran. The **river horse** was a strong swimmer kept for crossings. The **drag horses** were two or three less-favored mounts used for the worst position.

Horses were broken hard and used hard. Many were ruined by age seven. A cowhand who could break a horse without ruining it was worth keeping. A horse with kindness in it was a treasure.

The relationship between hand and horse was working, not romantic. The hand fed the horse before he ate. He pulled the saddle and brushed the back. He checked the feet. He doctored cuts. He talked to it as he worked. He did not name most of them — the remuda mounts were known by description (the bay, the gray, the snip, the stocking, the line-back) or by simple names (Buck, Star, Pete).

## 7. The Bunkhouse and the Off-Season

Between drives, hands worked the home ranch: repairing fence after barbed wire arrived in the late 1870s; riding bog and pulling cattle out of mud holes; branding calves at the spring round-up; cutting and stacking hay on ranches that had hayfields; breaking horses; riding line — a solitary ride along a stretch of range, often weeks alone in a line shack with two months of bacon and flour; hunting wolves or coyotes for the bounty.

The bunkhouse was a log or board building, eight to twenty bunks, a stove, a table, sometimes a deck of cards and a coal-oil lamp. Cold in winter, hot in summer. One wash basin outside, refilled from the well. Privy out back. The blanket-lice, the bedbugs, the rats in the rafters. The smell of unwashed wool, sweat, leather, tobacco, coal-oil, and the boot-grease the hands rubbed into their boots at night. Books were rare — the bible, an almanac, a copy of the _Police Gazette,_ a dime novel passed from hand to hand. Saturday night was bath night when the weather allowed: a wash in the creek or a tub in the bunkhouse, a change of shirts, a clean bandanna. The hands rode to town if there was a town within reach.

---

## 8. Hierarchy

The outfit was a small kingdom on the trail. The **trail boss** had absolute authority — often a part-owner, made every decision: route, speed, water, rest, who got fired. The **segundo** or top hand was the boss's deputy, took over if the boss was absent. The **cook** had authority over camp. The **hands** were equal among themselves; senior hands had moral weight, and disputes between hands were settled by the boss or by the men involved without the boss's interference. The **wrangler** or horse jingler was often a boy of fourteen to seventeen, learning the trade, slept apart with the remuda — the lowest of the camp. A new hand was tested. He was given the worst horse, the worst position, the worst chore. If he complained, he was finished. If he held up, he was a hand.

## 9. The Smell, the Sound, the Feel

This is the texture you put on the page.

- **Sound.** The bawl of cattle at first light, before they have fed. The creak of saddle leather in the cold. The chuckwagon iron clanging against itself as the wagon jolted. The night-rider's slow song to the herd — _Streets of Laredo,_ _Bury Me Not on the Lone Prairie,_ _Dogie Lullaby_ — sung slow and low to keep the herd quiet. Real practice. The singing told the herd a man was there. The wolf-howl half a mile off and the answering howl. The wind on the high plains, never stopping. The crack of a fire in the cook's pit at dawn. The thunk of a cinch buckle in the dark. The hiss of a hot iron on the calf's hide at branding.
- **Smell.** Cow manure, fresh and dried. The constant background. Wood smoke from the chuck fire, mostly mesquite or dry cottonwood. Coffee on a cold morning. Bacon grease. Saddle leather, sweet and old. Horse sweat. The man's own sweat, in the wool of his shirt, after a week. Tobacco smoke — chewing plug, cigarette papers, the rare cigar. Coal-oil lamps. Whiskey on a man's breath the morning after town. A dead horse three days in the sun, downwind.
- **Feel.** The cold of the saddle in the morning before the body warms it. The heat of the saddle in the afternoon. Saddle sores on the inside of the thigh after the first three days. Sun-blister on the lip and the back of the neck and the knuckles of the rein hand. Wet boot leather in the rain that does not dry until two days after. The shake in the hand after a day on the rope. Sleeping in clothes for weeks, the cloth going stiff with the sweat and the grease. The blister on the palm where the rope ran. Grit in the eye, in the mouth, in the food, in the bed.

---

## 10. The Myth and the Work

The 20th-century screen western removed almost everything in this chapter. The cowboy of the screen was clean, monogamous, hat-handed, slow to anger, fast to draw, alone on a horse against a sunset. The actual cowhand was filthy, single, profane, often cruel to stock and to himself, in a crew of ten or twelve, on a drive of weeks of monotony broken by hours of terror, paid poorly, used up by thirty. Write the actual cowhand. The myth is for a different shelf.

## Vignette — Four Days on the Cimarron

> The herd had been four days on the Cimarron with no water and the lead steer had begun to bawl. McNair sat his horse at the point and watched the gray morning come up over the grass. Behind him a thousand head moved slow in the dust their own hooves had raised. Mendez rode up out of the swing and reined in alongside.
>
> “They will smell water by noon,” Mendez said.
>
> “They will smell something.”
>
> They sat a while. The wind moved in the grass and a bird called once and went on. McNair pulled his hat down against the sun coming.
>
> “Ride back and tell the boy to bring the wagon up,” he said. “We will not make the river by dark and I want him close.”
>
> Mendez turned the horse and rode. McNair watched the steer bawl again and the herd lift its hundred heads and lower them. He spat into the dust and put his hand to the rein. The day was already old.

## Vignette — Six Weeks to Newton

> They left the Brazos in the second week of March with twenty-four hundred head and they crossed into the Indian Territory on the eighth day. The country opened out and the grass was good and they made fifteen miles a day. Two of the hands took fever at the Canadian and one of them died and they buried him in a sand draw and rode on.
>
> They crossed the Cimarron in late April. The cattle drank heavy and they lost an afternoon to it. North of the river the country changed. The grass was thinner and the wind was constant and at night the cold came down off the plains beyond. They had a stampede on the last night of the month and lost forty head and a horse with a broken leg they shot in the morning.
>
> They came up to the Arkansas on the eleventh of May and looked across at Kansas and the railhead at Newton was three days more. Henderson rode ahead to make the deal. The herd came in on the fourteenth, twenty-three hundred and nineteen head from the twenty-four hundred they had started with, and Henderson had eighteen dollars a head waiting for them at the bank.
