# Chapter 8 — Material Culture: Iron, Leather, Cloth

> _“THE GUN HAD RIDDEN IN HIS BELT THROUGH FIVE WET WINTERS AND THE BLUING WAS ALL OFF THE MUZZLE AND THE CYLINDER FACE AND THE GRIP WAS WORN TO HIS HAND.”_
>
> — A WICHITA GUNSMITH'S NOTEBOOK, 1881

This chapter is the gear reference. Every object in it should be written with weight, smell, sound, cost, failure mode, and use. A western that names a Colt without knowing it is single-action loses the audience that knows.

The frontier was, beneath everything else, a material culture: iron, leather, and cloth, made and remade by hand and by small machine, traded across the country in wagons and sold across the counters of mercantiles. What follows is the inventory of the things a person of the period actually held.

---

## 1. Firearms

### Revolvers

**Colt 1851 Navy.** .36 caliber percussion (cap-and-ball). Six-shot. Octagonal barrel, brass trigger guard. Carried by Confederate cavalry, by Wild Bill Hickok (his lifelong choice), by many veterans into the 1880s. Light, accurate, slow to reload — thirty to sixty seconds in good light.

**Colt 1860 Army.** .44 caliber percussion. Six-shot. Round barrel, larger frame than the Navy. The standard Union cavalry sidearm. Common in the West through the 1870s, carried over from the war. Many were converted to cartridge.

**Remington 1858 New Model Army.** .44 caliber percussion. Six-shot. Solid top-strap frame (stronger than the Colt), used by Union cavalry and many veterans. The cylinder could be swapped — a man with a spare loaded cylinder could reload in under thirty seconds.

**Colt Single Action Army (Peacemaker, Frontier Six-Shooter).** Introduced 1873. .45 Colt and later .44-40. Six-shot cartridge revolver. The defining sidearm of the West from 1873 forward. Single-action: hammer cocked manually for each shot. Loaded one round at a time through a loading gate. Empty cases ejected one at a time through the same gate with a rod. About seventeen dollars retail in 1873. About two pounds three ounces unloaded with a 7.5-inch barrel. Barrel lengths: 4.75" (the _Quickdraw_), 5.5" (the _Civilian_), 7.5" (the _Cavalry_).

**Smith & Wesson Model 3 (Schofield, Russian).** .44 American or .44 Russian or .45 Schofield. Top-break design — barrel hinged forward, ejecting all six empty cases at once. Faster to reload than the Colt. Used by the U.S. Army (Schofield variant). Carried by Jesse James, John Wesley Hardin (briefly), Frank James, Wyatt Earp's brothers.

**Derringer.** A small, large-bore, one- or two-shot pocket pistol. Remington Double Derringer in .41 rimfire was the common model. Carried by gamblers, women, men in suits. Lethal at the table; useless past ten feet.

### Rifles

**Sharps Model 1874.** .45-70, .50-70, .50-90 etc. Single-shot breech-loading falling block. The buffalo rifle. Accurate at five hundred yards in the hands of a hunter, lethal at a thousand. Heavy — ten to twelve pounds. Used to exterminate the southern buffalo herd 1872–1874 and the northern herd 1876–1883.

**Henry Rifle (Model 1860).** .44 rimfire. Lever-action, sixteen-round tube magazine under the barrel. _That damned Yankee rifle you load on Sunday and shoot all week._ Carried by some Union cavalry, by Confederate raiders who captured them, by westerners through the 1870s.

**Winchester Model 1866 (Yellow Boy).** Brass-frame lever-action, .44 rimfire. The Henry refined.

**Winchester Model 1873.** Iron then steel frame. Lever-action. Magazine twelve to fifteen rounds. Initially .44-40 — a cartridge also chambered in the Colt SAA, so a man could carry one ammunition for both rifle and pistol. Later .38-40 and .32-20.

**Winchester Model 1876.** Bigger frame for bigger cartridges (.45-75, .50-95). The buffalo-hunter's lever gun.

**Spencer Repeating Rifle / Carbine.** Used in the Civil War and after. Tube magazine in the buttstock, seven .56-50 rimfire rounds. Carried by Union cavalry, then by westerners. Out of production after 1869 but still in use through the 1880s.

**Springfield Model 1873 (Trapdoor).** .45-70. Single-shot breech-loading. Standard U.S. Army infantry and cavalry rifle through the 1880s. Carried by Custer's troopers at the Little Bighorn.

**Hawken rifle.** .50-cal percussion plains rifle. Pre-Civil-War mountain man's gun. Still in use by older trappers and hunters into the 1870s.

### Shotguns

**Coach gun.** Double-barreled 10- or 12-gauge with short barrels (typically 18–20"). Carried by Wells Fargo guards on the box of a stagecoach. Lethal at twenty feet. _Riding shotgun_ comes from this.

**Long-barrel double 12-gauge.** Hunting and farm gun. Brass shells, paper shells later, loaded with shot or with buckshot (_OO buck_) for serious work.

### The realities of period firearms

**Reloading is slow.** A Single Action Army takes about thirty seconds to fully reload by a competent man. A man in a fight reloads behind cover or not at all. Most fights used one cylinder.

**Range is short.** A pistol fight at thirty feet is a long pistol fight. Hits past fifty feet are unusual. The TV-western thirty-yard pistol shootout did not happen.

**Black powder smoke is thick.** A man fires three rounds in a saloon and the saloon is fogged. He cannot see his opponent or his own front sight after the second shot.

**Misfires are common.** Cap-and-ball especially — a damp cap, a fouled nipple, a cracked nipple. A pistol that has ridden in a holster through a rainstorm may not fire when needed.

**Accidental discharges are common.** A Single Action Army loaded with six rounds will fire if dropped (the firing pin rests on the primer). Knowledgeable men carried five and rested the hammer on an empty chamber. A loaded pistol left in a saddlebag could go off when the horse fell.

**The gun fight is rare.** Most western towns had one or two killings a year. Dodge City in its peak year had five. The Wild West of constant shootouts is myth.

**The body of a man shot does not fly back.** Hollywood rule. A man shot folds, sits, drops, kneels, sometimes stands a moment.

### The fast draw

Mostly myth. The classic stand-and-draw duel happened. It was not common. Most killings were an ambush, a shooting in the back, a close-quarters fight starting with words and a grab for the pistol, a drunken accident, or a killing of a man already disarmed.

Bat Masterson said most shootings were over before either man's gun cleared the holster fully — the man who started shooting first usually won. Wyatt Earp said the same. The myth of the choreographed quick-draw came after 1900.

## 2. Edged Weapons

**Bowie knife.** Heavy, single-edged, nine to twelve inch blade with a clip point. Designed for fighting and for general work. Worn on the belt by many westerners.

**Sheath knife.** Smaller working knife, five to eight inch blade. Skinning, butchering, eating, whittling.

**Jackknife.** Folding knife in the pocket. Tobacco-cutting, whittling, cleaning fingernails, opening a can.

**Hatchet, belt axe.** Standard camp tool.

**Cavalry saber.** Carried by some mounted units through the Civil War, ceremonial after that. The Plains tribes used the saber as a trade item. Largely absent from the cattle frontier.

## 3. Saddles and Tack

### Saddle anatomy

The **tree** is the wooden frame inside, covered in rawhide. The **cantle** is the raised back. The **horn** is the pommel projection, used for dallying the rope. The **skirts** are the leather that lies against the horse. The **stirrups** are wood with leather covers (tapaderos in the Spanish-influenced Southwest), or open iron later. The **cinch** (cincha) is a mohair or hair girth pulled tight under the horse's belly. The **latigo** is the leather strap that runs through the cinch and is tied off to the saddle ring. **Conchos** are decorative metal disks; saddle strings tied through them held tools and gear.

### Saddle types

The **California / Spanish saddle** was heavy, ornate, used in the cattle country of the Pacific coast. The **Texas / Plains saddle** was lighter, plainer, a working tool — the standard cowboy saddle. The **McClellan saddle** was the U.S. Cavalry saddle, with a skeletal frame, hard on the rider over many miles, easier on the horse than most civilian saddles.

### Bridles and bits

**Hackamore** — bitless bridle, used to start young horses. **Bosal** — a braided rawhide noseband. **Snaffle bit** — mild bit, jointed mouthpiece, for working young horses or finishing them. **Curb bit / spade bit** — stronger bits with a chin chain, used on finished horses and required a light hand; a heavy hand with a spade bit ruined a horse. **Ring snaffle, half-breed, full-spade** — variations.

### Other tack

**Saddle blanket / Navajo blanket** — wool, often woven by Diné weavers and traded throughout the Southwest, folded in two or four under the saddle. **Hobbles** — leather straps that limited a horse's stride; put on at night to keep the horse near camp. **Picket pin and rope** — for a night horse staked near the bedroll. **Quirt** — a short braided whip on the wrist. **Spurs** — with rowels, sometimes with jingle bobs that jingled as the man walked.

## 4. Clothing — by Class and Region

### Working cowhand

See Chapter 16 for the details of dress.

### Townsman

Sack suit (rough-woven wool), black or dark gray. Vest with watch chain. White shirt with collar (separate, attached daily). Bowler hat or top hat for the proper sort. Brogan boots or low shoes. Pocket watch on a chain across the vest.

### Gambler

Frock coat (long, fitted at the waist). Brocade or silk vest. Brocade tie or cravat. Diamond stickpin. Derringer in a vest pocket or under the lapel. Soft boots that did not creak.

### Lawman

Coat or duster, depending on weather. Vest with badge pinned to it (sheriff, deputy, marshal). Pistol on the right hip in a slim holster. Sometimes a coach gun under the arm.

### Soldier

Blue wool coat (Union surplus) or U.S. Cavalry blue with yellow piping (cavalry). Forage cap or campaign hat (the wide-brim of the Indian Wars). Brass-buttoned blouse. Sky-blue trousers with yellow stripe down the seam (cavalry). Boots, knee-length. Saber knot, cartridge belt, gauntlet gloves (mounted).

### Mexican working man

White cotton shirt and trousers (peón class). Serape (a wool blanket-cloak). Sandals or boots. Sombrero (the wide brim of working sun-country, not the high-peaked tourist version). For the rancho owner or vaquero of standing: short jacket, flared trousers, embroidery, silver conchos on the saddle.

### Native dress

Variable by nation, season, and ceremony. By the 1870s much Native dress incorporated Anglo trade goods (calico shirts, wool blankets, brass buttons, glass beads) alongside or replacing traditional buckskin. A Cheyenne man on a reservation in 1880 might wear blue Army-surplus trousers, a calico shirt, a beaded belt, moccasins, and a wool blanket over his shoulders. Detail by nation requires research; do not generalize.

### Women's dress

Working homestead woman: linsey-woolsey or calico dress, apron, sunbonnet outdoors, brogan shoes, stockings, hair pinned up. Town woman of the middle class: corseted dress, bustle (in fashion through the 1880s), petticoats, button-up shoes, hat with feathers. Working girl on the line: cheap dress imitating fashion, short of skirt by current standards (showing the ankle and the calf above the boot), corset, often no hat indoors.

## 5. Tools

### The cowhand's tools beyond the gun

Branding iron — long iron rod with the brand's shape on the end, heated red in a brush fire at branding. Wire stretchers (after 1875). Fence pliers. Shovel and pickaxe (for water-hole digging). Axe (for fence-post and firewood). Block and tackle (for pulling a cow out of a bog).

### The trade tools

**Smith:** anvil, hammer, tongs, bellows, files, vises, the forge.

**Carpenter:** handsaw, ripsaw, hand plane, chisel set, brace and bit, drawknife, plumb bob.

**Wheelwright:** spokeshave, dish-cone, tire-bender, hot tire forge.

**Saddler:** harness needles, awl, hammer, knife, edge bevelers.

**Doctor:** lancet, fleam (for bloodletting, still practiced), forceps, bone saw, scalpels, opium tincture, calomel, mercury, quinine.

**Surveyor:** chain (Gunter's chain, sixty-six feet), compass, transit, level rod.

### The household tools (homestead)

Cast-iron Dutch oven — cooking, baking, stewing. Skillet. Coffee mill. Boiler / wash kettle. Treadle sewing machine (after the Singer broke into the market in the 1860s — a major status object on the homestead). Butter churn. Hand-cranked corn mill. Coal-oil lamps and lanterns.

## 6. The Wagons

**Conestoga / prairie schooner.** Large wagon for emigrant family. Pulled by oxen (slower, cheaper, hardier) or mules (faster, more expensive, more delicate). Six oxen for a fully-loaded wagon.

**Studebaker farm wagon.** The standard work wagon. Iron tires, wood box, often converted into a chuckwagon or hay-rake or freight wagon.

**Freighter.** Heavy wagon, often hauled in trains of three or four behind a single team. The Murphy Wagon and the Studebaker Big Wagon. Oxen team of eight to fourteen.

**Stagecoach.** Concord coach (built by Abbot-Downing of New Hampshire). Six-horse team. Carried six to nine passengers inside, more on top.

**Buckboard.** Light spring wagon, two seats, used by ranchers and townspeople for short hauls.

**Buggy / surrey.** Town and Sunday driving.

## 7. Money

**Gold coins.** Twenty-dollar double eagle, ten-dollar eagle, five-dollar half-eagle, two-and-a-half quarter-eagle, one-dollar gold dollar.

**Silver coins.** Dollar, half-dollar, quarter, dime, half-dime (later nickel).

**Greenbacks.** Paper money issued during and after the war. Discounted in some places against gold.

**National bank notes.** Issued by individual banks.

**Mexican peso (silver dollar).** Widely used in the Southwest at par with the U.S. dollar through much of the period.

**Two-bit, four-bit, six-bit.** _Bit_ equals 12.5 cents (one eighth of a dollar). Two bits is a quarter; four bits is a half-dollar.

**Coppers.** Pennies. Used for trade with children, with the very poor, and in nickel-and-dime gambling.

A common cowhand carried his money in a leather poke (drawstring pouch) inside his shirt, or rolled in his bedroll, or in the chuckwagon's strongbox. He did not trust banks and often did not trust pockets.

## 8. The Smell, the Sound, the Wear

How these objects appear after use.

The **Colt** smells of black powder smoke and gun oil. The bluing wears thin on the muzzle, the cylinder face, the edges of the frame, where leather and hand contact rub it silver. The grip checkering wears smooth.

The **saddle** smells of sweet old leather, horse sweat, saddle soap. The fork and the cantle are worn pale. The seat is dished from the rider. The stirrup leathers are twisted.

The **boot** is cracked across the instep, worn through the sole, salt-crusted around the heel. The shaft is bowed forward where the calf has shaped it.

The **hat** is sweat-stained around the band, dented to its owner's preferred crease, gray with dust on the crown. A cowhand's hat fits no one else.

The **bedroll** is stiff with grease, smells of wood smoke, has a brown ring on the canvas where the bottle of laudanum the man used to keep against his back has slowly leaked.

The **chuckwagon** is gray-weathered, the iron tires worn thin, the box battered by tools and feet. The starter crock is sour-sweet on the lid. The coffee pot is black inside and out.

The **bunkhouse** smells of unwashed wool, leather, sweat, tobacco, coal-oil, the boot-grease in a dish on the table, the sourdough starter bubbling in its jar.

These are the textures the prose puts on the page and the table.

## Vignette — At the Mercantile

> Wesley came up the steps and the dog went to the corner of the porch and lay down and watched the street. Inside the door the air was the brown air of all such places — coffee in a barrel, kerosene, the thin sweet of the cured meat hanging from a hook in the back, the bitter at the bottom of it of the new harness oil on the leather.
>
> Mr. Pruitt was at the counter weighing nails into a paper for a man Wesley did not know. Wesley stood at the door and waited. The wall behind the counter held the goods on shelves to the ceiling — the tin cans labeled in red and black, the bolts of calico in five colors, the boxes of cartridges in their pasteboards, the bottles of patent medicine, the jars of penny candy. A pair of boots hung from the rafters by the laces. A Winchester leaned against the back wall with a tag tied to the trigger guard.
>
> Mr. Pruitt finished and the man took his nails and went out past Wesley without a word. Mr. Pruitt looked over and waited.
>
> _A pound of the green coffee,_ Wesley said. _And a tin of the matches. And a box of the .44-40s if you have them._
>
> Mr. Pruitt turned to the shelf for the cartridges. _On account?_
>
> _Cash,_ Wesley said, and laid two coins on the counter.
