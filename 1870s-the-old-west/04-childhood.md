# Chapter 4 — Childhood on the Frontier

> _“THE BABY WENT IN MARCH AND THE TWO OLDEST IN AUGUST AND THE LITTLE ONE THE NEXT MARCH AGAIN. THERE WERE STILL FIVE OF THEM AT THE TABLE. SHE COUNTED EVERY NIGHT.”_

The frontier child worked from the time he could carry a bucket. He was at risk of death from twenty diseases the East had tamed by the 1880s. He was educated by his mother, his older sister, and a five-month schoolmarm if his county had one. He buried siblings and was buried by parents at rates the modern reader finds difficult to credit. He played with what was at hand — the corn-husk doll, the willow whistle, the dog, the country itself. He was a small working person with a small body and a large country, and the country shaped him.

---

## 1. The Day, by Age and Place

The frontier child's day was the family's day with the child's piece of it carved out by capacity. Age boundaries are approximate. A capable seven-year-old might do work an idle eleven-year-old still avoided.

### The homestead child, three to five

In the cabin or the soddy underfoot. The mother set small tasks: carrying chips for the fire, fetching small objects, watching the baby in the cradle, sitting on the porch with the slate. The child was rarely out of sight of an adult. The chief danger was the open hearth, the kettle, the well, the team in the yard. The vocabulary the child first learned was the work vocabulary of the household: the names of the tools, the animals, the fields, the weather. The frontier three-year-old knew _sorghum_ and _harness_ and _norther_ and _croup_ before he knew _train_ or _street._

### The homestead child, six to nine

Real work begins. The boy carries water from the well, brings in stovewood, gathers eggs, drives the cow to pasture and back, learns to milk. The girl helps the mother — kneading bread, washing dishes, watching the younger children, learning to spin and to sew. Both sexes weed the garden, hoe corn, gather kindling, pick fruit in season.

The school year, where school existed, was three to five months centered on winter when field work was slack. The child walked to the school, often a mile or more, sometimes considerably more. He carried his dinner in a tin pail.

### The homestead child, ten to fourteen

Adult work in smaller portions. The boy plows behind the team, drives the wagon to town, helps with the butchering, hunts small game with a single-shot rifle, breaks and feeds the stock. He may be sent to work for a neighbor at harvest for fifty cents a day plus board. The girl is by now a substantial part of the household labor: she cooks meals on her own when the mother is sick or in childbed, she tends the younger children for whole days, she does the laundry on washday, she helps butcher and put up the meat. She is preparing, the family understands, to run her own house.

### The homestead child, fourteen to majority

Indistinguishable from an adult worker except in wages and in legal status. The boy works alongside the father or hires out for full hand wages. The girl is treated as a young woman: she is courted, she may marry at sixteen or seventeen, she may go out to service in a town household.

### The ranch boy

A specific path. By twelve he is a horse-holder at the branding, by fourteen he is gathering strays on the home pasture, by fifteen he has been hired as a hand at half wages, by seventeen he draws a man's pay. He sleeps in the bunkhouse with men two and three times his age. He learns the work, the vocabulary, the silence.

### The town child

Less labor, more schooling. The merchant's son attends school the full nine months and is expected to learn to read, to cipher, and to write a clean hand. He sweeps his father's store after school and runs errands. The merchant's daughter learns the same plus piano, plus the domestic accomplishments her mother thinks suitable. The teamster's child or the saloon-keeper's child inhabits the town's working margins. He runs in the street, knows every alley, knows which horses kick, knows the back doors of the saloons and the names of the women on the line whether his mother knows he knows or not.

### The mining-camp child

The most varied of the lot. The Cornish miner's child in a company town attends the company school and the chapel and is destined for the mine. The boarding-house child grows up among single men and develops an early adult composure. The line woman's child is raised in the parlor house among the women, often by a wet nurse, often loved by half a dozen of the women, often sent to a convent school in a distant city when the mother can afford it.

## 2. The Nursery and the Cradle

The frontier infant slept in a cradle the father had made or in a drawer pulled from the bureau. He was nursed for eighteen months to two years, longer than the modern infant. His mother carried him on her hip through her work or set him on a quilt where she could watch him. He was wrapped in a long gown and in winter in a knit cap.

The names came from the family Bible: father's name, grandfather's name, the saint of the day, the beloved aunt who had died. Repetition across generations was expected. A frontier family with a John, a John Jr., and a Little John in three generations was normal. Daughters were named for mothers, grandmothers, dead sisters.

The first photograph of the child, when there was one, was often a memorial. The infant who had died was photographed in the casket or in the mother's arms before the burial. The image was sent to relatives and kept on the mantel.

---

## 3. Childbirth from the Child's Side

The child arrived in his mother's bed in the cabin or the soddy. The midwife, if there was one within riding distance, was sent for. If there was not, the husband or an older daughter or a neighbor woman attended. The doctor was rarely present at the birth unless the labor went wrong; the doctor was sent for afterward only if mother or child needed him.

The other children of the household were often sent to a neighbor for the day. They returned to find the new sibling in the cradle and the mother in bed. The mother was kept in bed for a week or two depending on the household's economics. The neighbor women brought food.

Maternal mortality was high enough that every frontier child knew at least one family in which the mother had died in childbirth and the children had been parceled out among aunts, grandmothers, or charitable neighbors. The motherless household with the silent father and the ten-year-old daughter keeping house was a recurring frontier scene.

## 4. Mortality and the Small Grave

The graveyard behind the church or at the edge of the family's section contained more child markers than adult markers.

**Diphtheria** was the first killer — a bacterial infection of the throat with a leathery membrane that grew until the child suffocated. It hit preschool children hardest and could empty a frontier county of children in a single autumn. **Scarlet fever** — streptococcus — brought the characteristic rash and the fever. It killed by itself or left damage to the heart and the kidneys, and the survivor was often weakened for life. **Whooping cough** (pertussis) made the child cough in long racking spasms in which he could not catch breath. It lasted weeks. It killed the smaller children by exhaustion and by suffocation. **Measles** brought the rash, the fever, the cough; the measles itself sometimes killed; the secondary pneumonia killed more often. **Cholera infantum** was summer diarrhea in nursing infants, killing by dehydration in three days; rural mothers dreaded the August week of consecutive deaths in the neighborhood. **Typhoid fever** came from the contaminated well; the child took fever, took to bed, declined for three weeks, sometimes recovered, often did not. And then **accidents**: the kick of the horse or the mule, the fall from the wagon, the scald from the kettle, the drowning in the creek, the burn from the open fire, the cut from the axe, the rattlesnake at the woodpile.

The mother who buried three of her ten children was not unusual. The mother who buried five was within the range. The grave markers in any frontier churchyard tell the story; the period photographs of the markers show row after row of small stones with short dates.

How the household carried the death: the body was washed by the mother and an aunt or neighbor; the father made the box; the neighbor men dug the grave; the minister or, in his absence, the father read the service; the other children attended; the mother returned to her work that week or the next because the work would not wait.

The mourning lasted longer in private than the work allowed it to in public. The mother who lost a child in March might still be unable to speak the child's name in October. The father took it more silently; his grief came out in long silences and in the sudden rage at small frustrations a year later.

## 5. School

The one-room school is one of the period's recurring images. The reality varied widely.

### The schoolhouse

A single room, twenty by thirty feet, of frame or of log. A door at one end, a teacher's desk and blackboard at the other. Benches or desks for the pupils. A pot-bellied stove in the middle. Outhouses behind. A water bucket and a dipper at the back. A hook for each child's coat and dinner pail.

The school year ran three to five months in most frontier counties through the 1870s and was extended to eight or nine months by the 1880s in the more settled counties. The terms were placed where the children could be spared from the farm: typically a winter term (December to March) and sometimes a summer term (June and July).

### The teacher

The schoolmarm was usually a young woman, often sixteen to twenty-two, often herself the eldest daughter of a respectable local family or a town spinster, sometimes a widow supporting children. She was paid twenty to forty dollars a month and boarded with families in the school district by rotation — a week with the Andersons, a week with the Halls.

The schoolmaster, less common in the West, was often a young man in transit: a college graduate from the East working a year before reading law, a preacher between charges, a veteran trying to settle. He was paid more than the schoolmarm — forty to sixty dollars a month — and was expected to handle the older boys.

### The curriculum

The McGuffey Eclectic Readers (six graded readers, revised editions through the 1870s and 1880s) were the dominant text in the West as in the East. The child read his way up the readers as he grew: First Reader at six or seven, Sixth Reader at twelve to fourteen if he stayed in school that long. The Readers contained moral selections, poems by Whittier and Longfellow and Bryant, prose by Webster and Irving, Bible passages, fables. The child memorized and recited.

Beyond the Readers came the speller — Webster's _Blue-Backed Speller_ in the older households, Sander's or Swinton's in the newer; the arithmetic (Ray's _Arithmetic_, in graded volumes); the geography (Mitchell's or Cornell's); a copybook for penmanship in the Spencerian hand. Older students read Greenleaf's _Algebra_ or Davies' _Surveying_ if the teacher could teach them. History meant Goodrich's _Pictorial History of the United States_ or Quackenbos's similar volume.

### The school day

Opening prayer in most districts; in the Mormon districts a Mormon prayer; in the Catholic districts a Catholic prayer; in the German districts sometimes a hymn. The day proceeded by recitation: each class — the First Reader class, the Second Reader class, and so on — was called to the front in turn while the others worked at their seats. Slates and chalk for arithmetic. Penmanship after dinner. Spelling bees on Friday afternoons. Dismissal at four.

The recess: the boys played at the woodpile or in the field, the girls at the porch or under the trees. The games included tag, dare base, anty-over (a ball game over the schoolhouse roof), drop the handkerchief, blind man's buff, marbles in season, mumblety-peg with pocketknives.

### The discipline

The teacher kept order with a hickory switch or a ferule. The big boys, who came to school in winter when not needed at the plow, were sometimes nearly the teacher's age and sometimes openly defiant. The schoolmaster who could not handle them did not last long. The schoolmarm who could not handle them often appealed to a respected father in the district who came down to the school and addressed the matter.

The traditional gauntlet: the new male teacher in a rough district was tested by the older boys on his first day or his first week. If he won the fight, the term proceeded smoothly. If he lost, the term collapsed and the trustees hired a replacement.

### Beyond school

A frontier child's formal schooling typically ended at thirteen or fourteen. A few continued: the academy in a larger town, the territorial normal school, in rare cases a college in the East. The girls who continued became teachers themselves or married. The boys who continued became preachers, lawyers, doctors, or merchants' sons fitted for the counting room.

The child who never attended school existed in substantial numbers: the homestead too remote, the parents indifferent, the labor too pressing. The illiterate frontier adult was common enough that the census takers in the 1880 census recorded as many as twenty percent of adults in some western counties as unable to read or write.

## 6. Toys, Games, Songs

The frontier child played with what the household contained.

### Toys

The corn-husk doll was made by the mother or the older sister from the husks of the autumn corn, with a face drawn in pencil or charcoal and a scrap dress. It was the girl's first doll, often her only doll until a town doll arrived as a Christmas present at age seven or eight. The rag doll was the next stage — stuffed with cotton or with wool, with embroidered features, carried until the child was nine or ten and then passed to a younger sister. The town doll had a china head, a painted face, and a cloth body; bought at a mercantile or sent from relatives in the East, it was a possession to be cared for, dressed, undressed, taken to bed.

The willow whistle was cut from a green willow branch in spring. It was the boy's first instrument, and the technique was passed from older boys to younger. The tin soldier was bought in town and sold at a nickel each; the boy's collection might number six or eight, and Civil War uniforms predominated through the 1870s. The sled was father-built of split planks and iron runners, used hard and repaired each winter. The hoop and stick — a barrel hoop driven with a stick — was a town child's game. Marbles cost a nickel for a dozen and were played for keeps in the schoolyard; the clay marble was the common stake, the agate and the glass were the prizes.

The pocketknife came to a boy at eight or nine, given by the father — a real tool, expected to be cared for, used for whittling, for cutting his food at table, for the thousand small cuttings of the day. The .22 single-shot rifle came at twelve or so on the homestead, sometimes earlier, used for small game, varmints, and target. Father taught him the safe handling and the maintenance. The boy with his .22 over his shoulder walking the field for rabbits was a daily frontier sight.

### Games

Tag, hide and seek, prisoner's base were universal. Anty-over involved a ball thrown over the schoolhouse roof while teams ran around the building. Crack the whip was a line of children holding hands, the leader running and turning until the child at the end was flung off. Marbles came with its specialized vocabulary — _aggie, taw, mibs, knuckle down, no fudging._ Mumblety-peg was played with pocketknives, the loser pulling a peg from the ground with his teeth. Hopscotch, jacks, and jump rope were primarily girls' games. Snap the whip on the ice was the winter version. Skating, sledding, snow forts filled the cold months. Swimming in the creek was a summer matter, boys generally naked, girls in shifts at a separate hole. Fishing with a hook and line on a willow pole was a summer activity, primarily the boys', with girls in some households welcome.

The ranch child rode from age three or four — first behind a parent, then on his own pony. The town child rode less, the merchant's son rented a horse from the livery for a Sunday. The play-party was a young people's social with singing games (_Skip to My Lou, Old Dan Tucker, Sandy Land_) substituting for the dancing the Methodist or Baptist parents forbade.

### Songs

The frontier child sang what the family sang and what the school sang. The lullabies were the old ones — _Hush Little Baby, Bye Baby Bunting, Rock-a-Bye Baby._ The play songs were _Ring Around the Rosie, London Bridge, Farmer in the Dell._ The school songs were the patriotic ones — _America, My Country 'Tis of Thee, Yankee Doodle, Battle Hymn of the Republic_ — and the Sunday school hymns _Jesus Loves Me_ and _There Is a Happy Land._ The trail child of the cattleman or the freighter learned the trail songs from the hands.

### Books

The household with books had a Bible, a McGuffey Reader or two, a hymnal, sometimes a Pilgrim's Progress, sometimes a Webster's dictionary, sometimes a Foxe's Book of Martyrs. The well-fixed household added a few of the popular novels of the period — Cooper, Scott, Dickens, Mrs. Stowe, Mrs. Southworth. The dime novel (Beadle's, Munro's) was the boy's reading by 1870 — _Deadwood Dick, Buffalo Bill, the Frank Reade boy inventor stories_ — bought for a dime at the mercantile or borrowed from a friend. The mother often disapproved. The boy read them in the loft.

## 7. The Rites of Passage

The frontier child's growing up was marked by specific transitions.

**First trousers.** The boy went into long pants at five or six, having worn dresses and pinafores before. The transition was a small ceremony in some households.

**First chore that earned wages.** Often herding a neighbor's cow or holding horses at a wedding or a funeral for a few cents.

**First long ride alone.** The boy sent on horseback to the next ranch or the next town with a message or to fetch the doctor. Eight or nine for a short ride, twelve for a real distance.

**First gun of his own.** The .22 at twelve or so. A larger rifle (a single-shot Stevens or an old Springfield) at fourteen or fifteen. A revolver, if the family ran to it, in late teens.

**First horse of his own.** A pony at six or seven in a ranch family. A green colt to break and train at twelve or so. A grown saddle horse at fifteen.

**First work for hire.** The boy hired out at fourteen or fifteen for harvest, then for a season on a ranch, then for a year as a hand. The girl hired out at the same age as a hired girl in a neighboring household or as help at the hotel.

**First dance, first courting.** The play-party at twelve or thirteen, the real dance at fifteen, the courting at sixteen or seventeen.

**Confirmation, baptism, the public profession of faith.** The Methodist child went to the altar at the revival when he felt the call, often at twelve or thirteen. The Catholic child made his first communion at the age set by the parish (usually seven). The Baptist child was immersed in the creek when he professed at twelve or fifteen. The Mormon child was baptized at eight.

**Departure.** The boy left for work, for the army, for the West further west. The girl married out. The household contracted. The mother counted her children at table at twenty and again at thirty and the count did not match.

## 8. The Native Child, the Mexican Child, the Black Child, the Chinese Child

### The Native child

Specific nation matters; the Comanche and Lakota cases are instructive. The Native child was raised by the band, not only by the parents. Aunts, uncles, grandparents, cousins all had defined relationships with the child and defined obligations. Discipline was rare and almost never physical; shame and ridicule were the principal means. The child was praised generously and was expected to learn by watching.

The Comanche boy was on horseback by four or five, shooting a small bow by six, hunting small game with the older boys by ten, riding with a war or hunting party by fourteen. The girl learned the women's work from her mother and her aunts: the lodge, the processing of hides, the cooking, the gathering, the pack and unpack of the camp. The girl was eligible for marriage at her first menses; the boy when he had counted coup or made his first kill on a hunt. The Lakota child's progression was similar in structure with different specifics. The boy's vision quest at puberty was a defined rite. The girl's puberty was marked by a ceremony.

The destruction of the Native child's world by the 1880s reservation system: the boarding school removal (Carlisle Indian Industrial School opened 1879), the prohibition of native dress and language, the cutting of the boys' hair, the renaming. The Native child of the 1880s was a casualty of federal policy in a way the Native child of 1860 had not been.

### The Mexican child of the rancho

In the older Mexican families of New Mexico, Arizona, South Texas, and California, the child was raised in the extended household of grandparents, parents, unmarried aunts and uncles, and cousins. Catholic from infancy: baptism within weeks, the godparents (_padrinos_) a defined and lifelong relationship, first communion at seven, confirmation later.

The boy of the rancho learned the work from age six: the horse, the rope, the cattle, the sheep, the small farm. He spoke Spanish at home; English was acquired in the Anglo school if there was one. Schools in Spanish existed in some New Mexico parishes and in some Texas border towns into the 1880s.

The girl of the rancho was raised more closely. She remained in the household until marriage. She was educated by her mother and the household women in the household arts and, in literate families, in reading and writing Spanish. She was chaperoned in courting. She married within the extended Catholic network of related families.

### The Black child after Reconstruction

The freedman's child of the 1870s and 1880s in the West was the child of recent emancipation. The parents' generation had been enslaved; the grandparents had been enslaved or had been born free. The child's situation depended on where the family had settled.

The Black homestead child in Kansas (Nicodemus, the Exoduster settlements of 1879 onward) attended the community's school, often taught by a Black teacher sent from the East by the Freedmen's Bureau or by the Black Methodist or Baptist church. The literacy rate was low in the parents' generation and rose quickly in the child's.

The Black ranch hand's child in Texas or New Mexico attended the local school if it was open to him (many were not), worked alongside his father, and learned the trade. The Black soldier's child at a frontier post attended the post school with the white officers' children in some garrisons and was taught separately in others.

### The Chinese child

The Chinese child in the West of the 1870s and 1880s was rare in absolute numbers because the Chinese exclusion regime restricted family immigration and most Chinese workers were single men. The few Chinese children who did exist were concentrated in the Chinese quarters of San Francisco, Portland, Sacramento, Marysville, and the larger camps.

The Chinese boy was raised in the language and the culture of the home village, attended a Chinese school in the quarter where one existed, worked in the family laundry or restaurant or shop from age ten or so. The Chinese girl was raised more closely still and was often betrothed in childhood to a boy in China or in the quarter. The Chinese girl who had been bought in China and shipped over to the West Coast brothels was a different case: a child slave whose childhood was the brothel itself.

## 9. The Orphan, the Bound-Out, the Parceled-Out

A frontier child with both parents living to his majority was the lucky case. The unlucky cases were common.

### The motherless household

The mother dead in childbirth or of consumption or of typhoid. The father, with a six-year-old, a four-year-old, and a baby, kept the household by hiring a hired girl, by importing his sister or his mother-in-law, by remarrying within a year. The remarriage was sometimes hard on the children; the stepmother and stepchildren of the period are a recurring fictional subject because they were a recurring real one.

### The fatherless household

The father killed in the war, in the mine, on the range, by the bullet, by the disease. The widow with children kept the homestead if she could, took in laundry, took in boarders, taught school, remarried. The widow who could not keep the household sent the children out: the older to work for board with neighbors, the younger to her sister in Iowa, the baby to a childless cousin in Pittsburgh.

### The full orphan

Both parents dead. The children parceled out among relatives. The siblings often separated. The boy of twelve sent to an uncle's farm in Missouri, the girl of nine to an aunt's town household in Illinois, the baby of two to whoever would take her. The siblings might not see one another for ten years. They sometimes wrote. They sometimes did not.

### The bound-out child

The orphan or half-orphan placed by the county court with a farm family who took him in exchange for his labor until majority. The contract was binding; the child had no recourse. The good placements were genuine families in the receiving sense. The bad placements were free labor under conditions ranging from harsh to abusive.

### The orphan train

The orphan train movement (1854–1929) sent about 250,000 children from the eastern cities (mostly New York and Boston) to placement on western and midwestern farms. Children were displayed at a country church or a town hall when the train arrived; local families chose. The placements were not adoptions in the modern sense — they were indentures, with the family agreeing to feed, clothe, school, and treat the child decently in exchange for his labor.

The child arrived knowing nothing about the family that took him. He sometimes was renamed. He sometimes was sent back at the next train. He sometimes ran. The movement's record is mixed: many children found genuine homes; many did not.

The child stood on the church steps in the line. The local men walked the line and looked. The child whose hair was the color the chooser liked, whose teeth were straight, whose body looked like it could lift a hundred pounds, was taken first. The child who was sickly, who was too small, who did not look strong, was taken last or returned to the train. The agent kept the records; the family signed the indenture; the wagon drove away. The child did not know where he was going. He was eight.

## 10. The Child in the Work

The frontier child was a working person from a younger age than the modern reader expects. The child labor laws of the 1880s and 1890s had not yet reached most western states and would not reach agriculture until much later.

The work on the homestead is documented above: it began at three or four with small tasks and progressed by age, until by ten the child was a substantial labor contributor and by fourteen indistinguishable from an adult worker in capacity if not in legal status.

In the **mine**, the Cornish boy went down at twelve as a tool nipper or a trapper boy (opening and closing ventilation doors on the shift's passage) or as a mucker's helper. Underground work for a twelve-year-old was real and was done. The hours were the shift hours of the men: ten or twelve hours below ground. The pay was a third or half the man's wage.

On the **railroad** the child was the water boy, the message runner, the apprentice in the section gang. The Chinese boy in the Central Pacific work camps of the 1860s — over by the 1870s but the precedent established. The newsboy on the trains, called the _butcher_ or _candy butcher_, walked the cars selling newspapers, fruit, and candy.

In **domestic service** the hired girl was often fourteen or fifteen, sent out by her family to work for board and a few dollars a month in a neighbor's household. She did the work the mistress directed: the cooking, the washing, the cleaning, the care of the children. She slept in a small room off the kitchen or upstairs in the attic. The hired boy was less common in domestic service proper but worked at the livery stable, the mercantile, the smithy in the same way: room, board, small wage, the boss's discipline.

In the **trades** the apprentice was the boy of fourteen indentured to the printer, the wheelwright, the harness-maker, the carpenter, the smith. A formal contract for three to seven years; the apprentice received board, lodging, clothes, basic schooling, and at the end of his term the tools of his trade. The master was responsible for his moral as well as his vocational training. The system was already declining by the 1870s but persisted in the smaller trades.

In the **shows** the child rider in the Mexican circus, the child acrobat in the touring family troupe, the child performer in the medicine show. The child of the performing family was part of the act from an early age.

## 11. The Vocabulary, the Sound, the Smell

The child's world had a specific vocabulary.

The period words for the child by age are these. _Babe, infant, suckling_ for the nursing child. _Toddler, little chap, little one_ for two to four. _Tow-head, button, tyke_ for the small child, often blond. _Boy, girl, child_ as the general word. _Lad, lass_ slightly archaic, used by the older generation. _Youngster, young one_ common. _Young 'un_ the most common spoken form on the frontier. _Brat, whelp_ disparaging. _Half-grown, sprout, stripling_ for the boy of twelve to fifteen. _Young man, young woman_ for the grown child by fifteen or sixteen. _Hand, hired girl_ for the working teenager.

What the child smelled: the wood smoke of the cook fire; the lye of the washday soap; the tallow of the candles; the kerosene of the lamps after the late 1860s; the sweat of the team in the yard; the manure of the barn; the cured meat hanging in the smokehouse; the rising bread; the mother's apron; the father's tobacco; the dog wet from the creek; the pine of the new boards in the loft where he slept.

What the child heard: the mother's voice calling him in; the clock ticking; the wind in the eaves; the dog barking at the gate; the team's harness chains in the yard; the crackle of the fire; the rattle of the supper plates; the hymn the mother sang at the wash kettle; the father's silence; the neighbor's wagon on the trace; the schoolmarm's bell; the boys' shouts at recess; the cow lowing for the calf; the owl in the timber after dark.

What the child saw: the country at adult-knee height; the stove from below; the mother's skirts at face level; the yard's specific surfaces — the chip pile, the well curb, the wood-block by the back door; the road to school in all four seasons; the schoolhouse from the doorway; the boys' faces at the recess line; the father's back as he plowed; the mother at the churn; the grave markers at the family plot at the edge of the section.

## Vignette — Caleb at the Woodpile

> Caleb was at the woodpile when she called. He brought the chips in his apron held up by the corners and emptied them in the box by the stove. She was at the table flouring the board. He stood a moment and watched her hands. “Get on,” she said, not turning. He went out for the cow. The dog came around the corner of the barn and followed him. The cow was at the far end of the pasture. He went down through the grass with the halter over his shoulder.
