# Chapter 14 — Towns, Economy, and Law

> _"FRONT STREET IN DODGE WAS FIVE HUNDRED YARDS OF SALOON AND DANCE HALL AND CRIB. THE DEADLINE RAN DOWN THE TRACKS AND ARMED MEN DID NOT CROSS IT TO THE NORTH SIDE. THIS WAS LAW IN A CATTLE TOWN."_
>
> — A WORKING NOTE ON THE FRONTIER MUNICIPALITY

The frontier town is the second great setting of the West after the open country. Get the geography of a town right — what it has, who runs what, where the law sits and where it does not — and a town scene grounds itself. This chapter covers the cattle town, the mining camp, the railroad town, the army-post town, the small county seat, and the older Mexican town. It covers money, prices, businesses, lawmen, courts, and how violence ended, legally and otherwise.

---

## 1. The Town Types

### The cattle town

Abilene 1867 to 1871. Then Ellsworth, Newton, Wichita, Dodge City, Caldwell — the Kansas trail-head towns followed each other as the railroad pushed west and as quarantine lines pushed the cattle trade off the old ranges. Population ran five hundred to two thousand permanent, swelling to five thousand and more in shipping season (June through October).

The layout: Front Street, the main street, faced the railroad, with the saloons and the gambling halls and the dance halls. A red-light district sat on the far side of the tracks. In Dodge it was the _south side,_ across the _deadline_ — a literal line at the railroad track that armed men were not to cross to the respectable north side. The cattle town died after the cattle trade moved on. Many became farm towns; many vanished.

### The mining camp

Virginia City, Nevada (Comstock silver, 1859 onward). Leadville, Colorado (silver and lead, 1877). Cripple Creek, Colorado (gold, 1891). Tombstone, Arizona (silver, 1877). Deadwood, South Dakota (gold, 1875). Bodie, California (gold, 1859). Butte, Montana (copper, 1880s). Population ran from a few hundred to twenty-five thousand (Leadville in 1880).

The layout built onto the slope above the mine. Saloons and cribs and gambling halls outnumbered every other building. There was a red-light district. There was a single church if any. Often there was no school for years. Wages ran high — a hardrock miner in Leadville made three to four dollars a day, triple a cowhand. Prices ran high; everything was packed in by mule before the railroad reached the camp. The economy was boom-and-bust on the price of the metal.

### The railroad town

The terminus or the divisional point. Cheyenne, Laramie, North Platte, Ogallala. Many were founded by the railroad itself and were dependent on it. The layout ran parallel to the tracks: the depot, the section house, the roundhouse, the company hotel. The population was railroad workers, freight handlers, drovers, and the trade that followed.

### The army-post town

Hog ranches and sutler's stores grew up just outside Army posts, off the reservation, where the post commander could not enforce regulation. Whiskey and women for the soldiers. Often violent. The post itself: officers' quarters, enlisted barracks, parade ground, sutler's store, headquarters, magazine, guardhouse.

### The county seat

The town that had the courthouse. Often the largest town in the county. Lawyers, judges, the land office, the bank, the newspaper, the courthouse square. Stable. The cattle towns and mining camps came and went; the county seats endured.

### The Mexican town

Older than any Anglo town in the Southwest. A plaza in the center, a church on one side, a government building on another. Adobe construction. Markets in the plaza. Bilingual through the Anglo period. Santa Fe, Las Vegas in New Mexico, Tucson, Mesilla — each absorbed an Anglo overlay after the Mexican-American War and the Gadsden Purchase.

## 2. The Buildings, Building by Building

### The saloon

The dominant business. A cattle town of fifteen hundred might have twenty saloons. Dodge City in 1878 had nineteen licensed. The room ran on a long bar of milled wood with a brass rail at the foot, a mirror behind the bar, bottles on the back wall, a painting of a reclining nude (the _fancy painting_) above the bar in the better places. Tables along the walls and in the back. A faro layout, a chuck-a-luck wheel, a poker table. Sawdust on the floor, swept and replaced daily. Spittoons at intervals.

Drinks: beer (when ice and breweries permitted) at five cents the schooner. Whiskey at twenty-five cents the shot in the cattle towns, ten in the rougher places. Champagne and imported wines in the parlor saloons of the rich towns — Tombstone's Crystal Palace, Denver's Windsor Hotel bar.

The saloon was the meeting hall, the post office (mail held behind the bar), the unofficial courthouse, the male parlor. It was open all hours.

### The brothel and its hierarchy

The trade ran a clear hierarchy.

**The parlor house** was the high end. A two-story Victorian house with a parlor, a piano, a madame, six to twelve women. A gentleman caller bought drinks (champagne five dollars the bottle), spent time in the parlor, and went upstairs. Five to twenty dollars the visit. The women were paid relatively well and worked in conditions the rest of the trade did not have.

**The dance hall** charged customers ten to twenty-five cents the dance, after which the customer bought the woman a drink (the bar cut her in on the drink). Many dance-hall women were also prostitutes.

**The saloon girl** worked the floor of a saloon, pushing drinks. Often a prostitute as well. Worked on commission from the bar.

**The crib** was a single-room shack, often in a row of identical cribs along a back alley — Dodge's Tin Can Alley, Leadville's Stillborn Alley. The lowest rung. The woman might see twenty or thirty men a night at fifty cents to a dollar. Disease, addiction, violence, and suicide were the endpoints. Most crib girls were dead or gone by thirty.

**The hog ranch** was a roadhouse-brothel outside an Army post. Whiskey, gambling, women. Famously violent.

**The married-out** was a prostitute who married a customer or a saloon man and left the trade. A common path out for the lucky.

The reformer's account of the trade as uniformly degraded was a distortion; the celebrant's account of _soiled doves with hearts of gold_ was a distortion the other way. The real population ran from highly paid madames to children dying of syphilis in back alleys. Write any specific woman by her position. Do not generalize.

### The bank

A wood-frame or brick building. A small lobby with a teller's window. A back office. A safe — or a vault, in the better places. The bank was small; a county-seat bank might have twenty thousand dollars in deposits. A robbery of five thousand was a major event. Bank robberies were not common — the James-Younger gang's Northfield, Minnesota raid in 1876 ended in disaster (the townspeople shot the gang to pieces) and showed the danger. More money was taken from trains and from stagecoaches.

### The mercantile / general store

The center of trade for everything not gun, drink, or woman. Flour, sugar, salt, coffee, beans, salt pork, canned goods (Underwood deviled ham, Borden's condensed milk, canned peaches), bolts of cloth, ready-made clothes, boots, hats, hardware, harness, saddles, ammunition, tobacco, patent medicine, books, newspapers, school slates, hair tonic, lamp chimneys, kerosene. Often the post office. Often the unofficial bank — the merchant carried accounts on the books for the local ranchers (paid up at shipping time) and paid out cash from the till. A long counter. Glass cases for small valuable goods. Barrels of crackers and pickles. Hams hung from the ceiling. The smell of coffee, leather, kerosene, and tobacco.

### The hotel — three classes

The boarding house: a room in a frame building, twenty-five cents to a dollar a night, meals extra. The hotel: a two-story frame building, one to three dollars a night with meals. The grand hotel — the Driskill in Austin, the Brown Palace in Denver, the Crystal Palace in Tombstone — three to ten dollars a night, with electric lights and indoor plumbing in the better rooms after the 1880s.

### Stables, smith, paper, church, school, court, jail, and undertaker

The boarding stable held the traveler's horse for fifty cents to a dollar a day for hay, oats, and a stall. The town's blacksmith often worked at or next to the livery. The smith had the open-front building with the forge, the anvil, the bellows; he shod horses, made and mended hardware, mended wagons. He charged a dollar to shoe a horse all around. He was usually prosperous and respected.

The newspaper was a weekly broadsheet, often four pages. Editorials, local gossip, telegraph news from the East, advertisements, legal notices. The editor was a notable man and often shot at. The press was an iron Washington hand-press.

A church in any decent town. The Methodist circuit rider preceded permanent churches. Catholics where the population was Mexican or Irish. Episcopalians, Presbyterians, Baptists. In the rougher cattle towns the church and the pulpit were late arrivals.

A one-room schoolhouse with eight grades together. The schoolmarm — often a single Eastern woman who came west on contract — was paid poorly and watched closely. The schoolhouse doubled as a church, a meeting hall, a polling place.

The courthouse stood in the county seat — brick or stone if the county was rich, frame if not, with the courtroom on the upper floor and the offices below, the town square or plaza around it. The jail was one-story brick or stone in a rich county; a log or frame building in a poor one. Two to six cells. An iron bunk and a slop bucket. Often no separate cell for women, who were held in the jailer's house under guard.

The undertaker was usually the town carpenter. A photograph of the deceased posed in his coffin was period practice.

### The other businesses

A doctor (often above the drugstore). A lawyer (often above the bank). A drugstore (patent medicine, tobacco, soda fountain in the fancier towns). A telegraph office (in any town on the railroad). An express office (Wells Fargo, American Express). A railroad depot. A land office (in any settlement zone). A barber shop (haircut twenty-five cents, shave a dime, bath twenty-five cents). Restaurants, chop houses, and oyster houses (yes — canned and later iced oysters reached the West, where they were a delicacy). A Chinese laundry in any town with a Chinese community. Tinkers, harness makers, gunsmiths, jewelers, booksellers.

## 4. Law on the Frontier — Who Did What

### The federal layer

**The deputy U.S. marshal** was a federal officer with jurisdiction in federal cases — mail robbery, federal land disputes, crimes in federal territories, crimes in Indian Territory. The most respected lawmen on the frontier. Bass Reeves, Heck Thomas, Bill Tilghman, Chris Madsen — the _Three Guardsmen_ of Indian Territory — were deputy U.S. marshals. Paid by fee: so much per arrest, mileage, return. **The U.S. marshal** was the administrative head, appointed by the President, ran the deputies, kept a courthouse office. **The federal court:** Judge Isaac Parker at Fort Smith, Arkansas had jurisdiction over the Indian Territory through the 1880s — the _Hanging Judge,_ who sent seventy-nine men to the gallows.

### The territorial / state layer

**The sheriff** was the elected county officer who served warrants, ran the jail, collected county taxes, served civil process, and could appoint deputies. The most powerful local law in most Western counties. **The Texas Rangers** were a state mounted force of approximately five hundred men in companies of thirty to eighty, with statewide jurisdiction. Effective and brutal. Suppressed Comanche raiding, hunted outlaws, fought Mexican raiders along the Rio Grande, and played a role in extralegal violence against Tejanos. **Arizona Rangers** and **New Mexico Mounted Police** were similar state forces, later — 1900s.

### The town / municipal layer

**The town marshal** was hired by the town council with jurisdiction inside town limits. The town marshal in a cattle town like Dodge or Tombstone enforced the no-firearms-in-town ordinance (every cattle town had one in some form), broke up fights, walked his rounds at night, collected fines. **The constable** was a lower municipal officer. **The night watchman** was hired by businesses to walk a beat — often by Wells Fargo to guard the bank or the express office.

### The private layer

**Pinkerton agents** worked for the national detective agency hired by banks, railroads, and mine owners. Strikebreakers — often brutally — bounty hunters, infiltrators. Hated in working-class country. **Stock detectives and range detectives** were hired by the cattlemen's associations of Wyoming, Montana, and Texas. Some were killers (Tom Horn). The Johnson County War (Wyoming, 1892) was fought between the large ranchers' detectives and the small ranchers and homesteaders. **Wells Fargo detectives** investigated stage and express robberies; James B. Hume was the most famous. **Private guards** for companies, mines, and ranches.

### The extralegal layer

**Vigilance committees** were self-appointed citizen groups that arrested, tried, and hanged suspected criminals when (or before) the legal authority acted. The famous: the San Francisco Committee of Vigilance (1851 and 1856), the Montana Vigilantes (1864–65, who hanged Sheriff Henry Plummer's gang in Bannack and Virginia City), the Ku Klux Klan and the White Leagues in the Reconstruction South. **Lynch mobs** were less organized than vigilance committees — a crowd, sometimes drunk, that took a prisoner from the jail and hanged him. Common. Jails in the West frequently lost their prisoners. **Posses** — a sheriff or marshal could deputize the _posse comitatus,_ any able-bodied man within his jurisdiction, to pursue a fugitive. A posse was the line between law and mob, and the line moved.

## 5. The Court

The justice of the peace was the local elected magistrate. He heard small civil cases and minor criminal matters, could marry people, swore out warrants, fined drunks. He did not need legal training in most jurisdictions. The police court or mayor's court heard ordinance violations in town. The county or district court heard the major cases — often a circuit judge who rode a circuit of several counties, holding court in each in season.

A trial: twelve-man jury. The accused had counsel if he could pay. Court-appointed counsel for capital cases by the 1880s in most jurisdictions. Witness testimony, cross-examination, closing arguments. Verdict.

The sentence: hanging for murder in most jurisdictions, replaced by long prison terms in the 1890s in some states. Penitentiary for serious felonies — Yuma in Arizona, Cañon City in Colorado, Deer Lodge in Montana, Huntsville in Texas, Leavenworth federal. County jail for misdemeanors. Fines.

A real frontier trial sounded quick — a murder trial often lasted one or two days. Crowded; the town turned out. The press was present; the newspaper printed the testimony in full. The hanging, when it came, was public, well attended (in some places by ticketed admission), and followed by a picnic. Public hangings were largely abolished by state law in the late nineteenth century, but private ones (with selected witnesses inside the jail yard) continued.

## 6. The Reality of Frontier Crime

Two corrections to the popular myth.

**Most western towns were not violent.** Dodge City in its peak year had five killings. Tombstone in 1881 had five (one was the OK Corral). A modern American city of the same size has more. The bulk of arrests in any cattle town were for drunkenness, disorderly conduct, and prostitution — the latter licensed and fined as a revenue source.

**The big violence was in the country, not the town.** Range wars, Indian Wars, vigilante actions in the unsettled country, the lynching of Mexicans and Black men in the Texas borderlands and the Reconstruction South, the killing of homesteaders by hired guns of the cattle barons — the West's real body counts were here. The _gunfighter walking the dusty street at high noon_ is a single tradition out of many. Use it sparingly. Use the rest.

## 7. The Big Picture of the Economy

The West was a colonial economy supplying raw materials to the industrial East and to Europe. **Cattle:** beef on the hoof shipped east; hides shipped to Europe; tallow for candles and soap. **Mining:** gold, silver, copper, lead, zinc shipped east and to Europe. **Hides and furs:** buffalo hides (1872–83) shipped east for industrial belting and for fashionable robes; beaver had been the trade of the mountain men a generation earlier. **Lumber:** Sierra and Pacific Northwest timber. **Wheat:** California, Kansas, the Dakotas — shipped east and to Europe. **Wool:** the New Mexico, Wyoming, and Montana sheep flocks.

The frontier was capital-poor. Eastern and European money financed the cattle barons (Scottish syndicates owned vast ranches in Wyoming and the Texas Panhandle). British and Eastern money financed the railroads. Local currency was scarce. Credit ran the small economy.

This reality is missed in the screen-cowboy western. The cattle baron in the page version is not a man on a horse — he is a syndicate, a New York or Edinburgh director who has never been west of Pittsburgh. The drover who works for him is the bottom of a long financial chain whose top sits in a London office. Write this and the West gets larger and stranger.

## 8. What the Town Smells Like, Sounds Like

**Smell.** Horse manure in the street, constant — a town of two thousand had perhaps a thousand horses. Wood smoke from cookstoves and forges. Bakery bread in the morning. Tobacco. Whiskey through the saloon doors. Coal smoke from the railroad. Outhouse stench in summer. Flowers in the spring along the fenced yards of the respectable houses.

**Sound.** Wagon wheels on dirt or on plank sidewalks. Iron shoes on the same. Hammers in the smith's shop. Piano in the saloon at night, badly tuned. The locomotive's whistle three times a day. The court bell. The school bell. The church bell on Sunday. Drunks shouting in the late hours. A pistol shot now and then, often at the moon.

**Sight.** Mud or dust in the streets, depending on weather. False-front frame buildings with the storefront one story taller than the building behind. Boardwalks with hitching rails out front. Tied horses. Loose dogs. Children. Few trees in most western towns; planted ones were a status symbol.

A town scene with two or three of these on the page reads true. A town scene with none reads like a movie set, which is what too many published westerns settle for.

## Vignette — Saturday Night in Front Street

> The piano in the Long Branch was working over a tune nobody could place. The cowhands from the Slaughter outfit had come into town that afternoon, their horses tied three deep at the rail, and were down to silver dollars and shouting. Wyatt walked the boardwalk on the north side with the eight-pointed star on his vest and a sawed-off Belgian gun under his arm. The deadline ran along the tracks. The boys knew the deadline. None of them had crossed. One of them — Cleveland, the youngest, no older than seventeen — was in the alley between the saloon and the dance hall with a girl named May, and his pay for four months on the trail was in May's silk garter inside half an hour. Wyatt did not look down the alley. The piano kept on. The night went forward. A pistol shot from the south side a little after midnight; nobody dead, somebody's hat ruined. Wyatt walked back the other way and the lamps in the front windows of the saloons threw long yellow blocks on the dust of the street.
