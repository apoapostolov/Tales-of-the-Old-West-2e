# Chapter 13 — Prices and Anchors

> _"HE LAID TWO SILVER DOLLARS ON THE BAR AND THE BARTENDER SET THE BOTTLE DOWN BETWEEN THEM."_
>
> — A WORKING NOTE ON HOW TO WRITE A 1870S TRANSACTION

A price in prose is not a price tag. It is a fact that does work in the scene. The work is one of three things: the pinch — the character cannot afford it, and the number measures the gap between desire and means; the flush — the character can afford it easily, and the number measures freedom (the man with $300 in his pocket has options the man with $3 does not); or the pact — the price is the lever in a deal, _two hundred dollars in gold or one hundred head of your own steers._ A number that does no work in the scene is decoration; cut it. Drop a price the way the period dropped a price — plain, in the same line as the action: _Bowman laid two silver dollars on the bar._ Not _Bowman placed a sum of two dollars in silver coinage, representing payment for his drink, on the polished wood of the bar's surface._ The first earns the period; the second commits four felonies in a sentence. This chapter is the working anchor sheet. Drop the right number into the right scene and the prose grounds itself; pick the wrong one and the scene drifts.

## 1. Wages — The Working West

A worker's wage is the most useful anchor a scene can carry. It tells the reader what a man's day is worth and lets the reader weigh every price against it.

### Domestic, service, and the trade

A laundress earned fifty cents to a dollar a day or per piece. A domestic servant earned eight to fifteen dollars a month with room and board. A private cook fifteen to twenty-five. A wet nurse ten to fifteen a week. A midwife five to fifteen per delivery. A seamstress fifty cents to a dollar a day. A hash-slinger waitress twenty to thirty a month plus room, board, and tips.

In the trade, a top parlor-house madame who owned her house and took a share might clear three hundred to a thousand dollars a month or more. A parlor-house woman fifty to a hundred and fifty dollars a week kept. A dance-hall girl fifteen to thirty dollars a week plus commission on drinks. A saloon girl ten to twenty plus commission. A crib girl five to fifteen kept — the bottom of the trade.

## 3. Transportation — Fares and Hires

Stagecoach fare ran ten to fifteen cents a mile. Train fare in coach three to five cents a mile, first class five to ten, the emigrant car one to three. The Pullman berth two to three dollars a night extra. New York to San Francisco in 1879 was forty to sixty in coach, a hundred to a hundred and fifty first class, twenty to thirty in emigrant. Steamboat fare on the Missouri or Mississippi ran fifty cents to two dollars per hundred miles. Freight by rail ran one to three cents the ton-mile; by wagon ten to twenty.

Hire a buggy and team for the day, two to five dollars. Hire a saddle horse for the day, a dollar to two. Stage relays were included in the fare.

## 7. Distance and Time — How Far and How Long

The other half of the anchor is _how far_ and _how long._ A character can leave Lampasas at sunup and reach Austin by nightfall (eighty miles) only on a fresh horse and a hard ride that would lame the horse. A character on the trail between Texas and Kansas does not arrive in three weeks; he arrives in three months. The wrong distance breaks the scene the way the wrong gear does.

### By foot

Walking level and fresh, three miles an hour. Walking level all day, twenty-five miles in eight to ten hours. A military forced march thirty miles in a day, can be done two days running; an exceptional forced march forty miles in a day, hard on the men. A mountain trail one and a half to two miles an hour. Walking in snow without snowshoes, less than a mile an hour.

### By horse

Walking, four miles an hour. Trotting eight. Cantering twelve. Galloping twenty-five — sustained for one to three minutes only; longer ruins the horse. A long-day mixed walk-and-trot ride covers thirty-five to fifty miles in eight to ten hours. The Pony Express on relays of fresh horses made two hundred to two hundred and fifty miles in twenty-four hours. One man on one horse over a hard week makes two hundred and fifty to four hundred. A cattle drive on the trail keeps a ten- to fifteen-mile pace. A cavalry column on sustained march twenty-five to thirty miles a day.

### By wagon

An ox-drawn wagon covers two miles an hour and ten to fifteen miles a day, six to eight days a week to rest the oxen. A horse-drawn wagon three to four miles an hour and twenty to twenty-five miles a day. A mule-drawn freight wagon three miles an hour and twenty miles a day with a heavy load. A stagecoach on relays of fresh teams five to six miles an hour and a hundred to a hundred and twenty-five miles a day.

### By rail

A passenger express in 1875 averaged twenty-five to thirty-five miles an hour with forty to fifty top. A passenger local fifteen to twenty-five (frequent stops). Freight fifteen to twenty. New York to Chicago twenty-five to thirty-five hours. Chicago to Omaha twenty to twenty-four. Omaha to Sacramento (transcontinental) four to five days. Sacramento to San Francisco four hours. New York to San Francisco end-to-end seven days fast, ten typical.

### By steamboat

St. Louis to Bismarck upstream the Missouri, thirty to forty-five days. Bismarck to St. Louis downstream, ten to fifteen. St. Louis to New Orleans downstream the Mississippi, four to five. New Orleans to St. Louis upstream, ten to twelve.

### The trail drive (Texas to Kansas)

Leaving south Texas in April or May; reaching the Red River crossing in four to five weeks (three to four hundred miles); crossing Indian Territory in six to eight weeks (another three to four hundred); reaching the Kansas trailhead — Abilene early, later Dodge or Caldwell — in June, July, or August. Total drive duration three to four months. Total distance eight hundred to fifteen hundred miles depending on origin and destination.

### The freight haul

Independence to Santa Fe, six to eight weeks. Santa Fe to Chihuahua, four to six. Salt Lake City to San Francisco, eight to ten. Bismarck or Cheyenne to Deadwood in the Black Hills, two to three weeks. Tucson to Camp Apache, five to seven days.

### The mail

New York to San Francisco by rail, seven days in 1875. New York to Denver four. New York to Tucson (rail to El Paso, stage to Tucson) twelve to fifteen. New York to London by Cunard mail steamer, nine to ten. Western town to next county seat, one to three days. Western town to St. Louis, four to seven.

### The telegram

Any wired town to any wired town: minutes for transmission, plus a delivery boy at the destination (minutes to hours). New York to London by cable: hours.

## 8. Weights, Capacities, Sizes

The average adult man in the 1870s ran five-foot-seven to five-foot-nine and a hundred and forty-five to a hundred and sixty-five pounds. The average adult woman five-foot-two to five-foot-four and a hundred and fifteen to a hundred and thirty-five.

A mustang or cowpony stood thirteen to fourteen hands and weighed eight hundred to nine hundred and fifty pounds. A developing quarter horse fourteen to fifteen hands and nine fifty to eleven hundred. A cavalry mount fifteen hands and a thousand to twelve hundred. A draft horse sixteen to seventeen hands and fifteen to eighteen hundred. A riding mule eight hundred to a thousand. A pack mule eight hundred to twelve hundred. A draft mule twelve hundred to sixteen hundred. A horse carries a man and gear (two to two-fifty pounds) all day; a pack animal carries one-fifty to two hundred. A wagon team of two horses pulls fifteen hundred pounds on a level dirt road; four pull three thousand; six pull forty-five hundred to five thousand.

A Texas longhorn cow weighs seven to nine hundred pounds; a four-year longhorn steer on grass eight-fifty to eleven hundred; the same steer fattened and delivered to Kansas nine-fifty to twelve hundred. An improved British (Shorthorn, Hereford) steer twelve to fifteen hundred. A calf at branding two to four hundred. A cowhand on a drive looks after about two hundred and fifty to four hundred head. A drive crew of twelve men pushes twenty-five hundred to three thousand head north.

A light farm wagon carries fifteen hundred to two thousand pounds. A heavy farm wagon three to four thousand. A Conestoga six to twelve thousand. A Murphy wagon (Santa Fe trade) five to seven thousand. A mountain wagon for narrow trails eight hundred to fifteen hundred. A buckboard the same. A buggy six to eight hundred pounds of passengers and gear. A Concord stagecoach nine inside, six on top, about twenty-five hundred pounds total.

The standard sack of flour was a hundred pounds. A sack of beans fifty. A bale of hay eighty to a hundred. A whiskey barrel of fifty-three gallons full ran about five hundred. A flour barrel a hundred and ninety-six. A crate of canned goods fifty to seventy-five. A working saddle with rigging thirty-five to forty-five. A cowboy's bedroll with tarp twenty-five to thirty-five. A loaded Winchester '73 with fifteen rounds about nine and a half. A Sharps Model 1874 with the heavy barrel ten to twelve. A loaded Colt SAA with six in the cylinder about two and a half.

### Money in the hand

A twenty-dollar gold double eagle weighed 33.4 grams (just over an ounce). A ten-dollar eagle 16.7. A silver dollar (Trade or Morgan) 26.7 to 27 grams (just under an ounce). Sixty silver dollars came to about three and a half pounds.

A man carrying his year's earnings in coin: a cowman with $300 in $20 gold pieces (fifteen coins) carries about a pound, easily concealed. The same $300 in silver dollars (three hundred coins) is seventeen pounds and a sack — visible, heavy, and a target. A bank robbery yielding $5,000 in gold weighs nine pounds. The same in silver weighs nearly three hundred pounds — five men's load, requires saddlebags or a pack mule. The robber's choice between hitting the gold counter and the silver counter is a real choice.

## 9. Composite Sums — The Set Pieces

### The cowhand's roll at end of drive

Twenty-five dollars a month for four months on the trail comes to a hundred. Less small stable-hand and outfitting deductions, eighty to ninety dollars cash in the hand at Abilene or Dodge. Gone in ten to fourteen days. The bunkhouse routine: rooming a dollar; bath fifty cents; shave fifteen cents; new shirt a dollar; dinner a dollar; whiskey at the saloon five; faro losses twenty; dance hall five; parlor house fifteen. Repeat.

### The trail boss's stake

A hundred and twenty-five a month for five months is six hundred and twenty-five. A trail boss who has run drives for several years has enough to buy his own outfit and become a small cattleman.

### The mining strike

A placer claim that yields one ounce of gold a day at twenty dollars the ounce comes to six hundred a month. The claim that yields _only_ one ounce a day is a good claim. A bonanza claim yields pounds of gold in a week. The Bonanza Kings of the Comstock made millions.

### The bank robbery

The James-Younger gang took seven hundred dollars in the Northfield raid before being shot to pieces. The largest train robberies of the period took five to fifteen thousand in cash and gold from the express car safe. The largest stage robberies took one to five thousand. The bank in a small town held perhaps five to twenty thousand in the vault.

### The widow's annual budget (small Western town, 1875)

Income from taking in laundry and sewing, thirty to forty dollars a month, three sixty to four eighty a year. Rent on a small frame house, five to ten a month, sixty to a hundred and twenty a year. Food for one woman and one child, fifteen to twenty a month, a hundred and eighty to two-forty. Clothing, fuel, medicine, school for the child, ten to fifteen a month, a hundred and twenty to a hundred and eighty. Net: she breaks even in a good year, slips into debt at the mercantile in a bad one.

### The cattleman's profit

Twenty-five hundred head trailed from Texas to Kansas. Cost in Texas at fifteen to twenty dollars a head delivered to the trailhead in south Texas: forty thousand. Trail cost (cowhands, cook, horses, supplies, losses): five to seven thousand. Sale at Abilene at twenty-five to thirty a head: sixty-five thousand. Net profit: fifteen to twenty thousand. A successful drive made the trail boss rich and the cowman richer. A drive that lost half the herd to stampede or river ruined both.

### The doctor's fees over a year (small Western town)

Two hundred office visits at a dollar to two: two to four hundred. Fifty house calls at two to five: a hundred to two-fifty. Ten deliveries at five to fifteen: fifty to a hundred and fifty. Twenty minor surgeries and bone settings at five to ten: a hundred to two hundred. Two major surgeries at twenty-five to a hundred: fifty to two hundred. Patent medicine sales: a hundred to three hundred. Total gross: six hundred to fifteen hundred. Less unpaid debts (perhaps half): three to seven-fifty net. A frontier doctor without a wealthy practice barely got by.

### The schoolmarm's year

Thirty dollars a month for six to eight months (depending on district) comes to a hundred and eighty to two-forty. Board with a local family is free (counted as part of the pay). Net cash: she returns to the East with fifty to a hundred saved if prudent.

## 10. The Wages-to-Prices Ratio

A useful sanity check for any 1870s scene. A cowhand earns roughly a dollar a day after the trail's deductions, and his food is provided. His daily wage buys him at saloon prices: twenty schooners of beer; or four shots of decent whiskey; or one cheap meal at the hash house and four schooners; or four pouches of Bull Durham; or two cigars; or a bath; or a shave and a haircut and a beer.

A hardrock miner earns roughly $3.50 a day. After paying $1.25 to $1.50 for board (he buys his own), he has two dollars of discretionary income — twice the cowhand's. The miner's camp prices are higher, however; the actual margin runs similar.

A schoolmarm earns roughly a dollar a day after board. Her discretionary spending is mostly clothing (a five-dollar dress takes a full week of net wages) and savings.

A captain in the Army earns five dollars a day plus allowances. He can afford a hotel suite, imported wine, and the better tailor. He cannot afford a ranch.

A small-town doctor in a slow year earns fifty dollars a month — about the same as a sergeant — but is in debt for his medical education and his stock of patent medicine.

The largest gulf in the period's economy was between cash labor (the cowhand at a dollar a day) and the entrepreneurial classes (the cattleman, the saloon owner, the mine owner) who could clear thousands a year if they survived the cycle. A working cowhand who saved his pay for ten years could buy a small herd and become the next decade's small cattleman. Most did not save.

## 11. The Single Rule

Every number in your scene is doing work or it is decoration. If it is decoration, cut it. If it is work, get it right. The reader who knows the period will trust the prose that gets the silver dollar right. The reader who does not know the period will trust the prose anyway, because the numbers fit each other — the wage and the meal and the bullet and the bottle all sit at the same level. Coherence is the period's voice as much as diction is. Drop the right number. Move on. Do not explain it.

> _He laid two silver dollars on the bar and the bartender set the bottle down between them._

That is the period in eighteen words. The bottle is a one- to two-dollar rotgut bottle. Two silver dollars buys it. Both men know it. The reader reads the scene without being told. That is the use of an anchor.

## Vignette — Settling Up

> Ramsey came down to the mercantile the morning after the drive paid off and laid out what he owed against the year. The sheriff had wanted his fee for the inquest on the Mexican boy who had drowned at Doan's, ten dollars. The doctor had wanted his fee for the broken arm Jim had taken in the stampede at the Cimarron, eight. The smith had wanted a dollar fifty for the new shoes on the bay. The bunkhouse cook had wanted a dollar he had advanced Charlie at Caldwell. Ramsey paid them out of the leather pouch on his hip, in twenty-dollar gold pieces and in silver dollars and in Mexican pesos that the merchant weighed on the small balance and credited at face. When he was done he had four gold pieces and eleven silver dollars left and he was the closest thing to rich he had been in three years. He bought a new hat and a Sunday coat and rode home.
