# Chapter 3 — The Borderlands

> _“POBRE MÉXICO, TAN LEJOS DE DIOS Y TAN CERCA DE LOS ESTADOS UNIDOS.”_
>
> — PORFIRIO DÍAZ

The popular West has been written almost entirely from the Anglo side of the border. The historical West does not work that way. The border was a porous, violent, commercial, intermarried, and contested zone. The same families lived on both sides. The same cattle and horses crossed in both directions, often multiple times in their lives, often without anyone's permission. The same languages — Spanish, English, the various Native languages, the trade pidgin — were heard on both sides.

What follows is the country on both sides of the line: the Mexican side and the Anglo side, the institutions of each, and the population that lived between them.

---

## 1. The Geography of the Border

The 1870s U.S.–Mexico border ran from the mouth of the Rio Grande on the Gulf of Mexico to San Diego on the Pacific. It was established by the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo in 1848 and the Gadsden Purchase in 1854. Most of it was unfenced, unmarked except for occasional surveyor monuments, and unguarded.

### The eastern border

The Rio Grande from El Paso del Norte (now Ciudad Juárez) to the Gulf. The river itself was the border. It was crossed at fords and ferries. It also shifted course — Rio Grande meanders are characteristic — and the shifts created disputes about which side of the new channel a piece of land was on. The Chamizal dispute that grew out of this was not resolved until 1963.

The major crossings were Brownsville–Matamoros, Rio Grande City–Camargo, Laredo–Nuevo Laredo, Eagle Pass–Piedras Negras, Del Rio–Ciudad Acuña, and El Paso–Ciudad Juárez.

### The western border

A surveyor's line west from El Paso to the Pacific. This was the desert border, crossing the Sonoran and Chihuahuan deserts. Few settlements. The border was crossed where there was water: at the few springs and at the small towns near them.

### The country on both sides

The Mexican states bordering the U.S. in 1870 were Tamaulipas, Nuevo León, Coahuila, Chihuahua, Sonora, and — until the Gadsden Purchase had taken its northern part — Baja California. The northern Mexican states were thinly populated, dominated by large ranching estates (haciendas) and small farming villages, and as embattled by Apache raids as the American Southwest was.

---

## 2. Population and Language

### Hispanic populations on the U.S. side

The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo guaranteed citizenship and property rights to Mexicans in the ceded territory. Hispanic populations remained throughout the Southwest — in northern New Mexico, with the Hispano villages and the great families like the Oteros, Lunas, and Chaves; in southern Texas, with the Tejano ranchos and towns; in southern Arizona, with the Sonoran population around Tucson; in California, with the Californio ranchos declining rapidly under American pressure.

In 1870, much of the Southwest was Spanish-speaking. Court proceedings, town councils, newspapers, and social life were conducted in Spanish in many areas. The Anglo immigrant adapted or did not function. By 1900 the demographic and language balance had shifted decisively toward English, but in 1870 the shift was incomplete.

### The new Mexican migration

The 1870s saw the beginning of northward migration of working Mexicans seeking employment in the expanding U.S. Southwest economy: railroad construction, mining, agriculture (especially the cotton fields of south Texas and the citrus and vegetable fields of California). The labor migration that would define the twentieth-century border was just starting in our period.

### The languages of the border

Spanish was dominant south of the border and a major language north of it. English spread rapidly north of the border and was rare south of it. Native languages were spoken in their own communities — Yaqui, Mayo, Tarahumara, Comanche, Apache, and others. Border Spanish was a specific dialect with regional vocabulary, English loanwords, and idioms not found in Spain or central Mexico. Caló was the argot of the border, a mixed Spanish–English–Native vocabulary used in some specific communities; the pachuco caló was a later development.

### The languages in the room

On the border the language mix runs through the day. Spanish speakers and English speakers cross at every counter and every corral. The Spanish words that have no clean English equivalent — _mesa, arroyo, rebozo, alcalde, jefe, vaquero_ — go straight into the Anglo's working speech. The Spanish-speaker's English, when he has it, is the English of an educated bilingual; the Anglo who has been on the border long enough has the Spanish of a working bilingual. The _usted/tú_ distinction carries weight a Spanish-speaker hears that the English _you_ erases.

## 3. The Cattle and Horse Trade

The border's principal economic activity, legal and illegal, was the movement of cattle and horses.

The legal trade was substantial. Mexican cattle were imported into Texas and the Southwest legally through customs at the major crossings — tens of thousands of head per year through the 1870s, paying duties, herded north to U.S. markets.

The illegal traffic was perhaps as large. Mexican cattle stolen by U.S. cattle thieves and driven north for sale at U.S. markets. U.S. cattle stolen by Mexican thieves and driven south. Indian raiding parties, especially Apache and Comanche through the 1870s, drove stolen stock from one side to the other and sold it through fences on the receiving side. The cattle thief on the border was a specific type: operating on both sides, knowing the country, often allied with corrupt officials. The "Cattle Kingdom" of King Fisher and the operations of John King Fisher and others in south Texas are documentable cases.

A major specific trade was horses for the U.S. remount service. Mexican horses — the small, tough, hardy criollo stock descended from the Spanish horses — were valued by the U.S. cavalry. Whether purchased honestly or stolen and laundered, they moved north in numbers.

The fence on the border was different from the fence in the U.S. interior because the border itself provided the laundering. Cattle stolen in Texas were driven across the river, brands altered or left to grow over, sold to a Mexican rancher who might or might not know they were stolen, then perhaps sold north as Mexican cattle into the legal trade. The cycle could run several times for a single animal.

## 4. Mexican Law Enforcement — the Rurales

The Mexican federal rural police force — the _Cuerpo de Rurales_ — was established by Benito Juárez in 1861 and built up under Porfirio Díaz, who became president in 1876. The rurales were a mounted national police force charged with maintaining order in the countryside and pursuing bandits.

By 1880 the rurales numbered about 2,500 men in several corps. They were concentrated in the central Mexican states and the border states. They were mounted, armed (initially with rifles and revolvers, later more standardized), and uniformed in a distinctive gray-and-silver uniform with a wide sombrero.

The rurales had a fearsome reputation for summary justice. Suspected bandits were often shot on capture under the _ley fuga_ — the law of flight. The official explanation was that the prisoner was shot while attempting escape; the actual practice was execution without trial. The rurales were effective at suppressing banditry but at high cost in due process.

The rurale in fiction is rarer than he should be. He is a parallel to the Texas Ranger or the U.S. marshal — the federal mounted lawman in difficult country. A scene with a rurale officer crossing paths with a Texas Ranger is a scene the canon has not used enough. The academic standard is Paul Vanderwood's _Disorder and Progress: Bandits, Police, and Mexican Development._

## 5. The Texas Ranger

The Anglo counterpart, broadly. The Texas Rangers were re-established in 1874 as the Frontier Battalion, after the post-Civil-War period of dormancy. Their brief was Indian fighting, anti-banditry, and general law enforcement in the unsettled country.

The Frontier Battalion at full strength was several hundred men in six companies, each company assigned a region. Each company was about thirty to seventy men under a captain. They were mounted, armed (typically Winchester or Sharps rifle plus revolver), ununiformed (each Ranger provided his own clothes and equipment), and modestly paid.

The Texas Ranger's methods were aggressive and informally lawful. He pursued, he engaged, he arrested or killed. The Ranger reputation for violence on the border, especially against Tejano populations during the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, is documented and has been the subject of significant historical reassessment. The famous Rangers of the period — Leander McNelly, whose company conducted the controversial 1875 Las Cuevas raid into Mexico; Lee Hall; John Armstrong; Sergeant Ira Aten; John Hughes — provide character models. McNelly is a particular case: brilliant, brutal, dying of tuberculosis, willing to violate the border for what he understood as Texan interests.

A complicating fact: some Tejano men served as Texas Rangers, notably during the period. The Hispanic Ranger is a figure rarely written and worth writing.

## 6. The Corrido

The Mexican border ballad. The musical form that recorded the border's events from the Mexican perspective.

A corrido is a narrative folk ballad, typically in quatrains, with a regular meter (octosyllabic lines), a guitar accompaniment, and a sung narrative. The corrido tells a story — a famous killing, a folk hero's exploits, a notable event, an outlaw's career and capture, a flood or a fire.

A typical corrido opens with a salute to the listener (_Voy a cantar esta historia, señores, escuchen bien_ — _I am going to sing this story, gentlemen, listen well_), then states the protagonist and the situation, then carries the narrative as a sequence of events with remembered dialogue, then a moral or commentary, then a farewell to the listener.

The corrido tradition produced specific famous ballads about specific events. _El Corrido de Joaquín Murrieta_ kept the California bandit of the 1850s in memory long after Anglo accounts forgot or distorted him. _El Corrido de Gregorio Cortez_ recorded a 1901 event — just past our period, though the cultural formation is older — in which Cortez killed a Texas sheriff in a misunderstanding and was pursued for hundreds of miles. Américo Paredes's _With His Pistol in His Hand_ (1958) is the scholarly reference and one of the most important works of Mexican-American cultural studies. The corridos of the Mexican Revolution are a later development (1910s) but the tradition's high point.

A scene with a corrido sung in a Mexican border town cantina can carry historical and emotional weight. The corrido is the people's history, the version the Anglo records did not preserve. A character who hears the corrido of his own father, his own town, his own cause has been placed in a specific cultural context. Sources include Américo Paredes's _With His Pistol in His Hand_ and _A Texas-Mexican Cancionero_, and Manuel Peña's _Música Tejana._

## 7. The Bandit and the Social Bandit

The border bandit was a complicated figure. Some were criminals by any measure. Some were Robin Hood figures in their communities, robbing the Anglo establishment and sharing with Hispanic poor. Some were political — the boundary between banditry and revolutionary politics on the nineteenth-century border was thin.

**Juan Cortina** (Juan Nepomuceno Cortina) was a Mexican rancher of Brownsville who led an armed uprising in 1859 — the Cortina War — protesting Anglo abuses against Mexicans in south Texas. He continued to operate as a political-military figure on both sides of the border for decades. Both bandit and patriot, depending on perspective.

**Joaquín Murrieta** was a California gold-rush-era outlaw, killed (or perhaps not — the head shown was disputed) by California Rangers in 1853. The legend grew enormous on both sides.

**Tiburcio Vásquez** was a California outlaw, captured and hanged in 1875. He was the last of the major Hispanic outlaws of California.

The historian Eric Hobsbawm's concept of the _social bandit_ applies to many border figures: the outlaw who is supported by his community, who robs the powerful and shares with the poor, who embodies a resistance to dominant institutions. Not all bandits were social bandits, but many border figures fit the pattern. The Mexican bandit who robbed only Anglo ranches and never Mexican ones, and who could ride through Mexican villages unmolested, was operating in a different moral economy than the random highwayman.

## 8. The Trans-Pecos — the Specific Country

The country of west Texas west of the Pecos River through Big Bend to El Paso. Specific landscape, specific border culture.

Mountain ranges — the Davis, the Guadalupe, the Chisos — rise from desert basins. The Rio Grande runs through the Big Bend in deep canyons (Santa Elena, Mariscal, Boquillas). Settlement is sparse. Access is difficult. The country was Apache and Comanche raiding ground through the 1870s; the Army established posts (Fort Davis, Fort Stockton, Fort Quitman) along the southern overland route.

The Big Bend is a particular country: the great loop where the Rio Grande swings south and then north around the Chisos Mountains. Hot in summer, cool in winter. Almost no settlement in the 1870s. A few Mexican villages along the river — Boquillas del Carmen, San Vicente, Santa Elena. Cattle ranching beginning by the 1880s. The country became Big Bend National Park in 1944.

The Big Bend was, and is, smugglers' country. The unguarded border, the difficult terrain, and the few crossings made it perfect for moving goods that needed to move quietly. In the 1870s the principal contraband was livestock and ammunition; later it became liquor, during U.S. Prohibition, and other goods.

## 9. The El Paso–Juárez Crossing

El Paso and its sister city across the river, Ciudad Juárez (then called El Paso del Norte, renamed for Juárez in 1888), formed the major western border crossing. The combined urban area was a single economic unit.

The Pass of the North (the Paso del Norte) is a natural break in the southern Rockies, with the Rio Grande flowing through. The Spanish crossing was established in 1659. The U.S. side was a small village until the railroad's arrival in 1881 (Southern Pacific from the west, AT&SF from the north, just past our period); by 1880 El Paso was booming.

In the 1870s, El Paso del Norte — the Mexican side — was the larger settlement. The U.S. side was smaller, rougher, and held a reputation for violence. A man crossed the border by walking across the river's shallow ford or by ferry. Customs were nominal. The two cities operated as one for daily purposes.

A crossing scene at El Paso–Juárez can render the mixed character of border life: the customs man's desk, the Mexican family with their possessions on a wagon, the Anglo trader checking his bill of lading, the peddler with his pack, the soldier on detached duty going to or coming from Mexico for purposes officially obscure.

---

## 10. The Intermarried Families

The border's social fabric was knit by intermarriage. Anglo men married Tejana, Californio, and Sonoran women throughout the period. The Anglo–Hispanic families produced significant western figures: the Pico, Vallejo, Bandini, and other Californio families that allied with Anglo immigrants; the Otero family of New Mexico; many ranching families of south Texas.

A Hispanic–Anglo family's experience is one of the border's central stories. The children of such a marriage navigated two cultures, often being neither fully accepted in the Anglo-Protestant sphere of the nineteenth-century U.S. nor fully part of the Mexican-Catholic society. Their story is the border's story.

## 11. The Hacienda — the Mexican Rural Estate

A hacienda was a large estate in northern Mexico, typically including cattle ranching, agriculture, and a fortified compound — the casco — housing the owner (the patrón or hacendado), his family, the resident workers (peones acasillados), the chapel, the store (tienda de raya), and the workshops.

The hacienda system was the basis of rural Mexican economy through the nineteenth century. Workers were tied to the hacienda by debt — the tienda de raya extended credit at inflated prices, ensuring the worker could never pay off his debt and could not legally leave — and by tradition. The relationship between patrón and peón was paternalistic, exploitative, and stable.

A scene at a Mexican hacienda has specific furniture: the casco's adobe walls, the central courtyard with its well, the chapel where the family worshipped, the open kitchen where the women cooked, the stables for the patrón's horses, the corrals for the cattle, the small jacales — huts — where the peones lived beyond the casco walls. The hacendado's voice is formal Spanish, the voice of educated landowners. The peón's voice is the voice of working rural Mexicans, often with regional and Native-language influences. A scene at the hacienda can render the social distance through the linguistic distance.

## Vignette — The Trail Down to the River

> The trail came down out of the Chisos in the late afternoon and the river was visible from the divide as a thin green line in the brown country. Carlson sat his horse and looked at it.
>
> “Three more hours,” Romero said. “The crossing is at Boquillas. The water will be low this time of year.”
>
> “And then?”
>
> “Then we are in Coahuila, and the man we want is at his ranch ten miles south. If he is there.”
>
> Carlson nodded and started his horse down the trail. The country between him and the river was ocotillo and creosote and the sotol stalks standing dry from last year's bloom. A roadrunner crossed the trail ahead of them and was gone into the brush.

## Vignette — The Casco at Vespers

> They came up the long dusty road to the casco of Hacienda San Felipe in the late afternoon and the bell at the chapel was ringing for vespers. The walls of the casco were ten feet high and the gate stood open. Inside the courtyard the well was working — a woman in a black rebozo was drawing water and pouring it into the trough for the patron's horses.
>
> Don Eulalio Vargas came out of the front door of the main house in a clean white shirt and a dark jacket. He was an old man with white hair and a face the color of saddle leather and eyes that did not give anything away.
>
> “Caballeros,” he said, “bienvenidos. He estado esperando.”
>
> Romero answered him in the formal Spanish that Vargas would expect. Carlson stood behind, his hat in his hand.
