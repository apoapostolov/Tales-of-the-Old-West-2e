# Chapter 12 — What Existed and What Did Not

> _“SHE BROUGHT THE LAMP DOWN OFF THE HIGH SHELF AND TRIMMED THE WICK AND LIT IT WITH A MATCH STRUCK ON THE IRON OF THE STOVE. THE FLAME CAUGHT AND STEADIED AND THREW HER SHADOW LONG AGAINST THE WALL.”_

The wrong-decade object is the most reliable way to lose the West. The cowman drinking a Coca-Cola in 1879. The homesteader loading her .30-30. The sheriff thumbing on a flashlight. The saloon with electric bulbs above the bar. Each error breaks the spell, and the spell is fragile. The decade in question runs from January 1, 1870 through December 31, 1879 — early (1870–73), mid (1874–76), late (1877–79). Where a technology arrived mid-decade it is dated. Where it was widespread by the end of the decade it is so noted. The bias should always be toward _not yet._ The West of the imagination arrives later than the West of history because the technology took a year to a decade to reach a Texas ranch from a New York factory. A telephone existed in Boston in 1876; it did not reach the west Texas range for another twenty years.

---

## 1. The Technology Timeline

### By 1870 — what is already in place

Repeating rifles were available — the Henry, the Spencer, the Winchester '66. Cartridge revolvers existed but were rare; conversions of percussion arms were just appearing. The telegraph reached every major city and ran along every railroad; the transcontinental telegraph had been completed in 1861 and a ten-word message cost fifty cents to a dollar plus per-word charges, not the casual instrument the letter or the newspaper was. The transcontinental railroad had been completed at Promontory Summit on May 10, 1869; the Union Pacific ran Omaha to Promontory and the Central Pacific ran Sacramento to Promontory, with branch lines pushing out through the decade.

Steamboats worked the Missouri, the Mississippi, the Ohio, the Sacramento, and parts of the Columbia and the Snake — a major freight and passenger artery. Stagecoaches were everywhere; the great transcontinental stage lines (Butterfield, Wells Fargo) were dwindling as the railroads extended, but local stages still ran. Photography meant the wet-plate collodion process (1851–1880s); daguerreotypes (1840s–1850s) and tintypes (1856 onward) predated the period, with tintype portraits common in the West throughout, while glass-plate negatives were standard for studio work and flexible film would not arrive until 1885. The Singer sewing machine (1851) was widespread by 1870.

Canned food was standard on the mercantile shelf — tin cans dated to the 1810s, Borden's canned milk to 1856, Underwood deviled ham to 1868, with canned peaches, tomatoes, and oysters available in any town store and expensive on the frontier. Refined sugar, yes. Coffee, yes — green beans shipped in for parching at home or in camp until pre-roasted Arbuckles' Ariosa coffee (introduced 1864 and shipped widely from 1865) became the cowboy's coffee through the period. Whiskey — bonded brands (Old Crow, Old Forester arriving in 1870), unbonded rotgut everywhere.

### Arriving 1870–1873

The Colt Single Action Army — the Peacemaker — was introduced in 1873 and became the iconic western pistol; it was not in 1872 and not widespread until 1875. The Winchester Model 1873 in .44-40 came the same year and was common from 1874. The .44-40 cartridge, introduced with the Winchester '73, was adopted by the Colt SAA in 1878 (the _Frontier Six-Shooter_), so a man could carry one cartridge for both rifle and pistol. Levi Strauss and Jacob Davis patented the copper-riveted overalls on May 20, 1873; Levi's blue jeans are 1873 onward and not in 1872, with the 501 designation coming later. Joseph Glidden patented the type of barbed wire that succeeded in 1874, sold widely from 1875 — it transformed the open range through the late 1870s and 1880s; not in 1873. The Sharps Model 1874 — the buffalo rifle of the great hide hunts — arrived that year. The Sholes-Glidden typewriter, manufactured by Remington from 1874, was an office machine slowly adopted, and rare in the West before the 1880s. Cheap steel windmills for stock water came in from the late 1870s and were widespread on the Plains in the 1880s onward.

### Arriving 1874–1876

Bell patented the telephone on March 7, 1876. The first commercial telephone exchange opened in New Haven on January 28, 1878. There was no telephone in 1875. There was no telephone in 1876 except in Boston laboratories. It reached the major Eastern and Western cities in the 1880s. A telephone in a Texas ranch house in 1878 is wrong by twenty years. The Bissell carpet sweeper came in 1876. Yablochkov-candle electric arc lighting was demonstrated in 1876 and used in street lighting in major cities by the late 1870s — but not in homes or saloons. Edison's incandescent lamp was demonstrated in October 1879 in the laboratory; commercial installations began in 1882. There is no electric incandescent light in any 1870s setting.

### Arriving 1877–1879

Edison patented the phonograph in December 1877 and demonstrated it publicly in 1878, the tinfoil cylinder. It was a novelty curiosity in cities and not a practical music device. The cowboy in 1879 does not hear recorded music. The carbon microphone (1878) improved the telephone but did not put one in his bunkhouse. The Ritty cash register was patented in 1879 and diffused through the 1880s.

### Not yet anywhere in the 1870s

Smokeless powder came with French Poudre B in 1884 — _all_ 1870s firearms use black powder, and the smoke is part of every gunfight. Bolt-action smokeless rifles belong to the 1880s and later; the hunter in 1875 does not have a Mauser. The Winchester Model 1894 in .30-30 smokeless was introduced in 1894 and is not in any 1870s scene. The Borchardt semi-automatic pistol came in 1893 and is not in any 1870s scene. The standard 1870s western revolver is single-action; rudimentary double-action revolvers existed (Adams, Tranter, the Colt Lightning of 1877, mechanically poor and rare), but the working pistol of the period is single-action.

The automobile was a laboratory project in the 1880s and a commercial article in the 1890s — no car. The penny-farthing high-wheel bicycle was in cities from 1870 but rare in the West, and the safety bicycle (the modern form) is from 1885. Powered flight is 1903. Radio is Marconi 1895. Motion pictures are Edison's Kinetograph 1891. Cassette tape, vinyl record, transistor radio, television, computer, mobile phone, internet — obviously not.

Mechanical refrigeration was in meatpacking plants by the 1870s (Swift refrigerator car, 1878), but the domestic refrigerator is a 1920s machine. The 1870s home and saloon use ice, cut from northern lakes, shipped south by rail in insulated cars, and sold by the ice company in town. Aspirin is Bayer 1899 and not in any 1870s medicine chest. Penicillin is 1928, the antibiotic era post-1940. The 1870s doctor has whiskey, opium, morphine (yes, by hypodermic syringe from the late 1850s), quinine, mercury (for syphilis, ineffective), bismuth (for diarrhea), camphor, ipecac, paregoric, calomel, lime water, laudanum. Cocaine in patent medicine appears late 1880s — _not_ 1870s — and the Coca-Cola of 1886 is the cocaine reference. Heroin is Bayer 1898, not 1870s. Smallpox vaccination, yes (Jenner, 1796, routine in the 1870s); rabies vaccine 1885 (Pasteur); diphtheria antitoxin 1890s. The 1870s doctor cannot cure diphtheria, scarlet fever, cholera, typhoid, or tuberculosis. Anesthetics: ether (1846) and chloroform (1847) available to surgeons; nitrous oxide (1844). A frontier doctor may have ether or chloroform; a frontier dentist may, though many do not.

## 2. Goods — What Is in the Mercantile

A general store in 1875 carries the following inventory. If your scene calls for an item not on this list, verify before writing.

### Foodstuffs

Flour, cornmeal, oats, and barley by the sack. Sugar in loaf and granulated form, brown sugar in some stores. Salt in block and granulated. Coffee — Arbuckles' Ariosa from the late 1860s, with green beans available for home parching. Tea, mostly black, with some green tea in the coastal towns. Beans (pinto, red, navy). Rice. Lard in tin pails. Salt pork in the barrel. Bacon sliced from the side and sold by the pound. Ham whole or sliced. Hardtack in the barrel — both military surplus and commercial. Soda crackers in the barrel. Cheese by the wheel, cut by the pound. Butter and eggs when local. Canned milk — Borden's from 1856, Eagle Brand from 1853. Canned tomatoes, peaches, oysters, sardines, deviled ham. Pickles in the barrel. Vinegar in the barrel. Molasses in the barrel — New Orleans cane molasses, Vermont sorghum. Honey when local. Dried apples, peaches, raisins. Spices: pepper, cinnamon, nutmeg, cloves, allspice, ginger. Compressed yeast cakes from 1868; sourdough starter on the trail. Royal Baking Powder from 1866 was widespread.

Not present, or not as the modern reader knows them: bananas (yes by the 1880s in major cities, rare on the Western frontier in 1875). Citrus (oranges shipped from California or Florida, expensive in the interior). Frozen anything. Modern breakfast cereals — Granola was 1863 but corn flakes are 1894; no flakes in the 1870s.

### Drinks

Whiskey ran from bonded brands at three to five dollars the bottle to rotgut at one or two. Beer was lager, the work of German immigrant brewers, both bottled and on draft; refrigerated rail cars permitted shipment by the late 1870s. Domestic California wine had been on the market since the late 1860s; imported wines stocked the parlor saloons and grand hotels. Champagne, imported from France, ran five dollars and up in the parlor house. Soda water (carbonated water from a soda fountain) was available in cities. Coca-Cola was introduced in 1886 — _not_ in 1879. Pepsi, 1898 — _not_ in 1879. Hires Root Beer was a 1876 commercial product, available by mid-decade in cities but not necessarily on the frontier in 1875. Sarsaparilla was widely sold as a patent medicine syrup. Lemonade was made when lemons were to be had.

### Tobacco

Plug — chewing tobacco — was the dominant form. Bull Durham smoking tobacco from 1858 was widespread. Loose tobacco for the roll-your-own was standard. Cigars ran from a nickel each to several dollars, the product of the Cuban-American cigar industry. Cigarettes existed but were uncommon — the cowman _rolled his own_ with paper and tobacco, and pre-rolled cigarettes were an Eastern dandy's affectation in 1875 (the Bonsack rolling machine for mass-market cigarettes is 1881–84). Pipes: the corncob (Missouri Meerschaum from 1869), the briar, the clay.

### Clothing

Boots — custom-made by the local bootmaker for fifteen to thirty dollars, or ready-made for five to ten. The cowboy boot proper (high heel, narrow toe, tall shaft) developed through the period; in 1870 the _Wellington_ and the _military_ boot were dominant, and by 1880 a recognizable cowboy boot existed. Hats — the John B. Stetson _Boss of the Plains_ (1865) was the standard at five to ten dollars; other felt and straw hats were available, brim widths and crown styles varying by region. Levi's jeans from 1873 onward, copper-riveted overalls at $1.50. Wool trousers were standard for most occasions. Linen and cotton shirts ready-made in the mercantile. Bandanas in cotton, large, mostly red or blue paisley. Vest standard with the working suit and the cowman's rig. Coats — working (canvas or wool), Sunday (broadcloth or wool serge), buffalo for winter. Long underwear: the union suit (one-piece) from 1868, widespread in cold country.

Women's dress: ready-made or sewn at home from store bolts of cloth. The bustle was fashionable through much of the decade. Working dress was calico or gingham; Sunday dress was finer cloth. The corset was standard women's underwear, with stays of whalebone or steel. The hoop skirt was declining through the period, the bustle succeeding it. Leather work gloves and kid dress gloves. Knitted hosiery in cotton or wool, held by garters.

### Hardware and tools

Hammer, chisel, plane, saw, brace and bit, axe, hatchet, sledge, pick, shovel, spade — all iron-bodied. Nails were _cut_ nails until wire nails took over in the late 1880s; square-shanked, flat-tipped. Slotted-head screws (the Phillips head is 1933 — not in the 1870s). Files, rasps, augers, draw knives, planes. Plows in cast iron and steel — John Deere's steel plow from 1837 was the standard by 1870, the sulky riding plow from 1875 in some regions. Reapers and mowers, yes — the McCormick reaper from 1834, the mower from the 1850s, widespread on the wheat frontier. Threshing machines, horse- or steam-powered, the steam threshing rig a major investment but available from the 1860s. Steam tractors, rare and large, from the 1850s, widespread on the wheat frontier from the 1880s. Internal combustion was a late-1870s laboratory project — _not_ on any 1870s farm or shop.

### Household goods

The kerosene lamp was standard, kerosene refined from petroleum being the lighting fuel of the period (whale oil was declining). Tallow candles home-made, stearin manufactured. Friction matches from the 1830s, safety matches from 1855 — the lucifers, the sulphur matches. Soap: lye soap home-made, bar soap in the store (Procter & Gamble's Ivory from 1879 — not in 1878). Pears' soap, Castile soap. Fels-Naptha is 1894, not the 1870s. Washboards standard. Wringer washtubs from 1858, widespread. The sad iron heated on the stove. The cookstove, wood- or coal-burning cast iron, was standard in any settled home, ranging from the sheet-iron camp stove to the elaborate Acme cast-iron range with reservoir. The icebox — an insulated wooden cabinet with a compartment for a block of ice — served where the modern refrigerator would later. The Singer sewing machine standard. Mason jars (1858) for canning. Bottled patent medicine, abundantly.

### Stationery and writing

The steel-nib pen and the inkwell were standard, quill pens declining. The fountain pen is Lewis Waterman 1884 — not the 1870s (earlier reservoir pens existed and were unreliable). Faber pencils since 1761, mechanical pencils since the 1830s, the rubber eraser since 1770, combined pencil-and-eraser since 1858. Foolscap, letter paper, gummed envelopes since the 1840s. The Sholes-Glidden / Remington Number 1 typewriter from 1874, with office adoption slow and the machine rare in the West before the 1880s. Carbon paper since the 1820s, a standard office tool by the 1870s.

### Patent medicine

Hostetter's Bitters (heavily alcoholic), Lydia Pinkham's Vegetable Compound (women's tonic), Dr. Williams' Pink Pills, paregoric (camphorated tincture of opium), laudanum (opium in alcohol), morphine and the hypodermic syringe (a Civil War legacy; widespread addiction followed). Various cough syrups containing opiates. Calomel (mercury). Blue mass. Cocaine in patent medicine is from the late 1880s — _not_ 1870s. Heroin (1898), aspirin (1899) — neither.

## 3. Services — What a Town Has

A county-seat town of fifteen hundred in 1875 would be expected to have the following.

A general store or mercantile. A saloon, often several — a cattle town might have twenty. A hotel (one to several) and one or more boarding houses. A stable, livery, and feed yard. A blacksmith and farrier, often combined with the wheelwright. A doctor sometimes; smaller towns shared a circuit doctor. A dentist rarely, and often the town doctor or the blacksmith stood in. A lawyer in any county seat. A small bank, often. A weekly newspaper. A telegraph office in any railroad town. An express office (Wells Fargo, American Express). A stage office, declining but present. A post office, in the mercantile or in its own building. A land office in any new settlement zone. A railroad depot if the town was on the line. A saddler and harness maker. A gunsmith in larger towns. A tinsmith. A cobbler and bootmaker. A tailor in larger towns. A barber. A bath house, often combined with the barber. A Chinese laundry in any town with a Chinese community. A restaurant or chop house. One or several churches. A one-room school. A courthouse in the county seat. A jail. An undertaker, often the town carpenter. A brothel, dance hall, or parlor house, often. A photographer in larger towns and an itinerant photographer through smaller ones.

What the town did _not_ have. No hospital — hospitals existed in cities; the frontier town treated the sick at home or in the doctor's parlor, though the Sisters of Charity ran hospitals in some larger Western towns. No public library, occasionally yes; the Carnegie libraries are 1890s and after. No fire department — a volunteer brigade in larger towns, a bucket brigade in smaller. No sewer system — privies and outhouses, cesspools; municipal water and sewer infrastructure is late nineteenth-century in larger towns and twentieth in smaller. No indoor plumbing, rare; fancy hotels had it, private homes by the 1880s in cities and the 1890s in small towns. No electric lighting — commercial electric lighting begins in 1882 and after; no electric lights in 1870s buildings. No telephone exchange — 1878 New Haven; reaches the West in the 1880s and 1890s. No movie theater, no radio station, no record store.

## 4. Transportation — What Carries a Man

The saddle horse is standard. A man rides forty to fifty miles in a long day, twenty-five to thirty in a comfortable one. The pack horse or pack mule carries the gear — the mule two hundred pounds, the horse less. The wagon serves family transport and freight at two miles an hour with oxen, three to four with horses. The buggy, buckboard, and spring wagon serve light passenger transport.

By rail, by 1879, a man can travel from New York to San Francisco in seven days on the transcontinental, with changes in Chicago and Omaha — first class over a hundred dollars, second class about forty, the emigrant car twenty. Freight, yes — cattle by stockcar, grain by hopper. Pullman sleepers from 1864 at two or three dollars the berth a night. The decade's railroads in the West: the Union Pacific (Omaha to Promontory); the Central Pacific (Sacramento to Promontory); the Kansas Pacific (reaching Denver in 1870); the Atchison Topeka and Santa Fe (Atchison to La Junta by 1875, pushing south through New Mexico through 1879); the Texas and Pacific (chartered 1871, eastern Texas through the decade); the Northern Pacific (chartered 1864, bankrupted in the Panic of 1873, resuming construction late 1870s, Twin Cities to Bismarck by 1873, Pacific completion 1883); the Southern Pacific (Los Angeles to Yuma by 1877); the Denver and Rio Grande (Denver south to Pueblo and beyond through the late 1870s).

A character in 1875 cannot take the train from Dodge City to Tombstone — the Atchison reaches Dodge in 1872, but Tombstone is not on rail until later. A character in 1879 cannot fly. A character in 1873 cannot use a Texas and Pacific line that has not yet reached his town. The map of where the rails actually go in a given year is the map a player or GM must consult before writing a scene that turns on getting somewhere quickly.

The stagecoach operated on routes the railroad had not reached. It carried six to nine passengers and traveled four to six miles an hour with frequent changes of teams; long-distance fares ran ten to fifteen cents a mile. The great cross-country lines (Butterfield, Overland) had been displaced by the railroad. Local lines remained important — Tucson to Phoenix, Cheyenne to Deadwood, El Paso to San Antonio.

By foot, the standard for the poor — three miles an hour, twenty-five in a long day. By steamboat, on the Missouri (Bismarck to St. Louis), the Mississippi, the Ohio, the Sacramento, parts of the Columbia and the Snake, parts of the Colorado irregularly. A character in Wyoming Territory does not take a steamboat.

## 5. Communication — What Reaches Whom and How Fast

The U.S. Postal Service runs to any town. The letter rate is three cents the half-ounce within the United States — the three-cent rate runs through the 1880s. The postcard, introduced in 1873, costs a cent. Delivery in town is usually pickup at the post office (which is usually in the mercantile); free home delivery dates to 1863 in cities and to 1887 in towns of ten thousand and up — the Western villager picks up his mail in person. An Eastern letter to a Western town reaches its destination in a week to ten days by 1879, depending on rail connection. A letter between two Western towns in the same region takes two to three days. A letter from England takes two to four weeks, by Cunard mail steamer to New York and then by rail.

The telegraph has been national since 1844 and transcontinental since 1861, with Western Union the dominant network. The standard rate is fifty cents to a dollar for the first ten words plus per-word charges. The wire reaches any town on the railroad and many off it. The transatlantic cable, laid in 1858 and reliably operating from 1866, carries news from Europe to New York within a day; from New York to San Francisco the message is a matter of minutes. The local telegraph in a small town is at the railroad depot or the express office.

The local weekly newspaper exists in any town, with national news arriving by telegraph and being republished. New York and St. Louis dailies arrive by rail in the West — a man in Cheyenne reads yesterday's _New York Times_ on the day after. The Associated Press, founded 1846, supplies the wire-service national news to the local papers.

The telephone is _no._ Patent 1876. New Haven exchange 1878. Reaches the Western city in the 1880s and the small Western town by 1900. A telephone in any 1870s scene is wrong.

## 6. Food and Drink — What Is on the Table

The cattle-country breakfast for a working cowman runs to coffee (Arbuckles', boiled, strong); a sourdough biscuit or hardtack; bacon or salt pork; beans warmed over from the night before; sometimes eggs in settled country where chickens are kept; sometimes beef.

The town hotel breakfast in a 1875 county seat: coffee or tea; eggs, ham or bacon, hash browns or fried potatoes; biscuits or hot bread; steak (yes — beefsteak for breakfast was common); pancakes with syrup or molasses; stewed fruit; oysters (canned or iced, depending on the town).

Sunday dinner in a 1875 farmhouse: roast meat (beef, ham, pork roast, fried chicken); mashed potatoes with gravy; boiled greens in spring, squash in fall, root vegetables in winter; hot bread or biscuits; stewed dried fruit, pie, cake; coffee or buttermilk.

The trail-drive table from the cook on a chuck wagon: beans (sourdough beans cooked all day in the iron pot); beef or bacon; sourdough biscuit; hot Arbuckles' coffee; stewed dried fruit (peaches, apricots, raisins) on a good day; molasses sometimes; onion or wild onion when foraged.

The miner's camp table is heavier and better-paid: bacon, beans, biscuits, beef when available, coffee, more canned goods, more tobacco. The hardrock miner of Leadville eats better than the Texas cowhand on the trail.

The Mexican and Tejano table: tortillas (corn or wheat); beans, often pinto _frijoles_; chile, red and green, rehydrated dried chiles for sauces (the Hatch and the modern New Mexico chiles develop later); _carne asada,_ _carnitas,_ _machaca,_ _menudo;_ tamales, especially at Christmas; coffee Mexican-style, often with cinnamon; _atole_ (corn-based drink); chocolate Mexican-style, bittersweet, with cinnamon.

The Chinese cook's table in mining camps and railroad camps: rice imported from China through San Francisco; salt fish, dried fish, dried mushroom; pork when available, chicken; bok choy and other Chinese vegetables grown in market gardens around the camps; soy sauce, ginger, garlic; tea more than coffee.

## 7. Money and Currency

### The coin

Gold: the twenty-dollar double eagle (one ounce), the ten-dollar eagle, the five-dollar half eagle, the $2.50 quarter eagle, the gold dollar (minted 1849–1889). Silver: the dollar (the Liberty Seated dollar to 1873; the Trade dollar 1873–1885; the Morgan dollar 1878–1904); the half dollar; the quarter; the dime; the half dime to 1873. Nickel: the five-cent piece (the Shield nickel 1866–1883, the Liberty nickel from 1883). Copper: the cent (the Indian Head cent 1859–1909); the two-cent piece (1864–1873); the three-cent piece (1851–1873).

### The greenback

Federal paper currency. The U.S. Notes (greenbacks) issued during the Civil War remain in circulation. National Bank Notes are issued by national banks against deposits of U.S. bonds. Specie payments resumed in 1879 — the greenback became redeemable for gold and silver. Through the 1860s and 1870s the greenback fluctuated against gold; a man could receive his pay in greenbacks worth ninety cents on the gold dollar.

### The Mexican coin

The Mexican silver peso circulated freely in the Southwest alongside the U.S. dollar — roughly equivalent to one U.S. silver dollar but valued by weight in many trades. The _real_ (one-eighth of a peso, the _bit_ of _two bits, four bits, six bits_) survived as an accounting unit. _Two bits_ is twenty-five cents. _Four bits_ is fifty. _Six bits_ is seventy-five.

### Gold dust

In the gold-rush regions (California, Colorado, the Black Hills, later the Yukon) gold dust was weighed out at the bar and the mercantile counter. The merchant kept a small balance scale at the till.

### Credit

Long credit was the rule on the frontier. Cowmen settled with the merchant at shipping time once a year. Miners ran tabs at the saloon. Doctors waited months for payment. Bankruptcy on the frontier was common because the credit chain was long.

### Bank checks

Yes, in use through the period. A merchant or a cowman would pay by draft on a Kansas City bank or a San Francisco bank. Banks discounted checks from distant institutions.

### Paper bills

The 1869 _Rainbow_ Series one-dollar note carried George Washington and a vignette of _Christopher Columbus in sight of land._ Through the decade, U.S. Notes and National Bank Notes circulated. Federal Reserve Notes — the modern dollar bill — date to 1914 and are not in the 1870s.

## 9. Common Wrong-Decade Errors

A short field guide to the errors most often made about 1870s material life.

The flashlight — battery-powered electric torch invented 1899; in the 1870s the man takes the lantern. The matchbook — book matches 1892; in the 1870s the matches are in a tin or a small wooden box, or the lucifers are loose in the pocket. The flashbulb — magnesium flash powder from 1887; no photography flash before that, with studio portraits using natural light or skylights. The wristwatch — pocket watch only; the wristwatch is post-1900 (military adoption WWI). A man in 1875 takes out his pocket watch, snaps the case open, and consults it.

The zipper — patented 1893, widespread post-1920; no zipper in 1870s clothing. Buttons, hooks-and-eyes, lacing, drawstrings. The paper bag — Margaret Knight's square-bottom machine 1879, just arriving; earlier the cones and cylinders were rolled by the merchant. The handled paper sack is later. The mercantile wraps purchases in brown paper and twine. The Coca-Cola sign — 1886 onward. The Marlboro Man — 1955.

The cowboy hat with the _ten-gallon_ shape — the high-crown white cowboy hat is a twentieth-century rodeo and movie shape; the 1870s working hat is a _Boss of the Plains_ Stetson, flat-crowned, modest brim, practical. The cowboy quick-drawing from a low-slung holster — the low-slung tied-down holster is a twentieth-century movie invention; period holsters ride high on the belt and the pistol comes out by lifting up and forward, not by dropping the hand to the thigh. The pearl-handled six-shooter — existed but rare; the standard SAA grip is walnut, with pearl, ivory, and mother-of-pearl grips the gambler's or the showman's affectation.

The cowboy with mirrored sunglasses — sunglasses for general wear arrive in the early twentieth century; eye protection in 1875 is a hat brim and a bandanna (tinted glasses for medical use existed; Sam Colt wore tinted spectacles). The cowboy with a chrome belt buckle and a tooled belt — heavy show buckles are twentieth-century rodeo; the 1870s working cowboy's belt is plain leather, a simple brass or steel buckle, two inches wide. The cowboy listening to country music on the radio — radio is twentieth century; _singing_ is the music of the trail, by men around a fire and by riders on night-herd. The whiskey served on the rocks — ice in a glass of whiskey is rare in the 1870s saloon; whiskey is served neat or with water (and sometimes sugar — a _toddy_), and ice in the saloon is for the beer keg in the back.

## Vignette — The Wrong Decade

> The new hand at the bunkhouse pulled a metal cylinder from his pocket and thumbed a switch and a white beam fell across the floor. The boys looked at it. Charlie set down his coffee. “Where in the name of God,” he said, “did you get that.” The new hand looked at the thing in his hand and looked at Charlie and his face went the color of his shirt. The wind went out of the room. He put the thing back in his pocket and did not take it out again that winter and the boys did not speak of it but they did not forget it either, and when he drew his pay in the spring nobody was sorry to see him ride out.
