# Chapter 19 — Army Life

> _“THE HORSES CAME FIRST. THE HORSES CAME FIRST EVERY MORNING OF THE WORLD.”_

The frontier army of the 1870s was small — about twenty-five thousand men — scattered across hundreds of posts, undermanned, underpaid, and engaged in small-unit operations against highly mobile adversaries. The romantic cavalry charge of the movies was not the army's daily work. The daily work was garrison drudgery, escort duty, scouting, and the long, slow, grinding business of campaign that might never produce a battle. This chapter is the working knowledge of the post and the field column: layout, routine, pay, discharge, the ration, the cavalry on campaign, the Buffalo Soldier regiments, the officer's wife, discipline, the army's voice, and the campaign's recurring failure.

---

## 1. The Frontier Post

### Layout

The standard frontier post was an open square — the parade ground — surrounded by buildings.

**Officers' row** ran along one side of the square: wood-frame or adobe houses for the officers and their families, the post commander's house usually the largest. Officers' row was the post's social center. **Enlisted barracks** stood opposite officers' row — long single-story buildings (wood, adobe, or stone), each housing a company (about fifty men in the 1870s), a single open bay with bunks along both walls, small attached kitchen and mess, the first sergeant's room at one end. **Headquarters** or the adjutant's office was a small building, often near the flagpole at the parade ground's center. The **quartermaster** and **commissary storehouses** were heavy buildings containing the post's supplies, guarded. **Stables** stood at cavalry posts — long open barns, with the saddler's shop and farrier's shop nearby. The **hospital** was small (one or two wards), staffed by a contract surgeon. The **guardhouse** was the post's jail. The **sutler's store** or post trader's was a civilian business licensed to operate at the post, selling goods (tobacco, canned food, whisky, clothing) to officers and enlisted at posted prices — the post's only commercial space. **Laundresses' quarters** (Soapsuds Row) were often a separate row of shacks where the post's laundresses, frequently soldiers' wives, lived and worked.

Most posts had no walls. The stockade fort of the movies was largely a fiction by the 1870s; the open plan was standard. A few exceptions — Fort Apache, some older posts — had walls.

### Flagpole and hierarchy

The flagpole was the center of the parade ground, the garrison's formal heart. Reveille and retreat were called by the bugler at the flagpole. The flag went up at reveille and down at retreat, with the garrison at attention.

The post commander was a senior officer (lieutenant colonel or colonel), responsible for everything. The adjutant was a junior officer handling administration — the post's day-to-day manager. The quartermaster was responsible for supply. The surgeon was a contract or regular medical officer. Officers commanded companies — captains, with lieutenants under them; the company was the basic unit, and a post might have two to six companies. The first sergeants were the senior enlisted of each company — the actual day-to-day authority over the enlisted men. The first sergeant ran the company. Other NCOs (sergeants, corporals) and the privates filled out the bulk.

The relationship between the company commander and the first sergeant is one of the great relationships in army fiction. The captain is the formal authority; the first sergeant is the practical authority. A good pair worked together; a bad pair tore the company apart.

## 2. The Daily Routine

The army day was structured by bugle calls. The bugler was a critical figure; he announced every transition of the day to the entire post.

**Reveille,** about sunrise: bugle call, formation in front of barracks, roll call. **Stable call** (cavalry): men go to the stables to feed, water, groom, and clean stalls — cavalry was forty percent horse-care. **Mess call** (breakfast): coffee, hardtack or bread, salt pork or beef, occasional eggs. **Sick call:** men reporting illness present at the hospital. **Fatigue call:** work details — wood-cutting, hay-cutting, repairing buildings, carrying supplies, digging latrines. The soldier's daily work was largely manual labor, not military exercise. **Drill** when held: formation drill, weapons drill, mounted drill (cavalry); frequency varied — some posts drilled daily, some rarely. **Mess call** (dinner, midday): the main meal — beef stew, beans, bread, coffee; vegetables were rare. **Fatigue continues.** **Stable call:** evening care of horses. **Mess call** (supper): light meal, often leftovers. **Retreat** (sunset): formation, lowering of the flag. **Tattoo**, about 9 p.m.: final formation, roll call. **Taps:** lights out.

The day was punctuated by guard mount about 9 a.m., when the new guard detail relieved the old. Guard duty was twenty-four hours and was rotated through the company.

The frontier soldier's primary occupations, in order of time spent: manual labor (wood-cutting, hay-cutting, road work, building repair, latrine digging); horse care (cavalry; two formal stable calls plus continuous attention); guard duty (a regular rotation); drill (variable); escort duty (escorting paymasters, supply trains, surveyors, civilians); scouting (small patrols); combat (the rarest activity — years could pass between actions for many soldiers). The romantic image of the cavalry trooper as a man who rode and fought is misleading. He was a manual laborer who occasionally rode and occasionally fought.

## 3. Pay, Enlistment, Desertion

A private earned thirteen dollars a month in the 1870s (reduced from sixteen after 1871). Sergeants earned about twenty. Officers earned considerably more — a lieutenant about $120, a captain about $150. Pay came from the paymaster (a special officer) on his rounds, every two months at most posts. The paymaster carried the pay in cash, escorted by a detachment.

The thirteen dollars went largely to the sutler (where the soldier had run a tab), to the laundresses (about a dollar a month), to gambling, and to drink. The soldier rarely had money long after payday.

Enlistment was five years (cavalry) or three years (some periods, infantry). Re-enlistment bonuses were modest. Many soldiers re-enlisted because they had nowhere else to go; the army was a refuge for men with no other prospects. Honorable discharge produced a paper that documented service. Dishonorable discharge produced disgrace and a record. Desertion was the army's epidemic problem; about one in three enlistees deserted at some point. Penalties when caught ran from forfeiture of pay to imprisonment to (rarely) execution.

The deserter is a common western figure — the man who has slipped away from the army, often headed for the gold camps or for Mexico. The deserter was hunted by the army's provost marshals when worth the trouble. Many deserters made new lives under new names.

## 4. The Ration

Army rations were standardized. Per man per day: twelve ounces of pork or bacon, _or_ one pound four ounces of fresh or salt beef; eighteen ounces of flour or bread, _or_ one pound four ounces of cornmeal; one and a half ounces of beans or peas, _or_ one ounce of rice or hominy; one and three-quarter ounces of coffee (green unroasted), _or_ one and a half ounces of tea; two and four-tenths ounces of sugar; salt, vinegar, candles, soap in small quantities. A small quantity of dried fruit, molasses, or potatoes might be substituted. Fresh vegetables were rarely available; scurvy was a real threat at remote posts.

Coffee was the army's true fuel. The soldier roasted his own green coffee on a hot pan, ground it (often by crushing in a sock with a stone), and brewed it strong. Coffee was made at every halt. The phrase _boil the coffee_ defined the day's structure.

Each company had a mess kitchen, with a soldier-cook detailed to the duty. The cook was rarely trained. Food was monotonous: stew, beans, bread, coffee, fried meat. A good cook was treasured; a bad cook made life worse. In the field, the soldier carried hardtack — a flour-and-water cracker, sometimes weevily — salt pork, and coffee. The hardtack soaked in coffee or fried in pork grease was the day's substantial meal.

## 5. The Cavalry Column

The cavalry column on campaign is the iconic frontier army image. The reality was less romantic and more exhausting.

A typical column for an Indian-country campaign included several troops of cavalry (a troop was about fifty men); a pack train (mules) or a wagon train depending on country (mules carried more across difficult terrain; wagons carried more along good roads); a small detachment of infantry sometimes, to garrison supply camps or support fixed positions; Indian scouts (Crow, Pawnee, Apache, depending on the country and the campaign) — the scouts were essential, the cavalry could not find the enemy without them; a surgeon and his assistants; and a small staff (adjutant, quartermaster, signal officer if available).

A cavalry column on campaign moved fifteen to thirty miles a day depending on terrain, weather, and condition of horses. Pushing harder broke down the horses. The Indian opposition could move faster because the Indians had fresh horses (taken or spared) and the soldiers did not. The pursuit was the cavalry's recurrent failure. The column would find a trail, follow it, and lose it as the Indian band scattered into smaller groups across broken country. After weeks of pursuit, the column returned to base without engagement.

The end of the day's march. The column halted at water — the only consideration that mattered. Horses were unsaddled, watered, picketed, grain rations issued. Men cooked their suppers over small fires. Sentries posted around the perimeter. The column slept in their boots with weapons at hand. A camp on a campaign was not pitched as a fixed camp. Tents were rare in the field; men slept in their blankets under their saddles. The cold camp (no fire, when concealment mattered) was the most miserable form. The hot camp with coffee and bacon was the better night.

The column moved at a walk most of the time. The trot was used for short distances; the gallop was used for the rare charge. A man and a horse could walk all day; trotting and galloping broke the horse in hours. The column rode in column of twos or fours, on a trail or open country. Officers rode at the head and flanks. The pack train followed in the rear with its own escort.

## 6. The Buffalo Soldier Regiments

After the Civil War, four regiments of Black enlisted men were established under white officers: the 9th and 10th Cavalry, the 24th and 25th Infantry. They were stationed primarily on the southern plains and the Southwest.

_Buffalo Soldier_ was a name given by Plains Indians, its exact origin disputed (perhaps the men's hair, or their tenacity, or their winter coats). The men adopted the name as an honor. The Buffalo Soldier regiments served in the Indian Wars from the late 1860s through the 1890s — the southern plains campaigns, the Apache campaigns, the Red River War, the Geronimo campaigns. Their service record was exceptional; their combat record was strong. Eighteen Medals of Honor were awarded to Buffalo Soldiers for action during the Indian Wars.

Buffalo Soldier regiments received the worst horses (remounted last from the army's central depots), the oldest equipment, the least desirable posts. They faced civilian hostility at most posts in the South and Southwest. Their officers were exclusively white through the period. Their re-enlistment rate was very high — the army was, despite the prejudice within it, a better life than what was available to Black men in the post-Reconstruction South.

The Buffalo Soldier is one of the western canon's underwritten figures. The cavalry novels of the mid-twentieth century were almost exclusively about white regiments. A serious western from a Buffalo Soldier perspective remains an opportunity. A few sources: William Leckie, _The Buffalo Soldiers_; Frank Schubert, _Black Valor._

## 7. The Officer's Wife and Soapsuds Row

The frontier post's social world was substantially shaped by the officers' wives who lived on the post. Officers' quarters were small, often poorly built, shared with cooks (typically the wives of enlisted men) and sometimes with junior officers. The house might be a four-room adobe with a kitchen at the back, infested with mice and crawling insects, hot in summer and cold in winter. The wife's life was domestic management under difficult conditions.

Rank determined social precedence. The post commander's wife outranked all others. Calls were made and returned. Dinner parties were held. Children were schooled (often by an officer's wife who took on the duty). Books and newspapers arrived months late.

The frontier officer's wife is one of the period's significant memoirists. Read Elizabeth Custer (_Boots and Saddles, Following the Guidon, Tenting on the Plains_), Frances Roe (_Army Letters from an Officer's Wife_), Martha Summerhayes (_Vanished Arizona_). These books are the best primary source on post life. A scene from inside an officer's wife's life is a scene the reader rarely gets.

The enlisted man's wife — the laundress, often — lived in Soapsuds Row, did the post's washing, raised children in shacks, and was generally beneath the notice of the officers' families. Her life was harder than the officer's wife's life. Less documented, less written, equally real.

## 8. Discipline

Army discipline in the 1870s was severe by modern standards.

Minor offenses brought **extra duty** (additional fatigue assignments), **loss of pay** (forfeiture of part of a month's pay), or **confinement to barracks or the post.** Serious offenses brought **the guardhouse** — the post jail; a man could be confined for days, weeks, or months — **hard labor** (wood-cutting, ditch-digging, latrine work, with a chain on the leg), **the bucking and gagging** (a punishment of being tied in an uncomfortable position with a stick gag in the mouth — officially banned but unofficially used), **the wooden horse** (a man made to sit astride a narrow plank for hours, weighted at the ankles), and **court-martial** (for desertion, theft, striking a superior, mutiny — penalties up to dishonorable discharge with prison or, rarely, death).

The army drank. The post commander drank. The general drank — Grant's reputation was earned. Drunkenness on duty was an offense; drunkenness off duty was expected. The sutler's whisky and the smuggled rotgut were constant. The temperance soldier was rare and was suspected by his comrades. The soldier who could hold his drink without becoming a problem was respected.

## 9. The Army's Voice

(See Chapter 9 for the broader period speech reference.) Specific army features:

**Rank in address.** Officers were addressed by rank: _Captain, Lieutenant, Colonel._ Enlisted by _Sergeant, Corporal._ A private was addressed by surname or as _Soldier_ or _Trooper._

**The colonel's voice.** Officers from West Point had a Latin-saturated formal English. _I should be obliged, I am inclined to think, sir._

**The first sergeant's voice.** Old-army NCOs were often Irish or German immigrants. The voice was practical, profane (in the field, less in earshot of officers), specific. _Move it, you sons of bitches. Mount up. Mount up._

**The recruit's voice.** The new soldier in 1875 was often a recent immigrant (Irish, German, English, Italian) or an Eastern man with no other prospect. Multiple accents in the ranks.

**The reports.** Written reports used a formal bureaucratic register: _I have the honor to report, in compliance with instructions, the command marched at sunrise._ A field report had its own form. A letter of complaint had its own form. A request for transfer had its own form. A scene of an officer dictating a report is a scene in which the formal voice can be heard against the informal voice of the surrounding day.

## 10. The Campaign's Failure Modes

The army campaign in the West offered specific failure modes.

**Heat exhaustion.** Summer marches in the southern plains and the Southwest killed horses and men. A forced march in July might mean a third of the horses dead by the end. **Frostbite.** Winter campaigns (the army made many in the late 1860s and 1870s, on the theory that Indians were less mobile in winter) produced amputations from frostbite. Custer's 1868 winter campaign and the 1876–77 northern campaign were brutal. **Sickness.** Dysentery was the army's killer. Bad water, bad food, bad latrine discipline. A campaign could lose more men to sickness than to combat. **Desertion.** Men slipped away from the column, especially when the pursuit was futile and the column had been out for weeks. **Failure to find the enemy.** The most common campaign result. The column marched, marched, marched, and saw no Indians at all. **Ambush.** When the column did find the enemy, it was often on the enemy's terms. The Fetterman fight (1866), the Wagon Box fight (1867), the Little Bighorn (1876), the Wounded Knee aftermath (1890) — each is a study in the army's vulnerabilities.

A campaign novel that ends without a battle is historically more accurate than one that ends with glorious victory. The army's frustration is the campaign's real subject.

## Vignette — First Call

> The bugle sounded First Call at five fifty-five and the barracks lit up where the sergeants began going through them. Reilly sat up in his bunk and put his feet on the floor and reached for his pants in the dim light. The man in the next bunk, Karpinski, was already standing and pulling on his shirt.
>
> “Cold this morning,” Karpinski said.
>
> “It's August.”
>
> “Cold for August. I tell you this country is not made for men.”
>
> “It's made for somebody. I have not figured out who.”
>
> The bugle sounded Reveille at six and they fell in on the parade ground in the gray light with their carbines at order arms and their mounts standing in the picket line behind the barracks. The lieutenant came out from the orderly room with his hat under his arm and read the morning report. Three men sick. One man on extra duty for drunkenness on guard. The detail to escort the quartermaster wagon to the agency would leave at seven sharp. Sergeant Voss would command. Reilly heard his own name in the detail list.
>
> “Karpinski,” Reilly said as they went back toward the barracks. “I drew the agency run. You want my mess for the day?”
>
> “Bacon and beans?”
>
> “Bacon and beans and whatever else they are giving out.”
>
> “All right. I will save you a coffee for tonight.”
