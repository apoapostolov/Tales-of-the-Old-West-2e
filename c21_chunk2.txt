## 5. Cheating

The skilled cheat had a repertoire. A scene that includes cheating must show the right method for the game, and the prose should not over-explain.

- **The holdout.** A device worn under the coat (a "Kepplinger" or a strap holdout) that could deliver a card into the hand or take one away. The player palmed the card between the holdout and the play. Detection: a player whose hand somehow always had what he needed.
- **Marked cards.** Cards marked on the back so the cheat could read them without seeing the face. _Sand-tell_ — cards that have been touched with a fine sandpaper at specific positions; the cheat feels the marks; an unsuspecting player does not. _Luminous reader_ — a faint mark visible only through colored glasses or a specific light; mostly later than the 1870s, but appearing. _Edge work_ — cards trimmed slightly so a stripper deck can be cut to a desired card. _Daub_ — a paste applied to specific positions on the card backs, visible to the cheat who knows where to look.
- **Dealing seconds.** The dealer deals not the top card but the one underneath, holding the top for himself or his confederate. A skill that takes years; a true second-deal is invisible.
- **The cold deck.** A deck that has been pre-arranged and is switched in for the legitimate deck during play. The switch happens in a moment of distraction and the hand that follows is rigged.
- **The signaler / the partner.** Two cheats at the table communicating with signals (a touch of the ear, a cough, a particular phrasing). The partner knows the cheat's hand and bets accordingly. The partner is often unsuspected because he plays the role of an ordinary player.

A player who suspected cheating could call out the cheat. The traditional response was to flip the table, draw a derringer or a knife from a vest, and fight. Many famous gunfights began at the gambling table. The clean cheat — the one who could not be caught — often went his whole career undetected. The famous gambler Soapy Smith built a town-running operation around organized cheating.

## 6. The Professional Gambler

The professional gambler is a distinct character type with specific markers.

- **Dress.** Black broadcloth coat (frequently a frock coat), boiled white shirt, black string tie or a small bow, black wool trousers, polished boots, often a vest of patterned silk or brocade. The hat: a low-crown black wool. The watch chain across the vest. A diamond ring or a gold pin if successful. The dress signals: this man does not work outdoors, this man has standing, this man has money. The working dress and the appearance of money are part of the bank he brings to the table.
- **Manner.** Soft-spoken. Slow-moving. Long looks. Never visibly angry. The gambler who shows emotion at the table has lost his edge. **Hands** clean, nails trimmed; the hands move precisely; he does not gesture broadly; he does not slap the table; the hands are his instrument. **Voice** period-formal — the gambler speaks like a man who has read books: _I do believe, I am inclined to think, sir, ma'am._ The vocabulary is broader than a cowhand's. (See Chapter 9.)
- **The famous.** _Doc Holliday_ — tubercular dentist turned gambler, friend of Wyatt Earp; educated, deadly, sick; the model for the educated gambler with a pistol. _Wild Bill Hickok_ — former scout and lawman, turned to professional gambling in his last years; killed at the table. _Poker Alice (Alice Ivers)_ — female professional poker player; operated in Deadwood and elsewhere; the skilled woman gambler was rarer than the man but real. _Bat Masterson_ — lawman, gambler, eventually a New York sportswriter. _Luke Short_ — dapper, deadly, quiet; the model for the small, well-dressed gambler.

Most successful gamblers died of disease (consumption was epidemic in the trade) or of violence at the table. A few retired and died in beds. The gambler who lived past forty was unusual.

## 7. Stakes — What the Dollar Amounts Meant

The dramatic weight of a gambling scene depends on the stakes mattering to the reader. (See Chapter 13.) Brief reference:

- **A nickel ante** — the penny-ante game; cowhands killing time in the bunkhouse.
- **A quarter ante** — standard saloon poker; a pot might run to ten or twenty dollars.
- **A dollar ante** — a serious game; a pot might run to a hundred dollars; a cowhand's monthly wage was about thirty.
- **A five-dollar ante** — a high game; the professional gambler's table; pots could run to several hundred dollars; a scene at this table carries the weight of months of a working man's wages on each hand.
- **A bank bet at faro** — could be anything; players laid five cents up to a hundred dollars depending on the house; the famous Bird Cage in Tombstone had no limit.

The gambler who loses his last roll is a recurring western figure. The scene's mathematics: the man came in with three hundred dollars (a year's saved wages, or the proceeds of a small cattle sale), he leaves with nothing. The reader who knows what three hundred dollars meant in 1875 feels the weight.

## 8. The Table as Social Space

A faro or poker table was not only a place to gamble. It was a social center, an information exchange, and a place where deals other than the game's were made.

- **The cowhand on payday.** The drover's wages went to the saloon, and a portion went to the table. The scene of the cowhand losing his roll is a fundamental western figure.
- **The cattle deal at the table.** Two ranchmen at the same table might be negotiating a cattle sale between hands; the poker game was the cover for the business.
- **The rendezvous.** A man met another man at a specific table at a specific hour; information changed hands; money changed hands; the gambling was incidental.
- **The watcher.** A man sat at a table to watch — a Pinkerton detective tracking a fugitive, a sheriff watching a suspect, a relative watching a son; the watcher does not play seriously; he loses small amounts as the cost of the seat.

A scene at the table can carry any of these sub-currents. The game is the visible activity. The real business is what happens between the hands.

## 9. Women at the Gambling Table

The respectable woman did not gamble. The disreputable woman did. The third category — the professional woman gambler — was rare but real.

- **The saloon girl.** Many saloons employed women whose job was to entice men to play and to drink. They were not professional gamblers; they steered customers to the games. They took a cut of the house's win and often a cut of the drinks.
- **The brothel madam.** Some madams ran gambling tables in their houses (the sporting house was often part-saloon, part-brothel). The madam might deal faro or run the games herself. Some madams were known for fair dealing; others for the opposite.
- **The professional.** Poker Alice. Lottie Deno (an alias; her real name was Carlotta Thompkins). Madame Mustache (Eleanore Dumont, a French-born faro dealer who worked the gold camps of California, Nevada, and Montana). These women were known by name and by reputation. The professional female gambler dressed well, played her own bank or sat at a private game, and was treated with the respect a successful gambler commanded. She was not, by virtue of being a gambler, a prostitute; the two trades overlapped but were not the same.
