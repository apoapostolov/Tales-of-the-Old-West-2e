# Dramatis Personae and Statblocks

This appendix covers the named NPCs and the most useful generic profiles from [02-the-yellowstone-line.md](/mnt/c/git-public/tales-of-the-old-west-2e/supplement-2-montana-campaign/book-content/02-the-yellowstone-line.md).

Every named figure the party is likely to talk to, fight, guard, chase, or bargain with appears here.

The named blocks below follow the stronger-NPC guidance in [07-npc-creation-helper.md](/mnt/c/git-public/tales-of-the-old-west-2e/supplement-2-montana-campaign/research/07-npc-creation-helper.md). Starting player-character limits are not treated as caps for officers, scouts, fixers, or major antagonists.

## Named NPCs

**CAPTAIN NATHANIEL ROWE**

Rowe is a white man in his early forties, Army to the bone, narrow through the waist and still hard in the saddle. The first impression he gives is of order held together by temper. He keeps his gloves clean longer than any field officer should and has the habit of looking past a man before that man has finished speaking, as if measuring whether the rest is worth his time. He speaks in short clipped sentences and wastes no ornament unless he is dressing down a fool in public. In his general view, the player characters are useful irregular tools: necessary because the country is too large, too mixed, and too mean to be handled by enlisted men alone, but still not people he will trust until they prove they can hold their heads.

**LIEUTENANT EZRA HARLAN**

Harlan is a white man of twenty-seven, handsome in the polished cavalry way and not yet old enough to hide that he enjoys being looked at in uniform. The first impression is energy straining to pass for command. He stands a touch too straight, smiles a touch too quickly in company, and reaches for his belt or cuffs whenever he feels himself being judged. He speaks well, speaks fast, and speaks half a rank above his actual station when he gets excited. In his general view, the player characters are either the kind of rough useful people he may someday boast of having led, or the kind of disreputable witnesses he must not let see him fail.

**SERGEANT OWEN DUFFY**

Duffy is an Irish-born white man near fifty, broad through the chest, weathered, and going gray under the brim. He looks like a man built from old leather, smoke, and regulations remembered better than prayers. The first impression is steadiness. He wastes little motion, checks straps and loads without seeming to look, and can hear trouble in a horse line the way other men hear it in gunfire. He speaks plainly, often dryly, and with the tone of a man who has long since stopped being impressed by officers. In his general view, the player characters are either hands worth having on a bad road or one more set of mouths likely to complicate his work.

**PROFESSOR AMOS REED**

Reed is a white man in his mid-thirties, spare, near-sighted, and permanently overtired. He gives the impression of a schoolman who has stayed too long in hard country and learned to hate incompetence more than discomfort. He squints at ground, instruments, and men alike, fingers ink stains without noticing, and grows visibly colder the more exact the matter under discussion becomes. His speech is educated, dry, and increasingly sharp whenever commerce starts meddling with measurement. In his general view, the player characters are potential protection, labor, or witnesses to the truth of the work, depending on whether they prove more loyal to money or to fact.

**SILAS VANE**

Vane is a white man around forty, well-fed without looking soft, careful with his clothes, and too well groomed for any honest trail position. The first impression is smoothness. He never seems hurried even when events are hurrying around him. He lowers his voice when others would raise it, and he has the habit of stepping just close enough to make a private ally of whichever person he is speaking to. He talks in easy persuasive sentences, never quite saying who sent him or what authority he claims, only what sensible men ought to want. In his general view, the player characters are purchasable assets until proven otherwise, and if they cannot be bought they must at least be divided.

**ELI MERCER**

Mercer is a white man not yet thirty, neat-handed, narrow-shouldered, and too fond of bottle courage. The first impression is that of a clerk trying to look less afraid than he is. He smiles too soon, laughs where no laughter is needed, and keeps touching pockets, papers, and straps to reassure himself that things remain where he left them. He speaks quickly, softly, and with the habit of trimming every sentence toward safety. In his general view, the player characters are dangerous because they live closer to the kind of force a liar cannot write his way out of.

**THOMAS KEENE**

Keene is a white man in his early twenties, thin from bad sleep, bad whiskey, and worse nerves. He looks like the kind of young fool the frontier manufactures by putting weak ambition too near strong men. The first impression is fret and guilt. He sweats in cool air, glances over both shoulders before answering, and grips his own sleeves or vest as if he expects to be searched at any moment. He speaks in bursts, with excuses outrunning sense. In his general view, the player characters are possible rescuers, possible buyers, possible executioners, and he will try to guess which from one breath to the next.

**GIDEON PIKE**

Pike is a white man in his forties, heavy in the shoulders and gut without ever losing the look of someone who has worked teams by hand. The first impression is practical force. He dresses half like a freight boss, half like a man who knows exactly how much profit can be pulled from military need. He checks loads, counts sacks, and studies harness with more visible feeling than he shows toward most people. He speaks in the idiom of teamsters and provision men, but numbers sit behind everything he says. In his general view, the player characters are labor, risk, or leverage, and he can live with any of the three if the account ends in black ink.

**HANNAH CALDER**

Hannah Calder is a white woman in her late thirties or early forties, sun-cut, strong-handed, and dressed for work first and judgment later. The first impression is self-possession. She stands like a person used to carrying her own water, speaking for her own place, and burying her own dead if she must. She wastes no gesture, keeps tools where others keep ornament, and watches groups of men the way a rancher watches weather. She speaks flat, direct, and without asking leave. In her general view, the player characters are one more column of strangers until they prove they know the difference between needing a place and taking it.

In his general view, the player characters are worth listening to before they are worth trusting, which already puts him ahead of half the expedition.

## Generic Profiles

Use these for unnamed extras, reinforcements, and fast scene play. The list is split wide enough to cover the full expedition, hired interference, ranch locals, and the field enemies met during chapter `02`.

**CAVALRY TROOPER**

A cavalry trooper in this expedition is usually a young or middle-aged white enlisted man with sunburn on his neck, worn blue wool gone brown at the seams, and more road discipline than imagination. The first impression is fatigue held in order. He checks tack before speech, watches sergeants before officers, and trusts carbine, horse, and ration issue more than grand talk about the future of the territory. He speaks plainly, often in short borrowed Army phrases. In his general view, civilian player characters are either useful extra hands or one more set of irregular fools likely to get the line into trouble.

**CAVALRY CORPORAL**

A cavalry corporal looks much like the trooper he was yesterday, only now he carries the strain of being answerable for other men's mistakes. The first impression is brittle authority. He repeats orders harder than officers do, keeps one eye on the line behind him, and has learned that the easiest way to look weak is to hesitate in front of enlisted men. He speaks in a sharper version of camp language and uses rank as a shield when uncertain. In his general view, the player characters are useful if they obey fast and dangerous if they encourage the troopers to think for themselves.

**SURVEY HAND**

A survey hand is usually a clerkish or practical white civilian, often younger than he looks after a week in the field, with notebook grit under his nails and a bad way of carrying himself when armed men start talking over his work. The first impression is divided attention. He sees ground, numbers, and bearings first, danger second, and personal insult only after both. He speaks in technical fragments when nervous and in educated complaint when tired. In his general view, the player characters are protection if competent, interruption if not, and sometimes the only people in camp willing to believe the work matters on its own terms.

**SURVEY ASSISTANT**

A survey assistant is the useful middle sort: not the mind of the operation, not its laboring muscle alone, but one of the people who keep chain, rod, instrument, and notebook moving in the same direction. The first impression is nervous diligence. He or she is always carrying something, counting something, or trying not to drop something expensive in bad ground. Speech tends toward apology under pressure and competence when left alone with the actual work. In the general view of such a person, the player characters are either welcome shields against the camp's rougher appetites or dangerous meddlers who touch instruments with dirty hands.

**EXPEDITION CLERK**

An expedition clerk is often a neat white civilian, male or female, whose clothes were chosen for tables and offices rather than mud, though the country has already corrected that mistake. Speech is careful, deferential when needed, and quick to soften any statement that may later prove dangerous. In the general view of a clerk, the player characters are people who may solve problems too large for paper, but may also create the kind no ink can mend afterward.

**FREIGHT HAND**

A freight hand is a working frontier laborer, most often a white man worn thick through back and forearms, smelling of harness grease, mule sweat, tobacco, and flour dust. The first impression is endurance rather than grace. He lifts before he speaks, curses broken gear as if it had insulted his mother, and judges every road by axle strain and feed. Speech is blunt, often profane, and suspicious of anybody whose living comes from paper. In his general view, the player characters are either respectable if they can work and fight, or useless if they talk around both.

**TEAMSTER OR MULE SKINNER**

A teamster or mule skinner is a harder, more temperamental breed than an ordinary freight hand, usually older in the face than in the years, with rope-burned palms and a private vocabulary for animals no decent church would print. The first impression is force held on a rein. He reads stock before men, hears trouble in harness before gunfire, and can go from silence to rage in the space of one balking team. His speech is rich in insult, command, and practical detail. In his general view, the player characters are acceptable if they understand that frightened animals matter more than frightened clerks.

**FREIGHT TOUGH**

A freight tough is the kind of camp muscle that grows around valuable wagons, disputed contracts, and men who prefer threats to paperwork. The first impression is readiness for ugly work. He stands close, watches hands, and treats silence like a way of charging interest. Speech is spare, mocking, and built to provoke before the gun clears leather. In his general view, the player characters are rivals to size up, marks to lean on, or future allies if they prove they know how frontier money is really collected.
