## 6. The Drug Economies

The West ran on alcohol and laudanum and ran into morphine and opium. The addictions were widespread and were not hidden from period readers.

- **Laudanum.** A solution of opium in alcohol. Available without prescription in any drug store from the 1840s to the federal regulation of 1914. Used for everything: pain, cough, diarrhea, menstrual cramps, infant colic, the wife's nervous condition, the husband's bad back. The user did not know laudanum was opium-based in any meaningful sense. Many users became addicted without knowing they were taking the same drug as the Chinese opium den. The doctor's wife who carried a small bottle and took a sip at intervals through the day. The line woman whose madam kept the bottle and dispensed. The rancher's widow who started taking it for her grief and could not now stop.
- **Morphine.** Isolated 1804, manufactured commercially from the 1820s, used by injection from the 1850s after the hypodermic syringe. Civil War wounded who survived their injuries often returned home addicted; the condition was called _soldier's disease_ and it was widespread among veterans through the 1880s. The veteran who carried the syringe in a small case. The doctor who had access and who used. The gambler who started with a wound and stayed with the medicine.
- **Opium.** Smoked, not injected. The opium den was a real institution in the Chinese quarters of San Francisco, Portland, Denver, Tucson, and many smaller cities. The clientele was Chinese and Anglo. The Anglo customer was often a person of the local upper class slumming, sometimes a woman of the line, sometimes a working-class Anglo who had developed the habit. The den itself: a back room, low pallets, a small lamp on each pallet, the Chinese attendant who prepared each pipe. The smell was sweet and heavy and unmistakable. The Anglo backlash against the Chinese in the 1880s used the opium den as one of its rallying points. The same Anglo establishment was prescribing morphine freely.
- **Alcohol.** The course of frontier alcoholism. Stage one: the heavy drinker who functioned. Stage two: the man whose work suffered but who still worked. Stage three: the man who had lost his work and was on the slow slide. Stage four: the man on the street, the man under the porch, the man in the alley behind the saloon. Stage five: the body found in spring when the snow went off. The wife buying flour on credit she could not repay. The day the man did not come back from the saloon and the wife knew before she saw him that he was not coming back.

## 7. Domestic Violence and the Family Killing

The frontier homestead was a sealed environment. The husband was the law inside the cabin. The wife had no recourse beyond running, which usually meant running to her parents or her sister and which was often impossible because of distance. The neighbors did not interfere. The minister did not interfere unless the violence had become public.

The killings inside the cabin — the husband who killed the wife, the wife who killed the husband, the parent who killed the child, the child who killed the parent — appeared in the period coroner's reports at rates a modern reader would find shocking if they were collected.

The case that the period's law could not handle was the wife who killed the husband after years of beating. The defense was almost never accepted. She hanged or she went to the territorial prison or, in the rare case, she was acquitted by a jury of men who themselves had wives. The wife in the territorial prison at Yuma wrote letters home until the letters stopped.

## The Cold Stove

> She had not spoken since the second week of March. The cow was unmilked and the children were eating cold biscuit and the wind was in the stove pipe. Halvor stood in the door with his hat in his hand and looked at her sitting where she had been sitting since first light.
>
> _“Inga,”_ he said.
>
> She did not look at him. She was looking at the corner where the broom stood, and her hands were folded in her lap, and the youngest child was on the floor at her feet pulling at the hem of her dress.
>
> _“Inga, the cow needs milking.”_
>
> She did not answer. The wind moved a loose board on the north side of the soddy and the sound came through the wall.
>
> Halvor went out and milked the cow himself. When he came back into the soddy with the bucket she had not moved. He set the bucket down on the table and stood with his hat still in his hand and looked at her for a long moment. Then he put the hat on the peg and built up the fire in the cold stove and put the kettle on and lifted the youngest child off the floor and onto his hip.
>
> “Come on, then,” he said to the older girl. “Help me with the supper.”
>
> The older girl, who was nine, came over to the stove. She did not look at her mother either. She had stopped looking at her mother three days before.

## 8. Children

The frontier child was at risk in ways the period understood and did not understand.

- **The mortality.** The graveyard of small markers behind every frontier church. Diphtheria, scarlet fever, measles, whooping cough, the intestinal infections that killed infants in summer, the accidents on the working homestead — the kick, the fall from the wagon, the scald, the burn. A frontier mother who buried three of her ten children was not unusual. A frontier mother who buried five was within the range. The grave marker reads: _Aged 3 years 5 months._
- **The bound-out child and the orphan train.** The orphan train (1854–1929) carried about 250,000 children from East Coast cities to placement on western farms. The placements were not adoptions. The children were taken into households as workers and were expected to earn their keep. The good placements were genuine families in the receiving sense; the bad placements were free labor under conditions ranging from harsh to abusive. The bound-out child was a similar case at the local level: the orphan whose parents had died and who was placed by the county with a farm family who took him in exchange for his labor until he reached majority. The contract was binding. The child had no recourse. Beating, overwork to the point of disability, sexual abuse, withholding of food, withholding of clothes adequate for winter, denial of schooling. The child could run — and often did, ending up on the street in a frontier town or, worse, back into a similar placement at the next county over.
- **The line woman's child.** The child born to a woman of the line was raised by the mother in the parlor house or by a wet nurse hired with the mother's earnings. The child of the line had no legal father and no inheritance. The boys often ran away to the open country at twelve or thirteen and found work as horse-holders, camp boys, then cowhands. The girls often went into the trade themselves at fifteen or sixteen.
- **Abuse the period did not name.** The period had almost no concept of child sexual abuse as a prosecutable category. The cases that made the records were the most extreme — the prosecution of a man for what would now be called rape of a child usually required that the child had also been physically wounded. Most cases of abuse went unprosecuted and unspoken. The changed child afterward: the silence, the wetting, the nightmares, the avoidance of a particular adult, the school performance that fell.

## 9. Slavery Beyond the Civil War

Race-based chattel slavery in the U.S. was abolished in 1865. Slavery as an institution did not disappear from the West.

- **Comanche and other Native slave economies.** The Comanche raided into Mexico and into the Anglo settlements through the 1840s, 1850s, and 1860s and took captives as slaves. The captives were used for labor, for sexual service, for adoption into the band, and for trade with other nations. The Comanche economy through the mid-19th century included slave-trading with the Pueblos, the Diné (Navajo), and the Anglo and Mexican traders at the Comanchero markets. The Apache, Ute, and other nations practiced similar slave economies at smaller scales.
- **Diné (Navajo) and Pueblo slavery.** The Diné raided into the Pueblo and Spanish and Mexican communities and took captives. The Pueblo and Spanish raided back. The system of mutual captive-taking created a class of _genízaros_ — captives who were neither fully their birth nation nor fully their captor nation — that persisted in New Mexico into the 1880s. The federal government formally abolished the system in 1867. The system continued in practice for two more decades.
- **Debt peonage.** The peón system in New Mexico, Texas, and Arizona held workers in legally enforceable debt to a _patrón._ The worker could not leave the patrón's land until the debt was paid. The debt was structured so that it could not be paid. The system was formally outlawed by federal law in 1867 and continued in practice well into the twentieth century in the Southwest. A worker who tried to leave was returned by the sheriff to the patrón. A worker's children inherited the debt. A worker's daughter could be required to work in the patrón's household as part of the debt service, with all the consequences such an arrangement implied.
- **Chinese sex slavery.** The Chinese sex slavery trade ran from Guangdong province to San Francisco and Portland from the 1850s through the Exclusion period. Girls aged twelve to fifteen were bought from impoverished families, shipped under fraudulent papers, and sold in San Francisco for $300 to $1,000 each. They worked in the _sing-song_ houses of the Chinese quarter under contracts that were structured never to be paid off. The average working life was four to five years. Most died of disease, violence, or self-inflicted opium overdose. The Chinese-language reformer Donaldina Cameron and her mission rescued some of these women in the 1880s and after.

## 10. The Starving Homestead

The homestead failure that ended in death by starvation. The period had a name for it (_played out_). The historical record has many cases.

The progression: a homestead claim taken in spring, the first year's crop a partial failure, the savings exhausted to buy through the winter, the second year's planting on insufficient seed and worse soil, the second year's crop a worse failure. By the autumn of the second year the family was in a position where the husband had to choose between selling the team (without which the homestead was finished) and watching the children go hungry through the winter.

The deaths came in late winter and early spring. The family had eaten the seed corn. The cow had been killed and eaten by February. The husband had ridden out to look for work and not returned. The wife and the children stayed in the soddy. The oldest child died first, then the next, then the wife, then the youngest. The neighbor who came up the trace in April found the dugout and the bodies.

The wife's calculations through the winter: the flour barrel; the salt pork that had been gone since January; the youngest child's cough; the decision not to write to her sister in Indiana for help because she could not bear to write the letter that would say what she had to say. The graves were dug in ground still half-frozen. The husband, when he was found later in a different county where he had walked in search of work, had to be told what had happened.

## 11. Animal Cruelty as Institution

Practices that were entertainment or industry rather than necessity.

- **The pit fights.** Bear-and-bull fights in the California ranchos and the early Anglo California of the 1840s and 1850s. A grizzly bear chained by a hind leg to a stake; a fighting bull released into the pit; the bull gored the bear or the bear killed the bull. Sometimes bear-and-dogs. Sometimes bull-and-dogs. The audience paid admission. The fights continued in California through the 1860s and persisted in Mexican rural areas into the 1880s. Cockfighting was universal across the West. Dogfighting was common in the cities. The audience was male, working class, and gambled.
- **The systematic killing of horses.** The cavalry pursuit of a Native band on the southern Plains was understood by both sides as a contest of horseflesh. The pursuit ran the horses to death, killed and ate the dead horses, and continued on whatever horses could be commandeered. The 1874 Red River War destroyed the horse herds of the Comanche, Kiowa, and Cheyenne by deliberate slaughter — at Palo Duro alone the Army shot over a thousand captured ponies. The break of the Plains nations was in part the break of their horses.
- **The buffalo slaughter.** The hide hunters of the 1870s killed an estimated fifteen million buffalo in a decade. The bones were collected afterward and shipped east for fertilizer. The plains were emptied as policy. The hunter knew what he was doing and was doing it for the money. The Native witness to the slaughter watched a continent emptied of the animal his people had lived on for ten generations. The white settler who saw the bones piled at the railhead said nothing.
