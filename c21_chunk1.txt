# Chapter 21 — Gambling

> _“HE HAD BEEN AT THE TABLE FOUR HOURS.”_

Gambling was the central social activity of the working West. Cowhands gambled their wages on payday. Soldiers gambled garrison pay. Miners gambled the strike. Women in the towns ran some of the most respected games. The professional gambler — well-dressed, soft-spoken, with a clean hand and a quick eye — was a specific kind of man, neither outlaw nor citizen but his own thing. What follows is the four major games, the gambler's character, the cheating methods, the stakes, and the table as social space.

---

## 1. The Four Major Games

- **Faro.** The dominant bank game of the western saloon through the 1870s and into the 1890s. A house game, played against the dealer (the "bank"). Easy to play, fast, visible, and the house edge was small — making faro the most respectable bank game by the standards of the period.
- **Monte.** Two distinct games sharing a name. _Spanish monte_ is a bank game with a Spanish-deck heritage, dominant in the Southwest and in border country, a regional standard alongside faro. _Three-card monte_ is a confidence game (the dealer wins because the game is rigged), played by sharps on stages, in railroad cars, in saloons — properly a swindle, not a game. The two are not to be confused.
- **Poker.** The dominant private game of the West. Played between players, with the house taking a small rake or a table fee. Multiple variants in the period, evolving fast. The dominant western variants: _five-card draw_ (the classic — each player dealt five cards, may discard and draw replacements once, betting in two rounds) and _five-card stud_ (first card down, four up; betting after each up card; took hold from the late 1860s). Texas hold 'em did not exist in the 1870s. Stud and draw were the games.
- **Other games.** _Chuck-a-luck_ (a.k.a. birdcage) — a dice game played with three dice in a wire cage; players bet on which numbers will come up; house game, large house edge; common in cheap saloons. _Roulette,_ present in the better saloons; the American double-zero wheel (38 numbers) became standard in the West; played but never as common as faro. _Keno,_ a lottery-style game with numbered balls or beans, common in the larger saloons. _Brag, all-fours, euchre_ — card games of regional or class distribution; brag was a poker ancestor; euchre was popular in the army.

## 2. Faro — Procedure

The most-played game in the West deserves the deepest treatment.

- **The layout** is a green baize cloth or a printed leather mat showing the thirteen ranks (Ace through King) of one suit laid out in two rows. Players bet by placing chips (or coin) on the rank they predict. **The dealer's box** is a small metal or wooden box with one open side, sized to hold a deck of cards face-up. The cards are inserted face-up. The top card is visible through the window.
- **The deal.** The top card (the "soda") is removed and set aside; it does not figure in the betting. The dealer pulls the next card and lays it to one side of the box — this is the "loser" or "banker's card"; all bets on cards of that rank lose. The dealer pulls the next card and lays it on the other side — this is the "winner" or "player's card"; all bets on cards of that rank win. The two cards together are a "turn." The dealer pays winning bets and rakes losing ones. The deck is dealt down in turns until two cards remain (with the soda set aside). This is the "hock," and the last turn is dealt for double or triple stakes.
- **The casebook (case keeper)** is a separate device tracking which cards have been played — a wooden frame with thirteen rows of four beads, one row per rank. As each card is dealt, the case keeper moves a bead. Players can see at any moment which cards remain in the deck. This is the faro player's analytical instrument.

- **Copper.** A player can "copper" his bet — placing a small hexagonal token (a copper) on top of his chips — which reverses the bet. Now he wins if the rank loses and loses if it wins. This is a major flexibility of the game.
- **The split.** If both winner and loser cards in a turn are the same rank, it is a "split." The dealer takes half of all bets on that rank. The split was the bank's principal advantage.
- **The call.** On the last turn (the "hock"), if a player has correctly tracked the casebook, he may "call the turn" — predict the order of the last three cards. Pays four to one. The faro player's edge case.
- **The vocabulary.** _Bank_ (the dealer; he holds the bank); _lay_ (to place a bet — _he laid ten on the queen_); _soda_ (the top burned card); _hock_ (the last few cards); _casekeeper_ (the man tracking the deck); _copper_ (the reversing token); _heel_ (a bet specifying that one of two cards paired in a turn wins).

A faithfully dealt faro game had a house edge of about 1.5% — among the lowest of any house game ever offered. This is why faro dominated. A player who tracked the casebook well could in theory beat the game; in practice, dishonest dealing (rigged boxes, marked cards, dealing seconds) returned the edge to the house.

A faro game has its own pulse. The dealer pulls; pause; he pulls; pause. The two cards land and the bets are settled. The case keeper moves a bead. The next bets go down. The pause between turns is where the prose lives — the faces around the table, the sip of whisky, the look at the casebook.

## Faro at Midnight

> The dealer turned a king to the soda and a four to the hock and the layout went still while the bettors looked at the cards. Three coppers came off the king. One bet stayed on the four. The dealer pulled the cases (a six and a nine), then began the next turn.
>
> A jack to the loser. A queen to the winner. The dealer paid the queen bets and pulled the jack bets and the caseman moved the abacus beads. Two more turns went by without a hit on the bigger money on the table.
>
> Then the dealer turned a deuce to the loser and a deuce to the winner. A split. The house took half. The man who had the deuce stack — a little man in a Sunday coat from someplace east — pushed his chair back and stared at the dealer for a moment.
>
> “A split,” the dealer said. “House takes half. You want the other half down or pulled?”
>
> “Pulled.”
>
> The dealer counted out the half stack and pushed it across the layout to him. The man picked it up and put it in his coat pocket and stood up and walked out without looking back. He had been at the table four hours.
>
> “Cards,” the dealer said. The next turn began.

## 3. Spanish Monte

The dominant card game of the Southwest and of border country. Played with a Spanish 40-card deck (no 8s, 9s, 10s).

The dealer holds the deck face-down. He removes the top two cards and places them face-up in a row — the "top layout." He removes the bottom two cards and places them face-up in another row — the "bottom layout." Players bet that the next card turned from the deck will match (in rank) one of the four exposed cards. The dealer turns the top card of the deck. If it matches in rank one of the four exposed cards, players who bet on that card win. The dealer (the banker) pays even money on most bets. The house edge comes from the four-of-a-kind problem: if more than one card of a rank has already been dealt, the bet's mathematics shift.

The vocabulary is mostly Spanish: _banco_ (the bank), _puerta_ (the door — the top layout), _albur_ (the bottom layout), _gallo_ (the rooster — a winning card).

Monte was the Mexican gambler's game. A monte table in El Paso, Tucson, Las Vegas (New Mexico Territory), or any border town was a Spanish-language space. The Anglo who sat at a monte table was the outsider in his own country.

## 4. Poker — Procedure

Poker is a private game between players. The house provides the table, the cards, sometimes the dealer, and takes a rake (a small percentage of each pot) or a table fee.

- **Five-card draw — the dominant 1870s game.** Each player puts up an ante (a small forced bet). Each player is dealt five cards face-down. A round of betting: each player in turn may check (if no bet has been made), bet, call (match the current bet), raise, or fold. After betting, each player may discard up to three cards and draw replacements (some house rules allowed four if the player held an Ace). A second round of betting. Showdown — best hand wins.
- **Five-card stud.** Each player put up an ante. One card dealt face-down (the hole card), one face-up. Betting round, opened by the high card showing. Another card up. Betting round. Another card up. Betting round. Last card up. Final betting round. Showdown.

- **The hand rankings (1870s standard),** from low to high: high card; one pair; two pair; three of a kind; straight; flush; full house; four of a kind; straight flush. The straight flush ranking was settled in the 1860s. The royal flush as a separate name came later. A wild card was not standard. Lowball variants existed but were rare.
- **The vocabulary.** _Ante_ (forced opening bet); _pot_ (the accumulated bets); _call_ (match the current bet); _raise_ (match and increase); _fold_ (surrender the hand); _check_ (decline to bet without folding); _showdown_ (reveal hands); _tells_ (unconscious habits revealing the hand); _bluff_ (bet strongly with a weak hand to drive out callers); _the dead man's hand_ (aces and eights — the hand Wild Bill Hickok was reputedly holding when shot in Deadwood, August 1876; the phrase entered western lore quickly).

The professional player has specific behaviors. He stacks his chips neatly. He does not look at his hole card more than once. He plays slowly when he intends to play and quickly when he intends to fold. He does not drink at the table (or drinks colored water from a bottle that looks like whisky). He sits with his back to a wall.

The amateur drinks. He is loud or silent depending on the strength of his hand. He counts his money during the play. He looks at his cards too often. He makes his bet with the wrong number of chips and corrects it. The professional reads him in three hands.
