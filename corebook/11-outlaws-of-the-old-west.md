## Chapter 11 - Outlaws of the Old West

> *“AIN’T NO SUCH THING AS EASY MONEY. THERE’S JUST MONEY TOOK FAST, AND THE FASTER YOU TAKE IT THE FASTER MEN COME LOOKING.”*
>
> — OLD BORDER SAYING

### Outlaws and Their Trade

There are outlaws in almost every tale of the Old West. Some are no more than horse thieves and barn robbers. Some are killers who make a business of fear. Some are desperate men and women with nowhere left to go but the rough country. The dime novels lie about them all. They tell of bold riders and easy loot and a free life under the open sky. What they leave out is the cold camp, the empty belly, the partner who cheats the split, the horse gone lame fifty miles from water, the posse on your trail, and the knowledge that one bad score can leave your gang strung up from six different trees.

This chapter is an optional campaign framework for groups who want to play that life. It is written to sit alongside the Outlaws Group Concept in Chapter 2, not replace it. If your player characters are already outlaws, rustlers, raiders, train robbers, or fugitives, these rules help you run the gang around them: the hands who ride with them, the hideout that keeps them alive, the scores that put money in their pockets, and the pressure that comes down when the law, the railroads, or another gang finally decides it has had enough.

You do not need these rules for a one-off bank robbery or a short tale about a fugitive on the run. Use them when outlaw life is the campaign, or a major part of it.

#### The Outlaws Group Concept

If the group chose the Outlaws Group Concept during character creation, then this chapter grows naturally from that choice. The concealed hideout granted by the concept becomes the gang's first base, and any compadres gained through lifepath may be treated as the beginning of the gang's inner circle. If the player characters did not choose that concept, but become outlaws through play, they can still use these rules once they have a hideout, a handful of followers, and a reputation worth fearing.

> *Mexican Joe laughed when Jeb called them a Crew, not because the word was small but because it was honest. Six riders could vanish where sixteen left stories in the dust. Six could eat, sleep, and move before dawn. Six could still do ugly work if they stayed mean and smart.*

### The Outlaw Band

An outlaw band is more than a few player characters with pistols on their hips. It is a criminal outfit with a name, a leader, a place to hole up, and enough folk riding under its shadow that success or failure becomes everyone's problem.

An outlaw band requires:

- A leader, usually one of the player characters.
- A hideout.
- At least three other outlaws, camp followers, or gunhands beyond the player characters.

If the group has fewer than three such folk, then they are still a posse, not yet a gang.

#### Band Scale

| Scale | Numbers | What It Means |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Crew | 3-6 outlaws beyond the player characters | Fast-moving and easy to conceal. Gain `+1 die` on `Lie Low`, `Move Camp`, and concealment or escape checks where a smaller body of riders is an advantage. |
| Gang | 7-15 outlaws beyond the player characters | A real local threat. Gain `+1 die` on recruitment, intimidation, and score checks where a respectable show of force or numbers matters. |
| Outfit | 16-30 outlaws beyond the player characters | Too large to pass unnoticed, but hard to push around once formed. Gain `+1 die` on checks to hold territory, overawe a town, or absorb casualties and still press the job. |

Anything larger than this is no longer a simple outlaw gang. It becomes a private army, a political problem, or an outright insurrection, and should be handled as a special case by the GM rather than routine outlaw play.

> *Jeb called for a `Lie Low` season while the sheriff was still whipping up a posse. Because the Harper gang counted as a `Crew`, they took the `+1 die` for being small enough to disappear. Mexican Joe rolled `HAWKEYE`, scored `2` successes, and by the end of the week the law was searching three wrong canyons while the gang cooked in silence above a dry wash. Had they been an `Outfit`, they would have left too many tracks, too many empty tins, and too many mouths needing food from the same frightened valley.*

#### Gang Leadership

Every outlaw band needs one acknowledged leader. This may be the most feared gun in camp, the best rider, the cleverest planner, or simply the one who keeps the cash box and knows where the hideout lies. If two or more folk claim leadership, trouble follows soon enough.

When the rules in this chapter call for a leadership roll, the leader makes a `PRESENCE` roll. If they are trying to settle the gang with charm, lies, speechifying, or the promise of easy money, they may use `PERFORMIN'` instead. The GM may grant bonus dice for clear advantages:

- `+1` if the gang was paid fairly after the last score
- `+1` if the leader's Fame is 3 or more
- `+1` if the gang has had a recent success with little bloodshed
- `-1` if the gang has gone hungry, cold, or unpaid
- `-1` if one of the gang was left captured, wounded, or dead through the leader's bad judgment
- `-1` if the leader's Reputation suggests cowardice, treachery, or meanness of spirit

The GM should not stack every possible modifier. Apply only those that truly matter in the moment, to a practical maximum of `+3` or `-3`.

### Gang Cohesion

Outlaw gangs hold together for three reasons: fear, profit, and loyalty. Most are built on two of the three. Few are built on all of them. Gang Cohesion measures how close your outfit is to staying in the saddle together or breaking apart into grudges, desertions, and murder.

Gang Cohesion is tracked on a scale from 1 to 5. A newly formed outlaw band starts at 3.

| Cohesion | State | Effect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 5 | Keen | The gang is full of confidence. Gain `+1` die on rolls to recruit new hands, steady the gang, or begin a score boldly. |
| 4 | Solid | The gang is riding well together. No modifier. |
| 3 | Uneasy | The camp is full of muttering and second thoughts. Once each week in hard times, or after any ugly setback, the leader must make a leadership roll to keep arguments from turning into trouble. |
| 2 | Fraying | `-1` die on rolls to steady the gang. Once every 3 days in hard times the leader must make a leadership roll or suffer a desertion, feud, or act of disobedience chosen by the GM. |
| 1 | Broken | The gang is on the edge of collapse. The leader must make a leadership roll each day the gang remains under pressure. Any failed Cohesion test causes a split, betrayal, or outright flight unless the leader acts immediately. |

#### Testing Cohesion

Test Gang Cohesion when one of the following occurs:

- A score fails badly.
- The gang is not paid fairly.
- One or more gang members are captured and abandoned.
- The gang suffers heavy casualties.
- The hideout runs short of food, ammunition, horses, or shelter.
- The gang is forced to keep moving through a hard winter or in brutal weather.
- The leader cheats the split, plays favorites, or protects one of their own after a ruinous mistake.
- The gang commits a massacre or some other useless cruelty that brings heat without profit.

When Cohesion is tested, the leader makes a leadership roll. On a success, the gang holds together. On two or more successes, increase Cohesion by 1 if the circumstances warrant it. On a failure, reduce Cohesion by 1 and roll or choose one result from the Gang Trouble table in this chapter. At Cohesion 1, a failed test means the gang breaks in some serious way: several hands ride off, a notable member turns against the leader, or the whole outfit goes to ground in a panic.

#### Keeping the Camp Together

At `Uneasy`, `Fraying`, or `Broken`, the pressure of outlaw life does some of its work between scores. Men sulk. Women pack. Someone wants a bigger share, a rescue attempt, a move south, or blood for a dead brother. The exact grievance does not need its own subsystem. The pressure is the rule.

When the cadence in the Cohesion table calls for it, the leader makes a leadership roll.

**0 successes:** The gang's temper gets away from it. The GM chooses one: reduce Cohesion by 1, lose 1 point of Loyalty from a notable member, lose one ordinary hand, or trigger Gang Trouble.

**1 success (limited):** The leader keeps a lid on things, but only by spending something real. Promise an extra share, accept a delay, restore a slighted notable member's dignity, or suffer `-2 die` on the gang's next band action check.

**2 successes (full):** The gang grumbles but stays in line. No further effect.

**3 successes or more:** The argument turns in the leader's favor. Remove one temporary camp penalty, or restore 1 Loyalty to a notable member who was wavering.

#### Cohesion Changes

The GM may increase or reduce Cohesion directly when events plainly justify it.

| Event | Cohesion Change |
| --- | --- |
| Clean success on a profitable score | `+1` |
| Fair shares paid after a rich take | `+1` |
| The gang survives a hard season and keeps the hideout supplied | `+1` |
| Lean spell, no profitable work, and tempers sour | `-1` |
| One of the gang is hanged or captured and left to their fate | `-1` |
| The leader cheats the shares | `-2` |
| The gang is driven out in winter with poor supplies | `-1` |
| A pointless public massacre or cruelty brings law and outrage down on the gang | `-1` |

Cohesion should not swing every session. Save changes for moments that really tell on the gang.

> *When Jeb chose not to rescue the hand taken in Lincoln, the GM called for a Cohesion test. Jeb rolled `PRESENCE` and came up empty. Cohesion dropped from `3` to `2`, and the GM chose to cut Luther Vale's Loyalty by `1`. Nobody drew that night, but the next morning Luther saddled before breakfast and asked who exactly the leader meant to leave behind next time. That is what a failed Cohesion roll should feel like: not noise for its own sake, but the camp turning mean around a choice that cannot be taken back.*

### Band Resources

An outlaw band tracks three resources beyond cash and Cohesion. They are not ledger entries. They are pressure gauges that tell the GM and the players how much room the gang still has to move.

#### Refuge

`Refuge` measures how the gang stands with a single important settlement, camp, trading post, ranch, or hidden community. Track it only for places that actually matter to the gang's survival.

| Refuge | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `+2` | The place will hide you, feed you quietly, pass messages, and lie to the law if pressed. |
| `+1` | The place deals with you, but wants discretion and quick business. |
| `0` | No special help. You are just another party of riders. |
| `-1` | The place is wary, talks to strangers, and may refuse service. |
| `-2` | The place will warn the law, deny shelter, or sell you out. |

Refuge starts at 0 for any settlement the gang has not yet dealt with. Raise it by spending money, doing favors, or making yourself useful. Lose it by leaving a mess behind, killing someone local, cheating a merchant, or making the town look dangerous to outsiders. A town's `Law`, `Civic`, and `Mercantile` aspects all bear on what Refuge can do for the gang and what costs them.

A starting outlaw band may choose one village, camp, ranch settlement, or hidden community with a `Law` rank no higher than 2 as its first quiet corner in the world. Begin with `Refuge +1` there. If the Outlaws Group Concept clearly ties the gang to that place through kin, old favors, or shared blood, the GM may instead allow `Refuge +2`, but only if the gang is not already loudly notorious there.

Refuge should be tracked only for places that can actually matter. One gang might have Refuge with a river village that keeps a skiff ready at midnight, a Nuevomexicano rancheria that trades corn and silence for coin, and a shabby mining camp hotel where no one asks names after dark. That is enough. More than three such places is usually clutter, not play.

#### Calling on Refuge

When the gang needs something from a settlement—shelter from a posse, a doctor for the wounded, supplies, a safe stable, or word about law activity—the character with the strongest relationship there makes a `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` roll. Add dice equal to the current Refuge score if positive, or remove dice equal to its absolute value if negative.

**0 successes:** The place will not help and the refusal has consequences. A merchant talks to the wrong man, a farmer bolts his door and sends his boy to town, or an innkeeper quietly passes word to the deputy. If Refuge with this settlement is `0` or higher, reduce it by 1. If it is already negative, do not reduce it further unless the GM has a separate reason to do so.

**1 success (limited):** Help is given, but grudgingly and at a price—higher costs, visible discomfort, or conditions attached. The gang spent goodwill to get this. Refuge holds but the debt is felt.

**2 successes (full):** The settlement does what the gang needs without incident or complaint. Refuge holds steady.

**3 successes or more:** The place goes further than the gang asked. A warning about a posse they had not heard of, an introduction to a potential fence, or something quietly set aside against a harder day. Consider raising Refuge by 1 if the relationship has been well tended.

#### Fence Access

`Fence Access` measures whether the gang can turn stolen goods into clean money.

| Fence Access | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `0` | No fence. Hot loot sits in camp or must be sold openly at heavy risk. |
| `1` | A petty fence. Can move jewelry, guns, whiskey, and small goods at a fair discount. |
| `2` | A serious fence. Can move cattle, horses, payroll paper, and larger goods through a network. |

A fence is usually a notable gang member, a corrupt merchant, a Nuevo Mexicano trader, or a settlement contact reached through Refuge. Losing a fence—through arrest, betrayal, or the law's attention—drops Fence Access immediately. Building a new one takes time, goodwill, and often money.

#### Where Fences Exist

Most towns do not need a separate fence map. Use the town's `Law` and `Mercantile` ranks to judge how likely criminal trade is.

| Town Conditions | What They Usually Support |
| --- | --- |
| `Law 1-2` and `Mercantile 2+` | A petty fence is common enough. The town may support `Fence Access 1` without special pleading. |
| `Law 1-2` and `Mercantile 4+` | Serious criminal trade is plausible. The town may support `Fence Access 2` if the fiction supports it. |
| `Law 3-4` and `Mercantile 3+` | A fence exists only behind a face, a back room, or a specific crooked contact. Positive Refuge is usually required to reach it. |
| `Law 5-6` | No open fence market. Any fence is personal, fragile, and dangerous to use. |

In short: low Law makes fences possible, high Mercantile makes them useful, and positive Refuge makes them yours rather than somebody else's rumor.

> *Mexican Joe took the stage loot into town with `Fence Access 1` and `Refuge +1`, so the fence roll started with `+1 die`. He got `1` success. The trader still moved the rings and revolvers, but only for half value, and he made Joe wait three nights before coming back for the money. That was a limited success: the gang got cash, but every extra day in that town gave the deputy another chance to hear the wrong name.*

#### Horsestock

`Horsestock` measures the state of the gang's riding animals as a band resource.

| Horsestock | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `4` | Prime stock: fresh remounts, tribal-bred runners, choice Appaloosas or Cayuses, or other first-rate horses fit for hard country and sudden war. Gain `+1 die` on pursuit and flight checks. |
| `3` | Fresh remounts, sound tack, and horses fit for hard flight or hard pursuit. |
| `2` | Serviceable. The gang can ride, but cannot outrun professionals for long. |
| `1` | Tired, lame, hungry, poorly shod, or too few mounts for the gang's size. |
| `0` | The gang cannot move fast or far. Flight is a stumble. |

Horsestock drops after hard flight from a posse, a bad winter at the hideout, drought, poor grazing, or stolen mounts lost in a score gone wrong. Restore it by taking the `Buy Through Fences` hideout activity, rustling a fresh remuda, or dealing with a competent wrangler.

When Horsestock is 0 or 1, any well-mounted pursuer gains an advantage overtaking the gang.

> *After two hard rides and one dry camp, the GM judged that the gang had met enough bad conditions to lose Horsestock at season's end. Mexican Joe made an `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` roll while trading for oats and shoeing through a livery friend. He got `2` successes, so Horsestock held at `2` instead of dropping to `1`. A failure there would not have looked dramatic at first. It would have shown up a week later, when the posse's horses were still blowing hard and the gang's were already stumbling.*

#### Maintaining Horsestock

Good horses do not stay good by wishing. At the end of a season, reduce Horsestock by 1 if two or more of the following are true and the gang did nothing about them:

- the gang rode hard for weeks
- the hideout has poor grazing or water
- the gang could not afford feed, tack repair, or shoeing
- wounded or blown horses were not replaced
- winter, drought, or rough country worked against them

A wrangler with `ANIMAL HANDLIN'`, a friendly livery, or money spent through a fence can prevent this loss. So can choosing `Buy Through Fences`, spending time in good grazing country, or stealing enough decent remounts to solve the problem for now.

#### Buying and Improving Horses

The following are useful price anchors for improving the gang's stock:

| Purchase | Typical Price | Effect on Horsestock |
| --- | --- | --- |
| rough mustang, mixed trail horse, or tired remount | `$80-$120` per mount | keeps the gang from falling further, but does not raise Horsestock above `2` by itself |
| average work horse | about `$150` per mount | enough buying of this quality can raise Horsestock to `2` or hold it there |
| good saddle horse | about `$200` per mount | enough buying of this quality can raise Horsestock to `3` |
| prime runner, Appaloosa, Cayuse, Arabian, or similar top stock | `$250+` per mount | enough buying of this quality, with a good wrangler and tack, can raise Horsestock to `4` |
| saddle and tack for a new mount | about `$30` per rider | without this, good horses do not stay good long |

The gang does not need to buy a perfect horse for every soul to improve the band. As a rule of thumb, if at least half the mounted hands are upgraded and the rest are serviceable, raise Horsestock by 1. To reach `4`, most of the important riders need excellent stock and the gang needs the feed, tack, and horseflesh sense to keep them sound.

> *After the spring score, Jeb spent enough ready cash on four good saddle horses and fresh tack to improve more than half the gang's mounts. The GM raised Horsestock from `2` to `3`. That looked expensive on the table, but later it spared the gang worse expense. Better horses are the sort of purchase outlaws only appreciate after the first hard chase they survive because of them.*

### Towns and Outlaw Life

Towns are not neutral ground. They have economic interests, political divisions, moral tolerances, and men with guns. The outlaw who walks into one without reading it first has already made a mistake.

#### Reading a Town

The town aspects from Chapter 8 do double work in outlaw play. Each one answers a different question for the gang.

| Aspect | What It Tells the Gang |
| --- | --- |
| `Law` | How fast the town organizes pursuit, posts notices, and supports the sheriff or marshal |
| `Mercantile` | Whether there are fences, corrupt clerks, liquid markets for stolen goods, or buyers who ask few questions |
| `Civic` | Whether the town stands together against trouble or is divided by old grudges, class hatred, and competing loyalties |
| `Welfare` | Whether strangers can disappear quietly, find a doctor without comment, stable horses without remark, and rent a room without filling out a ledger |

A town with high Law and low Mercantile will hunt you hard and refuse your money. A town with low Law and high Mercantile will deal with you if you keep things quiet. The same town can be lawful on paper and corrupt in practice. That is more western than any flat lawful/lawless distinction.

#### Town Tags for Outlaw Play

For settlements that matter to the campaign, the GM may add one of the following tags to capture its character for outlaw purposes. These can be derived from existing aspects rather than tracked separately if preferred.

| Tag | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `Soft Town` | Weak law, divided interests, or enough vice that a gang can vanish there briefly without drawing attention |
| `Fence Town` | Stolen goods can be moved here through merchants, drovers, gamblers, or rail contacts. Fence Access rolls gain `+1 die` in this town |
| `Sheriff’s Town` | The local law, ranchers, and merchants cooperate quickly against trouble. Law is treated as one step higher for outlaw purposes |

The same town can hold more than one tag. A cattle market town might be both a `Fence Town` and a `Sheriff’s Town` during roundup season, which is a western truth of its own.

#### Example New Mexico Towns

The following values are example outlaw-use readings of the New Mexico campaign towns in Chapter 9. They are not immutable law. They are working judgments for outlaw play.

| Town | Law | Mercantile | Civic | Welfare | Tags | Fence Outlook |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Jornada Springs | 3 | 2 | 3 | 3 | none | petty fence only through personal ties |
| Steaming Rock | 2 | 3 | 3 | 2 | `Soft Town` | petty fence likely, serious fence possible through contacts |
| Carson’s Folly | 1 | 2 | 2 | 2 | `Soft Town` | petty fence likely, but rough and unreliable |
| Santa Fe | 5 | 5 | 5 | 4 | `Sheriff’s Town` | fences exist, but only through powerful corrupt contacts |
| Albuquerque | 2 | 4 | 3 | 3 | `Soft Town`, `Fence Town` | one of the better places in the territory to find a fence |
| Lincoln | 4 | 3 | 3 | 3 | `Sheriff’s Town` | criminal trade exists, but it is tied to powerful local interests |
| Silver City | 1 | 4 | 2 | 2 | `Soft Town`, `Fence Town` | excellent fence opportunities, but treachery is common |
| Las Vegas | 2 | 4 | 3 | 3 | `Soft Town`, `Fence Town` | strong trade and vice make fences easy enough to find |

#### Doing Business in a Dangerous Town

When an outlaw character does business in a town—buying supplies, moving goods, consulting a doctor, or sitting in a saloon—roll `PERFORMIN'` to stay hidden, or `PRESENCE` if brazening it out. Apply the following modifiers:

| Condition | Modifier |
| --- | --- |
| Town has `Soft Town` tag | `+1 die` |
| Refuge with this town is `+1` or better | `+1 die` |
| Disguise or convincing false name in play | `+1 die` |
| Refuge with this town is `-1` or worse | `-1 die` |
| Wanted is Regional | `-1 die` |
| Wanted is Federal | `-2 dice` |
| Town has `Sheriff’s Town` tag | `-1 die` |

**0 successes:** Someone recognizes the gang or draws the wrong conclusion. The law is notified or a reward poster is pulled from a drawer and studied. Reduce Refuge with this town by 1 and test Cohesion if the business was critical.

**1 success (limited):** Business done, but not cleanly. Someone noticed. The town grows warier. If Refuge is `0` or less, reduce it by 1.

**2 successes (full):** In and out without incident.

**3 successes or more:** Business done and something useful learned or gained—a rumor about a posse’s movements, a contact who might serve as a fence, a piece of intelligence about an upcoming score, or a stranger who nods instead of staring.

#### Seasonal Town Interaction

At each `Turn of the Season`, the gang should answer these questions for any settlement important to their survival:

- Did the gang strengthen or damage Refuge here this season?
- Did events in town—a marshal passing through, a booming market, a crop failure, a political shift—change what this town can or will offer?
- Did the gang invest money, favors, or fear to keep one place open to them?

If the gang invested money or effort to maintain a good relationship, no roll is needed—just note it. If they left a mess behind, killed someone local, or cheated a merchant, reduce Refuge by 1 without a roll.

### Band Procedures

When one outlaw acts alone, use the ordinary rules. When the gang's fate turns on whether the whole outfit can move, hold together, disappear, recruit, or profit as a body, use a band procedure instead.

Choose one player character or notable member as the acting hand for the moment. That is the one making the roll. Then ask which part of the gang's condition truly matters here: Cohesion, Refuge, Fence Access, Horsestock, or Wanted pressure. Do not pile on every possible factor. Pick the one or two that make this moment dangerous and real.

#### Band Action Checks

Use this sequence whenever the gang is acting as a group rather than as a handful of separate characters:

1. State the gang's aim.
2. Choose the acting hand and the most relevant roll.
3. Apply the most important band modifier or two.
4. Roll and read the result against the whole gang, not just the one rider who threw the dice.

The following are good defaults:

| Gang Aim | Usual Roll | Most Important Band Factor |
| --- | --- | --- |
| steady the camp after fear, loss, or bad judgment | Leadership roll | Cohesion |
| recruit new hands | `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` | Cohesion and Wanted |
| get shelter, food, warning, or silence from a settlement | `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` | Refuge |
| move Hot Goods or Hot Stock | `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` | Fence Access |
| stay ahead of pursuers over rough country | `NATURE`, `HAWKEYE`, or `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` | Horsestock |
| move camp quietly | `NATURE` or `HAWKEYE` | Horsestock and Wanted |
| lie low for a season without being smoked out | `HAWKEYE` or `PERFORMIN'` | Refuge and Wanted |

The band check list should not be trapped in two or three social rolls. Use the gang's full toolkit whenever the fiction points there:

| Gang Aim | Usual Roll | Most Important Band Factor |
| --- | --- | --- |
| crack a safe, lockbox, or barred door | `LIGHT-FINGERED` or `MAKIN'` | Fence Access and Wanted |
| move heavy cargo, wagons, or siege-like equipment | `LABOR` or `OPERATE` | Crew size and Hideout |
| keep wounded hands alive through a bad stretch | `DOCTORIN'` | Cohesion and Hideout |
| hold together in a fight or riot | `FIGHTIN'` or `SHOOTIN'` | Cohesion and Notable Member Loyalty |
| read a town, notice a trap, or judge a liar | `INSIGHT` or `HAWKEYE` | Refuge and Wanted |
| find water, forage, or safe country | `NATURE` | Horsestock and Hideout |
| alter brands, forge papers, or build false evidence | `MAKIN'` or `BOOKLEARNIN'` | Fence Access and Wanted |
| keep the camp fed, counted, and moving | `LABOR` or `OPERATE` | Cohesion and Hideout |
| raise a false face in town or in camp | `PERFORMIN'` | Refuge and Wanted |
| hold a crowd or make a public appeal | `PRESENCE` | Refuge, Cohesion, and Wanted |
| call remounts, drive stock, or manage a herd | `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` | Horsestock and Fence Access |

When a band property modifies a check, use the following unless a more specific rule in this chapter replaces it:

| Band Factor | Modifier |
| --- | --- |
| Cohesion 5 | `+1 die` |
| Cohesion 4-3 | no modifier |
| Cohesion 2 | `-1 die` |
| Cohesion 1 | `-2 dice` |
| Horsestock 4 | `+1 die` |
| Horsestock 3 | `+1 die` |
| Horsestock 2 | no modifier |
| Horsestock 1 | `-1 die` |
| Horsestock 0 | `-2 dice` |
| Wanted Local | no modifier |
| Wanted Regional | `-1 die` where recognition, pursuit, or local fear matters |
| Wanted Federal | `-2 dice` where recognition, pursuit, or local fear matters |

Use the result in the same spirit throughout the chapter:

**0 successes:** The gang fails and pays for it. A property drops, heat rises, time is lost, or Gang Trouble follows.

**1 success (limited):** The gang gets what it wanted, but not cleanly. Pay in time, money, horses, goodwill, or safety.

**2 successes (full):** The gang gets the result sought without further cost.

**3 successes or more:** The gang gets the result and something extra: better terms, a new contact, lost ground recovered, or pressure shifted onto someone else.

#### Abilities in Band Play

Do not let band play collapse into two social rolls and a chase roll. A gang is a machine with many moving parts, and almost every ability can matter if the fiction supports it.

| Ability | Band Use |
| --- | --- |
| `FIGHTIN'` | overawing mutineers in camp, leading a brutal breach, or breaking a wavering line in a gang fight |
| `LABOR` | digging hides, throwing up rough works, moving heavy loot, building blinds, or improving a hideout |
| `PRESENCE` | leadership, intimidation, bargaining, recruitment, and managing frightened or angry hands |
| `RESILIENCE` | enduring hunger, cold rides, dust, thirst, and a season of hard country without the camp going weak |
| `LIGHT-FINGERED` | picking locks, palming keys, lifting papers, or slipping one rider into a guarded place for the gang |
| `OPERATE` | climbing roofs, crossing yards, handling wagons or stage teams, using machinery, crossing rivers, or keeping a score moving on time |
| `SHOOTIN'` | covering fire, planned ambushes, and score scenes where the gang's marksmanship decides whether the plan holds |
| `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` | maintaining horses, driving stolen stock, calming remounts, and keeping the gang mounted in bad country |
| `HAWKEYE` | spotting tails, watching roads, finding good ground, and detecting a trap before the gang rides into it |
| `INSIGHT` | judging whether a contact is lying, whether a town is ready to turn, or whether the gang itself is about to crack |
| `NATURE` | route finding, forage, water, weather sense, concealment, and life in rough country |
| `BOOKLEARNIN'` | reading manifests, timetables, warrants, deeds, coded ledgers, and court papers tied to a score |
| `DOCTORIN'` | saving wounded hands, keeping disease out of camp, and preserving the gang after a bad fight |
| `MAKIN'` | altering brands, fixing tack, shaping false evidence, repairing weapons, or improving the hideout |
| `PERFORMIN'` | false names, public confidence, smooth lies, tavern charm, and gang leadership by talk rather than fear |

> *On one score Mexican Joe opened the way with `HAWKEYE`, spotting the deputy's lantern before it topped the ridge. Jeb followed with `PERFORMIN'` to sell a lie to the freight clerk, and Charley used `LIGHT-FINGERED` to lift the key instead of blowing the office door. That is the point of band play. The gang does not solve every problem with one silver-tongued outlaw. Different hands make different checks, and the whole band lives or dies by the combined result.*

> *Another morning the camp had three opinions and one fire. Luther wanted to move south, Charley wanted to hang the prisoner they had taken, and old Mrs. Vale wanted breakfast before foolishness. Jeb rolled the leadership check with `PRESENCE` and got `3` successes. He not only kept the camp from splitting, he restored `1` Loyalty to Luther by giving him first say on the next scouting ride. That is what a strong result should do: it does not just prevent disaster, it wins back ground that was already slipping.*

### Recruitment, Notable Members, and Loyalty

Most gangs are held together by a hard core of known hands and a shifting fringe of drifters, cousins, camp women, old soldiers, horse thieves, and fools with more nerve than judgment.

#### Recruiting New Hands

To recruit new outlaws, the player characters need three things:

- Somewhere rough enough to find them
- Money, promises, or protection to offer
- A reason for folk to throw in with them

Most ordinary hands want an advance, a horse to ride, a fair share, or some assurance the gang knows what it is doing. The leader makes a `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` roll when trying to gather new hands. The GM should judge the number and quality of recruits by the result, the local conditions, and the gang's Fame and Reputation.

Recruiting is easier:

- in boomtowns, mining camps, border settlements, and rough cattle country
- after a famous score
- when the gang is known for paying fair

Recruiting is harder:

- where the Law aspect is high and strangers are watched
- when the gang is hungry, hunted, or known for betrayal
- after a recent disaster

Apply `+1 die` if Cohesion is 4 or 5, `-1 die` if Cohesion is 2, and `-2 dice` if Cohesion is 1. Apply `-1 die` for Wanted Regional or `-2 dice` for Wanted Federal in settled country where posters and rumor travel faster than horses.

> *Mexican Joe tried to recruit in a mining camp while the gang was Wanted `Regional`, so he took `-1 die` before he even opened his mouth. He rolled `PERFORMIN'` and got `0` successes. By midnight he had one drunk, one fool, and one man who asked too careful a set of questions about where the gang was camped. That is a proper recruiting failure: not merely disappointment, but fresh danger wearing the shape of help.*

#### Recruiting Results

**0 successes:** Nobody worth having comes in. At best the gang attracts one fool, one drunk, or one informer. If the gang is visibly desperate, test Cohesion.

**1 success (limited):** One or two ordinary hands join, but they want an advance, a horse, or immediate proof the gang can feed them. If the gang cannot provide it, one rides off before the season ends.

**2 successes (full):** The gang recruits enough ordinary hands to matter for the local circumstances. In most places that means one to three steady riders, or a single specialist's recommendation that opens the way to more later.

**3 successes or more:** The gang recruits well. Gain several likely hands for the region, or one especially useful notable member prospect. If the gang is known for fair dealing, consider raising Cohesion by 1 after the next profitable split.

Ordinary gang hands should usually remain background figures. Name them when needed, but do not build every rider as a full character.

#### Notable Gang Members

Some outlaws matter more than the rest. They might be the leader's brother, the best scout in camp, the old safecracker who knows every railroad clerk between Wichita and Cheyenne, or the mad dog who keeps starting trouble in saloons. These are Notable Gang Members.

Build them using the existing `compadre` rules from Chapter 6, then add the following:

| Trait | What to Add | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Role | scout, second gun, safecracker, horse wrangler, fence, killer, lover, preacher, camp cook, and so on | This is the job they are known for when the gang needs them to matter. |
| Experience | Give them `+1` ability point and one extra talent beyond an ordinary compadre, or `+2` ability points for a harder veteran, whichever better fits the fiction. | They should feel one step sharper than a normal hand. |
| Loyalty | `1-3` | See the Loyalty rules below. |
| Ambition | What they want from the gang | This is what keeps them in play after the first score is over. |
| Breaking Point | The one thing they will not forgive | Striking this should hurt Loyalty immediately. |
| Leverage | One concrete thing they control for the gang, such as the remuda, a fence contact, the cook fire, the dynamite, or the route to a safe town | This gives the GM a handle for pressure, aid, and betrayal. |

Only two to five such people should be tracked in detail at once. More than that is bookkeeping, not drama.

#### Loyalty

Each Notable Gang Member has a Loyalty rating from 1 to 3.

| Loyalty | Meaning | Mechanical Effect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 3 | They are personally invested in the leader, the gang, or the gang's cause. | They give `+1 die` when their role directly helps a band check. Once per season they may also soak one Loyalty loss meant for another notable member by stepping in. |
| 2 | They are reliable so long as the gang treats them fair and things are going tolerably well. | No modifier. They help as expected. |
| 1 | They are halfway out the door already. One hard slight, one missed share, or one ugly season may lose them. | They give no bonus dice unless they were just paid fair, reassured, or their Ambition is in sight. After any failed Cohesion test, the GM may immediately bring their grievance forward. |
| 0 | They are gone, turned, or openly disloyal. They no longer count as a loyal notable member. | Treat them as absent, mutinous, or secretly opposed. |

Increase Loyalty by 1 when:

- the leader treats the notable member fair in front of the gang
- the leader helps them pursue their ambition
- the gang risks something real to save them

Reduce Loyalty by 1 when:

- the leader cheats or humiliates them
- their breaking point is struck
- the gang falls to Cohesion 1

At Loyalty 1, the next serious slight or failure should send them away, turn them mutinous, or push them into betrayal.

> *Luther Vale had Loyalty `3`, so when the gang tried to force stolen horses through a storm-swollen crossing his help gave Mexican Joe `+1 die` on the `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` roll. The dice came up strong and the remuda got through with only one lost mule. A month later Jeb humiliated Luther in front of hired hands and knocked that Loyalty down to `2`. Same man, same skill, but the next time trouble came he was only helping because the gang still happened to be his best bad option.*

#### Common Roles for Notable Gang Members

| Role | Useful Ability | What They Are Good For |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Scout | `HAWKEYE` or `NATURE` | Tracking, watching roads, finding safe country, and spotting posses first |
| Second Gun | `SHOOTIN'` or `FIGHTIN'` | Backing the leader in a fight and overawing the rest of camp |
| Safecracker or Burglar | `LIGHT-FINGERED` or `MAKIN'` | Getting through locks, boxes, safes, and barred windows |
| Wrangler | `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` | Keeping the horses fit, hidden, and ready to move |
| Fence or Trader | `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` | Turning stolen goods into cash and finding buyers who ask few questions |
| Camp Doctor | `DOCTORIN'` | Keeping wounded hands alive after a bad score |
| Camp Cook | `LABOR` or `DOCTORIN'` | Feeding the gang, keeping order in camp, and hearing everything |
| Dynamiter | `MAKIN'` or `OPERATE` | Blasting safes, rail lines, gates, and rockfalls without killing the whole gang by accident |
| Drover | `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` or `LABOR` | Moving Hot Stock, cutting out cattle, and holding a herd together under pressure |
| Forger or Clerk | `BOOKLEARNIN'` or `PERFORMIN'` | False papers, timetables, land records, freight notes, and forged signatures |
| Preacher or Talker | `PERFORMIN'` or `PRESENCE` | Calming camp, stirring crowds, or giving the gang a respectable face in town |
| Trail Boss | `NATURE` or `PRESENCE` | Organizing movement, watches, camp order, and rough discipline on the march |
| Quartermaster | `OPERATE` or `LABOR` | Counting stores, buying supplies, and making sure the gang does not run out of rope, cartridges, or coffee |
| Livery Friend | `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` or `PRESENCE` | Access to stables, shoeing, feed, and replacement mounts |
| Courtesan or Cardsharp | `PERFORMIN'` or `INSIGHT` | Reading saloons, gathering rumor, finding weak marks, and hearing who is flush or frightened |

### Shares, Cash, and Hideout Upkeep

Outlaws do not get wages in the usual way. They live by the take. When the take is good, spirits rise and the whiskey runs. When it is poor, men begin to count what they are owed and whose fault it is.

#### Splitting the Take

There is no single honest way to split stolen money. Every gang does it differently, and every gang thinks its own way is common sense. As a good default, use the following:

- Each ordinary gang hand gets `1 share`
- Each player character gets `1 share`
- Each notable member gets `2 shares`
- The leader gets `3 shares`

This split should be agreed before the job. If the player characters change it afterward in their own favor, test Gang Cohesion.

Stolen stock, jewelry, bonds, silverware, church plate, or railroad goods are not cash until fenced. Until then they are trouble wrapped in canvas.

#### Loot Categories

Not all take converts to cash the same way. Three categories cover most of what outlaws steal:

| Category | Examples | How It Converts |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `Clean Cash` | Coin, notes, payroll money pulled directly from a box | Spendable at once. No fence required. |
| `Hot Goods` | Jewelry, guns, whiskey, watches, express packages, negotiable bonds | Requires `Fence Access 1` or better to move at reasonable value. Without a fence, sells at a fraction of value or raises heat with anyone watching. |
| `Hot Stock` | Cattle, horses, mules | Requires `Fence Access 2` or special local conditions—an altered brand, a trusted drover, a territory where no one is asking. The longer it sits, the more it talks. |
| `Captives` | Witnesses, prisoners, hostages, rescued kin, kidnapped rivals | Not spent like money. They are leverage, burden, and risk. They require food, watch, and a decision. |

Most scores will produce a mix. A bank vault yields Clean Cash. A stage hold-up is mostly Hot Goods with some cash if the passengers have coin. A cattle raid is entirely Hot Stock. The gang that steals cattle has a resource and a problem in the same animal.

Non-liquid goods should not be hand-waved into dollars the same night:

- `Hot Goods` left in camp for a week or more increase the chance of heat, betrayal, or foolish private selling by desperate hands
- `Hot Stock` must be fed, watered, hidden, or moved; if held through a season they create a fresh problem even if no lawman appears
- unusually bulky loot like safes, crates, church silver, machinery parts, or heavy paper ledgers may require `LABOR`, `OPERATE`, or `MAKIN'` checks before they can even be moved or opened

#### Handling Hot Stock and Awkward Loot

When the gang keeps non-liquid loot rather than cashing it out at once, choose one pressure it creates:

- it ties down riders who must guard or move it
- it costs feed, time, or storage
- it risks recognition if shown in the wrong town
- it gives somebody in camp a temptation to skim, steal, or talk

At the next `Turn of the Season`, any unresolved `Hot Stock` or awkward `Hot Goods` force a choice: sell them, move them, consume them, or suffer a consequence chosen by the GM such as reduced Horsestock, reduced Refuge, a Wanted rise, or Gang Trouble.

#### Captives

Captives are not a side note. They are what turns a score into a moral and practical problem.

When the gang takes captives, choose one of the following:

- `Ransom` them if the gang has a buyer and a place to make the exchange
- `Recruit` them if the captive has a reason to switch sides and the gang can support that tale
- `Trade` them if the captive can buy access, information, or another prisoner
- `Hold` them if the gang needs leverage and can spare a guard
- `Release` them if mercy, heat, or reputation matters more than leverage
- `Silence` them only if the campaign is dark enough to carry that weight

Keeping a captive under guard requires one rider or notable member's attention unless the fiction says otherwise. A captive held through a season counts as a pressure. At the next `Turn of the Season`, if they have not been resolved, reduce Cohesion or raise Wanted, whichever better fits the fiction, and let the GM choose whether the captive becomes a liability, a witness, or a bargaining chip.

**0 successes:** The captive escapes, talks, or causes trouble at the worst possible moment.

**1 success (limited):** The captive stays put or comes to terms, but only under tight watch, visible fear, or a debt the gang now owes.

**2 successes (full):** The captive is resolved cleanly for now: exchanged, recruited, hidden, or released without immediate blowback.

**3 successes or more:** The captive becomes an asset instead of a burden. They talk, turn, or pay off in a way that gives the gang information, leverage, or a useful ally.

> *After the stage hold-up the gang kept a cattle buyer and his nephew as captives, meaning to trade them for safe passage. Jeb rolled to manage the matter and got `0` successes. The boy slipped his rope at dawn, ran barefoot into the mesquite, and reached town half dead with enough of the story left in him to raise Wanted by one step. Captives should do that when the dice sour. They turn one bad decision into a longer, uglier consequence.*

#### Turning Hot Goods to Cash

When the gang wants to move Hot Goods or Hot Stock through a fence, the character handling the deal makes a `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` roll. Apply the following modifiers:

| Condition | Modifier |
| --- | --- |
| Fence Access is 2 | `+1 die` |
| Refuge with the fence’s town is `+1` or higher | `+1 die` |
| Refuge with the fence’s town is `-1` or lower | `-1 die` |
| The goods are clearly connected to a recent notorious crime | `-1 die` |
| Selling Hot Stock with only Fence Access 1 | `-2 dice` |

**0 successes:** The deal falls through. The fence is nervous, cuts contact, or refuses this batch. Hot Goods stay Hot. If the gang presses, the fence’s fear makes them a liability—they may talk, drift away, or cost the gang Fence Access.

**1 success (limited):** The fence takes the goods but drives a hard bargain. Receive half the fair value, or receive full value with a delay of weeks that risks a Wanted escalation.

**2 successes (full):** Clean deal at the fence’s usual rate. Receive fair value after the fence’s cut.

**3 successes or more:** The fence has a ready buyer or unusually good terms. Receive better-than-fair value and learn something useful—law movements, an upcoming opportunity, or a contact worth having.

If the gang has no fence (`Fence Access 0`), they may still try to sell Hot Goods openly, but the roll becomes `PERFORMIN'` at `-2 dice`, and even a limited success generates heat.

#### Finding a New Fence

When the gang has lost its fence or wants to build a better one, this is not just a shopping errand. It is a criminal courtship. The acting character makes a `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` roll in a town or camp that could plausibly support such business.

Apply the following modifiers:

| Condition | Modifier |
| --- | --- |
| Town has `Fence Town` tag | `+1 die` |
| Refuge with the place is `+1` or better | `+1 die` |
| Refuge with the place is `-1` or worse | `-1 die` |
| Wanted is Federal | `-1 die` |
| The gang recently burned or exposed a fence | `-1 die` |

**0 successes:** No one trustworthy will deal. Worse, questions begin. Wanted may rise or Refuge may fall if the approach was clumsy.

**1 success (limited):** A petty buyer or nervous intermediary can be found. Gain `Fence Access 1` for this place, but the relationship is weak and expensive.

**2 successes (full):** The gang establishes a working fence on decent terms. Gain `Fence Access 1`, or restore a lost `Fence Access 1` cleanly.

**3 successes or more:** The gang finds stronger criminal trade than expected. Gain `Fence Access 2` in the place if that makes sense, or improve an existing petty fence into something broader and safer.

#### Gang Expenses

Outlaw life burns money faster than many imagine. Horses founder. Ammunition is spent. Whiskey disappears. Hungry men grumble. A hideout must be provisioned if it is to remain a refuge and not turn into a freezing camp in the rocks.

At the end of each `Turn of the Season`, the gang must pay the following hideout and upkeep costs:

| Scale | Seasonal Upkeep |
| --- | --- |
| Crew | `$60` |
| Gang | `$150` |
| Outfit | `$300` |

These figures assume a western gang living mostly off rough country and stolen opportunities, not paying full town prices for every bite. Even so, the costs are real. Contemporary prices put an average work horse around `$150`, a good saddle horse around `$200`, and a saddle around `$30`; horse feed and shoeing eat money even when pasture does part of the work. The upkeep figure therefore covers the part the gang cannot steal, graze, or improvise away: food stores, grain, lamp oil, spare tack, shoeing, ammunition, bribes to informants, and the ordinary waste of outlaw life.

If the gang cannot or will not pay it, reduce Cohesion by 1 and the GM should choose an appropriate shortage: hungry horses, bad powder, a leak in the hideout roof, a fence refusing further credit, or something of that kind.

#### The Hideout

A hideout is not a town and should not be treated like one. It does not use Settlement Points and does not grow by adding amenities every season. It is a place to lie low, hide stolen goods, doctor the wounded, and survive until the next score.

A basic hideout should usually have the following:

- One strong advantage: concealed canyon, hard approach, spring water, a nearby safe town, caves, ruined buildings, thick timber, and so on
- One weakness: poor grazing, bad water, narrow escape route, nearby settlers, smoke visible from a distance, snakes, floods, and so on

If the gang already has the concealed hideout from the Outlaws Group Concept, use that as the starting point and add its local details.

#### Hideout Rating

Track the gang's hideout on a scale from 1 to 5.

| Hideout | Name | What It Means | Seasonal Maintenance |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Bolt-Hole | A cave, shack, wash, or abandoned line camp. Good for days, not seasons. Enemies need `1` success to locate it once they are truly looking. | `$5` |
| 2 | Camp | A serviceable outlaw roost with some stores and a planned escape path. Enemies need `2` successes to locate it. | `$15` |
| 3 | Strong Hideout | A proper refuge with caches, good lookouts, and a sensible approach. Enemies need `3` successes to locate it. | `$35` |
| 4 | Hidden Redoubt | Hard country, false trails, layered caches, and real thought behind the place. Enemies need `4` successes to locate it. | `$70` |
| 5 | Legendary Refuge | The sort of hideout that lives in whispers: hidden water, trusted locals, deep country, and prepared exits. Enemies need `5` successes to locate it. | `$120` |

When enemies investigate, track their progress as accumulated successes from scouting, questioning, trailing, or betrayal until they reach the hideout's rating.

#### Improving the Hideout

Raising a hideout's rating by 1 usually costs one season of work, suitable country, and money:

| New Rating | Typical Cost |
| --- | --- |
| 2 | `$20` |
| 3 | `$60` |
| 4 | `$125` |
| 5 | `$250` |

Improvement should also require at least one fitting action: `LABOR` to build, `MAKIN'` to reinforce, `NATURE` to choose better ground, `OPERATE` to move stores, or `PERFORMIN'` and `PRESENCE` to secure local silence. A hideout can also be improved by story rather than cash alone, if the gang gains caves, a ranch ally, or a hidden spring through play.

### Scores and Criminal Work

A score is any planned criminal undertaking from which the gang expects profit. Some are great affairs requiring scouting, false identities, and a hard ride across three counties. Some are little more than waiting in a draw with rifles and a mean intent.

#### Common Kinds of Score

| Score Type | Common Target | Likely Take | Likely Trouble |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Robbery | Bank, payroll office, express box, gambling room | Cash or negotiable paper | Lawmen, armed guards, witnesses |
| Rustling | Cattle or horse herd | Livestock to fence or drive away | Ranch hands, trackers, brand inspectors |
| Stage or Rail Hold-Up | Stagecoach, baggage car, payroll train | Cash, valuables, mail, bonds | Company guards, posses, federal attention |
| Prison Break | Jail, prison wagon, courthouse | No direct profit, but allies or leverage | Sheriffs, marshals, heavy heat |
| Unlawful Escort | Stolen goods, guns, whiskey, wanted folk | Cash payment from a patron | Ambush, betrayal, seizure by the law |
| Hired Intimidation | Rancher, merchant, witness, juror, union man | Cash payment and future favors | Moral rot, heat, retaliation |
| Revenge Job | One man or one family | Whatever can be looted afterward | Blood feud, witnesses, pointless killing |
| Counter-Bounty Work | Witnesses, posters, informants, bounty hunters | Protection of the gang more than money | Escalating law pressure |
| Protection Racket | Shops, miners, saloons, gamblers | Steady small income | Civic anger, rival gangs, the sheriff |

#### Building a Score

Every score should answer six questions:

- Who wants it done, if anyone?
- What is the target?
- What is the likely take?
- Who stands in the way?
- What heat follows if it goes badly?
- What fresh trouble follows even if it goes well?

The GM can build scores by answering those questions directly or by rolling on the table below.

#### Running a Score as an Operation

Many scores should not be reduced to one roll and a shrug. Run them as an operation made up of linked situations. The GM presents the next immediate problem. The players say how the gang means to handle it. One acting hand rolls for the band using the most relevant ability. Successes are accumulated toward the job's completion, while Trouble marks the job's slide toward disaster.

Use the following operation scales:

| Operation | Progress Needed | Trouble Limit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Small Hit | `4` | `3` |
| Medium Heist | `6` | `4` |
| Big Operation | `8` | `5` |

Each successful roll adds its successes to the operation's Progress. The score succeeds once the Progress target is met.

Before each operation check, the GM says whether the attempt is:

- `Safe`
- `Risky`
- `Desperate`

The players then roll the normal pool only. No Trouble Dice are added to the roll.

Mark Trouble like this:

- `Safe`: No Trouble from the roll itself unless the gang chooses noise, blood, speed, or some other plainly dangerous trade.
- `Risky`: On `0` successes, mark `1` Trouble. Then count the `1`s rolled; for every two `1`s, mark `1` additional Trouble.
- `Desperate`: On `0` successes, mark `1` Trouble. Then count the `1`s rolled; each `1` marks `1` additional Trouble.
- If a pushed check still fails, mark `1` additional Trouble.

Successful `Risky` and `Desperate` rolls usually ignore `1`s unless the fiction says the gang got what it wanted in a loud, bloody, or compromising way.

Use Trouble as a rising pressure track:

| Trouble | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| `1` | minor alarm, delay, a wounded hand, or a lost opportunity |
| `2` | somebody notices, a witness survives, or the gang's path narrows |
| `3` | Wanted rises, Horsestock suffers, or the law closes a road or town |
| `4+` | the score is turning into a chase, fight, split, or disaster even if profit is still possible |

When Trouble reaches the operation's limit, the job has failed in its intended form. The gang may still escape, fight clear, snatch a smaller gain, or turn the scene into a desperate flight, but the clean plan is dead.

Different stages of an operation should call on different abilities. A bank job might use `HAWKEYE` to watch the street, `PERFORMIN'` for a false identity, `LIGHT-FINGERED` for a lock, `OPERATE` to handle a wagon escape, and `SHOOTIN'` only when everything goes bad. A cattle raid might use `NATURE`, `ANIMAL HANDLIN'`, `OPERATE`, `LABOR`, and then `PRESENCE` to keep the hands from scattering when the first rifle cracks in the dark.

These targets are calibrated so a normal operation usually takes about 3 to 6 checks. A Small Hit should feel like a fast caper, a Medium Heist like a rough evening or a long day, and a Big Operation like a real campaign event. If a plan is likely to need more checks than that, split it into phases or let the job become two linked operations. That keeps Trouble meaningful instead of turning every score into a slow grind. In practice, a competent gang should expect Trouble to matter before the work is finished, not after it is already safe.

Use `Safe` when failure would be inconvenient but not immediately compromising. Use `Risky` when discovery, delay, suspicion, or narrowing options are plausible. Use `Desperate` when the gang is forcing the issue under pressure and any ugly turn could break the shape of the operation.

> *The first check got them into the bank yard. The second, a `Risky` `PERFORMIN'` roll, got the clerk to open the inner door. On the third, Charley failed the `LIGHT-FINGERED` roll on the safe and rolled three `1`s, so Trouble climbed to `2`. They pushed on anyway and still failed, which raised Trouble to `3`. Jeb then got `2` successes on `PRESENCE` to hold the room together and the gang reached its Progress target just before Trouble hit the limit. That is the shape you want: a score that still succeeds, but only after the dice have made the players feel the walls closing in.*

#### Hidden Bad Luck

Sometimes a job goes sour not because of one bad roll, but because the gang made several bad decisions and luck finally came to collect. This is a hidden GM-side check, not another thing for the players to manage.

Consider a hidden bad-luck check only when at least two of the following are true in the same operation or immediate aftermath:

- the gang already chose noise over caution
- the gang already chose speed over concealment
- the gang left a witness, captive, or wounded man unresolved
- the gang pushed a key check and failed
- the gang kept working after Trouble had already become serious
- the gang trusted a weak contact or shaky refuge anyway

If that pattern is there, the GM may introduce a hard delayed consequence: the wrong deputy is warned, a horse throws a shoe at the wrong hour, a captive talks too soon, a posse cuts the gang off at water, or a nervous fence decides to save his own neck. This should feel like compounded outlaw misjudgment catching up with the gang, not random punishment from nowhere.

#### After the Score

When a score is over, do not just count dollars and move on. Resolve the band consequences while the dust is still in the air.

Ask the following questions:

- Was the take `Clean Cash`, `Hot Goods`, `Hot Stock`, or a mix?
- Were shares paid fairly?
- Did the gang leave witnesses, kill someone important, or make a public spectacle?
- Did the gang lose horses, suffer wounds, or ride hard enough to cost Horsestock?
- Did the score strengthen or poison Refuge with any nearby settlement?

Then apply the obvious outcomes at once:

- test Cohesion if shares were disputed, captives abandoned, or casualties felt pointless
- raise Wanted if the score was public, bloody, or aimed at a railroad, bank, mail route, or federal interest
- reduce Horsestock if the gang lost mounts, outran a posse, or drove stock too hard
- reduce Refuge if the gang made a mess in a place they depend on
- make a fence roll if the take is still hot and the gang tries to move it quickly

If the players are unsure whether the gang has truly escaped, make a `Hard Ride and Flight` roll immediately instead of hand-waving the road between score and hideout.

| 36 | The gang hears of children being moved west by a so-called orphan train. One of the wagons also carries deed papers, bond certificates, and a hard-eyed matron with a pistol in her carpetbag. |
| 41 | A judge carries bribe money home each month in a plain valise. Everybody in town knows it and nobody has yet dared touch him. |
| 42 | A rival gang stole your fence, your buyers, and one of your women or one of your boys. The score is a raid on their own hideout. |
| 43 | A bank job is made possible by a clerk inside, but the clerk wants out afterward and has a family to move somewhere safe. |
| 44 | A county fair draws purses, cattle buyers, gamblers, and drunks. It also draws law from three towns. |
| 45 | A prospector comes to camp with the location of a rich strike and a bullet in his side. Men are already hunting him. |
| 46 | A gang member's kin is in debt bondage to a brutal outfit owner. Buying them free would cost less than stealing them free, but outlaw pride seldom chooses the cheaper road. |
| 51 | A posse is camped careless and complacent. Their horses, ammunition, and prisoner wagon are there for the taking if the gang dares strike first. |
| 52 | A Pinkerton man is in town under another name, asking questions. He carries notes that would be worth more than cash if the gang knew who was buying information on them. |
| 53 | A railroad bridge crew is being paid in cash and whiskey before a holiday. Half the crew will be drunk by midnight. |
| 54 | A famous gambler wants safe conduct to a riverboat, but the men he cheated on the way west are finally close behind. |
| 55 | A county land office is full of claims, maps, seals, and records. A clever theft there could make a poor man's holding vanish and a rich man's claim appear lawful. |
| 56 | A captured gang member is to be hanged in three days. They know where money is buried, but might not be alive long enough to tell it. |
| 61 | A military payroll is rumored to be light on guards because the commanding officer thinks nobody would dare. He is half right. |
| 62 | A dry-gulch artist in the gang has struck an arrangement with a saloon madam to drug a man carrying railroad bonds. Now the man has arrived with three partners instead of one. |
| 63 | A fire in town forces the bank, telegraph office, and assay office to shift their money and papers in confusion. Opportunity rides beside panic. |
| 64 | A notorious outlaw offers to ride with your gang on one score only. If the job succeeds, your name grows. If it fails, the law will think it was your idea all along. |
| 65 | A rich rancher's son joins the gang for the thrill of it and knows where his father keeps the strongbox. He does not know hardship and may break the first time blood is spilled. |
| 66 | A former member of the gang returns from prison with a map, a grudge, and a claim that the old leader hid enough gold to buy a county. |

### Wanted Men

Every outlaw hopes to be feared. Few understand what fear costs once it settles on their name. A price on your head brings fools, professionals, and the law itself.

#### Wanted Status

Wanted status should be tracked in three rough tiers:

| Wanted Level | Who Wants You | What It Means |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Local | One town, one county, one sheriff, one ranching concern | Posters, posses, local bounty hunters, and folk willing to point you out for reward money |
| Regional | Several counties, a railroad, a territory, or multiple sheriffs | Professional trackers, organized posses, rail agents, private detectives, and hard travel for anyone recognized |
| Federal | Marshals, Army interest, multiple territories, major rail or bank concern | No easy sanctuary, strong witnesses hunted, and real pressure on fences, kin, and friendly towns |

Wanted status rises when:

- the gang leaves witnesses
- a lawman, judge, railroad man, or notable citizen is killed
- the score strikes at a railroad, bank, mail route, or federal business
- the gang keeps working the same region instead of moving on

Wanted status can fall if:

- the gang lies low for a whole season
- they leave the region entirely
- a scapegoat is taken, whether justly or not
- the gang bribes, bargains, or quietly kills the right people

#### Example Bounties

These are useful starting numbers for wanted men in the Old West. The GM should adjust them for the size of the crime, the power of the injured party, and whether the local law can afford to pay.

| Wanted Level | Typical Bounty | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Local | `$25-$200` | horse thief, stage hold-up man, town bully, or a one-county troublemaker |
| Regional | `$200-$1,000` | repeat train robber, stock thief, marshal shooter, or well-known gang rider |
| Federal | `$1,000-$5,000+` | gang leader, railroad murderer, prison breaker, or a man wanted across territories |

If the crime hit a family or a local institution, the reward may be lower but the pursuit more personal. If it hit a railroad, bank, or federal office, the reward may be higher and the hunt much broader.

> *When the railroad posted `$750` for Jeb Collier, the number did more work than a dozen deputies. The next time Mexican Joe went into town for wire news, every stranger's stare counted. The GM applied the `Wanted Regional` penalty, Joe missed the roll, and the barber who had trimmed him twice before walked out the back door to fetch a deputy. That is what bounty money means in play. It turns ordinary business into a risk roll and makes neutral faces worth fearing.*

#### Wanted and Reputation

Wanted status does not replace `Fame` and `Reputation`. It sharpens them. When the gang's crimes become widely known, the GM may award Fame as normal. Just remember that Fame earned this way is dangerous Fame. It may make rough folk respect you, but it will also sour Reputation among decent townsfolk, raise the cost of doing business, and make every stranger's stare worth noticing.

#### Who Is Chasing You

Different pursuers work in different ways. A county sheriff and a railroad detective are both trouble, but they are not the same trouble.

| Pursuer | How They Pressure the Gang |
| --- | --- |
| Sheriff and Posse | Quick local response. Neighbors and townsmen. Short pursuit that fades after two hard days in rough country if no blood was spilled. |
| Ranch Outfit or Range Detectives | Know the stock, the brands, and the trails. Best at tracing stolen animals and tracking across cattle country. May work quietly before acting. |
| Railroad or Express Detectives | Company money and station networks. They travel ahead by telegraph. Stay interested long after a county sheriff has moved on. |
| Federal Marshals | Warrants, persistence, cross-jurisdiction authority. Slower to arrive but harder to lose. A federal warrant does not stop at the county line. |
| Rival Gang | No interest in due process. Will take horses, fence access, hideout location, and heads if the price is right. Can also be bought or bargained with. |

Each pursuer class has different tactics. A ranch outfit confiscates horses and shuts down the local fence. A railroad detective buys informants and watches the wire. A federal marshal crosses county lines. The gang that knows who is chasing them can make better choices about what to give up and where to run.

#### Escalation

| D6 | Who Comes Next? |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | A local sheriff and a few hard-ridden deputies or townsmen |
| 2 | Independent bounty hunters scenting easy money |
| 3 | Ranch hands, rail guards, or hired gunmen paid by the injured party |
| 4 | A neighboring sheriff, territorial men, or a larger posse |
| 5 | A federal marshal, Pinkerton man, or company detective |
| 6 | A rival outlaw gang hoping to sell you out or claim your horses, hideout, or fence |

When the heat rises, the GM need not send these enemies straight at the player characters. Often it is more interesting if they seize horses, question a fence, frighten a compadre, or close a town against the gang instead.

#### Hard Ride and Flight

When the gang must stay ahead of pursuers, cross rough country fast, or drag stolen stock out from under armed attention, one acting hand makes a `NATURE`, `HAWKEYE`, or `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` roll.

Apply the following modifiers:

| Condition | Modifier |
| --- | --- |
| Horsestock is 4 | `+1 die` |
| Horsestock is 3 | `+1 die` |
| Horsestock is 1 | `-1 die` |
| Horsestock is 0 | `-2 dice` |
| The gang knows this country well | `+1 die` |
| The gang is driving `Hot Stock` | `-1 die` |
| The gang includes wounded riders or captives | `-1 die` |
| Pursuers are Federal or professional detectives | `-1 die` |

**0 successes:** The gang is run close, cut off, or forced into a bad choice. Reduce Horsestock by 1 and put a hard scene in front of them: abandon loot, split the gang, leave someone behind, or fight.

**1 success (limited):** The gang stays ahead for now, but pays for it. Reduce Horsestock by 1, or let the gang keep its horses and instead lose time, food, or concealment. The next Refuge or town-business roll is at `-1 die` if the pursuers are still near.

**2 successes (full):** The gang keeps the lead and reaches the next hiding place, water source, or turn in the country without further loss.

**3 successes or more:** The gang does more than escape. It throws the trail off, gains clean distance, or reaches a place of temporary safety before the pursuers know where to look. If the gang now lies low, the GM may reduce Wanted by one step in the current district after the season turns.

If both sides are mounted and the GM wants a sharper contest, compare Horsestock first:

- if one side's Horsestock is 2 or more points higher, that side controls the range unless the dice say otherwise
- if the difference is 1 point, the lower side rolls at `-1 die`
- if the values are equal, only terrain, weather, and riding skill decide it

This is enough for most western chases. Better horses matter before heroics do.

> *Mexican Joe made the `Hard Ride and Flight` roll with Horsestock `1` and took the `-1 die`. He got `1` success. The gang stayed ahead for the night, but only by ruining two more horses and reaching the hideout hungry and half blind with dust. A full success would have bought distance. A limited one bought only another day of fear, which is often all outlaw life gives you.*

### Hideout Life

Most outlaw tales are not all gun smoke and hoofbeats. A gang spends much of its life waiting: cleaning guns, doctoring wounds, arguing over shares, lying still while posses comb the country, or planning the next ugly little miracle.

If outlaw play is the heart of the campaign, then during each `Turn of the Season` the gang may choose one Hideout Activity. This does not replace the usual Town Amenity choice if the player characters still have a town to call home. It is simply the gang's own business for the season.

#### Hideout Activities

| Activity | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| Lie Low | Reduce Wanted by one step in the current region, if the gang commits no loud crime during the season. |
| Drill the Gang | Gain `+1` die on the first Cohesion test of next season. |
| Move Camp | Shift the hideout to a safer or more profitable area, but test Cohesion if weather, distance, or shortages make the move harsh. |
| Scout a Target | Gain `+1` die on the first score of next season if the gang acts on the intelligence gathered. |
| Buy Through Fences | Spend cash to ensure ammunition, horses, and supplies are plentiful; no risk of ordinary seasonal shortage this season. |
| Launder Loot | Turn awkward stolen goods into cleaner cash at a fairer rate than a hasty fence would offer. |
| Patch Up the Wounded | Wounded gang members or notable members recover better; the GM may soften the consequences of an earlier disaster. |
| Settle Internal Trouble | Make an immediate leadership roll; on a success, raise Cohesion by 1 or restore 1 Loyalty to a single notable member. |
| Court Informants | Establish a useful contact in a town, saloon, rail office, sheriff's department, or ranch. |
| Blow Money and Raise Hell | The gang has a wild good time in town. Gain `+1` Cohesion now, but Wanted rises if things get out of hand, which they often do. |

The gang should only gain the benefit of one such activity each season unless the GM judges the campaign has given ample time for more.

#### The Outlaw Season

At each `Turn of the Season`, resolve the gang in the following order:

1. Pay upkeep for the gang's scale.
2. Pay the hideout's own maintenance if it is more than a temporary bolt-hole.
3. Choose one Hideout Activity.
4. Apply obvious changes to `Refuge`, `Fence Access`, `Horsestock`, and `Wanted` from the season just played.
5. Test Cohesion if the season was lean, cruel, unpaid, or divided.
6. Roll or choose Gang Trouble if a failure, shortage, betrayal, or outside pressure calls for it.

Do not force a roll for every line of this. If the answer is obvious, apply it and move on. Roll when the table genuinely does not know whether the gang can hold together, keep moving, or keep a relationship alive.

As a rule of thumb, a season should leave one thing better and one thing worse. A profitable robbery may raise Cohesion while also raising Wanted. A quiet winter spent lying low may reduce Wanted while dropping Horsestock or souring tempers. Outlaw life should feel like pressure redistributed, not pressure erased.

> *At the `Turn of the Season`, the Harper gang paid upkeep, chose `Settle Internal Trouble`, and made the leadership roll because summer had gone lean and ugly. Jeb got `2` successes. Cohesion held, Luther's Loyalty stopped slipping, and the hideout stayed a camp instead of becoming a quarrel with bedrolls. On another season that same sequence could have gone bad and sent one ordinary hand off in the night with a horse, a blanket, and half the gang's patience.*

### Consequences and Collapse

No gang runs clean forever. Sooner or later somebody dies badly, cheats the split, talks under drink, gets taken alive, or rides off with the best horse in camp. When that happens, use the gang's trouble to make more tale, not simply to end the campaign.

#### Gang Trouble

When the rules call for Gang Trouble, roll D66 or choose a result that fits:

| D66 | Trouble |
| --- | --- |
| 11-13 | One ordinary hand deserts in the night, taking a horse, some cartridges, or a little money. |
| 14-16 | Two gang members are feuding openly. Unless the leader steps in, one will soon be dead or gone. |
| 21-23 | A notable member feels cheated and their Loyalty drops by 1. |
| 24-26 | A local fence refuses further dealings until paid, appeased, or protected. |
| 31-33 | Somebody in camp is talking too much in town. Wanted rises one step unless they are stopped. |
| 34-36 | Horses sicken, stray, or are stolen. The gang's next move is slower and more dangerous than planned. |
| 41-43 | One member of the gang has kin trouble, debt, or a private grudge and wants the gang's help right now. |
| 44-46 | A captured or missing former member knows too much about the hideout. |
| 51-53 | A posse, rival gang, or bounty hunter finds sign too close to the hideout for comfort. |
| 54-56 | Whiskey, gambling, jealousy, or boredom turns camp mean. Test Cohesion. |
| 61-63 | A patron wants another job done before the last one has settled. Refusal may cost future work, but acceptance may ruin the gang. |
| 64-66 | Someone in authority offers a dirty bargain: amnesty for one, money for one, and rope for the rest. |

#### Captured Members

If a gang member is captured, the leader must quickly decide whether to rescue them, ransom them, silence them, or abandon them. No choice is clean. Rescuing them may be glorious and stupid. Leaving them may be wise and poison the gang. Either way, test Cohesion if the gang believes the leader has failed one of their own.

#### Gang War

When two gangs want the same hideout country, fence, herd, or line of escape, open war may follow. Do not reduce this to a single fight unless the campaign demands it. Gang war should mean stolen horses, dead informants, ambushes on lonely roads, town folk forced to choose sides, and the fear that one wrong move will bring the law down on everyone.

#### Winter and Hard Times

Outlaws suffer badly in winter. Trails vanish, horses lose weight, wet blankets kill, and every nearby settlement grows suspicious of strangers wanting food and a warm stove. If the gang goes into winter under-supplied, hunted, or divided, test Cohesion and consider adding Gang Trouble even before the first score of the season.

#### When the Gang Breaks

A broken gang need not end the campaign. It may simply change it.

When the gang finally comes apart, one or more of the following may happen:

- half the hands ride off with their share
- a notable member betrays the leader to the law or a rival
- the player characters are forced to flee and build again somewhere new
- the gang splits into rival outfits, each with a grudge and a memory

That is not failure. That is a new chapter in the tale.

### Premade Outlaw Bands

The following gangs may be used as allies, enemies, or examples for a new campaign.

#### The Dead Mule Boys

A family rustling crew from rough cattle country. Mean when cornered, practical the rest of the time. They would rather steal horses than blood a man, but will do either if pushed. Their old mother runs the hidden camp and knows every stolen brand in three counties.

#### The San Marcos Riders

A border gang moving between Texas and the territories, half raiders and half smugglers. They know dry country, hidden springs, and men willing to buy things quietly. They are held together by kinship, old war loyalties, and hatred of the Rangers.

#### The Widow Pike Outfit

A mixed gang built around a hard widow who lost her ranch to debt and fraud. She takes scores against rail men, crooked banks, and land sharks, which makes her sound romantic until you see the bodies she leaves behind when crossed.

#### The Gospel Lantern Gang

A false-preacher outfit using revivals, donations, medicine shows, and dry-gulch work as cover. Half grift, half murder. Their leader can quote scripture while cutting a throat.

### A Season of Outlaw Life

> *The Harper gang had one spring where the split was fair, one summer where the trail turned mean, and one autumn where the dice quit being kind. In spring a strong fence roll and a fair split gave them money and Cohesion. In summer a failed score, a limited `Hard Ride and Flight` result, and one bad leadership roll left them poorer, meaner, and one argument from breaking. That is the shape of outlaw life when the rules are doing their job: every success buys room, every failure makes somebody pay, and the bill is seldom sent to the same soul twice.*
>
>The Harper gang consists of two player characters, four ordinary hands, and one notable member called Mexican Joe, who acts as scout and horse wrangler. That makes them a `Crew`. Their Cohesion is 3, Horsestock is 2, Fence Access is 1, and their hideout is a level 2 `Camp`—Mexican Joe also rides as the gang's connection to a crooked trader in a river town. Refuge with that town is +1. They have a concealed hideout in the bad hills north of town from the old Outlaws Group Concept.
>
>In spring they rob a stage carrying courthouse fees. The score yields Hot Goods—jewelry and a locked dispatch box—and some Clean Cash from the passengers. Because the robbery was quick and the shares were paid fair, Cohesion rises to 4. One player character then makes the fence roll through the river-town contact. On a full success, the Hot Goods move at a decent rate and the gang has enough ready money to buy grain, ammunition, and two fresh remounts. Horsestock rises to 3. At the `Turn of the Season` they choose `Scout a Target`, spending much of the next three months learning the habits of a railroad payroll clerk.
>
>In summer the payroll score goes wrong. One ordinary hand is captured, two horses are shot out from under the gang, and the take is poor—a payroll box that turns out to be mostly vouchers, which is Hot Goods they cannot easily move. Mexican Joe makes the `Hard Ride and Flight` roll as the gang cuts for the bad country with a posse and a railroad detective both behind them. He gets only a limited success. The gang escapes, but two more horses are blown and the trail is not as cold as he would like. Horsestock drops to 1. The leader decides to leave the captured man, fearing a marshal trap. That choice calls for a Cohesion test. The leader fails. Cohesion drops to 3 and the GM rolls Gang Trouble: a notable member feels cheated and their Loyalty falls by 1.
>
>Come fall, the gang faces a proper outlaw season. Upkeep still has to be paid. A refuge roll may keep the river town open to them or close it against them if the detective has been asking questions. If they choose `Lie Low`, Wanted may ease while morale sours and the horses stay poor. If they choose one rich rescue or revenge score instead, they may save the captured hand, raise Cohesion, and ruin themselves with fresh heat all in the same week. That is outlaw play in *Tales of the Old West*: profit, pressure, memory, and no safe road for very long.
