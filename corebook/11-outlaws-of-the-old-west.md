## Chapter 11 - Outlaws of the Old West

> *“AIN’T NO SUCH THING AS EASY MONEY. THERE’S JUST MONEY TOOK FAST, AND THE FASTER YOU TAKE IT THE FASTER MEN COME LOOKING.”*
>
> — OLD BORDER SAYING

### Outlaws and Their Trade

There are outlaws in almost every tale of the Old West. Some are no more than horse thieves and barn robbers. Some are killers who make a business of fear. Some are desperate men and women with nowhere left to go but the rough country. The dime novels lie about them all. They tell of bold riders and easy loot and a free life under the open sky. What they leave out is the cold camp, the empty belly, the partner who cheats the split, the horse gone lame fifty miles from water, the posse on your trail, and the knowledge that one bad score can leave your gang strung up from six different trees.

This chapter is an optional campaign framework for groups who want to play that life. It is written to sit alongside the Outlaws Group Concept in Chapter 2, not replace it. If your player characters are already outlaws, rustlers, raiders, train robbers, or fugitives, these rules help you run the gang around them: the hands who ride with them, the hideout that keeps them alive, the scores that put money in their pockets, and the pressure that comes down when the law, the railroads, or another gang finally decides it has had enough.

You do not need these rules for a one-off bank robbery or a short tale about a fugitive on the run. Use them when outlaw life is the campaign, or a major part of it.

#### The Outlaws Group Concept

If the group chose the Outlaws Group Concept during character creation, then this chapter grows naturally from that choice. The concealed hideout granted by the concept becomes the gang's first base, and any compadres gained through lifepath may be treated as the beginning of the gang's inner circle. If the player characters did not choose that concept, but become outlaws through play, they can still use these rules once they have a hideout, a handful of followers, and a reputation worth fearing.

### The Outlaw Band

An outlaw band is more than a few player characters with pistols on their hips. It is a criminal outfit with a name, a leader, a place to hole up, and enough folk riding under its shadow that success or failure becomes everyone's problem.

An outlaw band requires:

- A leader, usually one of the player characters.
- A hideout.
- At least three other outlaws, camp followers, or gunhands beyond the player characters.

If the group has fewer than three such folk, then they are still a posse, not yet a gang.

#### Band Scale

| Scale | Numbers | What It Means |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hideout Crew | 3-6 outlaws beyond the player characters | Fast-moving, easy to conceal, good for robberies, rustling, ambushes, and flight. |
| Gang | 7-15 outlaws beyond the player characters | A real local threat. Sheriffs take notice, ranchers form posses, and rival outlaws begin to weigh your strength. |
| Big Outfit | 16-30 outlaws beyond the player characters | Too large to pass unnoticed. Feeding, paying, and hiding such a body of riders is a campaign in itself. |

Anything larger than this is no longer a simple outlaw gang. It becomes a private army, a political problem, or an outright insurrection, and should be handled as a special case by the GM rather than routine outlaw play.

#### Gang Leadership

Every outlaw band needs one acknowledged leader. This may be the most feared gun in camp, the best rider, the cleverest planner, or simply the one who keeps the cash box and knows where the hideout lies. If two or more folk claim leadership, trouble follows soon enough.

When the rules in this chapter call for a leadership roll, the leader makes a `PRESENCE` roll. If they are trying to settle the gang with charm, lies, speechifying, or the promise of easy money, they may use `PERFORMIN'` instead. The GM may grant bonus dice for clear advantages:

- `+1` if the gang was paid fairly after the last score
- `+1` if the leader's Fame is 3 or more
- `+1` if the gang has had a recent success with little bloodshed
- `-1` if the gang has gone hungry, cold, or unpaid
- `-1` if one of the gang was left captured, wounded, or dead through the leader's bad judgment
- `-1` if the leader's Reputation suggests cowardice, treachery, or meanness of spirit

The GM should not stack every possible modifier. Apply only those that truly matter in the moment, to a practical maximum of `+3` or `-3`.

### Gang Cohesion

Outlaw gangs hold together for three reasons: fear, profit, and loyalty. Most are built on two of the three. Few are built on all of them. Gang Cohesion measures how close your outfit is to staying in the saddle together or breaking apart into grudges, desertions, and murder.

Gang Cohesion is tracked on a scale from 1 to 5. A newly formed outlaw band starts at 3.

| Cohesion | State | Effect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 5 | Keen | The gang is full of confidence. Gain `+1` die on rolls to recruit new hands or keep the gang steady after a hard score. |
| 4 | Solid | The gang is riding well together. No modifier. |
| 3 | Uneasy | No modifier, but any major setback should test Cohesion. |
| 2 | Fraying | `-1` die on rolls to steady the gang. A failed Cohesion test causes a desertion, feud, or act of disobedience. |
| 1 | Broken | The gang is on the edge of collapse. Any further serious trouble causes a split, betrayal, or outright flight unless the leader acts immediately. |

#### Testing Cohesion

Test Gang Cohesion when one of the following occurs:

- A score fails badly.
- The gang is not paid fairly.
- One or more gang members are captured and abandoned.
- The gang suffers heavy casualties.
- The hideout runs short of food, ammunition, horses, or shelter.
- The gang is forced to keep moving through a hard winter or in brutal weather.
- The leader cheats the split, plays favorites, or protects one of their own after a ruinous mistake.
- The gang commits a massacre or some other useless cruelty that brings heat without profit.

When Cohesion is tested, the leader makes a leadership roll. On a success, the gang holds together. On two or more successes, increase Cohesion by 1 if the circumstances warrant it. On a failure, reduce Cohesion by 1 and roll or choose one result from the Gang Trouble table in this chapter. At Cohesion 1, a failed test means the gang breaks in some serious way: several hands ride off, a notable member turns against the leader, or the whole outfit goes to ground in a panic.

#### Cohesion Changes

The GM may increase or reduce Cohesion directly when events plainly justify it.

| Event | Cohesion Change |
| --- | --- |
| Clean success on a profitable score | `+1` |
| Fair shares paid after a rich take | `+1` |
| The gang survives a hard season and keeps the hideout supplied | `+1` |
| Lean spell, no profitable work, and tempers sour | `-1` |
| One of the gang is hanged or captured and left to their fate | `-1` |
| The leader cheats the shares | `-2` |
| The gang is driven out in winter with poor supplies | `-1` |
| A pointless public massacre or cruelty brings law and outrage down on the gang | `-1` |

Cohesion should not swing every session. Save changes for moments that really tell on the gang.

> *Jeb Collier had held men together on less than this, but not much less. Two boys were dead, the Wells Fargo box had turned out to be mostly vouchers and papers, and Luther Vale was pacing the firelight with his hand never far from the butt of his Remington. Jeb stood up when the talk began to turn. He did not shout. He named the dead boys, said what they had earned, and counted twenty-dollar pieces into their brothers' hands where everyone could see it. The camp stayed ugly but it stayed a camp. That was enough for one night.*

### Recruitment, Notable Members, and Loyalty

Most gangs are held together by a hard core of known hands and a shifting fringe of drifters, cousins, camp women, old soldiers, horse thieves, and fools with more nerve than judgment.

#### Recruiting New Hands

To recruit new outlaws, the player characters need three things:

- Somewhere rough enough to find them
- Money, promises, or protection to offer
- A reason for folk to throw in with them

Most ordinary hands want an advance, a horse to ride, a fair share, or some assurance the gang knows what it is doing. The leader makes a `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` roll when trying to gather new hands. The GM should judge the number and quality of recruits by the result, the local conditions, and the gang's Fame and Reputation.

Recruiting is easier:

- in boomtowns, mining camps, border settlements, and rough cattle country
- after a famous score
- when the gang is known for paying fair

Recruiting is harder:

- where the Law aspect is high
- when the gang is hungry, hunted, or notorious for betrayal
- after a recent disaster

Ordinary gang hands should usually remain background figures. Name them when needed, but do not build every rider as a full character.

#### Notable Gang Members

Some outlaws matter more than the rest. They might be the leader's brother, the best scout in camp, the old safecracker who knows every railroad clerk between Wichita and Cheyenne, or the mad dog who keeps starting trouble in saloons. These are Notable Gang Members.

Build them using the existing `compadre` rules from Chapter 6, then add the following:

- `Role` : scout, second gun, safecracker, horse wrangler, fence, killer, lover, preacher, camp cook, and so on
- `Loyalty` : 1-3
- `Ambition` : what they want from the gang
- `Breaking Point` : the one thing they will not forgive

Only two to five such people should be tracked in detail at once. More than that is bookkeeping, not drama.

#### Loyalty

Each Notable Gang Member has a Loyalty rating from 1 to 3.

| Loyalty | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| 3 | They are personally invested in the leader, the gang, or the gang's cause. |
| 2 | They are reliable so long as the gang treats them fair and things are going tolerably well. |
| 1 | They are halfway out the door already. One hard slight, one missed share, or one ugly season may lose them. |

Increase Loyalty by 1 when:

- the leader treats the notable member fair in front of the gang
- the leader helps them pursue their ambition
- the gang risks something real to save them

Reduce Loyalty by 1 when:

- the leader cheats or humiliates them
- their breaking point is struck
- the gang falls to Cohesion 1

At Loyalty 1, the next serious slight or failure should send them away, turn them mutinous, or push them into betrayal.

#### Common Roles for Notable Gang Members

| Role | Useful Ability | What They Are Good For |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Scout | `HAWKEYE` or `NATURE` | Tracking, watching roads, finding safe country, and spotting posses first |
| Second Gun | `SHOOTIN'` or `FIGHTIN'` | Backing the leader in a fight and overawing the rest of camp |
| Safecracker or Burglar | `LIGHT-FINGERED` or `MAKIN'` | Getting through locks, boxes, safes, and barred windows |
| Wrangler | `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` | Keeping the horses fit, hidden, and ready to move |
| Fence or Trader | `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'` | Turning stolen goods into cash and finding buyers who ask few questions |
| Camp Doctor | `DOCTORIN'` | Keeping wounded hands alive after a bad score |
| Camp Cook | `LABOR` or `DOCTORIN'` | Feeding the gang, keeping order in camp, and hearing everything |

### Shares, Cash, and Hideout Upkeep

Outlaws do not get wages in the usual way. They live by the take. When the take is good, spirits rise and the whiskey runs. When it is poor, men begin to count what they are owed and whose fault it is.

#### Splitting the Take

There is no single honest way to split stolen money. Every gang does it differently, and every gang thinks its own way is common sense. As a good default, use the following:

- Each ordinary gang hand gets `1 share`
- Each player character gets `1 share`
- The leader gets `2 shares`
- A specialist whose role made the score possible may get `+1 share`

This split should be agreed before the job. If the player characters change it afterward in their own favor, test Gang Cohesion.

Stolen stock, jewelry, bonds, silverware, church plate, or railroad goods are not cash until fenced. Until then they are trouble wrapped in canvas.

#### Gang Expenses

Outlaw life burns money faster than many imagine. Horses founder. Ammunition is spent. Whiskey disappears. Hungry men grumble. A hideout must be provisioned if it is to remain a refuge and not turn into a freezing camp in the rocks.

At the end of each `Turn of the Season`, the gang must pay the following hideout and upkeep costs:

| Scale | Seasonal Upkeep |
| --- | --- |
| Hideout Crew | `$25` |
| Gang | `$75` |
| Big Outfit | `$150` |

This cost covers food stores, ammunition, lamp oil, spare tack, bribes to informants, and the ordinary waste of outlaw life. If the gang cannot or will not pay it, reduce Cohesion by 1 and the GM should choose an appropriate shortage: hungry horses, bad powder, a leak in the hideout roof, a fence refusing further credit, or something of that kind.

#### The Hideout

A hideout is not a town and should not be treated like one. It does not use Settlement Points and does not grow by adding amenities every season. It is a place to lie low, hide stolen goods, doctor the wounded, and survive until the next score.

A basic hideout should usually have the following:

- One strong advantage: concealed canyon, hard approach, spring water, a nearby safe town, caves, ruined buildings, thick timber, and so on
- One weakness: poor grazing, bad water, narrow escape route, nearby settlers, smoke visible from a distance, snakes, floods, and so on

If the gang already has the concealed hideout from the Outlaws Group Concept, use that as the starting point and add its local details.

### Scores and Criminal Work

A score is any planned criminal undertaking from which the gang expects profit. Some are great affairs requiring scouting, false identities, and a hard ride across three counties. Some are little more than waiting in a draw with rifles and a mean intent.

#### Common Kinds of Score

| Score Type | Common Target | Likely Take | Likely Trouble |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Robbery | Bank, payroll office, express box, gambling room | Cash or negotiable paper | Lawmen, armed guards, witnesses |
| Rustling | Cattle or horse herd | Livestock to fence or drive away | Ranch hands, trackers, brand inspectors |
| Stage or Rail Hold-Up | Stagecoach, baggage car, payroll train | Cash, valuables, mail, bonds | Company guards, posses, federal attention |
| Prison Break | Jail, prison wagon, courthouse | No direct profit, but allies or leverage | Sheriffs, marshals, heavy heat |
| Unlawful Escort | Stolen goods, guns, whiskey, wanted folk | Cash payment from a patron | Ambush, betrayal, seizure by the law |
| Hired Intimidation | Rancher, merchant, witness, juror, union man | Cash payment and future favors | Moral rot, heat, retaliation |
| Revenge Job | One man or one family | Whatever can be looted afterward | Blood feud, witnesses, pointless killing |
| Counter-Bounty Work | Witnesses, posters, informants, bounty hunters | Protection of the gang more than money | Escalating law pressure |
| Protection Racket | Shops, miners, saloons, gamblers | Steady small income | Civic anger, rival gangs, the sheriff |

#### Building a Score

Every score should answer six questions:

- Who wants it done, if anyone?
- What is the target?
- What is the likely take?
- Who stands in the way?
- What heat follows if it goes badly?
- What fresh trouble follows even if it goes well?

The GM can build scores by answering those questions directly or by rolling on the table below.

| D66 | Score Seed |

### Wanted Men

Every outlaw hopes to be feared. Few understand what fear costs once it settles on their name. A price on your head brings fools, professionals, and the law itself.

#### Wanted Status

Wanted status should be tracked in three rough tiers:

| Wanted Level | Who Wants You | What It Means |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Local | One town, one county, one sheriff, one ranching concern | Posters, posses, local bounty hunters, and folk willing to point you out for reward money |
| Regional | Several counties, a railroad, a territory, or multiple sheriffs | Professional trackers, organized posses, rail agents, private detectives, and hard travel for anyone recognized |
| Federal | Marshals, Army interest, multiple territories, major rail or bank concern | No easy sanctuary, strong witnesses hunted, and real pressure on fences, kin, and friendly towns |

Wanted status rises when:

- the gang leaves witnesses
- a lawman, judge, railroad man, or notable citizen is killed
- the score strikes at a railroad, bank, mail route, or federal business
- the gang keeps working the same region instead of moving on

Wanted status can fall if:

- the gang lies low for a whole season
- they leave the region entirely
- a scapegoat is taken, whether justly or not
- the gang bribes, bargains, or quietly kills the right people

#### Wanted and Reputation

Wanted status does not replace `Fame` and `Reputation`. It sharpens them. When the gang's crimes become widely known, the GM may award Fame as normal. Just remember that Fame earned this way is dangerous Fame. It may make rough folk respect you, but it will also sour Reputation among decent townsfolk, raise the cost of doing business, and make every stranger's stare worth noticing.

#### Escalation

| D6 | Who Comes Next? |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | A local sheriff and a few hard-ridden deputies or townsmen |
| 2 | Independent bounty hunters scenting easy money |
| 3 | Ranch hands, rail guards, or hired gunmen paid by the injured party |
| 4 | A neighboring sheriff, territorial men, or a larger posse |
| 5 | A federal marshal, Pinkerton man, or company detective |
| 6 | A rival outlaw gang hoping to sell you out or claim your horses, hideout, or fence |

When the heat rises, the GM need not send these enemies straight at the player characters. Often it is more interesting if they seize horses, question a fence, frighten a compadre, or close a town against the gang instead.

### Hideout Life

Most outlaw tales are not all gun smoke and hoofbeats. A gang spends much of its life waiting: cleaning guns, doctoring wounds, arguing over shares, lying still while posses comb the country, or planning the next ugly little miracle.

If outlaw play is the heart of the campaign, then during each `Turn of the Season` the gang may choose one Hideout Activity. This does not replace the usual Town Amenity choice if the player characters still have a town to call home. It is simply the gang's own business for the season.

#### Hideout Activities

| Activity | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| Lie Low | Reduce Wanted by one step in the current region, if the gang commits no loud crime during the season. |
| Drill the Gang | Gain `+1` die on the first Cohesion test of next season. |
| Move Camp | Shift the hideout to a safer or more profitable area, but test Cohesion if weather, distance, or shortages make the move harsh. |
| Scout a Target | Gain `+1` die on the first score of next season if the gang acts on the intelligence gathered. |
| Buy Through Fences | Spend cash to ensure ammunition, horses, and supplies are plentiful; no risk of ordinary seasonal shortage this season. |
| Launder Loot | Turn awkward stolen goods into cleaner cash at a fairer rate than a hasty fence would offer. |
| Patch Up the Wounded | Wounded gang members or notable members recover better; the GM may soften the consequences of an earlier disaster. |
| Settle Internal Trouble | Make an immediate leadership roll; on a success, raise Cohesion by 1 or restore 1 Loyalty to a single notable member. |
| Court Informants | Establish a useful contact in a town, saloon, rail office, sheriff's department, or ranch. |
| Blow Money and Raise Hell | The gang has a wild good time in town. Gain `+1` Cohesion now, but Wanted rises if things get out of hand, which they often do. |

The gang should only gain the benefit of one such activity each season unless the GM judges the campaign has given ample time for more.

> *The hideout lay in a fold of bad country where the cottonwoods twisted up from the creek and the rock broke a rider's sight from every side. In the day the place looked dead. By dusk you could smell bacon grease and woodsmoke if the wind was wrong, hear a horse stamp in the dark, and catch the quick low laugh of a woman who knew every hand in camp and trusted none of them. That was outlaw life as often as not. Waiting, mending, counting cartridges, and trying not to think on the men counting you somewhere else.*

### Consequences and Collapse

No gang runs clean forever. Sooner or later somebody dies badly, cheats the split, talks under drink, gets taken alive, or rides off with the best horse in camp. When that happens, use the gang's trouble to make more tale, not simply to end the campaign.

#### Gang Trouble

When the rules call for Gang Trouble, roll D66 or choose a result that fits:

| D66 | Trouble |
| --- | --- |
| 11-13 | One ordinary hand deserts in the night, taking a horse, some cartridges, or a little money. |
| 14-16 | Two gang members are feuding openly. Unless the leader steps in, one will soon be dead or gone. |
| 21-23 | A notable member feels cheated and their Loyalty drops by 1. |
| 24-26 | A local fence refuses further dealings until paid, appeased, or protected. |
| 31-33 | Somebody in camp is talking too much in town. Wanted rises one step unless they are stopped. |
| 34-36 | Horses sicken, stray, or are stolen. The gang's next move is slower and more dangerous than planned. |
| 41-43 | One member of the gang has kin trouble, debt, or a private grudge and wants the gang's help right now. |
| 44-46 | A captured or missing former member knows too much about the hideout. |
| 51-53 | A posse, rival gang, or bounty hunter finds sign too close to the hideout for comfort. |
| 54-56 | Whiskey, gambling, jealousy, or boredom turns camp mean. Test Cohesion. |
| 61-63 | A patron wants another job done before the last one has settled. Refusal may cost future work, but acceptance may ruin the gang. |
| 64-66 | Someone in authority offers a dirty bargain: amnesty for one, money for one, and rope for the rest. |

#### Captured Members

If a gang member is captured, the leader must quickly decide whether to rescue them, ransom them, silence them, or abandon them. No choice is clean. Rescuing them may be glorious and stupid. Leaving them may be wise and poison the gang. Either way, test Cohesion if the gang believes the leader has failed one of their own.

#### Gang War

When two gangs want the same hideout country, fence, herd, or line of escape, open war may follow. Do not reduce this to a single fight unless the campaign demands it. Gang war should mean stolen horses, dead informants, ambushes on lonely roads, town folk forced to choose sides, and the fear that one wrong move will bring the law down on everyone.

#### Winter and Hard Times

Outlaws suffer badly in winter. Trails vanish, horses lose weight, wet blankets kill, and every nearby settlement grows suspicious of strangers wanting food and a warm stove. If the gang goes into winter under-supplied, hunted, or divided, test Cohesion and consider adding Gang Trouble even before the first score of the season.

#### When the Gang Breaks

A broken gang need not end the campaign. It may simply change it.

When the gang finally comes apart, one or more of the following may happen:

- half the hands ride off with their share
- a notable member betrays the leader to the law or a rival
- the player characters are forced to flee and build again somewhere new
- the gang splits into rival outfits, each with a grudge and a memory

That is not failure. That is a new chapter in the tale.

### Premade Outlaw Bands

The following gangs may be used as allies, enemies, or examples for a new campaign.

#### The Dead Mule Boys

A family rustling crew from rough cattle country. Mean when cornered, practical the rest of the time. They would rather steal horses than blood a man, but will do either if pushed. Their old mother runs the hidden camp and knows every stolen brand in three counties.

#### The San Marcos Riders

A border gang moving between Texas and the territories, half raiders and half smugglers. They know dry country, hidden springs, and men willing to buy things quietly. They are held together by kinship, old war loyalties, and hatred of the Rangers.

#### The Widow Pike Outfit

A mixed gang built around a hard widow who lost her ranch to debt and fraud. She takes scores against rail men, crooked banks, and land sharks, which makes her sound romantic until you see the bodies she leaves behind when crossed.

#### The Gospel Lantern Gang

A false-preacher outfit using revivals, donations, medicine shows, and dry-gulch work as cover. Half grift, half murder. Their leader can quote scripture while cutting a throat.

### A Season of Outlaw Life

The Harper gang consists of two player characters, four ordinary hands, and one notable member called Mexican Joe, who acts as scout and horse wrangler. That makes them a `Hideout Crew`. Their Cohesion is 3 and they have a concealed hideout in the bad hills north of town from the old Outlaws Group Concept.

In spring they rob a stage carrying courthouse fees. The score is messy but profitable. They pay fair, buy feed, and take their cash to a fence in a river town. Cohesion rises to 4. At the `Turn of the Season` they choose `Scout a Target`, spending much of the next three months learning the habits of a railroad payroll clerk.

In summer the payroll score goes wrong. One ordinary hand is captured, two horses are shot out from under the gang, and the take is poor. The leader decides to leave the captured man, fearing a marshal trap. That choice calls for a Cohesion test. The leader fails. Cohesion drops to 3 and the GM rolls Gang Trouble: a notable member feels cheated and their Loyalty falls by 1.

Come fall, the gang can ride for easier country, try to rescue the captured hand before he talks, or strike one rich score to settle the camp and buy a winter's peace. That is outlaw play in *Tales of the Old West*: profit, pressure, memory, and no safe road for very long.
