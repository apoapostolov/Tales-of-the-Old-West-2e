# Proposal: Outlaw Reality and Light Town / Resource Tie-Ins

**Status:** Research proposal  
**Target:** `Tales of the Old West 2e`, Chapter 11 support design  
**Scope:** Proposal only. No immediate manuscript integration.

## Purpose

The current outlaw appendix is already playable, but it still leans toward the familiar western loop of score, heat, hideout, and betrayal. What it does not yet model strongly enough are the practical truths that made outlaw life in the West possible for more than a single gunfight:

- stolen goods were often hard to turn into cash
- horses, feed, and remounts mattered as much as pistols
- gangs survived through kin, lovers, corrupt traders, ranch ties, and tolerated towns
- pursuit was not always a showdown; often it was questions, gossip, posters, and supply denial
- some country could hide you and some country spat you back out

The design problem is not a lack of flavor. It is a lack of a few light pressures that make outlaw play feel materially western rather than merely criminal.

The design goal is therefore:

- add only the missing realities that produce play
- keep bookkeeping at the seasonal and regional scale
- tie outlaw life into existing `Tales` terms and, where useful, the lighter lessons from `Forbidden Lands 2e`
- reject any subsystem that would turn Chapter 11 into a second game

## Research Summary

The historical pattern is clear enough to support design.

### 1. Rustling was skilled work, not generic theft

Rustlers were often cowboys who knew brands, trails, and cattle behavior. They altered brands, took unbranded calves, used hidden country, and relied on the difficulty of proving ownership in open-range conditions. Once ranching hardened into fenced country and associations increased inspection, rustling became harder and more professionally hunted.

**Design implication:** outlaw play should distinguish cash from stock and from other "hot" loot. Cattle and horses are not immediate dollars.

### 2. Hideouts worked because they were tied to rough country and friendly ground

The Outlaw Trail linked remote country, hideouts, and resupply points. Outlaws did not live on legend alone. They needed isolated valleys, canyons, rough crossings, winterable country, and at least a few locals willing to trade, keep quiet, or look away.

**Design implication:** a hideout should not be a self-contained base. It should depend on access to sheltering country and on at least one settlement relationship.

### 3. Pursuit was often logistical before it was violent

Fort Smith deputy marshals and their posses operated as mobile outfits. They hired guards or posse members, bought supplies, stayed out for weeks, and tracked suspects across a huge territory. That is a different pressure than a single sheriff riding into town. It means the threat to outlaws is not only gunmen. It is time, endurance, warrants, informants, and the loss of safe access to settlements.

**Design implication:** wanted pressure should escalate from local inconvenience to regional denial of shelter to organized manhunts.

### 4. Private and semi-private enforcement mattered

Pinkertons began as railway-theft specialists protecting trains and apprehending train robbers. Cattle associations and their inspectors, along with Rangers and local officers, made rustling riskier where commercial interests were strong.

**Design implication:** not all pursuit should come from sheriffs. Railroads, ranching outfits, and other economic powers should generate a different kind of heat.

### 5. Frontier law was uneven and personal

In some country a sheriff could raise a posse quickly. In other places federal jurisdiction, tribal jurisdiction, company power, distance, weather, and local loyalties all mattered. A town could be openly lawful and still quietly shelter thieves if the merchants profited or the locals hated the railroad more than the robbers.

**Design implication:** outlaw play benefits from a settlement-level relation that is not the same as general Fame or Reputation.

### 6. Horseflesh and provisions were campaign truth

Cowboy life and frontier travel were structured by remounts, grazing, feed, weather, and the physical limits of men and animals. A gang with poor horses was a gang whose options narrowed fast. This matters more to outlaw play than imported daily ration tracking.

**Design implication:** if any resource pressure is added, horses should matter before bullets do.

## What Is Missing From The Current Outlaw Loop

The present draft already covers:

- gang scale
- cohesion
- notable members
- scores
- wanted pressure
- hideout upkeep

What it does not yet cover strongly enough:

1. `Fencing and laundering`
2. `Safe settlement relations`
3. `Horse condition and remount pressure`
4. `Supply denial during pursuit`
5. `Different kinds of pursuit`
6. `Gang dependence on social networks`

These are the missing western bones.

## Proposal A: Add Four Light Outlaw Tags

This is the recommended design. It adds four abstract campaign tags to an outlaw band and keeps all of them at a scale the group can actually remember.

### 1. Refuge

`Refuge` is the outlaw band's standing with a specific settlement, camp, ranch, or hidden community. It is the outlaw answer to `Forbidden Lands` settlement `Standing`, but narrower and more criminal in shape.

Track it only for places that matter.

| Refuge | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `+2` | The place will hide you, feed you quietly, pass messages, and lie if pressed. |
| `+1` | The place will deal with you, but wants discretion and quick business. |
| `0` | No special help. You are just passing through. |
| `-1` | The place is wary, talks too much, and may refuse service. |
| `-2` | The place will warn the law, deny shelter, or sell you out. |

How it fits `Tales`:

- separate from global `Fame` and `Reputation`
- modified by a town's `Law`, `Civic`, and `Mercantile` aspects
- earned or lost through play, not season rolls alone

Why it matters:

- it answers whether a gang can resupply, doctor wounds, stable horses, or hear warnings
- it gives the town system teeth in outlaw play
- it models safe towns without building a second reputation lattice

### 2. Fence Access

`Fence Access` measures whether the gang can turn hot goods into cash or usable credit.

| Fence Access | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `0` | No fence. Hot loot sits in camp or must be sold openly at terrible risk. |
| `1` | Petty fence. Can move jewelry, guns, whiskey, and small goods. |
| `2` | Serious fence. Can move stock, wagons, payroll paper, and larger goods through a network. |

How it fits `Tales`:

- can be represented by a `compadre`, notable gang member, trader, corrupt merchant, or settlement contact
- ties naturally to `Mercantile`

Why it matters:

- it prevents every successful robbery from becoming instant clean money
- it gives a concrete reason to cultivate towns, merchants, and crooked allies
- it differentiates outlaw types: robbers, rustlers, smugglers, and raiders

### 3. Horseflesh

`Horseflesh` measures the practical state of the gang's riding animals as a single band resource.

| Horseflesh | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `3` | Fresh remounts, sound tack, and horses fit for flight or pursuit |
| `2` | Serviceable |
| `1` | Tired, lame, hungry, poorly shod, or too few mounts |
| `0` | The gang cannot move fast or far without immediate trouble |

When it drops:

- long hard flight
- bad season at the hideout
- poor grazing or drought
- loss of remounts in a score
- no access to wranglers, pasture, feed, or blacksmithing

Why it matters:

- it models western mobility without daily feed accounting
- it makes winter, drought, and rough country relevant
- it gives bounty hunters and posses a real tactic: strip away the gang's speed

### 4. Exposure

`Exposure` is the real campaign pressure underneath `Wanted`. `Wanted` answers who is looking. `Exposure` answers how easy the gang is to find right now.

| Exposure | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `0` | Well hidden. False trail, quiet friends, no talk. |
| `1` | Some talk. A deputy or rival can begin asking after you. |
| `2` | The gang's movements are becoming known. |
| `3` | The next serious pursuit should find you or cut you off. |

Why add this when `Wanted` already exists:

- because many western manhunts were solved through information, not combat
- because it lets hideout actions and settlement behavior matter
- because it creates pressure even when the law is weak

This should not replace `Wanted`. It should sharpen it.

## Proposal B: Distinguish Three Kinds of Loot

The current draft treats loot mostly as money or trouble. That is too flat.

Use three categories:

| Loot Type | Examples | Conversion |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `Clean Cash` | coin, notes, direct payroll cash | usable at once |
| `Hot Goods` | watches, guns, jewelry, express goods, whiskey | needs `Fence Access` |
| `Hot Stock` | cattle, horses, mules | needs `Fence Access 2` or special local conditions |

Effects:

- `Hot Goods` without a fence lose value and raise `Exposure`
- `Hot Stock` is powerful but slow, noisy, and easy to trace in cattle country
- some jobs pay less in immediate cash but more in long-campaign pressure

This is one of the cleanest ways to make rustling feel distinct from robbery.

## Proposal C: Tie Outlaws Into The Town System

This is the strongest place to borrow the spirit of `Forbidden Lands` settlement play without importing its full machinery.

### Use Existing Town Aspects As Outlaw Modifiers

Outlaw play should read the town through these aspects:

- `Law`: how quickly the town organizes pursuit, notices strangers, posts notices, and supports sheriffs or marshals
- `Mercantile`: whether there are fences, crooked merchants, freight opportunities, bribable clerks, or liquid markets for stolen goods
- `Civic`: whether the town stands together against gangs or is divided by feuds, class hatred, or political patronage
- `Welfare`: whether strangers can disappear into the crowd, find a doctor, stable horses, and rent rooms without remark

This means the same town can be:

- bad for robbery but good for laundering money
- lawful on paper but corrupt in practice
- hostile to open outlaws but happy to buy stolen cattle cheap

That is more western than a flat lawful/chaotic distinction.

### Add Three Optional Outlaw Town Tags

Only for settlements important to the campaign.

| Tag | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `Soft Town` | weak law, divided interests, or enough vice that a gang can vanish there briefly |
| `Fence Town` | stolen goods can be moved here through merchants, drovers, gamblers, or rail contacts |
| `Sheriff's Town` | the local law, ranchers, and merchants cooperate quickly against trouble |

These tags can be derived from town aspects rather than tracked separately if the GM prefers.

### Seasonal Town Interaction

At the `Turn of the Season`, outlaw bands should answer:

- Did the gang strengthen or damage `Refuge` in any important settlement?
- Did a town become harder or easier to use because of `Lawlessness in Town`, `Federal Marshal in Town`, `Booming Market Days`, `Native Trade`, or similar fortunes?
- Did the gang invest money, favors, or fear into keeping one place open to them?

This is light and keeps outlaw play inside the same seasonal frame as the rest of the game.

## Proposal D: Add A Light Resource Loop

Do not import daily `Forbidden Lands` food-and-water dice. That would be false weight in `Tales`.

Use one required resource and one optional one.

### Required: Horseflesh

This is the resource that most deserves rules support.

Why:

- mounted mobility is the outlaw's real armor
- it creates meaningful regional pressure
- it connects directly to travel, pursuit, rustling, and escape

### Optional: Provisions

Only track `Provisions` if the campaign is wilderness-heavy or the gang spends whole seasons in bad country.

| Provisions | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `2` | camp is fed, watered, and stocked |
| `1` | lean |
| `0` | hunger, poor powder, bad coffee, sour tempers, and shortage |

This should interact with `Gang Cohesion`, not become a separate hardship game.

### Why Not More

Do not separately track:

- bullets
- whiskey
- blankets
- lamp oil
- horse feed
- medicine

Unless a specific score or winter crisis makes one of them the point of play.

Those details belong inside seasonal upkeep and consequences.

## Proposal E: Different Pursuers Should Feel Different

Right now pursuit risks blending together. It should not.

Use broad pursuer classes:

| Pursuer | How They Pressure The Gang |
| --- | --- |
| `Sheriff and Posse` | quick local response, neighbors, short chase, angry townsmen |
| `Ranch Outfit / Range Detectives` | tracking stock, brands, trails, local riders, cattle-country pressure |
| `Railroad / Express Detectives` | informants, company money, station networks, harder to hide after robbery |
| `Federal Marshals` | warrants, distance, persistence, cross-jurisdiction pursuit |
| `Rival Gang` | ambush, theft, betrayal, no interest in due process |

This does not require separate combat rules. It changes what the gang must do next:

- leave town fast
- change horses
- abandon stock
- lie low with a `Refuge`
- bribe a merchant
- move the hideout

## Recommendation

For the manuscript, the best next step is not a broad expansion. It is a narrow reinforcement.

### Add In The Next Design Pass

1. `Refuge`
2. `Fence Access`
3. `Horseflesh`
4. `Loot categories`
5. `Pursuer classes`

### Keep Optional Or Later

1. `Exposure`
2. `Provisions`
3. outlaw town tags as explicit rules instead of guidance

This gives the outlaw appendix materially western pressures without dragging it toward `Forbidden Lands` mercenary-scale accounting.

## Explicit Rejections

Do not import:

- daily resource dice for full outlaw bands
- detailed payroll ledgers for every rider
- multi-settlement standing maps
- stronghold build trees for hideouts
- mass tactical posse rules
- separate mini-games for horse management

The right level for `Tales` is:

- seasonal
- local
- concrete
- human

## Suggested Next Design Questions

If this proposal is accepted, the next memo should answer:

1. How exactly does `Refuge` rise and fall in play?
2. Should `Fence Access` be a gang stat, a compadre role, or both?
3. Is `Horseflesh` best reduced by failed travel, failed scores, seasonal neglect, or a mix?
4. Which current Chapter 11 sections should absorb these rules with the least bloat?
