# Proposal: FL2e Grafts and Outlaws of the Old West

**Status:** Draft planning proposal
**Target:** `Tales of the Old West 2e`
**Primary future deliverable:** Chapter 11 / Appendix A, `Outlaws of the Old West`
**Primary source chapter:** `Forbidden-Lands-2e/corebook/12-mercenaries-of-forbidden-lands.md`

## Design Stance

`Forbidden Lands 2e` is operating at a much heavier simulation level than `Tales of the Old West 2e`. That is a strength in FL2e, but it would become drag in ToOW2e if transferred wholesale.

The correct conversion principle is therefore:

- graft only rules that create a strong frontier or outlaw campaign loop
- prefer rules that reuse ToOW2e terms already on the page
- reject rules that require a second economy, second reputation map, or second campaign framework
- favor seasonal and narrative pressure over daily ledger management
- make outlaw play feel distinct from ranchers, lawmen, and business owners without turning it into a separate game

In short: take the strongest bones, not the full body.

## What ToOW2e Already Has That This Must Respect

The clearest integration anchors already in the manuscript are:

- group concept support for `Outlaws`, including a gang identity and wilderness hideout
- campaign play centered on the town, `Settlement Points`, amenities, and the `Turn of the Season`
- money split between cash and `Capital`
- an existing Fame and Reputation model built from `Morals` and `Public Spirit`
- compadres as semi-loyal supporting NPCs
- a seasonal business and prosperity loop
- town and personal fortunes that already generate outlaw pressure

That means the outlaw appendix should plug into the existing town and seasonal engine, not replace it.

## FL2e Rules That Can Be Easily Grafted Onto ToOW2e

### 1. Band Scale Tiers

This is the cleanest direct graft.

Use a light Old West adaptation of FL2e's size tiers:

- `Hideout Crew`: 3-6 outlaws
- `Gang`: 7-15 outlaws
- `Big Outfit`: 16-30 outlaws

Why it fits:

- it gives immediate campaign scale
- it tells the GM what sort of jobs, manhunts, and enemies the group can realistically face
- it supports the existing outlaw group concept

Required simplification:

- no separate tactical mass-combat layer
- no host-scale command rules
- no second-sheet roster accounting for ordinary play

### 2. Simplified Group Cohesion

FL2e's `MORALE` is useful, but ToOW2e should not import the full grievance machine.

Recommended ToOW2e version:

- track `Gang Cohesion` on a simple 1-5 scale
- test it only at major stress points: failed job, unpaid shares, heavy casualties, betrayal, long pursuit, harsh winter, hideout shortages
- resolve with `PRESENCE` or `PERFORMIN'`, modified by Fame, Reputation, recent success, and whether the gang was treated fairly

Why it fits:

- it gives outlaw bands a campaign pressure track
- it creates consequences for leadership without adding excessive bookkeeping
- it meshes with ToOW2e's social-stat logic

What should not be imported:

- stacked grievance ladders
- weekly roll cadence
- constant internal procedure

### 3. Named Men Recast as Notable Gang Members

FL2e's `Named Men` is one of the best chapter ideas, but ToOW2e should express it through existing compadre logic.

Recommended adaptation:

- create `Notable Gang Members` as higher-weight compadres
- each gets a role, one want, one pressure point, and one loyalty risk
- only 2-5 should be tracked in detail at once

Why it fits:

- ToOW2e already wants relationship-driven NPCs
- outlaw fiction depends on volatile lieutenants, siblings, lovers, trackers, drunks, and ambitious shooters
- this gives the gang a human face without requiring every hand to be statted

### 4. Job Board / Contract Structure Recast as Scores

FL2e contracts and bounties are highly portable if renamed and condensed.

Recommended ToOW2e job categories:

- robbery
- rustling
- stage or rail hold-up
- prison break
- escort for unlawful cargo
- hired intimidation
- revenge job
- bounty counterplay
- protection racket

Why it fits:

- outlaw campaigns need repeatable job structures
- ToOW2e already has salaries, bounties, and business money, so cash stakes are native to the game
- this supports long campaign play without forcing every session to be a freeform caper

### 5. Wanted Pressure

FL2e's bounty and consequence structure is highly relevant, but ToOW2e should express it through existing public reputation systems.

Recommended adaptation:

- track `Wanted` locally, regionally, or federally
- let wanted status modify Fame and Reputation consequences rather than create a separate moral universe
- towns, sheriffs, railroad companies, ranchers, Pinkertons, and federal marshals become escalating response tiers

Why it fits:

- the Old West runs on public memory, posters, rumor, and armed pursuit
- ToOW2e already has the social machinery to make fame dangerous

### 6. Camp / Hideout Activities Between Jobs

FL2e's `Campaign Life` section has good structure that can be reskinned.

Recommended Old West hideout activities:

- lie low
- drill the gang
- move camp
- scout a target
- buy supplies through fences
- launder stolen goods
- court informants
- blow money and cause trouble
- patch up the wounded
- settle internal disputes

Why it fits:

- outlaw play needs an in-between-jobs loop
- this gives texture to seasonal and travel play
- it ties the hideout to the gang as strongly as ranch or saloon rules tie owners to their outfit

### 7. Serving in Another Outfit

This is an optional but easy graft from FL2e's `Serving in Another's Company`.

Old West equivalent:

- the PCs temporarily ride with another outlaw gang
- or work as hired gunhands for cattle barons, railroad men, strikebreakers, political bosses, or lawmen

Why it fits:

- strong for lifepath support
- strong for short campaigns and transition arcs
- useful for explaining how gangs form, fracture, and inherit grudges

## FL2e Rules That Should Not Be Grafted Or Should Only Be Used As Hidden Design Material

These are good FL2e rules but poor ToOW2e imports:

- `Host Play`
- `Warmaster` authority
- war-room / stronghold command functions
- hired casters
- settlement tribute and occupation in the direct FL2e form
- detailed loot-share matrices
- deep ransom procedure
- highly granular supply accounting
- full grievance stack architecture

These can still inform the appendix's writing logic, but should not appear as-is.

## Recommended Shape Of Outlaws of the Old West

The strongest conversion is not "mercenary company management, western skin." It is:

`a seasonal outlaw-band campaign framework built on hideouts, scores, pursuit, loyalty, and public consequence`

That means the chapter should center on five loops:

1. recruit and hold a gang together
2. choose or generate outlaw work
3. survive the consequences of success and failure
4. manage the hideout and gang between jobs
5. endure the changing pressure of town, territory, and law across seasons

## Proposed Chapter Structure

### 1. What This Appendix Is

Explain the purpose:

- for groups playing outlaws, rustlers, train robbers, desperadoes, or fugitive crews
- optional expansion, not required core play
- designed to sit beside the existing outlaw group concept, not replace the main game

### 2. The Outlaw Band

Core rules:

- gang scale tiers
- gang leadership
- gang cohesion
- what counts as a gang versus a few player characters with accomplices

### 3. Recruitment, Notable Members, and Loyalty

Core rules:

- how you pick up new hands
- what kinds of people join gangs
- notable gang members as advanced compadres
- betrayal, desertion, and rivalry

### 4. Shares, Supplies, and Hideout Upkeep

Core rules:

- simple split of stolen cash
- baseline supply pressure
- what a hideout costs to maintain
- what happens when the gang goes hungry, unpaid, or cold

This section must stay light and seasonal.

### 5. Scores, Jobs, and Criminal Work

Core rules:

- job categories
- patrons and fences
- heat, risk, and likely response
- basic score generation tables or frameworks

### 6. Wanted Men

Core rules:

- local, territorial, and federal wanted status
- bounties
- who comes after you
- how posters, rumor, and witnesses expand pressure

### 7. Hideout Life

Core rules:

- what the gang does between jobs
- training, scouting, moving hideouts, laying low, laundering loot, patching wounds
- event hooks for tensions inside the band

### 8. Consequences and Collapse

Core rules:

- unpaid shares
- betrayal
- captured members
- gang war
- winter shortages
- public massacres
- when the gang splits, hangs together, or goes to ground

### Appendix A. Premade Outlaw Bands

Not medieval companies, but western outlaw archetypes such as:

- family rustlers
- border raiders
- ex-bushwhacker killers
- railroad robbers
- mixed desperado gang
- former lawmen gone bad

## Core Conversion Moves From Mercenaries To Outlaws

The major concept shifts should be:

- `contract` -> `score`, `job`, `arrangement`, or `patron's work`
- `mercenary band` -> `outlaw band`, `gang`, `crew`, or `outfit`
- `morale` -> `gang cohesion`
- `named men` -> `notable gang members`
- `standing with settlements` -> pressure through local reputation, bounty, town fear, and fence support
- `allegiance to employer` -> ties to patrons, fences, corrupt sheriffs, ranchers, rail agents, political bosses
- `war room / stronghold logistics` -> hideout maintenance and movement
- `atrocity` -> public outrage, posse formation, federal attention, vigilante violence, and moral collapse

## Specific Easy Mechanical Ports

These are the highest-value direct ports for early drafting:

### Port A: Gang Cohesion Trigger Table

Trigger examples:

- clean success on a profitable score: +1
- fair shares paid: +1
- long lean period: -1
- one of the gang hanged or captured and abandoned: -1
- leader cheats the shares: -2
- gang forced to run in winter with poor supplies: -1
- high-profile massacre or unnecessary killing: immediate pressure, not always a cohesion bonus

### Port B: Notable Gang Member Template

Each notable member should have:

- role
- current attitude to the leader
- personal ambition
- breaking point
- one adventure hook

### Port C: Job Package Template

Every outlaw job should specify:

- patron or source
- target
- likely take
- likely resistance
- heat if it goes badly
- follow-on consequence if it goes well

### Port D: Wanted Escalation Ladder

Basic escalation:

1. local sheriff and posses
2. private bounty hunters
3. rancher or railroad gunhands
4. territorial or federal law
5. rival gang betrayal pressure

## Research Demands For Later Prompts

This appendix will need heavy research, but not all in one pass. The largest research tracks are:

- real outlaw band organization in the 1865-1880 West
- size and composition of gangs
- how loot was divided
- how hideouts functioned in practice
- regional law enforcement response times and methods
- bounty hunting as business reality versus dime-novel myth
- railroad, stagecoach, cattle, and bank robbery patterns
- rustling economics
- fences, safe towns, and corrupt officials
- winter survival and mobility of outlaw bands
- women, family members, and noncombatants inside outlaw networks
- ethnic and regional variation: Texas, New Mexico, borderlands, plains, mountain territories

## Development Plan

### Phase 1. Define the mechanical spine

Produce a short design memo that locks:

- gang scale
- gang cohesion
- notable members
- score structure
- wanted escalation
- hideout upkeep scope

This phase should explicitly reject any rules that feel too FL2e-heavy.

### Phase 2. Research by subsystem

Run separate research passes for:

- gang structure and loyalty
- outlaw economy and shares
- wanted systems and pursuit
- hideouts and seasonal survival
- outlaw job types by region and year

Each pass should end in a short research memo with direct design implications.

### Phase 3. Draft the section skeleton

Create the chapter shell with headings, integration notes, and open questions.

### Phase 4. Write the minimum viable outlaw loop

Draft only the essential sections first:

- what the appendix is
- the outlaw band
- recruitment and loyalty
- scores and jobs
- wanted men

This produces a playable appendix fast.

### Phase 5. Add hideout and between-job structure

Draft:

- shares and upkeep
- hideout life
- consequences and collapse

### Phase 6. Add examples and outlaw bands

Add:

- one full worked campaign example
- several notable gang members
- premade outlaw bands

### Phase 7. Integration audit

Check against:

- `corebook/02-your-player-character.md`
- `corebook/06-life-in-the-old-west.md`
- `corebook/08-campaigns-in-the-old-west.md`

The appendix must not silently contradict money, compadres, reputation, or the Turn of the Season.

## Recommendation

The best immediate path is:

- adopt `Band Scale Tiers`
- adopt a simplified `Gang Cohesion`
- adapt `Named Men` into `Notable Gang Members`
- port `Contracts and Bounties` into `Scores and Wanted Men`
- port light `Campaign Life` into `Hideout Life`
- discard `Host Play`, `Warmaster`, full grievance procedure, and heavy logistics

That combination gives ToOW2e a powerful outlaw campaign engine with low enough overhead to remain true to the lighter manuscript.
