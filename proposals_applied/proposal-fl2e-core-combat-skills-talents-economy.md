# Proposal: Useful FL2e Rule Grafts for Tales of the Old West 2e

**Status:** Draft proposal  
**Target:** `Tales of the Old West 2e` core game  
**Primary source:** `C:\git-public\Forbidden-Lands-2e\corebook`

## Purpose

`Forbidden Lands 2e` and `Tales of the Old West 2e` share the same family of dice logic, but they aim at different densities of play.

`Forbidden Lands 2e` is:

- more simulationist
- more procedural
- more willing to track wear, supply, labor, and layered tactical choices

`Tales of the Old West 2e` is:

- broader
- faster
- more socially and fictionally framed
- less interested in daily bookkeeping

That means the question is not "what is good in FL2e?" The question is:

- which small rules improve clarity, tension, and long-term play in `Tales`
- which rules create useful friction rather than bureaucratic friction
- which procedures can be transplanted without forcing the whole game to become heavier

This document identifies those rules by subsystem.

## Design Stance

The correct standard for a graft is:

- it should sharpen decisions at the table
- it should make outcomes easier to read
- it should reduce repeat arguments or vague GM calls
- it should not require a second game layer to support it

In practice, the best FL2e grafts are usually:

- threshold logic
- pacing rules
- advancement rules
- light tactical procedures
- scarcity and access procedures

The worst graft candidates are usually:

- high-frequency resource attrition
- deep crafting prerequisites
- stronghold labor accounting
- fully ranked talent trees
- anything that requires several new ledgers

## Summary Recommendations

### Strong candidates

- success tiers and declared effort levels
- one-chance rule for repeated attempts
- clearer group roll procedures
- stronger gear wear / repair logic for hard use
- morale and rout for NPC groups
- local availability and scarcity rules for goods and services
- slower training-based advancement
- teacher / mentor access as a gate on growth
- stronger economic distinction between common, uncommon, and rare goods

### Conditional candidates

- rerolled initiative each round
- quality tiers for gear
- consumables as resource dice
- strongerhold-style reputation and hireling availability logic
- profession-path style talent organization

### Poor candidates

- full Willpower economy
- five-rank talent trees as written
- full resource-die survival economy for everyday play
- full stronghold building and labor simulation
- detailed crafting material chains

## Core System

### 1. Success Tiers

**FL2e value:**  
FL2e is clearer than many YZE descendants when it distinguishes between ordinary success, greater success, and critical payoff.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
`Tales` often has strong fiction but can still drift into vague result reading. The game benefits when the table knows what one success versus three successes means by default.

**Proposal:**  
Use this as the default interpretive ladder for non-combat procedures:

- `0 successes`: failure
- `1 success`: limited success
- `2 successes`: complete success
- `3+ successes`: critical success

**Status:**  
Already a strong fit and already partly migrated in current manuscript work.

### 2. Declared Effort Levels

**FL2e value:**  
FL2e's `Normal / Challenging / Difficult` threshold framing is excellent because it lets the table choose not only whether to roll, but what level of payoff is being chased.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
This is one of the cleanest small-core improvements still worth importing. It gives players a voluntary way to ask for more from a roll without inventing ad hoc modifiers every time.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
Add an optional declared effort ladder before suitable checks:

- `Standard`: 1 success needed
- `Bold`: 2 successes needed for the upgraded payoff
- `Desperate`: 3 successes needed for the exceptional payoff

If the player rolls at least 1 success but misses the higher threshold, they still get the ordinary result unless the procedure says otherwise.

**Best use cases:**

- crafting something fine rather than merely functional
- gaining more than basic information
- making a faster escape
- closing a negotiation with extra leverage
- executing a score quietly rather than merely successfully

**Recommendation:**  
High-value graft.

### 3. Only One Chance

**FL2e value:**  
The "you get one real try until circumstances change" rule is one of the best anti-grind procedures in the game.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
It prevents skill spam, protects tension, and forces fictional repositioning.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
State plainly:

- once a roll has been made and any push is resolved, that approach is spent
- the same character cannot simply roll again for the same objective
- a new attempt requires a changed method, changed position, changed tool, changed helper, changed time pressure, or changed fictional circumstances

**Recommendation:**  
High-value graft. Low cost.

### 4. Group Roll Procedures

**FL2e value:**  
FL2e distinguishes between:

- one character rolling for the group
- everyone rolling when everyone must individually succeed

That separation is clean and useful.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
`Tales` would benefit from clearer shared-action handling, especially for travel, stealth, outlaw operations, and settlement labor.

**Proposal for Tales:**

- if the group succeeds or fails together, one lead character rolls and others help
- if every member must personally get through, everyone rolls
- extra successes from one character may cover failed companions if the fiction supports it

**Recommendation:**  
High-value graft.

### 5. Negative Dice

**FL2e value:**  
Negative skill dice are elegant in a very simulationist game.

**Why it is risky for Tales:**  
It adds a second mental step and is easy to forget. It also pushes the game toward more exact probability engineering than `Tales` usually wants.

**Proposal:**  
Do not import as a general rule.

**Recommendation:**  
Reject.

## Skill System

### 1. Push Logic Framed by Consequences

**FL2e value:**  
FL2e is very clear that pushing is not just "roll again." It is a conscious escalation with known risk.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
The current `Tales` push language can still be improved by emphasizing changed fictional commitment, not only mechanical rerolling.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
Whenever a roll is pushed, require the player to say how the character presses harder:

- more force
- more speed
- more exposure
- more emotional commitment
- more collateral risk

This is a writing and procedure improvement more than a mechanical change.

**Recommendation:**  
Good low-cost graft.

### 2. Help Has Practical Limits

**FL2e value:**  
FL2e caps help and requires narrative presence.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
This prevents absurd pile-ons and keeps scenes intelligible.

**Proposal for Tales:**

- helpers must be present and meaningfully able to help
- maximum of three helpers
- each helper gives `+1 die`
- the GM may reduce effective help if the scene is cramped, rushed, secretive, or socially delicate

**Recommendation:**  
Strong graft.

### 3. Gear Gives Only One Main Bonus

**FL2e value:**  
FL2e avoids bonus stacking from multiple tools.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
This is a very good anti-bloat rule. It protects tool use without turning every roll into inventory optimization.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
For any non-combat roll, only the single best applicable gear bonus applies unless a rule explicitly says otherwise.

**Recommendation:**  
Strong graft.

### 4. Gear Wear and Repair

**FL2e value:**  
Hard use can degrade tools and weapons, and repair matters.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
This is useful in frontier play, but `Tales` should not apply it universally. It works best where wear is dramatic and materially meaningful.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
Add a light wear rule for:

- ropes
- wagons and harness
- lockpicks
- digging tools
- saddles and tack
- firearms in foul conditions
- hideout or ranch equipment under strain

Use it only:

- after pushed hard-use rolls
- after travel disasters
- after violent misuse
- after repeated neglect

Do not make players tick durability every ordinary day.

**Recommendation:**  
Conditional graft, but good.

## Combat System

### 1. NPC Morale and Rout

**FL2e value:**  
The morale procedure is one of the best small combat rules in the book. It stops every fight from becoming a fight to the last corpse.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
This is especially valuable in western play. Most people are not fanatics. Deputies, ranch hands, rustlers, miners, drunks, and hired gunmen should often break before they die.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
Add a simple NPC-side morale check when:

- the leader goes down
- half the group is down or fleeing
- the group is badly outmatched
- a terrifying display or slaughter breaks confidence

On failure they:

- flee
- surrender
- scatter
- freeze up and take penalties if trapped

**Recommendation:**  
Very strong graft. Likely one of the best combat imports available.

### 2. Sneak Attack / Ambush Procedure

**FL2e value:**  
FL2e cleanly distinguishes surprise, sneak attack, and ambush.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
Western play wants this constantly:

- alley shootings
- laying for a posse
- dawn raids
- slipping up to a sentry

**Proposal for Tales:**  
Add a short ambush procedure:

- opposed stealth / awareness check
- success grants one free opening action or denies active defense
- full ambush by a group gives a first-round positional edge

**Recommendation:**  
Strong graft.

### 3. Zone Traits

**FL2e value:**  
`Open`, `Rough`, `Cramped`, and blocked or obscured borders are simple and useful.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
This would improve chases, saloon fights, canyon fights, and horseback gunplay without requiring a map-heavy game.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
Keep the current range structure, but let scenes carry 1-3 zone traits:

- `Open`
- `Rough`
- `Cramped`
- `Obscured`
- `Elevated`

These can modify:

- movement
- mounted use
- cover
- line of sight
- heavy weapon handling

**Recommendation:**  
Strong graft if kept light.

### 4. Rerolled Initiative Every Round

**FL2e value:**  
Dynamic initiative creates momentum shifts.

**Why it is mixed for Tales:**  
It is exciting, but it adds handling overhead. In a gunfight-heavy western game, some groups will like the volatility and others will find it slows play.

**Proposal:**  
Keep as an optional module, not as the default.

**Recommendation:**  
Conditional only.

### 5. Expanded Fast / Slow Tactical Menu

**FL2e value:**  
FL2e has many useful minor combat actions.

**Why it is risky for Tales:**  
Importing too many of them would overcomplicate the game.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
Select only a few high-value additions:

- `Aim`
- `Retreat`
- `Brace`
- `Interpose` or `Protect`
- `Disarm`
- `Shove`

Avoid importing a whole close-combat maneuver catalog.

**Recommendation:**  
Selective graft only.

## Talent System

### 1. Talent Grouping by Function

**FL2e value:**  
FL2e organizes talents by kin, profession, and general groups. Even if the trees are too large for `Tales`, the organizational principle is good.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
`Tales` would benefit from cleaner categorization, especially if the talent list keeps growing.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
Organize talents in clearer groups:

- background or heritage talents
- combat talents
- frontier talents
- social talents
- trade / craft talents
- outlaw / law talents

This is mostly editorial, but it improves learnability.

**Recommendation:**  
Good graft.

### 2. Stronger Mentor Requirement

**FL2e value:**  
FL2e makes teachers matter.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
This is one of the most valuable long-campaign disciplines in FL2e. It ties advancement to place, relationships, and cost.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
Keep the slower training rules and lean into them harder:

- many talents should require a teacher, practice context, or narrative access
- some frontier talents can be self-taught slowly
- rare social, legal, medical, or technical talents should need a tutor

**Recommendation:**  
Very strong graft.

### 3. Multi-Rank Path Talents

**FL2e value:**  
FL2e's path talents are rich and characterful.

**Why it is risky for Tales:**  
Five-rank trees are too much for the current talent philosophy of `Tales`, which favors broader two-rank talents.

**Proposal:**  
Do not import full path trees.

Instead:

- use them as inspiration when rewriting weak talents
- let stronger `Advanced` ranks feel like a meaningful capstone

**Recommendation:**  
Reject as a direct graft. Use only as design fuel.

### 4. Talent Gating by Prior Growth

**FL2e value:**  
Some FL2e talents unlock only after broader growth.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
This is useful for especially weighty talents.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
For exceptional talents only, require:

- a minimum relevant ability
- a mentor, organization, or life event
- possibly previous total XP spent

Use this sparingly, for example:

- elite gunfighter techniques
- advanced surgery
- legal mastery
- industrial engineering

**Recommendation:**  
Conditional, limited use.

## Economy

### 1. Supply / Availability Ratings

**FL2e value:**  
The distinction between `Common / Uncommon / Rare / Extremely Rare` is simple and powerful.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
This is one of the best economy grafts available. It makes towns feel different without requiring a full market simulation.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
Add availability tags to major goods and services, modified by settlement traits such as:

- `Law`
- `Mercantile`
- `Isolation`
- `Rail connection`
- `Military presence`
- `Frontier roughness`

Use this especially for:

- weapons
- ammunition
- horses
- tack
- medical treatment
- lockpicks
- explosives
- legal services
- tutors
- fences and illicit goods buyers

**Recommendation:**  
Very strong graft.

### 2. Scarcity Inflates Price

**FL2e value:**  
FL2e treats rare goods as more expensive when actually found.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
This is historically and dramatically appropriate. A frontier town should not sell everything at book price.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
If an item is scarce in the current settlement:

- add `+25%` for merely uncommon
- add `+50% to +100%` for genuinely rare or urgent demand
- reduce price only where there is surplus, competition, or desperate sellers

**Recommendation:**  
Strong graft.

### 3. Services as a Real Economy Layer

**FL2e value:**  
FL2e prices services, not just objects.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
This matters a lot in a western game where the economy is full of:

- stabling
- laundering
- baths
- doctoring
- blacksmith work
- messengers
- guards
- guides
- teachers

**Proposal for Tales:**  
Expand the service lists and anchor them by town type. This gives cash a stronger campaign role and helps distinguish settlements.

**Recommendation:**  
High-value graft.

### 4. Consumables as Resource Dice

**FL2e value:**  
Resource dice are elegant for arrows, torches, food, and water.

**Why it is mixed for Tales:**  
Useful in wilderness campaigns, but too survival-heavy as a global default.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
Use only as an optional frontier hardship module, best for:

- remote expeditions
- mountain crossings
- cavalry patrols
- outlaw flight in bad country

Do not make every normal campaign track food and water this way.

**Recommendation:**  
Optional module only.

### 5. Quality Tiers

**FL2e value:**  
Quality creates a good middle layer between "normal gear" and "artifact."

**Why it helps Tales:**  
The Old West absolutely wants distinctions between cheap, standard, quality, and exceptional gear.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
Use light quality grades:

- `Poor`
- `Standard`
- `Fine`

These can affect:

- price
- wear resistance
- resale value
- social impression for clothing, tack, or guns

Avoid turning this into extra dice types unless necessary.

**Recommendation:**  
Strong graft.

### 6. Teacher and Hireling Pricing

**FL2e value:**  
Training, labor, and specialist access are priced directly.

**Why it helps Tales:**  
This is extremely useful for a town-centered western campaign. It turns advancement and local infrastructure into economic decisions.

**Proposal for Tales:**  
Add stronger price anchors for:

- teachers
- doctors
- blacksmiths
- guides
- guards
- ranch hands
- stable hands
- cooks
- outfit workers

**Recommendation:**  
Strong graft.

## What Should Be Studied, Not Imported

These FL2e systems are valuable as reference material, but should not be moved over whole:

- full Willpower engine
- five-rank talent architecture
- full consumable resource-die baseline
- full stronghold construction ledger
- detailed raw-material crafting chains
- high-frequency wear and upkeep accounting
- full survival procedure density from journeys and camp routines

They are useful because they show how to build pressure. They are not useful when copied directly into `Tales`.

## Priority Order

If these grafts are implemented in phases, the best order is:

1. `Declared effort levels`
2. `Only one chance`
3. `Group roll procedures`
4. `NPC morale and rout`
5. `Supply / availability ratings`
6. `Scarcity price modifiers`
7. `Service price expansion`
8. `Gear wear / repair for hard use`
9. `Talent organization improvements`
10. `Optional modules: consumables, rerolled initiative, quality grades`

## Final Recommendation

The strongest FL2e improvements for `Tales of the Old West 2e` are not the large systems. They are the small procedures that make play cleaner and sharper:

- clearer success thresholds
- clearer permission for harder asks
- one real attempt per approach
- better handling of group action
- better morale and surrender behavior
- better access and scarcity rules for frontier economies
- stronger teacher-based advancement and service pricing

That is the right level of grafting.

`Tales` should become more decisive, more grounded, and more procedurally reliable. It should not become heavier than its own design wants.
