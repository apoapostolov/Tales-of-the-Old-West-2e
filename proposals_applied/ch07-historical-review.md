# Chapter 07 — Historical Review

## Summary

A line-by-line review of **Chapter 7: The West in the 1870s** identifying factual errors, instances where modern political correctness softens the historical record, and real historical facts that are absent and should be considered for inclusion.

---

## SECTION A — Historical Inaccuracies

These are factual errors in the text that should be corrected.

### A-1. Fort Sumter Is in South Carolina, Not North Carolina

> **Current text:** "The first shots were fired at Fort Sumter, North Carolina, when Confederate forces attacked a federal fort."

Fort Sumter is in Charleston Harbor, **South Carolina**. This is a straightforward factual error.

### A-2. Smith & Wesson Did Not "Invent Their Revolver" in 1854

> **Current text:** "1854: Smith & Wesson invent their revolver."

Samuel Colt patented the first practical revolver in **1836**. Smith & Wesson introduced their first cartridge revolver (the Model 1) in **1857**, after Colt's patent expired. In 1854, Horace Smith and Daniel Wesson were developing lever-action pistols (the Volcanic). The entry either needs a corrected date (1857) or should be reframed to credit what S&W actually did—introduce a **cartridge-firing** revolver, which was the real innovation.

### A-3. El Grito Was a Revolt Against Spain, Not France

> **Current text:** "El Grito… from the shout that urged the people of Mexico to revolt against the French who had defeated the Spanish for control of Mexico."

El Grito de Dolores (September 16, 1810) was Father Miguel Hidalgo's call to revolt against **Spanish colonial rule**. The French intervention in Mexico did not begin until **1861–1867**, over fifty years later. This is a significant error.

### A-4. Wyoming Women's Suffrage Was 1869, Not 1870

> **Current text (under 1870):** "Wyoming Territory passes a law granting women the vote…"

Wyoming Territory passed its women's suffrage law on **December 10, 1869**. It appears under the 1870 heading. It should be moved to 1869 or the date clarified.

### A-5. The Homestead Act Was Signed in 1862, Not 1863

> **Current text:** "1863: The Homestead Act is passed into law on January 1st"

President Lincoln signed the Homestead Act on **May 20, 1862**. It took _effect_ on January 1, 1863. The timeline entry conflates the signing with the effective date and files it under the wrong year.

### A-6. The Transcontinental Railroad Description Is Misleading

> **Current text:** "The Central Pacific and Union Pacific Railroads are completed, linking Omaha in the east to San Francisco on the west coast. The completed line is known as the Union Pacific Railroad."

- The two railroads met at **Promontory Summit, Utah**, on May 10, 1869. The Central Pacific ran from **Sacramento** (not San Francisco) to Promontory. A ferry service connected Sacramento to San Francisco.
- The combined route was called the **First Transcontinental Railroad**, not "the Union Pacific Railroad." The Union Pacific was only the eastern half (Omaha to Promontory).

### A-7. Timeline Chronological Disorder

The timeline jumps around: 1874–1876 appears before 1867; 1877 before 1869; 1878 before 1870, etc. While possibly an intentional layout choice, it reads as careless and is confusing for reference use. Consider reordering into strict chronological sequence.

### A-8. The Bison "90 Years of Hunting" Claim

> **Current text (1889):** "After 90 years of hunting, the American bison, once numbering 60 million, is all but wiped out"

90 years back from 1889 is 1799. While bison were hunted for centuries, the **industrial-scale slaughter** that actually destroyed the herds began in earnest in the **1860s–1870s** with the railroads and the hide trade. The "90 years" framing obscures that the near-extinction happened in roughly **20 years** of concentrated killing. The 60 million figure is on the high end of estimates (30–60 million is the accepted range).

### A-9. Formatting Error: "Smoky Hills" as a Section Heading

> **Current text:** `### Smoky Hills to the west.`

This appears to be a continuation of the Kansas landscape description that has been accidentally formatted as a Markdown heading.

## SECTION B — Modern Political Correctness / Sanitization

These are places where the text softens, omits, or sanitizes the historical record in ways that weaken the chapter's stated goal of honest history.

### B-1. Texas Rangers Described Too Mildly

> **Current text:** "militias such as the famous Texas Rangers were formed to protect local communities from crime and Native American raids, but were frequently as lawless as those they were supposedly protecting against."

The Texas Rangers have a well-documented history of atrocities against both Mexican and Mexican-American communities and Native Americans. They conducted extrajudicial killings, participated in ethnic cleansing along the Rio Grande, and committed massacres (e.g., the Porvenir Massacre of 1918, slightly outside the period, but the pattern was established decades earlier). "Frequently as lawless" significantly understates this. The Rangers were, at times, a state-sanctioned instrument of racial terror.

### B-2. Sexual Violence Against Enslaved Women Is Entirely Absent

The chapter discusses slavery in some depth but never mentions the epidemic of sexual violence against enslaved women. Rape by slaveholders was legal, routine, and economically incentivized—children born of these assaults were enslaved, increasing the owner's "property." This was not a marginal phenomenon; it was structural to the institution. The DNA record confirms its pervasiveness. For a chapter that aims to not "whitewash" history, this is a glaring omission.

### B-3. The Enslaved Section Quotes "Jolly Times" Without Sufficient Context

The Josiah Henson quote about "jolly Christmas times" is historically legitimate, but the framing could more clearly note that these moments were **survival strategies and psychological resistance**, not evidence that slavery had a pleasant side. The follow-up sentence ("the jolly times… didn't prevent Henson from running away") helps, but the passage still risks reading as "slavery was complicated—there were good times too." Henson himself was clear that these moments existed _despite_ slavery's horror, not alongside it.

### B-4. The "Five Civilized Tribes" Term Needs More Context

The text uses "so-called" before "Five Civilized Tribes" but doesn't explain that the term is a colonial construct that implied other tribes were _uncivilized_—and that this manufactured distinction was used to justify different treatment. It's worth a brief note.

### B-5. Reconstruction Is Called "Restoration"

> **Current text:** "The postwar Restoration Program, designed to help newly emancipated African Americans get the foothold they needed, was fraught with political infighting and did not go nearly far enough."

The standard term is **Reconstruction**, not "Restoration". Beyond the naming issue, this description is deeply sanitized. Reconstruction was actively and violently dismantled by white supremacist groups (the KKK, White Leagues, Red Shirts) through terrorism, murder, and voter suppression. It didn't just "not go far enough"—it was systematically destroyed.

### B-6. Chinese Immigrant Section Is Extremely Brief

An entire community's experience in the West is covered in one short paragraph. The depth given to Native American and African American history (rightfully) makes the brevity of the Chinese section conspicuous. The text mentions riots and massacres but says nothing about the specific conditions—the exploitative contract labor system, the Foreign Miners' Tax, the queue-cutting violence, or the systematic legal exclusion that culminated in the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882.

### B-7. Anti-Mexican Violence and Land Theft Are Absent

The Mexico section covers cultural history well but entirely omits the massive, systematic dispossession of Mexican-American landowners after the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo. Despite treaty guarantees, the vast majority of Mexican-American families lost their land through legal manipulation, discriminatory taxation, and intimidation. Anti-Mexican lynching was widespread—between 1848 and 1928, thousands of Mexicans and Mexican Americans were lynched in the Southwest.

## SECTION C — Uncomfortable Historical Facts That Should Be Added

These are real, documented historical realities that the chapter does not address and that would strengthen its authenticity and stated commitment to honest history.

### C-1. The California Indian Genocide

California's Native population dropped from roughly 150,000 in 1846 to approximately 30,000 by 1870. This was not passive decline—it was state-sponsored extermination. California's first governor, Peter Burnett, declared in 1851 that "a war of extermination will continue to be waged… until the Indian race becomes extinct." The state legislature funded militia campaigns specifically to kill Native people. The California Act for the Government and Protection of Indians (1850) legalized the kidnapping, indenture, and forced labor of Native Americans, including children.

### C-2. The Long Walk of the Navajo (1864)

In 1864, the U.S. Army under Kit Carson forcibly marched approximately 8,000–9,000 Navajo people 300 miles to the Bosque Redondo reservation at Fort Sumner, New Mexico. Hundreds died on the march; hundreds more died at the reservation from disease, starvation, and exposure. They were held for four years before being allowed to return. This is a defining event for the Navajo nation and occurs squarely within the chapter's timeframe.

### C-3. Buffalo Extermination as Deliberate Policy

The chapter mentions the near-extinction of the bison but frames it as "hunting." In reality, the slaughter was explicitly encouraged by the U.S. military and politicians as a strategy to subjugate the Plains tribes. General Philip Sheridan reportedly told the Texas legislature that buffalo hunters "have done more to settle the vexed Indian question than the entire regular army." Colonel Richard Irving Dodge said: "Kill every buffalo you can. Every buffalo dead is an Indian gone." This was not careless over-hunting—it was deliberate economic warfare.

### C-4. Native American Boarding Schools

Beginning in the late 1870s (squarely in the game's period), the U.S. government began forcibly removing Native American children from their families and sending them to boarding schools. The Carlisle Indian Industrial School, founded in 1879 by Captain Richard Henry Pratt, operated under the explicit philosophy: **"Kill the Indian, save the man."** Children were forbidden to speak their languages, practice their religions, or maintain cultural ties. Abuse, disease, and death were rampant. Mass graves have been discovered at several former school sites.

### C-5. Convict Leasing and Black Codes

Immediately after emancipation, Southern and Western states passed "Black Codes"—laws designed to criminalize Black life and funnel freed people back into forced labor through the convict leasing system. Minor offenses (vagrancy, loitering, being unemployed) carried heavy sentences, and convicts were leased to private businesses under conditions often worse than antebellum slavery. Mortality rates were staggering. The Thirteenth Amendment's exception clause ("except as punishment for crime") made this legal.

### C-6. Scalp Bounties

Multiple states, territories, and the Mexican government offered cash bounties for Native American scalps. California, Texas, and several Mexican states all had scalp bounty programs at various points. Some bounty hunters (such as James Kirker and John Joel Glanton) made it a profession, and were not always selective about whose scalps they took—Mexican civilians were also murdered and scalped for bounty payment.

### C-7. Anti-Miscegenation Laws

Laws prohibiting interracial marriage were widespread across Western states and territories. These laws varied by state but commonly targeted Black-white, Native-white, and Asian-white unions. They were actively enforced and carried criminal penalties.

### C-8. Sundown Towns

Many Western communities openly expelled or prohibited non-white residents after dark—or entirely. These "sundown towns" posted signs, passed local ordinances, and enforced exclusion through violence. This was not limited to the South; it was widespread across the West.

### C-9. The Chinese Exclusion Act (1882)

The chapter's timeline includes the 1868 Burlingame Treaty allowing Chinese immigration but omits the **Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882**—the first U.S. law to ban immigration by an entire ethnic group. This is a critical event that should appear in the timeline, especially since it falls within the game's period.

### C-10. Lynching as Racialized Terror

The chapter mentions "lynchings and murder were all too common" in passing, in the context of general lawlessness. This framing obscures the reality that lynching in the West was disproportionately used as a tool of racial terror against Black, Mexican, Chinese, and Native American people. It was often public, ritualized, and carried out with the implicit or explicit support of law enforcement.

### C-11. Prostitution and Sexual Trafficking

Prostitution was one of the largest industries in Western towns and mining camps. Many women were trafficked, coerced, or left with no economic alternatives. Chinese women were particularly targeted—the Page Act of 1875 was ostensibly about preventing "immoral" immigration but was used to restrict Chinese women specifically. Conditions in brothels and "cribs" were often horrific.

### C-12. Environmental Devastation

Hydraulic mining in California destroyed entire mountainsides and choked rivers with sediment, devastating downstream communities and ecosystems. Mercury used in gold processing poisoned waterways. Logging stripped forests. The environmental cost of Western expansion was immense and immediate, not a modern concern projected backward.

### C-13. The Sand Creek Massacre Details

The chapter mentions Sand Creek but understates what happened. Colonel John Chivington's troops attacked a peaceful, unarmed camp flying both a U.S. flag and a white flag of surrender. Soldiers mutilated corpses, including women and children—taking scalps, fingers, and genitalia as trophies. Chivington reportedly displayed these at a Denver theater to applause. A Congressional investigation condemned the attack, but no one was punished.

### C-14. Slavery Among Some Native American Tribes

Some Native American tribes, particularly the Cherokee, Chickasaw, Choctaw, Creek, and Seminole (the "Five Civilized Tribes"), practiced chattel slavery of African Americans, modeled on the Southern system. Some tribal members brought enslaved people with them on the Trail of Tears. The Cherokee Nation did not emancipate enslaved people until 1863, and the integration of Black freedmen into tribal nations remained contentious for generations. This complicates the narrative and is worth including for historical honesty.

## Notes for the Author

- The chapter's stated intent—to present honest, unsanitized history—is commendable and largely well-executed. These proposals are in service of that intent, not in opposition to it.
- Adding uncomfortable truths does not require graphic or gratuitous detail. Brief, factual acknowledgment is often sufficient for a gaming sourcebook.
- Several of these topics (boarding schools, convict leasing, sexual violence, California genocide) are increasingly well-documented and widely discussed in modern historiography. Their absence would be noticed by historically informed readers.
- The timeline's most glaring factual errors (Fort Sumter in NC, El Grito against France, S&W in 1854) should be corrected regardless of other editorial decisions.
