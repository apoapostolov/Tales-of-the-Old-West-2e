# Chapter 25 — Regional Landscapes

> _“THE COUNTRY EAST OF THE PECOS LAY FLAT AND PALE TO A HORIZON THAT DID NOT SEEM TO COME ANY CLOSER FOR THE RIDING. A MAN COULD SEE A MOUNTED FIGURE TEN MILES OFF AND NOT KNOW WHETHER IT WAS AN HOUR AWAY OR FOUR.”_

The American West is not one country. It is a dozen distinct ecologies, each with its own light, weather, plants, animals, terrain, and seasonal rhythm. The Llano Estacado is not the Black Hills, the Sonoran is not the Sierra, the Great Basin is not the Pacific Northwest. What follows walks through the major regions a man of the 1870s might cross. Each section gives the country's sensory palette — light, sound, smell, texture — together with its key plants and animals, its weather, and its seasonal rhythm.

---

## 1. The Llano Estacado — Southern High Plains

The southern high plains of the Texas Panhandle and eastern New Mexico. A vast, level, near-treeless country, named by the Spanish for the staked markers needed to navigate it. The Comanche heartland through the early 1870s; broken by the army in 1874–75; opened to ranching after.

The Llano's defining feature is its light. The sky is enormous because there is nothing on the ground to diminish it. The horizon is a perfect circle. The light at noon is brutal and white; the light at sunset is the long red shadow. Storm light — the green-black sky before a plains thunderstorm — is specific to this country and unforgettable.

Wind is almost always the country's voice. Long, low, sometimes rising to a howl. Other sounds: cattle when ranching has come, the meadowlark in spring, the killdeer's call, the coyote at dusk, silence otherwise. After rain the country smells of wet caliche and wet sage. In summer, of hot dry grass and the sweetness of crushed grama. In winter the cold scrubs the smell out of the country.

Underfoot is short-grass prairie — buffalo grass and blue grama in the better years, bare caliche and red dirt in the dry years. The country is not flat in the strict sense; it rises and falls in long slow swells. _Playas_ (shallow temporary lakes) catch the rain in low spots and dry to a white crust by midsummer.

**Plants and animals.** Buffalo grass, blue grama, side-oats grama — short, dense, nutritious; the best cattle range when watered. Almost no trees on the open plains. Mesquite and a few hackberry along the dry watercourses. Larger trees only in the canyons (Palo Duro Canyon's cottonwoods, the bottoms of the Canadian and Brazos drainages). Buffalo (gone by 1875), pronghorn, prairie dogs in vast colonies (_dog towns_ of hundreds of acres), coyote, occasional gray wolf. Burrowing owl in the prairie dog towns, meadowlark, killdeer, lark bunting, mountain plover. Hawks and eagles overhead.

The Llano weather is extreme. Summer days over a hundred. Winter nights below zero. Hailstorms that flatten what they hit. Tornadoes in spring. Blizzards in winter. Long droughts (the 1870s included severe drought years). The weather is not a backdrop; it is an actor.

**The canyons.** The Llano's flat surface is broken by deep canyons cut by the headwaters of the Red, the Brazos, and the Canadian. Palo Duro Canyon is the most famous. The canyons offer water, wood, and shelter from the wind; they were the Comanche winter refuge. The Battle of Palo Duro Canyon (1874) ended the Comanche resistance when Mackenzie's troops killed the band's horse herd at the bottom of the canyon.

> The country south of the Canadian was as flat as a table for fifty miles. The grass was high enough in May to brush a horse's belly and short enough by August to show the white caliche under it. There were no trees and no landmarks except the playas, which dried each summer to a white pan in the middle of nothing. The wind worked all day at the same low note. The lightning on the Llano walked. It came down in single slow bolts that stood for a full second burning between sky and ground. The thunder followed at its proper interval. The horses stood with their tails to it and waited.

## 2. The Northern Plains — Dakotas, Eastern Montana, Wyoming

The high plains north of the Platte. Lakota and Cheyenne country in the 1870s; broken by the Great Sioux War of 1876–77; opened to cattle and homestead through the 1880s.

The northern light is colder, thinner, more northerly. Long summer days (sixteen hours of light in midsummer at the Canadian border). Long winter nights. The northern light has a hard edge to it that the southern light does not have.

Wind is the same constant companion, blowing colder. Other sounds: the pronghorn's whistle, the buffalo's grunt (when the herds remained), the long howl of the prairie wolf. The crack of trees in deep winter cold. Silence at forty below. The smell of sagebrush is stronger than on the Llano. The smell of buffalo (the herds smelled like nothing else — a strong musk visible from a mile downwind) was a defining feature of the northern plains until the herds went. Woodsmoke on the wind, miles from the source.

Mixed-grass prairie underfoot: taller and more varied grasses than the southern plains. Sagebrush in the drier basins. The country rolls in long swells, broken by buttes (flat-topped erosion remnants standing several hundred feet above the surrounding country) and by the Black Hills (the Lakota's sacred mountains, rising from the plains in northwestern South Dakota).

**Plants and animals.** Western wheatgrass, blue grama, needle-and-thread, big bluestem in the better bottoms. Big sagebrush throughout the drier country. Cottonwood, willow, box elder along rivers. Ponderosa pine in the Black Hills and on the buttes' tops. Aspen in the higher country. Buffalo (gone from open plains by 1883), pronghorn, mule deer, white-tail in river bottoms, elk in higher country, grizzly bear (declining), gray wolf (declining), coyote, beaver (much depleted from trapping era), prairie dog. Sharp-tailed grouse, sage grouse, golden eagle, ferruginous hawk, sandhill crane in migration, Canada goose.

The weather is brutal. Summer storms violent. Winter blizzards deadly. The temperature can drop sixty degrees in twelve hours when an Arctic front pushes south. The chinook (the warm winter wind that descends from the Rockies) can raise a winter temperature fifty degrees in an hour. The "winter of the blue snow" (1886–87) ended the open-range cattle era by killing hundreds of thousands of cattle. The 1870s winters were milder but still capable of killing the unprepared.

**The Black Hills.** A small mountain range (about a hundred miles long) rising from the plains in northwestern South Dakota. Sacred to the Lakota (Pahá Sápa, the heart of everything that is). Heavily forested with ponderosa pine. Granite peaks (Harney Peak / Black Elk Peak the highest). Cool streams, abundant game, gold (discovered 1874, triggering the gold rush that broke the 1868 treaty and led to the Great Sioux War).

> They came up out of the breaks of the Belle Fourche in the late afternoon and the Hills stood ahead of them, dark with their pines, rising out of the brown plains like an island. The grass under their horses was the western wheatgrass, gray-green and not yet headed out. A pronghorn watched them from a rise a quarter mile to the east and trotted off when they did not stop. The wind was at their backs and smelled of pine and of the cold water in the streams that ran down out of the Hills. Two ravens went over them headed into the Hills, calling to each other in the long slow voices of ravens. _They are headed where we are headed,_ McKinney said.

## 3. The Texas Hill Country and South Texas

The country south of the Llano: the limestone-and-oak Hill Country (Edwards Plateau) and the brushland of South Texas (the Brasada). German settlers, Hispanic Tejanos, Anglo cattlemen. Comanche raiding ground until the 1870s; King Ranch country through and after.

Southern light. The sun is high and hot in summer. Spring and fall are the country's good seasons — mild days, cool nights, the bluebonnets (in spring) turning the hills blue. Cicadas in summer (a thick wall of sound from the trees in late afternoon). The white-winged dove. The chuck-will's-widow at evening. Wind in the live oaks. The bawl of cattle moving through brush. The language of the country is mixed — Spanish, German, English, all heard at the same time in many towns.

The smells are cedar (juniper, technically), live oak, and the dusty-dry brush of South Texas — mesquite, prickly pear, brasil, granjeno. After rain, a sharp green smell rises. Limestone underfoot in the Hill Country (the white caliche shows through the thin soil, the rocks break under the feet, the springs run cold and clear out of the limestone). South Texas brush is dense, thorny, low — moving cattle through the brasada was a hard, scratched, bloody work that produced the brush-popping cattle culture and its specific saddle, dress, and language.

**Plants and animals.** Live oak (massive, spreading, evergreen), pecan, juniper ("cedar"), cottonwood along streams. South Texas: mesquite, huisache, cenizo, brasil. Prickly pear (the dominant brush of South Texas), cholla, occasional sotol. Whitetail deer, javelina (peccary — small wild pig of the southwestern brush), mountain lion, occasional jaguar in the deep south (extirpated by the early twentieth century), armadillo. Wild turkey, white-winged dove, the green jay (in deep south Texas), the scissor-tailed flycatcher, the painted bunting.

Hot summers (over a hundred routine in July and August). Mild winters with occasional cold fronts ("blue northers") that drop the temperature dramatically in a few hours. Spring brings storms, sometimes tornadoes. Drought is recurrent — the 1870s included serious dry years.

**The cattle country.** The brush of South Texas was the cradle of the American cattle industry. The longhorn cattle ran half-wild through the brasada from the 1830s through the 1870s. Roundups gathered the half-feral cattle for the trail drives north. The vaquero tradition (Mexican and Tejano) was the foundation; the Anglo cowhand built on it.

> The Hill Country in May was as green as it ever was and the bluebonnets stood blue along the edges of the wagon road. They came down off the divide into the Pedernales bottoms and the live oaks made a shade so deep that the air under them was ten degrees cooler than the air on the open hill. The river ran clear over the limestone, six inches deep at the ford and faster than it looked. Cicadas were beginning in the heat. The German farmer at the ford was an old man with a white beard down to his second button. _Vorsicht beim Übergang,_ he said. _Das Wasser ist tiefer als es aussieht._

## East of the Pecos

> The country east of the Pecos lay flat and pale to a horizon that did not seem to come any closer for the riding. A man could see a mounted figure ten miles off and not know whether it was an hour away or four. There was no shade. There was no water for thirty miles in any direction the riders cared to name. A coyote had crossed their track in the night. They saw its prints in the morning, fresh, going south.

## 4. The Rocky Mountain West — Colorado, Wyoming, Idaho, Montana

The high country of the Rocky Mountains and their basins. Mining country (Colorado's silver, Idaho's gold). Cattle country in the foothills and the intermountain basins. Aspen and pine and granite.

Mountain light is sharp. The thin air at altitude makes the colors more saturated and the shadows harder. Distances are deceiving — what looks like a mile away is five miles in mountain country. In the high pines: silence broken by wind in the needles, the call of the gray jay, the running of mountain water. Avalanche thunder in spring. Rockfall in summer. The bugle of the bull elk in September — one of North America's most haunting wild sounds. Lightning-strike thunder rolling around the cirques.

The smells: pine resin in summer heat, aspen leaves in fall (a particular sweet-musty smell), snowmelt on rock, woodsmoke from a mountain camp. The smell of a high-country meadow in July, when the wildflowers are at their peak — lupine, paintbrush, columbine, glacier lily.

Granite and ponderosa under the boots. Aspen groves on the north slopes. Lodgepole pine in the middle elevations. Spruce and fir at higher elevation. Above timberline (about 11,000 feet in Colorado, lower in northern Idaho and Montana): tundra and talus. The high mountain country is broken by _parks_ (the mountain term for a large open meadow): South Park, Middle Park, North Park in Colorado; the Big Hole in Montana; the Lemhi Valley in Idaho. The parks were summer pasture and Native hunting grounds.

**Plants and animals.** Ponderosa pine (the lower forest), lodgepole pine (middle forest), Douglas fir, Engelmann spruce, subalpine fir, aspen, cottonwood in the bottoms. Elk, mule deer, bighorn sheep, mountain goat (in Idaho and Montana, not in Colorado), grizzly bear (declining; gone from Colorado by the 1970s but present in 1870s Montana and Idaho), black bear, mountain lion, bobcat, lynx, wolverine, marmot, pika, beaver. Bald eagle, golden eagle, the dipper (a small gray bird that walks underwater on stream bottoms; one of the West's loved birds), Steller's jay, gray jay, the white-tailed ptarmigan above timberline.

Mountain weather is its own climate. Summer afternoons in the high country bring thunderstorms almost daily — the moisture rises with the day's heat and breaks by three or four. Winter snow at altitude is feet deep and stays for months. The chinook can warm a mountain valley fifty degrees in hours. The high passes close in October and open in May.

**The mining camp in the mountains.** Most western mining was mountain mining. Leadville, Cripple Creek, Aspen, Telluride (Colorado); Silver City, Idaho City, Coeur d'Alene (Idaho); Helena, Butte, Virginia City (Montana); the Boise basin. The camps were established in the high country where the ore was, often at altitudes that strained work and health. Pneumonia killed miners as much as accidents did (see Chapter 18).

> The trail came up out of the Blue River in the afternoon and into the lodgepole and they rode for two hours in the long quiet shade of the trees. The needles were thick on the trail and the horses made no sound. They came out of the timber at a meadow at the head of a small tributary and the meadow was deep in summer flowers — paintbrush red and purple lupine and the white columbines along the creek. A bull elk was standing at the far edge with his head down in the grass. He had raised his rack from velvet and the antlers were still the color of polished hardwood. He saw them and lifted his head and watched them come and did not move. Above the rim the afternoon thunderhead was building toward its three-o'clock break. They had two hours.

## 5. The Southwest Deserts — Arizona, Southern New Mexico, West Texas

The arid country of the Sonoran, Chihuahuan, and Mojave deserts. Apache country. Hispanic and Native country. The stark, beautiful, dangerous landscape that has become the iconic western backdrop.

Desert light is the most extreme western light. The sun at noon is an enemy. The shadows are sharp-edged. The sunset paints the country impossibly in red and purple and gold. The starlight on a moonless desert night is bright enough to ride by. Quiet — the desert is quieter than other western countries. Wind in the creosote bushes. The scream of the elf owl (a small owl of the saguaro cactus). The bark of the coyote. The buzz of the rattlesnake at the wrong moment. The silence at noon is the country's signature.

Creosote bush after rain (the desert's defining smell — sharp, medicinal, instantly identifying). Mesquite smoke from a cooking fire. The tang of dry juniper. The dust of the road, which gets into everything. Sand, gravel, rock — the desert floor is rarely soft. Sharp-edged volcanic rock in some areas. Cactus spines that can pierce a leather glove. The country is broken by mountain ranges (the basin-and-range country of Arizona and New Mexico), each range rising abruptly from the desert floor and providing its own ecology — pinyon and juniper in the lower ranges, ponderosa and Douglas fir in the higher.

**Plants and animals.** Saguaro (the great columnar cactus of the Sonoran), barrel cactus, prickly pear, cholla. The saguaro grows only in a specific climatic zone of southern Arizona and northern Sonora. Mesquite in the washes. Cottonwood at springs. Joshua tree in the Mojave. Pinyon and juniper in the foothills. Yucca and ocotillo in the open desert. Coyote, kit fox, mule deer (in the foothills), bighorn sheep (in the desert mountains), javelina (Sonoran), desert cottontail, jackrabbit, ringtail, kangaroo rat, packrat. Western diamondback rattlesnake, Gila monster, desert tortoise, several iguana and whiptail lizards. Cactus wren, roadrunner, Gambel's quail, Gila woodpecker, Harris hawk (a desert hawk that hunts in cooperative groups), the elf owl in the saguaro.

Hot summers (over a hundred routine, over a hundred and fifteen possible). Cool winters in the lower desert (frost rare, daytime mild). Summer monsoon (July through September) brings violent afternoon thunderstorms — the country is dry until July, then floods in flash floods through August, then dry again. The arroyos (dry washes) can run a foot deep in twenty minutes when a thunderstorm hits the mountains miles away.

**The water.** Water is the desert's organizing principle. Settlements are at springs, rivers (the Rio Grande, the Gila, the Colorado, the Pecos, the Salt), or artificial water (tanks, cisterns). A man without knowledge of the country's water cannot cross the desert and survive (see Chapter 11).

> They came down out of the Whetstones in the afternoon and the Sulphur Springs Valley opened ahead of them, a long flat-bottomed basin between ranges, the floor of it stitched with creosote bush and the further mountains burning blue at the horizon. The temperature was a hundred and four. McGee had been measuring the water for two days and he measured it again and capped the canteen. _Tomorrow noon at Pearce,_ he said, _we drink it all._ A small whirlwind crossed the basin a mile to the south, lifting a column of dust two hundred feet and moving north at the pace of a slow-walking horse. They watched it go. It dissolved into the basin and the basin was still again.

## 6. The Pacific Northwest — Oregon, Washington, Northern Idaho

The wet country west of the Cascades and the dry country east of them. Two distinct ecologies under one regional name. Oregon Trail emigrants ended their journeys here through the 1840s and 1850s; by the 1870s the Willamette Valley was settled country.

- **West of the Cascades.** The Willamette Valley, the Puget Sound country, the Olympic Peninsula. Heavy forest. Rain. The wet green. The light is soft, often overcast. Long summer days in the dry summer (July through September); short winter days under near-permanent overcast. Douglas fir (the dominant tree, growing to enormous size), western red cedar, hemlock, bigleaf maple, alder along streams, vine maple in the understory. Wind in the Douglas fir. Rain on a tin roof. The varied thrush's haunting single note in the deep forest. Salmon splashing in spawning streams in fall. The smell of wet cedar, rotting log and forest duff, salt water near the coast, the smoke of green wood fires (the wood was hard to dry in the wet).
- **East of the Cascades.** The Columbia Plateau, the Palouse, eastern Oregon. Dry country. Sage and grassland. The rain shadow keeps the country dry. Brilliant light. Big sagebrush in the lower country, bunchgrasses, ponderosa pine in the foothills of the Cascades and the Blue Mountains. Wind in the sage and the grass. The sage grouse's booming in spring. Coyote. Sage — sweet, sharp, the country's defining smell. Wheat dust at harvest in the Palouse country.
- **The salmon rivers.** The Columbia, the Snake, and their tributaries carried the great salmon runs. The Native fisheries at Celilo Falls (until 1957) were the West's greatest fishing site. By the 1870s commercial canning was beginning to industrialize the salmon industry. The salmon shaped the Native cultures of the region (Nez Perce, Yakama, Wasco, Wishram, the coastal nations).

> The forest in the Willamette bottom in February was wet from canopy to ground. The Douglas firs went up two hundred feet and disappeared into the gray. The moss hung off every branch and the rain came down through the moss in slow large drops. The trail was a black mud the horses sank to the fetlock in. Halsey rode in his oilskin with his hat pulled to his ears. He could not have lit a match in this country if his life had depended on it. He could not see fifty feet through the rain and the trees and the falling water. His clothes had been wet for three days and he could not remember dry clothes. Oregon, he thought, again. A varied thrush called somewhere in the gray, a single hollow note that stood and went out. He rode on.

## 7. The California Country

The diverse country of California — coast, valley, foothill, mountain. The Sierra Nevada to the east. The Coast Range to the west. The Central Valley between. The Sacramento and San Joaquin rivers.

- **The Central Valley.** A vast flat valley between the Sierra and the Coast Range, drained by the Sacramento (north) and the San Joaquin (south). Hot dry summers. Mild wet winters. Grasslands originally; agricultural land through the late nineteenth century. In summer, the valley smells of sun on dry grass and of irrigation water in the fields. In winter, of wet earth. The tule fog (a thick low fog of late fall through early spring) is a defining feature.
- **The Sierra Nevada.** The granite range running the length of California. Snowfield in winter, brilliant in summer. Yosemite Valley, the Tuolumne Meadows, the high lake country, the giant sequoias of the western slope. Ponderosa pine, sugar pine, Douglas fir on the lower slopes. Red fir and lodgepole at higher elevations. Whitebark pine near timberline. The giant sequoia in scattered groves on the western slope. Mule deer, black bear, mountain lion, marmot, pika. Grizzly bear (extirpated from California by the 1920s but present in the 1870s). Bighorn sheep on the eastern escarpment.
- **The Coast.** The Pacific Coast: rocky, cold, fog-shrouded, with beaches and bluffs and the redwood forests of the northern California coast. The redwoods are the world's tallest trees, growing to over three hundred feet. The coastal redwood forest in the 1870s was being logged hard — the extraction of the redwoods was one of California's major industries. The understory of redwood sorrel and ferns under three-hundred-foot trees in constant cool fog is one of the West's distinctive ecosystems.

> The valley north of Stockton in late September was as dry as a country can be without burning. The grass was the color of bread crust and crackled when a horse walked through it. The oaks stood alone in the open country, their leaves dusty and their crowns the only shade for miles. The sky was a hard pale blue. The Sierras rose in the east, blue and not quite snowless even now. Cantwell stopped at one of the oaks and got down and led his horse into the shade and sat on the ground with his back against the trunk. The valley was silent in the heat. A red-tailed hawk circled high up above the next oak, hunting.

## 8. The Great Basin — Nevada, Western Utah

The vast interior basin between the Sierra Nevada and the Wasatch. No drainage to the sea. Sagebrush. Dry mountain ranges. Salt flats. The country the emigrants hated and feared on the Humboldt route to California.

Like the Sierra Nevada — sharp and clean light. The basins are bright. The mountain shadows are sharp against the basin floors. The basin-and-range topography: long valleys (twenty to fifty miles wide) running roughly north-south, each separated from the next by a narrow mountain range. The valleys are filled with sage. The mountains are dry, rising sharply, with pinyon-juniper at lower elevations and limber pine and bristlecone (the world's oldest living trees) at higher.

Wind in the sage — the basin's constant. The pinyon jay's call in the foothills. The horned lark on the flats. Coyote at evening. The smell is sage. Always sage.

- **Plants and animals.** Sagebrush — the defining plant. Pinyon and juniper in the foothills; the pinyon nut harvest was a major Native (Western Shoshone, Paiute, Washo) food source. Bristlecone pine at high elevation — the oldest individual trees on Earth (over 4,000 years). Pronghorn, mule deer, bighorn (on the steeper ranges), kit fox, coyote, pika, marmot. The Lahontan cutthroat in the cold streams of the western basin (much depleted by the 1870s).
- **The salt flats.** The remnants of ancient lakes (Lahontan in the west, Bonneville in the east). Brilliant white. Lethal to cross in summer because of heat and water absence. The Salt Lake itself, Pyramid Lake, the playas of the Black Rock Desert.

> The valley they crossed in the morning was forty miles wide and they could see the next range from any point in it. The sage was up to the horses' bellies and the smell of it filled their clothes. Nothing else grew in the valley but the sage and the wild rye in patches and a few rabbitbrush along the dry watercourses. The Toiyabes ran along the eastern side of the valley like a wall. They were forty miles long, ten thousand feet high, and the only timber on them was in the high canyons. The sun was already brutal at ten in the morning and they had thirty miles to go to the spring at the base of the range.

## 9. The Regions in Brief

- **Llano Estacado:** vast, level, treeless, the great sky, the wind, the playas, buffalo and pronghorn, the canyons.
- **Northern Plains:** rolling grass, sage, buffalo (until 1880s), brutal weather, the buttes, the Black Hills.
- **Texas Hill Country / South Texas brush:** limestone, live oak, cedar, German and Hispanic voices, the brasada, longhorn cattle.
- **Rocky Mountain West:** granite, pine and aspen and spruce, mountain parks, mining camps, summer storms, elk and bighorn.
- **Southwest Deserts:** creosote and saguaro, the brutal sun, the monsoon, the Apache country, springs as life.
- **Pacific Northwest:** the wet west, the dry east, Douglas fir and salmon and rain.
- **California:** valley, Sierra, coast, the redwoods, the dry summers and wet winters.
- **Great Basin:** sage, basin and range, the salt flats, the bristlecone pines.
