This appendix supports **Chapter 26 — The Yellowstone Line**. It covers the named NPCs and the most useful generic profiles for the example adventure at the back of the book.

The blocks below follow the stronger-NPC guidance from the Montana campaign notes in `../supplement-2-montana-campaign/research/07-npc-creation-helper.md`. The chapter expects ordinary player-character limits to be ignored for officers, scouts, fixers, major antagonists, and other people who can plausibly command the field.

## Named NPCs

### Captain Nathaniel Rowe

Rowe is a white man in his early forties, Army to the bone, narrow through the waist, and hard in the saddle. He reads as order held together by temper. He keeps his gloves clean longer than most field officers would manage and looks past a man before the man has finished speaking. In his view the PCs are useful irregular tools: necessary on a hard frontier, but not people he trusts until they prove they can hold their heads.

### Lieutenant Ezra Harlan

Harlan is a white man of twenty-seven, polished like cavalry brass and too proud of the uniform. He strains for command, stands too straight, smiles too quickly, and touches his belt when judged. He talks fast and half a rank above his station. The PCs are either rough useful people he can boast of leading or disreputable witnesses who must not see him break.

### Sergeant Owen Duffy

Duffy is an Irish-born white man near fifty, broad through the chest, weathered, and going gray under the brim. He looks built from old leather, smoke, and regulations remembered better than prayers. He wastes little motion and can hear trouble in a horse line the way other men hear it in gunfire. The PCs are either hands worth having on a bad road or mouths likely to complicate his work.

### Professor Amos Reed

Reed is a white man in his mid-thirties, spare, near-sighted, and permanently overtired. He hates incompetence more than discomfort and grows colder as the matter under discussion becomes more exact. His speech is educated and dry, then sharp when commerce starts meddling with measurement. The PCs are protection, labor, or witnesses depending on whether they prove loyal to money or to fact.

### Silas Vane

Vane is a white man around forty, well-fed without looking soft, careful with his clothes, and too well groomed for any honest trail position. He never seems hurried. He lowers his voice, steps close, and talks in easy persuasive sentences without naming who sent him. The PCs are purchasable assets until proven otherwise, and if they cannot be bought they must be divided.

### Eli Mercer

Mercer is a white man not yet thirty, neat-handed, narrow-shouldered, and too fond of bottle courage. He smiles too soon, laughs where no laughter is needed, and keeps touching pockets and papers to reassure himself. He speaks quickly and trims every sentence toward safety. The PCs are dangerous because they live closer to force than a liar can write his way out of.

### Thomas Keene

Keene is a white man in his early twenties, thin from bad sleep, bad whiskey, and worse nerves. He looks like the kind of young fool the frontier manufactures by putting weak ambition too near strong men. He sweats in cool air, glances over both shoulders before answering, and grips his sleeves as if he expects to be searched. The PCs are possible rescuers, buyers, or executioners.

### Shows-the-Ford

Shows-the-Ford is a Crow man in mature adulthood, lean in the saddle and hard to surprise. He sits a horse as if horse and rider settled the matter years ago. He does not fidget, does not explain himself twice, and does not offer emotion where position will do. The PCs are not yet important, but unlike the officers they may still be worth measuring correctly.

### Iron Hail

Iron Hail is a Lakota war leader in his thirties, already old enough to have lived by judgment rather than speed alone, but still quick enough to kill a slower man before the slower man names the threat. The men around him talk about him as someone who chooses where the blow will fall. He rides hard and speaks little. The PCs are part of the white line until they do something costly enough to prove otherwise.

A cavalry trooper is usually a young or middle-aged white enlisted man with sunburn on his neck, worn blue wool gone brown at the seams, and more road discipline than imagination. He trusts carbine, horse, and ration issue more than grand talk about the future of the territory.
