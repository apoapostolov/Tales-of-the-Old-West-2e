# 1870s: The Old West

The American West of the 1870s was a country built on iron, horse sweat, and staggering distance. It belonged to Mexican vaqueros descended from families who had run cattle in the borderlands for two centuries before the United States ever claimed the map. Black freedmen rode the southern plains as Buffalo Soldiers. Chinese laborers broke rock in the high passes with black powder and picks. German sodbusters broke the prairie, Irish navvies spiked down rail ties, and Cornish hard-rock miners bled in the dark beneath the mountains. Dozens of Native nations—each holding to their own language, their own law, and their own long memories of the land—fought brutal, desperate wars against the encroaching wire and the wheel.

This book is the record of that country. It is built from the cold facts of the ledger, not the painted backdrops of a theater.

It covers the mud-choked streets of the mining camps and the railhead cattle towns. It catalogs the cost of a wool blanket, the daily wage of a man swinging a hammer, and the casual, sudden violence that hung over the territories like a summer storm moving off the escarpment. It details the sound a herd makes when the cattle have gone four days on the Cimarron without water.

> The fever came up the second night. By the third he could not be got to drink. The doctor pulled the dressing back and looked at the wound and put it back the way it had been. He did not say anything to the woman who sat by the bed but he stood a moment longer than he needed to before he picked up his bag and went out.

> She sat by him through the fourth day. He spoke twice. The first time he asked for his mother. The second time he spoke a name she did not know and after that he did not speak again. The smell that had come into the room on the third day stayed in her hair after he was buried. She would wash and wash and at night smell it still on the pillow.

Welcome to the 1870s. The land does not care. That indifference is the story.
