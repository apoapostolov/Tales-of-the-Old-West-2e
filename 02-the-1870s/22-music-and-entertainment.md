# Chapter 22 — Music and Entertainment

> _“THE FIDDLER AT THE WAGON HAD QUIT AT SUPPER AND WAS ASLEEP UNDER THE WAGON.”_

The popular West has rendered music as a tin cup of coffee at the campfire and a lonesome harmonica at sundown. The reality was louder, more various, and more constant. The frontier ran on music. The trail hand sang to settle the herd. The homestead family sang at evening worship. The town fiddler played the Friday-night dance until the lamps were turned down at one in the morning. The German immigrant brought his concertina and his choral society. The Mexican household sang the corridos of the border war. The Black soldier at Fort Davis sang the spirituals his mother had taught him in Louisiana. The Cornish miner sang the chapel hymns of his village. The opera house in Leadville and the opera house in Tombstone booked Edwin Booth and Sarah Bernhardt and the Black Crook road company and pulled them off the Atchison train. Most of what the reader imagines as silence on the frontier was filled with sound the period made for itself.

---

## 1. The Fiddle and the Dance

The fiddle was the West's principal instrument. It was portable, it was loud, and one player could furnish a whole evening's dance. A fiddler in any frontier community of more than fifty people was within riding distance.

A factory fiddle from a mail-order house cost three to seven dollars in the 1870s. The traveling peddler sold them. A homemade fiddle, often badly made, often with a cigar box for a body, existed in poorer households. The professional fiddle (a German factory instrument or, rarely, an old Italian) cost twenty to a hundred dollars and was the fiddler's working capital. Strings were gut (the lower three) and steel (the top E). They broke. The fiddler carried spares wrapped in oiled paper in a small tin in his case. Rosin was a small block in the case; the bow was horsehair on a wooden stick.

The fiddler was a man, almost always. He learned the tunes by ear from his father, his uncle, his neighbor. He played without notation. His repertoire was a hundred or two hundred tunes carried entirely in his head and his fingers. He was paid in cash, in drink, in a cooked meal, in the privilege of dancing the last waltz with the prettiest girl. A working dance fiddler in a country county might earn five dollars a Saturday night plus supper. A town fiddler at a regular saloon engagement earned more — twenty to forty dollars a month plus tips. He was sometimes also a barber, a teamster, a cobbler. He was often known by his fiddle name (_Fiddlin' Bob, Fiddler Pete_) more than his proper name.

The fiddle tunes that travel through the western record:

- **Reels and breakdowns:** _Soldier's Joy, Devil's Dream, Old Joe Clark, Turkey in the Straw, Arkansas Traveler, Sally Ann, Soldier's Return, Cripple Creek, Sally Goodin, Bonaparte's Retreat._
- **Hornpipes:** _Fisher's Hornpipe, Rickett's Hornpipe, Durang's Hornpipe._
- **Jigs:** _Irish Washerwoman, Garryowen, Haste to the Wedding._
- **Waltzes and slow airs:** _Wearing of the Green, Aura Lee, Lorena, When You and I Were Young, Maggie._
- **Schottisches and polkas:** Imported by the German immigrants, picked up by the Anglo fiddlers in the 1860s and 1870s — _Heel and Toe Polka, Five-Step Schottische._
- **Trail and ballad airs:** _Streets of Laredo (The Cowboy's Lament), The Old Chisholm Trail, Sweet Betsy from Pike, Buffalo Gals, Whoopee Ti Yi Yo, Doney Gal, The Dying Cowboy._

The fiddler called the tune by name and the dancers knew the figures. The same tune might be played for fifteen minutes for a long set of figures and then the next tune called.

The frontier dance was a square dance or a round dance and was the principal social institution of the rural West. It was held in a barn freshly swept out, in a schoolhouse with the desks pushed against the wall, in a settler's parlor with the rug rolled up, in the dance hall of a town saloon, at the ranch headquarters at the end of a roundup. The caller (often the fiddler doubling) called the figures: _form your squares, allemande left, do-si-do, ladies in the center, gents around, swing your partner._ The dancers walked or danced through the figures while the fiddle drove the rhythm. The tunes ran twenty minutes or longer for the long sets. Round dances (waltz, polka, schottische, two-step) were the European imports, danced couple-to-couple around the room. They were faster, more intimate, and were what the young people preferred. The Methodist parents sometimes objected; the square dance and the play-party were the moral substitute (see Chapter 10).

The country dance went from sunset to one or two in the morning. The ranch dance, when the neighborhood gathered for a wedding or a barn-raising, ran all night and breakfast was served at dawn.

The trail hand sang to the cattle on night watch. The function was practical: the steady singing voice settled the herd and prevented a sudden sound from starting a stampede. The song could be anything the hand knew — a hymn, a sentimental ballad, a rough trail song, a Mexican song picked up at a previous outfit, a song he had made up himself. The trail repertoire that has come down: _The Old Chisholm Trail_ (its hundred-plus verses, most of them obscene), _Streets of Laredo_ (an Anglo adaptation of the Irish _Bard of Armagh_), _The Dying Cowboy (Bury Me Not on the Lone Prairie), Doney Gal, Goodbye Old Paint, The Cowboy's Lament, The Strawberry Roan, Sam Bass, Jesse James._ The hand sang quietly, almost to himself, often in a register higher than his speaking voice. The other hands at their stations could hear him faintly across the pasture.

## 2. The Home Instruments

- **The harmonica** (mouth organ, French harp) was the cheapest instrument. A small Hohner from Germany cost a dollar in the 1870s and the mercantile carried them. Every camp, every bunkhouse, every line shack had at least one. The hand played for himself and for the others around the fire. The repertoire overlapped the fiddle's but was simpler — single melody lines, the chord work limited.
- **The banjo** of the period was the four-string instrument descended from the African gourd banjo, by the 1870s standardized as a five-string factory instrument. The banjo was originally the Black instrument and was taken up by the white minstrel troupes from the 1840s onward. By the 1870s it was widespread among Anglo musicians, particularly in the Appalachian and Southern homestead families that had carried it west. A factory banjo cost five to fifteen dollars. Strings were gut. The technique was clawhammer or, by the late 1870s and 1880s, the new "guitar-style" picking that would become the bluegrass technique. The repertoire was the fiddle repertoire plus minstrel songs (_Old Dan Tucker, Camptown Races, De Boatmen's Dance_) and parlor songs.
- **The guitar** was less common in the rural West before the late 1880s. The Spanish guitar was the dominant instrument in Mexican households and at fandangos in the borderlands and California. The Anglo guitar arrived with the railroad and became common in the 1880s mail-order catalogues. A factory guitar cost six to fifteen dollars. The Mexican guitar player at a fandango or a serenade was a known frontier figure: the guitar across his lap, the soft Spanish songs, the corridos in his repertoire if he was political.
- **The parlor organ** (reed organ, melodeon) was the status instrument of the prosperous frontier home. A small reed organ from a mail-order catalogue cost forty to a hundred dollars by the 1880s and was found in the parlor of any merchant, doctor, or successful rancher. The mother or the elder daughter played it. The repertoire was hymns, parlor songs (_Sweet Hour of Prayer, Home Sweet Home, In the Sweet By and By, Beautiful Dreamer, Listen to the Mockingbird, Aura Lee, Lorena_), and simplified arrangements of the operatic airs. The Sunday evening at home gathered the family around the organ.
- **The piano** was the town instrument. The merchant's parlor in a substantial frontier town had one. The hotel parlor had one. The opera house had one or two grands. The upright cabinet piano, the standard parlor instrument from the 1870s onward, cost two to four hundred dollars. The square piano (older, going out of fashion in the 1870s) was cheaper used. The shipping of a piano to a frontier town was a major operation: by rail to the nearest depot, by freight wagon the rest of the way, the instrument crated and padded and unloaded by four men with ropes and rollers. The piano teacher (almost always a woman, often the schoolmarm, often the wife of a professional) gave lessons at fifty cents to a dollar a lesson.
- **The accordion and concertina.** The German immigrant brought the accordion and the concertina (different instruments — the accordion larger and louder, the concertina smaller and used for English country dance and the Royal Navy sailor's instrument). Both became part of the western repertoire in the German settlement areas of Texas, Nebraska, the Dakotas, Wisconsin, and Iowa. The Mexican accordion came up from Mexico in the 1850s and 1860s and became the heart of what would later be called Tejano and conjunto music. The polka rhythms of the German Hill Country of Texas crossed with the Mexican corrido tradition. The vocabulary, the technique, the dance forms all developed in the borderlands through the 1870s and 1880s.

## 3. The Saloon and the Dance Hall

The saloon piano was an upright in the corner near the bar. It was usually a battered factory instrument, often badly out of tune, often with keys missing. The professional saloon pianist (the "professor" or the "perfesser") played for tips and a small wage and free drinks. The repertoire was dictated by the customers' requests and the period's popular songs. By 1880 it included the parlor songs (_Silver Threads Among the Gold, In the Gloaming, Grandfather's Clock, The Old Oaken Bucket_), the minstrel songs, the sentimental ballads (_Lorena, Listen to the Mockingbird, Aura Lee, The Vacant Chair, Just Before the Battle Mother_), and the dance tunes.

Ragtime is the next decade. _Maple Leaf Rag_ was published in 1899. The 1870s and 1880s saloon pianist did not play ragtime; he played what preceded it. The earliest "coon songs" and syncopated dance music were emerging in the 1880s but ragtime as a defined form belongs to the 1890s and after.

The saloon pianist's social standing was low. He was assumed to drink. He was often a man whose respectability had broken down and who had landed at the saloon piano because the saloon piano was where he had landed. He was sometimes also a card sharp, a procurer, a small-time crook. He was sometimes a genuine artist whose circumstances had brought him there. The customer did not look closely.

The dance hall was a separate establishment from the saloon, though often connected. It contained a polished floor, a small stage for the musicians (typically a fiddle, a piano or banjo, sometimes a small horn), a row of chairs along the walls, and a bar. The women employed at the dance hall (the "dance hall girls," the "hurdy-gurdy girls") were not necessarily prostitutes, though the line moved by establishment and town. The respectable hurdy-gurdy of the 1860s and early 1870s in the mining camps employed German girls who danced with the miners for fifty cents a dance and split the take with the house. By the late 1870s and 1880s the dance hall had in many towns become a thinly disguised brothel; in others it remained a dance hall. The term covered both. The local reputation determined which.

The parlor house at the upper end (San Francisco, Denver, Leadville at its peak, Storyville in New Orleans by the 1890s) employed a piano player, a Black piano player by the 1880s as a frequent matter, who played the parlor songs and the sentimental ballads as the customers waited. The crib at the lower end had no music.

## Second Watch

> Pruitt was on second watch and had been singing “Streets of Laredo” for an hour when he ran out of verses he knew and started over. The herd was bedded along the dry wash and was not going to bolt. He sang the verses in the same low voice he had heard his uncle sing them in Lampasas in the year his mother died. The fiddler at the wagon had quit at supper and was asleep under the wagon. The other men were asleep where they had thrown their rolls. Pruitt rode the slow circle around the herd and sang the verse about the boy who had been shot in the breast and was dying.

## 4. Immigrant Musical Communities

The German singing society — the Liederkranz, the Sängerbund, the Turnverein — was a fixture of the German communities of San Antonio, New Braunfels, Fredericksburg, Denver, St. Louis, Davenport, Milwaukee. The society met weekly for choral rehearsal, performed at concerts and festivals, held an annual Sängerfest with neighboring societies. The repertoire was German part-songs, operatic choruses, the patriotic German repertoire. The Turnverein (turners' society) added physical culture to the music. The combined institution provided the German immigrant community with choir, gymnasium, social hall, lecture room, and beer garden under one roof.

The Welsh miners of the western coal fields and the slate quarries brought their chapel choral tradition. The four-part chapel choir, rehearsed weekly, performing at the chapel's services and at the annual Eisteddfod competition, was the center of Welsh community life.

The Czech communities of central Texas and central Nebraska brought the brass band and the polka tradition. The town band (cornets, alto horns, trombone, tuba, snare and bass drums) played at the town's Fourth of July, at the picnic, at the funeral, at the Saturday night dance.

The Mexican community of the borderlands and California maintained the corrido tradition (the narrative ballad — _El Corrido de Joaquín Murrieta_, the older Mexican romances of the colonial period). The fandango (the traditional dance), the serenata (the serenade under a young woman's window), the wedding dance with the mariachi-style ensemble (though "mariachi" as a labeled genre came later), the Catholic feast day with its processions and its singing — the calendar of Mexican community music was full.

The Chinese quarters of San Francisco, Portland, and the larger camps maintained Cantonese opera performances at the joss house or at a dedicated theater. The instruments (the erhu, the moon-guitar, the gongs, the cymbals, the wood block) and the high-pitched Cantonese vocal style were unfamiliar to Anglo ears and were generally described in the Anglo press of the period as a noise. The Chinese audience knew the operas and the actors and held them in the regard the Anglo audience held Edwin Booth.

## 5. Black Musical Traditions in the West

The Black population of the West was small in absolute numbers (fewer than thirty thousand in the western territories combined in the 1870 census) but musically significant.

The Buffalo Soldier regiments (the 9th and 10th Cavalry, the 24th and 25th Infantry) carried the spirituals, the work songs, and the post-bellum sacred song tradition with them. The regimental chaplain ran a choir at the post chapel. The soldiers sang on the march. The fiddlers and banjo players among them played at the post dances.

About a quarter of the trail crews on the post-war trail drives included Black hands. They contributed to the trail song repertoire and brought the Black musical sensibility to the drive. Some specific trail songs (_Get Along Home, Cindy_ in its trail variants) carry the Black imprint.

The Exoduster settlements of Kansas (Nicodemus and the smaller settlements from 1879 onward) maintained the Black church musical tradition: the spirituals, the camp meeting songs, the sacred harp shape-note tradition where it was carried. The church was the center of the community's music as it was of the community's life.

By the 1880s the Black piano player was working in the western saloons and dance halls of the larger towns. He played the popular repertoire and added the syncopated and the bluesy elements that would within a decade emerge as ragtime and within two as the early blues. The Black pianist's wage was typically lower than the white pianist's; his conditions were often worse; his musical contribution to the period's saloon music was foundational and was at the time uncredited.

## 6. The Medicine Show

The medicine show was the West's distinctive traveling entertainment. A wagon, sometimes elaborated into a small troupe with two or three wagons, traveled from town to town selling patent medicines under cover of a free show.

The wagon arrived in town in mid-afternoon and parked on the main street or the public square. The proprietor (the "doctor" or "professor") unloaded a small platform stage from the wagon, hung a banner advertising his medicine, and announced that a free show would commence at sundown. The show ran an hour or two: a banjo player, a comic, a magician or escape artist, a small "Indian" in feathered costume (often a non-Native performer in costume), a singer, a dance act. The acts were short. Between each act the proprietor gave a five-minute pitch for his medicine.

The medicine was a bottled compound of alcohol, opiates, herbal extracts, and water, sold for a dollar or two a bottle. The label promised cure for rheumatism, dyspepsia, female complaints, catarrh, the consumption, the ague, the falling hair. The medicine sometimes contained an active ingredient that produced a temporary effect; it sometimes contained nothing but alcohol. The customers bought.

The Kickapoo Indian Medicine Company was the largest of the medicine show operators by the late 1870s, with multiple shows on the road across the Midwest and West, employing white actors as "Kickapoo Indians," selling Sagwa and other nostrums. The Kickapoo shows were elaborate by medicine-show standards and could put a dozen performers on the platform.

The actual show was lower and rougher than the cinematic version. The customers were not fooled in the modern sense; they bought the medicine because the medicine contained alcohol and opium and worked on the customer's symptoms in the short term and because the show had been a free hour's pleasure on a Wednesday night in a town with no other amusement.

## 7. The Touring Theater and the Opera House

The West of the 1870s and 1880s was on the national theatrical circuit. The opera houses of San Francisco, Denver, Leadville, Tombstone, Virginia City, Salt Lake, Portland, Seattle, and the smaller mining towns at their peaks all booked touring companies from the East.

- **The actors who came:** Edwin Booth, the leading American tragedian, toured the West repeatedly in the 1870s and 1880s — Hamlet, Lear, Iago, Richelieu, Brutus, the standard tragic repertoire. Lawrence Barrett, Booth's frequent partner. Joseph Jefferson, the popular comic actor, famous for his Rip Van Winkle, which he played for forty years. Adelaide Neilson, the English tragedienne, toured America in the late 1870s playing Juliet, Rosalind, Imogen. Sarah Bernhardt toured America in the 1880s and reached the West Coast. Lillian Russell, Lotta Crabtree (who had begun her career in the California mining camps in the 1850s), Maude Adams. John McCullough, the American tragedian, a rival to Booth, toured extensively in the West before his collapse in 1884.
- **What they played:** The Shakespeare repertoire (Hamlet, Macbeth, Romeo and Juliet, Othello, Lear, Richard III, Julius Caesar, the comedies less often). The popular sentimental melodramas (_East Lynne, Camille, The Lady of Lyons, Lady Audley's Secret, The Octoroon_). The blood-and-thunder spectacles (_The Black Crook_, the long-running musical extravaganza, toured the West repeatedly). New work (Ibsen arrived late in the period and was controversial).

Genuine opera (sung-through European opera) reached the larger western cities. The Tabor Grand Opera House in Denver (opened 1881) booked opera tours from New York. The San Francisco opera houses had genuine opera from the 1850s onward. The "opera house" in a smaller town was often a multipurpose theater; the term carried prestige and was used to attract bookings. The Tombstone Bird Cage Theatre, the Leadville Tabor Opera House, the Central City Opera House (Colorado, the oldest of the surviving frontier opera houses, opened 1878) were genuine theaters that booked the same companies that played the East Coast.

The minstrel show was the dominant American popular theatrical form from the 1840s through the 1880s. A minstrel troupe (a dozen white men in blackface plus a handful of musicians) toured the country performing a defined three-part program: the opening (the troupe seated in a semicircle with the interlocutor at the center and the endmen, Tambo and Bones, on the wings, with songs, witticisms, and stump speeches), the olio (a variety bill), and the afterpiece (a short comic sketch). The minstrel show was viciously racist by any modern standard. It was also enormously popular and was the principal form through which Black musical material entered white American popular culture (in distorted and demeaning form). The period audience laughed at the racial caricature without reflection. The smaller "specialty" companies — the Christy Minstrels, the Bryant Minstrels, Callender's Original Georgia Minstrels (which by the 1870s was a Black troupe in blackface, an additional layer of period racial complexity) — reached the smaller mining towns.

The Tom show was a traveling theatrical company that toured a single show, _Uncle Tom's Cabin_, often as an elaborate spectacle with bloodhounds and ice floes and a dozen Eliza-with-baby crossings. The Tom shows were a recognized subcategory of the touring theater and toured continuously from the 1850s through the 1920s.

The lyceum lecture circuit (older, established in the 1830s) and the Chautauqua (newer, established 1874 in upstate New York and spreading west by the 1880s) brought the lecture-and-edification model to the West. The traveling lecturer (the temperance lecturer, the political orator, the science demonstrator with his magic lantern, the elocutionist with her recitations) appeared at the local opera house or church for an evening's edifying entertainment.

## 8. The Circus

The traveling circus reached the West by rail in the 1870s and by wagon before. P. T. Barnum's Greatest Show on Earth (1871, by 1881 in combination with the Bailey show as Barnum & Bailey's) and the smaller competitors (Forepaugh, Sells Brothers, Hagenbeck, Robinson) toured the West annually.

The arrival was the morning's event. The wagons unloaded from the railroad cars; the parade formed and went down the main street with the band wagon, the calliope, the elephants, the caged tigers, the clowns, the costumed riders; the tents went up on the lot at the edge of town; the matinee at two and the evening performance at eight; the takedown by midnight; the train out before dawn for the next town.

The performance was the standard three-ring (after 1872, when Barnum and Coup pioneered the form): horseback acts, acrobats, clowns, animal acts (lions and tigers, elephants, performing dogs), the high-wire act, the trapeze, the strongman, the freak show in the side tent.

The side tent's "human curiosities" (the bearded woman, the giant, the dwarf, the conjoined twins, the tattooed man, the wild man, the fat lady, the albino) were a defined category of frontier entertainment. The customers paid an extra dime to enter the side tent and saw the performers on small platforms with their banners and their patter. The treatment of the human freak by the period was exploitative by any modern measure and was also, for the performer, often the only available livelihood. Some performers maintained their dignity within the format and earned substantial wages. Most were paid little and lived in the side tent's specific small society on the road.

## 9. Wild West Shows

The Wild West show was a specific late-period phenomenon. Buffalo Bill Cody opened his "Wild West, Rocky Mountain and Prairie Exhibition" in 1883 in Omaha. By the late 1880s it was an international touring spectacle. An outdoor arena performance combined cowboy contest acts (rough-riding, roping, shooting), historical reenactments (the attack on the Deadwood stage, the attack on the settler's cabin, Custer's last stand), Native performers (genuine Lakota and other Plains performers under contract), the sharpshooting acts (Annie Oakley and her contemporaries), the cavalry drill team. Two and a half hours of action.

The Wild West shows employed Native performers under contract through the 1880s and 1890s. The performers were genuine Lakota, Cheyenne, Arapaho, Pawnee, and others, often warriors or chiefs of recent reservation memory. They earned wages, they traveled, they saw the East and Europe, they returned home with cash and stories. The arrangement was ambiguous: it provided income and a measure of dignity unavailable on the reservation, and it depended on the public performance of the warrior identity that the federal policy was simultaneously trying to eradicate. Sitting Bull toured with Buffalo Bill briefly in 1885 and disliked it but the income mattered. Other figures spent years on the show circuit.

The Wild West show emerged at the moment the historical West it depicted was ending. The 1880s audience watched the buffalo hunt staged in an arena while the actual buffalo were almost gone and the actual hunting had already ended. The conventions of the western that the twentieth-century movies inherited (the cowboy as romantic hero, the Native as warrior antagonist, the sharpshooter, the stagecoach holdup) were codified by the Wild West show before the dime novel had quite finished codifying them. A scene set at a Wild West show is a scene about the gap between what was and what is now being sold as what was.

## 10. The Rodeo

The rodeo as a defined competitive event begins in the 1880s. Before that, informal cowboy contests at the end of a roundup or at a town's Fourth of July had existed since the Mexican vaquero tradition arrived in the Anglo Southwest in the 1830s and 1840s. The Pecos, Texas claim of "first rodeo" (1883) and the Prescott, Arizona claim (1888, the first formal "Cowboy Tournament" with prizes and a program) mark the period when the informal contest crystallized into a public event with admission charged.

The events of the early rodeo: **bronc riding** (the cowhand mounted a green unbroken horse and rode until the horse was thrown or the rider was — no saddle in the early contests; saddle bronc riding was added later); **steer roping** (the mounted hand roped a running steer by the horns or by the front feet and threw it; the time was kept); **steer wrestling (bulldogging)** added later in the period (Bill Pickett, the Black cowboy from the 101 Ranch, popularized the technique by 1903 — not a 1870s or 1880s event in the standard form); **calf roping** (as steer roping but for the smaller animal); **wild horse race** (a team race to catch, saddle, and ride a wild horse across a finish line); **ladies' relay race** (added by the late 1880s as the rodeo developed its public character). The bull riding event in its modern form is later (twentieth century).

The Mexican vaquero contests of the California ranchos and the Texas ranchos predated the Anglo rodeo and contributed many of its events. The charreada (the Mexican formal mounted contest) is an older and more elaborate form that continues in Mexico. The Anglo rodeo borrowed from it and simplified it.

By the late 1880s the cattle towns held annual "Frontier Days" and "Cowboy Reunion" celebrations combining the rodeo events with horse races, parades, and a dance. Cheyenne Frontier Days began 1897; the Pendleton Round-Up began 1910. The 1880s precursors were less formal.

## 11. The Newspaper, the Magazine, the Dime Novel

Print was entertainment as well as information.

The frontier weekly small-town paper (covered also in Chapter 9) was read aloud at the family table or at the saloon by the man who could read for the men who could not. The serialized fiction in the newspaper (Dickens, the popular novelists, the local color writers) was a substantial portion of the household's reading.

The frontier home subscribed to one or two magazines and read them through: The Atlantic Monthly, Harper's Magazine, Scribner's Monthly, the Century, Frank Leslie's Illustrated Newspaper, Harper's Weekly, the Police Gazette (at the lower end). The arrival of the magazine was an event.

Beadle's Dime Novels (founded 1860) and its competitors (Munro's, Street & Smith's, Frank Tousey's) sold cheap pulp fiction for ten cents. The western was the dominant subject by the 1870s. _Deadwood Dick, the Black Rider of the Black Hills_ (Edward Wheeler, 1877 and after); _Buffalo Bill, King of the Border Men_ (Ned Buntline, 1869, the foundational novel of the Buffalo Bill myth); _Calamity Jane, the Heroine of Whoop-Up_; the Frank Reade boy-inventor stories; the Old Sleuth detective stories.

The dime novel was the boy's reading and the working-class adult's reading. It was disapproved of by the educators and the ministers. It was read anyway. The conventions of the western (the gunfighter, the road agent, the cattle rustler, the Indian fight, the climactic shootout) were partly invented by the dime novelists in the 1870s and 1880s and were imposed retroactively on the actual West when the Wild West show and then the early movies needed material. The 1870s and 1880s reader already knew the conventions. The dime novel was already shaping what the actual frontier participant thought he was doing while he was doing it.

## 12. The Vocabulary

- **Music.** _Fiddle_ (the working term; _violin_ was the parlor and concert term for the same instrument). _Bow_ — the fiddler's hair-on-stick; _hair_ was the bow's working portion. _Rosin_ — the block of pine resin the fiddler rubbed his bow with. _Tune_ (the piece itself); _air_ for the slower ones. _Reel, hornpipe, jig, breakdown, schottische, polka, waltz, two-step_ — the dance forms. _Call_ (verb and noun) — what the caller did at the square dance. _Set_ — the square of four couples. _Hoedown_ — the country dance (originally the fast breakdown tune; by extension the dance itself). _Frolic_ — the country dance, particularly the informal at-home version. _Play-party_ — the young people's gathering with singing games in the Methodist and Baptist households where dancing was forbidden. _The professor_ (or _perfesser_) — the saloon pianist. _Hurdy-gurdy_ — the dance hall; the dance hall girl. _Calliope_ — the steam-organ on the circus wagon and the steamboat (pronounced _kal-LIE-oh-pee_ in the period American).
- **Theater.** _The boards_ — the stage. _The bill_ — the advertised program. _The opry house_ — the rural pronunciation of opera house. _Drop, flat, flies, wings, apron_ — the stage technical vocabulary. _Trouper_ — a traveling performer. _Barnstormer_ — a performer who toured the small towns. _Stock company_ — the resident company at a theater playing a rotating repertory. _Star, leading man, leading lady, juvenile, ingenue, character, heavy_ — the roles.
- **Circus.** _The lot_ — the field where the tents were pitched. _The big top_ — the main tent. _The side show._ _The midway_ — the path between the tents with the food vendors and the games. _The ballyhoo_ — the pitch the side show talker gave. _The mark_ — the customer. _The grift_ — the cheating games run by the short-con men attached to some circuses. _Roustabout_ — the circus laborer. _Razorback_ — the roustabout who unloaded the wagons from the train (origin: _raise her back_ — the call to lift the wagon's back from the flatcar).
- **Saloon and dance hall.** _Crib_ — the single-occupancy prostitute's room. _Parlor house_ — the high-end brothel. _Madam_ — the woman who ran the parlor house or the dance hall. _Sporting woman, fancy woman, soiled dove, girl on the line, woman of the line_ — the prostitute, by register. _Painted lady_ — disparaging. _Bouncer_ — the man employed to handle drunk or violent customers. _Dive, deadfall, fancy house, sporting house_ — categories of saloon by reputation.

## 13. Sound, Smell, Light

- **The country dance.** Fiddle and banjo and the stamp of boots on the puncheon floor. The caller's voice over the music. The smell of the kerosene lamps and the men's sweat and the women's lavender water and the chewing tobacco. The cool night air through the open door. The light: kerosene lamps in the corners and the lantern hung over the caller. The dancers' faces flushed and shining. The mothers' chairs along the wall and the babies asleep on the bed in the back room.
- **The saloon.** The piano and the off-key voice of the man at the bar and the click of the chips at the faro table. The smell of stale beer and tobacco smoke and unwashed men and the sawdust on the floor. The kerosene chandeliers and the mirrored back-bar reflecting them. The pianist's back to the room.
- **The medicine show.** The proprietor's voice carrying down the street. The banjo and the bones. The smell of the lamplight and the crowd. The kerosene-and-reflector lamps on poles around the platform. The bottle held up in the proprietor's hand and the lamplight catching it.
- **The opera house.** The orchestra tuning. The murmur of the audience. The smell of the gaslight (where there was gas service) or the kerosene (where there was not) and the perfume of the women in the boxes. The gas footlights or the kerosene chimney lamps along the front of the stage. The curtain. The first hush as the curtain rises.
- **The circus.** The calliope from a quarter mile off. The drum and the horn of the band wagon. The roar of the animals from the menagerie tent. The smell of the elephants and the cooking grease and the sawdust and the canvas. The kerosene flares around the tent's perimeter at night. The crowd's faces upturned to the high wire.
