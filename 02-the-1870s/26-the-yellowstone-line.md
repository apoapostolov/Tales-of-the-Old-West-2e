# Chapter 26 — The Yellowstone Line

> _"The flies were thick on the horse lines. A mule stamped and rattled its chain. Farther off, by the parade ground, somebody was laughing too loud over a bottle. Fort Ellis looked steady in the late light. Whitewashed walls. Flag limp in the heat. Wagons in a row. Men who believed a post and a pay chest made a hard country obey._
>
> _It took one dead teamster and a saddle cut loose in the dark to show what the country thought of that."_

## Historical Frame

The Yellowstone basin forces an `1873` Montana campaign open.

Nothing in it sits quiet. The Army wants prestige and safe roads. Surveyors want a route other men will turn into stock, town lots, and contracts. Gallatin merchants want the rails bent toward their stores. Crow scouts want the Army deaf to everything but Crow interests. Lakota riders mean to bleed the white line before it crosses good hunting ground. The margins wait to see which ruin arrives first. The player characters drop into that violence. They start as contract hands: guards, teamsters, scouts, hunters, clerks, discharged soldiers, gamblers, or guides. By the end of the chapter, they see Army weakness, private greed, Lakota force, and the cost of carrying paper men kill for.

The column goes one direction. The party moves with it. Inside the march, players choose who they serve and what they sell, hide, save, or burn. Those choices build the reckoning.

> Historical note: In `1873`, railroad pressure in Montana remained mostly survey stakes. Men fought over routes, grades, and paper before locomotives crossed the territory.

## The Shape of It

The party joins the column at Fort Ellis. A killing and a night raid prove the rot started before the wagons moved. Field work turns political. A Crow warning offers a clean path while greed and Army pride demand another. The papers split. Men shoot one another in camp. The expedition shatters into frightened men, wounded stock, and rival truths. What survives the ride back becomes the first leverage players hold in Montana.

The events are tight but not locked. Parties search wagons early, press liars, ride after the wrong man, pocket papers, threaten officers, bargain with enemies, or wash their hands of the white enterprise. Let them shift the balance. Controlling the line changes who fears them, who trusts them, and who uses them.

## Why a Character Rides with the Line

No decent person loves this expedition. Motives come mixed. A discharged trooper signs on because Army bacon beats luck, or because Captain Rowe knows his name from older roads. A hunter rides because survey wages feed a horse through winter. A drifter only wants a place near the payroll. Montana holds men who start without belief and stay because money and blood already changed hands.

Ties bind tighter. One character holds Gallatin kin and knows an open-country route choice lifts one town and starves the next. Another hauled freight for Pike and reads theft in his wagon harness. A clerk or draughtsman rides because paper means power, and ink shows where the real money settles first. Give each character one thread: prior service with Rowe, odd work under Reed, debt in Bozeman, shelter taken at Calder Ranch, or acquaintance with LaRoche. Those ties turn the lies personal.

## What Is Moving Here

The visible task is escort and survey. Four fights run beneath it. The Army wants safety and a clean report. Surveyors want route control and private paper. Crow scouts shape movement and terms. Lakota resistance bleeds the line before it hardens to rail. Players begin low enough to catch truth from all corners. Nobody thinks them important enough to lie to carefully yet.

## Consequence Ledger

Track party standing with four groups: `Army Respect`, `Survey Leverage`, `Crow Trust`, and `Local Goodwill`. Start each at `0`. When players make a hard choice, shift the tracks `+1` or `-1`. The ride back into settlement cashes the totals.

- High `Army Respect` opens Fort Ellis work and legal shelter.
- High `Survey Leverage` means players hold paper Bozeman interests crave.
- High `Crow Trust` opens warnings and southern country access.
- High `Local Goodwill` makes ranchers and merchants a net beneath the party.
- Low scores build walls, ruin credit, and run bad stories ahead of them.

## What the Main Factions Want

- **Rowe and the Army:** Rowe wants the column out and back cleanly. He refuses fraud or pointless death.
- **Reed and the survey men:** Reed fights bad politics. He is failing.
- **Vane and the private line:** Vane wants route knowledge before public release.
- **Pike and the freight interest:** Pike wants billable delay and profitable urgency.
- **Shows-the-Ford and Crow interests:** Shows-the-Ford slows the column and redirects the blind push.
- **Iron Hail and Lakota resistance:** Iron Hail intends to bloody the survey and strip its paper.

## Clue Discipline

One failed roll does not end the chapter.

Spread facts across layers.

- Bell's killing points to corruption.
- Keene points to copied notes.
- Mercer points to divided packets.
- Vane points to route dealing.
- Pike points to freight theft.
- Shows-the-Ford points to the cost of the push.

If players miss a clue, move the truth into another mouth, pocket, or shallow grave.

## Camp Scenes Between Major Events

Use these scenes when players want to live in the column.

- **The bad coffee fire:** Troopers and a survey hand argue over axle-grease coffee.
- **Reed at the notebook:** Reed scratches margin notes by lantern light.
- **Pike counting sacks:** Pike checks flour, bacon, and harness when nobody watches.
- **LaRoche listening in the dark:** LaRoche catches three fires at once without moving.
- **Harlan with a bottle:** Harlan takes enough drink to confess bad ambition.
- **Mercer trying to be liked:** Mercer pushes tobacco and cards to build cover.
- **Horse line at midnight:** The line lives. Animals stamp, blow, and spook.
- **A distant fire in the wrong place:** One watcher catches a fire where no camp sits.
- **A prayer over Bell:** A laundress says hard words over Bell's body.
- **The spoiled flour barrel:** One Pike barrel rots low where the leak hides.
- **Cards with Keene:** Keene joins a game trying to look ordinary.
- **A horse gone lame for no clean reason:** A good mount comes up sore from bad luck or sabotage.
- **LaRoche and the weather sign:** LaRoche reads cloud and wind while others argue.
- **Hannah's plain ledger:** Hannah shows hard accounts: high nails, bought-damp flour, and heavy team hire.

## Side Events on the Line

Use these stops to drive pressure and divide the expedition.

- **The wrong grave by the creek:** Weather exposed a trail grave.
- **A rider under white cloth:** A man rides in under white cloth claiming to carry Vane's friends or Pike's contractors.
- **The missing instrument screw:** A piece from Reed's gear vanishes.
- **A wounded horse and a faster choice:** A horse snaps a leg in bad ground.
- **A settler's claim marker:** A pathetic claim marker sits on good ground.
- **The wrong cartridges:** Army issue turns up in civilian pockets, or civilian bullets hide in survey bags.

## If the Players Freeze or Break the Plan

The opener survives broken fights and dead men.

- **If Keene dies early:** Move his copy to Mercer, Bell's satchel, or Reed's spare case.
- **If Mercer dies early:** Players find his cipher marks, account slips, or letter drafts.
- **If Vane drops dead in the field:** Shift his role to a Bozeman banker, a hired rider, or Pike.
- **If Rowe loses command:** Duffy holds the line. Reed forces the field books back.
- **If the party openly sides with Pike:** Let them. Pike pays them and works them until silence becomes cheaper than loyalty.
- **If the party sides with Iron Hail or refuses the white mission:** The chapter becomes sabotage, flight, and political rupture.
- **If the party brings court-proof evidence:** Give them marked pay slips, mismatched copies, and a living witness holding explicit orders.

## Arc I: Fort Ellis and the First Miles

Let the opening breathe. The party hears claims before field mud proves them worthless.

### Fort Ellis

Read or paraphrase:

> _Late summer hangs dry over Fort Ellis. The mountains look blue in the south. The parade ground churns under hooves and wagon wheels. Soldiers haul crates. Laborers bicker. A survey tripod sits near stacked grain. Nobody rests. Too many men draw pay from the same table._

> Historical note: Fort Ellis sat east of Bozeman, planted in `1867` to cover the Gallatin Valley and watch the roads to the Bozeman, Bridger, and Flathead country.

Captain Rowe or Sergeant Duffy musters the players. Army pay lacks weight but offers cash, rations, and a standing horse line.

The fort reeks of horse sweat, old ash, and thin soap. Pike's yard shows the rot early. Mercer tests for loneliness with tobacco and cards. The players can haggle pay, challenge civilian authority, search paper chests, or keep their heads down and listen.

### Supper with liars

The principals eat near the officers' quarters.

Rowe talks duty and escort limits. Reed talks grade, crossings, and the damage done by map-dreamers. Vane talks territorial future without binding his word. Pike talks freight cost and forage lines like he holds the only arithmetic in Montana.

### The picket-line killing

Near dark, mule skinner Joe Bell bleeds out by the remuda.

A heavy blow struck him. A blade cut his jaw to finish it. One hitch rope snapped. A saddle blanket and a dispatch satchel vanished.

The ground tracks trouble. Army issue heels cross civilian boots. A survey pencil shaving rests near Bell's hand. Bell drank with Mercer. Earlier, Bell watched Thomas Keene dig through a chest.

### Raid on the remuda

The first shot drops from outside. The second comes from the line. The horses break. A bay tears loose on a stake rope. A mule screams. Men shout for a satchel and pray to God in the dark.

Muzzle flashes cut bad angles. Raiders hunt panic, a loose remuda, an inside cutter's escape, and the leather wallet. Saving the line matters. A lost line cripples the march.

**Combat Encounter: Raid on the Remuda**
- **Enemies:** `1 REMUDA CUTTER` inside the line, `4-6 HIRED RIDERS`, and `1-2 HIRED WHITE SABOTEURS`.
- **Area:** The Fort Ellis horse yard: picket ropes, hitch rails, feed crates, wagons, and packed animals.
- **Terrain and Effects:** Darkness blurs sightlines. Loose horses knock men down, foul firing lines, and force `MOVE` and `ANIMAL HANDLIN'` tests.

## Arc II: Markers, Forks, and the Closing Country

Escort work dies. The fight for the map begins.

### Sorting the column

The column breaks Fort Ellis at first light. The road turns to wheel tracks, cut banks, and the knowledge that turning back demands an explanation.

### The route fork

The line hits a split.

One route drops easy and feeds freight and Bozeman pockets. The other holds better grade, serves military logic, and crosses deeper Lakota hunting ground. Reed narrows his eyes at the dirt. Vane narrows his eyes at Reed.

### Attack on the marker line

The hit drops while the crew spreads out. Open ground. Surveyors exposed. Saddles empty. Iron Hail's riders want smashed glass, dead confidence, and burning notebooks.

**Combat Encounter: Attack on the Marker Line**
- **Enemies:** `1 IRON HAIL`, `1-2 LAKOTA WAR SCOUTS`, and `5-7 LAKOTA RIDERS`.
- **Area:** Open ground, brush pockets, markers, chains, tripods, and the paper wagon.
- **Terrain and Effects:** Rises and brush offer partial cover. Standing still invites execution.

### Camp after the attack

Night fractures the camp. Rowe demands discipline. Reed demands measurements. Vane demands blood on the line to prove its worth. Pike demands safety for his wagons.

### The Crow warning

Shows-the-Ford rides in under white caution or calls Baptiste LaRoche. He offers no free salvation. He calls the heavy line an error and trades safer crossings for truth, trade goods, medicine, or blank maps.

### The ford fight

The column hits the water. Slick mud, half-loaded wagons, and thigh-deep freezing water drag on the party. Iron Hail strikes the weakest point.

**Combat Encounter: The Ford Fight**
- **Enemies:** `1 IRON HAIL`, `1 LAKOTA WAR SCOUT`, `4-6 LAKOTA RIDERS`.
- **Area:** Deep current, muddy banks, stuck wagons, and heavy brush.
- **Terrain and Effects:** Water slows tests and punishes movement. Mud forces `MOVE` survival.

### What the dead man carried

Mercer drops after the ford, or he survives long enough to babble.

His body yields copied field notes, Pike's false checks, route notations, and the initials tying Bell's throat to Keene's terror.

## Arc III: Broken Column

The expedition shatters around three leather folders.

### Camp of accusations

The truce snaps. Rowe demands military guard. Reed demands technical possession. Vane offers gold for copies. Pike commands the destruction of his billings. Keene trades his life for a horse.

### The copied packet

Players control the core packet: field notes, Pike's fraud, Vane's treason, or Mercer's copies.

Delivering everything boosts `Army Respect`. Holding routes locks `Survey Leverage`. Holding Pike's fraud jacks `Local Goodwill`. Withholding Lakota maps builds `Crow Trust`.

### Midnight in broken camp

The violence breaks inside the line. Vane's hired men, Pike's freight muscle, or Lakota riders drive the last shock through the tents.

**Combat Encounter: Midnight in Broken Camp**
- **Enemies:** `2-4 FREIGHT TOUGHS` and `2-4 HIRED RIDERS` for Pike/Vane. `4-6 LAKOTA RIDERS` for outer pressure. `1 HIRED WHITE SABOTEUR` for total camp collapse.
- **Area:** A split camp: tents, mules, wounded men, and volatile crates.
- **Terrain and Effects:** Lanterns light islands. Canvas offers little protection. Panic forces `PRESENCE` and `MOVE` responses.

### Breaking point

Morning forces the final choice. Rowe wants the wounded. Reed wants the books. Vane buys the map. Pike dumps the liability. Players organize the retreat.

## Reckoning

Fort Ellis or Bozeman hold the finale. Offices judge what carbines could not finish.

### Who receives the papers

Every man wants the delivery room. Rowe demands the desk. Reed demands the office. Vane demands silence. Pike demands ash.

### Local pressure

`Local Goodwill` lands here. High marks force open doors and honest witnesses. Low marks lock rooms and distribute bribes.

### Final standoff

Rowe arrests Vane in a stable. Vane issues a buyout in a warehouse. Pike attacks in daylight. The choice boils between legal custody and living survival.

**Combat Encounter: Final Standoff in Settlement Space**
- **Enemies:** `1 GIDEON PIKE` with `2-4 FREIGHT TOUGHS`, or `1 SILAS VANE` with `2-3 HIRED RIDERS` and `1 HIRED WHITE SABOTEUR`.
- **Area:** Alleys, stairwells, boarding rooms, offices, or freight yards.
- **Terrain and Effects:** Wood, mud, windows, and packed spaces. Witnesses penalize blind shooting.

### End states

- **Army victory** — the packet lands clean.
- **Railroad shadow victory** — the players sell or hold copies and become hunted.
- **Local Montana victory** — fraud hits the light and ranchers win a breath.
- **Crow understanding** — respect buys safer passage.
- **Bad ending, good sandbox** — liars prosper and the party flees carrying proof.

## Sandbox Exits

Bozeman and Fort Ellis offer military runs. Helena demands paper and blackmail. Virginia City demands law. Fort Benton demands river freight. Southern boundaries demand Crow promises fulfilled.

## GM Notes on Tone

Men demand cash, horses, contracts, and power. No one speaks their theme. They demand the payload. The rest bleeds out later.
